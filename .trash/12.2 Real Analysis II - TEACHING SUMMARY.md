## Motivation & Historical Context

### Why Integration Theory Matters

Real Analysis II bridges intuitive calculus integration with rigorous theory, then extends to even more powerful frameworks.

**Key Problems It Solves:**
- **Which functions are integrable?** Riemann's criterion isn't obvious
- **When can we interchange limits and integrals?** Critical for series and sequences
- **What's the "right" notion of integral?** Lebesgue's approach handles more functions

**Historical Development:**
- **1854**: Riemann defines his integral
- **1870s**: Weierstrass discovers continuous nowhere-differentiable functions
- **1902**: Lebesgue introduces measure-theoretic integration
- **1910s**: Functional analysis emerges from studying function spaces

## Core Concepts

### Concept 1: Rigorous Integration

Riemann integration via Darboux sums: partition interval, take sup/inf on each subinterval, refine partitions.

**Teaching insight**: Contrast upper/lower sums visually—area of rectangles above/below curve.

### Concept 2: Uniform Convergence

Stronger than pointwise convergence—one N works for all x simultaneously.

**Why it matters**: Preserves continuity, allows term-by-term operations.

**Common confusion**: Students think pointwise convergence is enough—it's not!

### Concept 3: Function Spaces

Treat functions as points in infinite-dimensional vector spaces.

**Key insight**: C[a,b] with sup norm is complete (Banach), but polynomials are dense (Weierstrass).

---

## Key Definitions

**Darboux Integral**: Upper integral = lower integral.

**Uniform Convergence**: $f_n \to f$ uniformly if $\sup_x |f_n(x) - f(x)| \to 0$.

**Banach Space**: Complete normed vector space.

**Hilbert Space**: Complete inner product space.

---

## Main Theorems

### Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Rigorous)

If $f$ continuous on $[a,b]$ and $F(x) = \int_a^x f$, then $F'(x) = f(x)$.

**Proof strategy**: Use Mean Value Theorem for integrals.

### Uniform Limit Theorem

Uniform limit of continuous functions is continuous.

**Counterexample when not uniform**: $f_n(x) = x^n$ on $[0,1]$ converges to discontinuous function.

### Dominated Convergence Theorem

If $f_n \to f$ pointwise and $|f_n| \leq g$ with $g$ integrable, then $\int f_n \to \int f$.

**Why crucial**: Allows swapping limit and integral under mild conditions.

## Problem-Solving Techniques

### Proving Integrability

Show upper and lower Darboux sums converge to same value.

**Strategy**: For continuous functions, use uniform continuity on compact intervals.

### Testing Uniform Convergence

1. Find pointwise limit first
2. Compute $\sup_x |f_n(x) - f(x)|$
3. Show this supremum → 0

**Common trick**: Maximum often occurs at endpoints or critical points.

### Applying Convergence Theorems

**Checklist**:
- Pointwise convergence? ✓
- Dominating function? ✓
- Measurable? ✓
- Then swap limit and integral!

## Teaching Tips

### Explaining Uniform Convergence

**Analogy**: Pointwise = everyone eventually arrives (but at different times). Uniform = everyone arrives by time N.

**Visual**: Draw epsilon band around limit function—all $f_n$ (for large n) must stay in band everywhere.

### Common Misconceptions

|Misconception|Correction|
|---|---|
|"Pointwise convergence preserves continuity"|Need uniform convergence|
|"All continuous functions are Riemann integrable"|True, but not all bounded functions|
|"Lebesgue integral is always different"|Agrees with Riemann when both exist|
|"L^2 convergence implies pointwise"|No! Only convergence in mean|

### Assessment Strategies

**Conceptual questions**:
- "Why doesn't $x^n$ converge uniformly on [0,1]?"
- "Give example where $\lim \int f_n \neq \int \lim f_n$"
- "Why do we need measure theory?"
