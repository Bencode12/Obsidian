## Leibniz Integral Rule

### Statement

If $f(x,t)$ is continuous on $[a,b] \times [c,d]$ and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ exists and is continuous, then:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_c^d f(x,t)\,dt = \int_c^d \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,t)\,dt$$

We can **differentiate under the integral sign**!

> [!info] Tao Reference
> See *Analysis II* (4th ed.), Chapter 11: The Riemann Integral, Section 11.11 (pp. 330-335)

---

## Variable Limits

### General Formula

If the limits also depend on $x$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_{a(x)}^{b(x)} f(x,t)\,dt = f(x,b(x))b'(x) - f(x,a(x))a'(x) + \int_{a(x)}^{b(x)} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,t)\,dt$$

This combines the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus with differentiation under the integral!

### Special Cases

**Constant limits**:
$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_c^d f(x,t)\,dt = \int_c^d \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,t)\,dt$$

**Variable upper limit**:
$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(x,t)\,dt = f(x,x) + \int_a^x \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,t)\,dt$$

## Examples

### Example 1: Simple Case

Compute $\frac{d}{dx}\int_0^1 e^{xt}\,dt$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_0^1 e^{xt}\,dt = \int_0^1 te^{xt}\,dt$$

Evaluate:
$$\int_0^1 te^{xt}\,dt = \left[\frac{te^{xt}}{x} - \frac{e^{xt}}{x^2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{e^x}{x} - \frac{e^x-1}{x^2}$$

### Example 2: Variable Limits

Compute $\frac{d}{dx}\int_0^{x^2} \sin(xt)\,dt$:

Using the general formula:
$$= \sin(x^3) \cdot 2x + \int_0^{x^2} t\cos(xt)\,dt$$

## Applications

### Computing Difficult Integrals

**Example**: Evaluate $I = \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(x)}{x}\,dx$

Consider $I(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha x}\frac{\sin(x)}{x}\,dx$ for $\alpha > 0$.

Differentiate:
$$I'(\alpha) = -\int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha x}\sin(x)\,dx = -\frac{1}{1+\alpha^2}$$

Integrate back:
$$I(\alpha) = -\arctan(\alpha) + C$$

As $\alpha \to \infty$, $I(\alpha) \to 0$, so $C = \frac{\pi}{2}$.

Therefore: $I(0) = \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(x)}{x}\,dx = \frac{\pi}{2}$

### Gaussian Integral Derivative

Compute $\frac{d}{da}\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-ax^2}\,dx$:

$$\frac{d}{da}\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-ax^2}\,dx = \int_{-\infty}^\infty -x^2e^{-ax^2}\,dx$$

This generates moments of the Gaussian distribution!

## Justification: When Can We Do This?

### Sufficient Conditions

We can differentiate under the integral if:
1. $f(x,t)$ is continuous in both variables
2. $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ exists and is continuous
3. The integral converges uniformly (for infinite intervals)

### Dominated Convergence (Advanced)

If there exists an integrable function $g(t)$ such that:
$$\left|\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,t)\right| \leq g(t)$$

Then differentiation under the integral is valid.

---

## Higher-Order Derivatives

We can differentiate multiple times:

$$\frac{d^n}{dx^n}\int_c^d f(x,t)\,dt = \int_c^d \frac{\partial^n f}{\partial x^n}(x,t)\,dt$$

Provided all derivatives exist and are continuous.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Forgetting Boundary Terms
> When limits depend on $x$, don't forget the FTC terms! Use the full formula.

> [!warning] Ignoring Convergence Issues
> For improper integrals, verify uniform convergence before differentiating.

> [!warning] Assuming It Always Works
> Need continuity of the partial derivative! Pathological counterexamples exist.
