## Definition

A **function space** is a vector space whose elements are functions, often equipped with additional structure like a norm or metric.

> [!info] Tao Reference
> See *Analysis II* (4th ed.), Chapter 14: Uniform Convergence, Section 14.5 (pp. 385-390)

---

## Common Function Spaces

### Continuous Functions $C([a,b])$

The space of all continuous real-valued functions on $[a,b]$.

**Norm**: $\|f\|_\infty = \sup_{x \in [a,b]} |f(x)|$ (supremum norm)

This makes $C([a,b])$ a **normed vector space**.

### Bounded Functions $B([a,b])$

All bounded functions on $[a,b]$, with the same supremum norm.

Note: $C([a,b]) \subset B([a,b])$.

### Riemann Integrable Functions $\mathcal{R}([a,b])$

All Riemann integrable functions on $[a,b]$.

Not a normed space with sup norm (functions can differ on sets of measure zero).

---

## Metric Structure

### Supremum Metric

For $f,g \in C([a,b])$:

$$d(f,g) = \|f-g\|_\infty = \sup_{x \in [a,b]} |f(x) - g(x)|$$

This metric induces **uniform convergence**!

```desmos-graph
y = sin(x)
y = sin(x) + 0.5
y = sin(x) - 0.5
x = [0, 6.28]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Two functions are "close" in this metric if their graphs stay within a narrow band of each other everywhere.

## Completeness

### Definition

A metric space is **complete** if every Cauchy sequence converges to a point in the space.

### Key Theorem

**Theorem**: $C([a,b])$ with the supremum norm is complete.

This means: If $(f_n)$ is Cauchy in the sup norm, then $f_n \to f$ uniformly for some continuous $f$.

> [!info] Why This Matters
> Completeness allows us to construct limits of function sequences rigorously. It's essential for analysis!

## Subspaces and Density

### Polynomials $\mathcal{P}([a,b])$

The space of polynomial functions on $[a,b]$ is a subspace of $C([a,b])$.

**Key fact**: Polynomials are NOT complete (Cauchy sequences of polynomials may converge to non-polynomial functions).

### Weierstrass Approximation Theorem

**Theorem**: For any $f \in C([a,b])$ and $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a polynomial $p$ such that:

$$\|f - p\|_\infty < \epsilon$$

In other words: Polynomials are **dense** in $C([a,b])$.

---

## Stone-Weierstrass Theorem

### Statement

Let $A$ be a subalgebra of $C(X)$ where $X$ is compact. If:
1. $A$ separates points (for $x \neq y$, $\exists f \in A$ with $f(x) \neq f(y)$)
2. $A$ contains constants

Then $A$ is dense in $C(X)$.

### Applications

- Polynomials are dense in $C([a,b])$ (classical Weierstrass)
- Trigonometric polynomials are dense in $C([0,2\pi])$ (Fourier series foundation)

## Other Important Spaces

### $L^p$ Spaces (Preview)

For $1 \leq p < \infty$:
$$\|f\|_p = \left(\int_a^b |f|^p\right)^{1/p}$$

These require Lebesgue integration for completeness.

### Smooth Functions $C^\infty([a,b])$

Infinitely differentiable functions. Dense in $C([a,b])$ but not complete.

---

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Pointwise and Uniform Topology
> Different norms give different notions of convergence! Sup norm → uniform; $L^2$ norm → mean square.

> [!warning] Assuming All Function Spaces Are Complete
> $C^\infty$ is not complete in sup norm. Polynomials are not complete.

> [!warning] Forgetting Domain Restrictions
> $C([0,1])$ and $C(\mathbb{R})$ have very different properties!

## Practice Problems

Work with norms:

- Show that $\|f\|_\infty = \max_{x \in [a,b]} |f(x)|$ for continuous $f$
- Prove the triangle inequality for the sup norm
- Find $\|x^n - x^{n+1}\|_\infty$ on $[0,1]$

Test completeness:

- Show that $(f_n)$ where $f_n(x) = x^n$ is NOT Cauchy in $C([0,1])$
- Prove that polynomials are not complete in sup norm
- Construct a Cauchy sequence in $C^\infty$ that doesn't converge to a smooth function

Apply approximation:

- Use Weierstrass theorem to approximate $|x|$ on $[-1,1]$ by polynomials
- Show that $\sin(x)$ can be uniformly approximated by polynomials on $[0,\pi]$
- Explain why Taylor polynomials don't always give uniform approximation

Conceptual:

- Why is completeness important for analysis?
- What does it mean for one space to be "dense" in another?
- How does the choice of norm affect convergence?
