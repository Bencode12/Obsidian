## Pointwise vs. Uniform Convergence

### Pointwise Convergence

A sequence of functions $(f_n)$ **converges pointwise** to $f$ on $D$ if:

$$\forall x \in D, \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists N \text{ such that } n \geq N \implies |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \epsilon$$

Note: $N$ can depend on BOTH $\epsilon$ AND $x$.

### Uniform Convergence

$(f_n)$ **converges uniformly** to $f$ on $D$ if:

$$\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists N \text{ such that } \forall n \geq N \text{ and } \forall x \in D, |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \epsilon$$

Key difference: $N$ depends ONLY on $\epsilon$, not on $x$.

> [!info] Tao Reference
> See *Analysis II* (4th ed.), Chapter 14: Uniform Convergence, Sections 14.1-14.3 (pp. 365-380)

```desmos-graph
y = x^n
n = 1
n = 2
n = 5
n = 10
x = [0, 1]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Uniform convergence means the entire graph of $f_n$ eventually stays within an $\epsilon$-tube around $f$. Pointwise allows different parts to converge at different rates.

## Examples

### Example 1: Pointwise but NOT Uniform

$f_n(x) = x^n$ on $[0,1]$:
- Pointwise limit: $f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x \in [0,1) \\ 1 & x = 1 \end{cases}$
- NOT uniform (limit is discontinuous!)

### Example 2: Uniform Convergence

$f_n(x) = \frac{x}{n}$ on $\mathbb{R}$:
- Limit: $f(x) = 0$
- Uniform: $|f_n(x) - f(x)| = \frac{|x|}{n} < \epsilon$ when $n > \frac{|x|}{\epsilon}$

Wait—this depends on $x$! Not uniform on $\mathbb{R}$, but uniform on bounded intervals.

### Example 3: Uniform on Compact Sets

$f_n(x) = \frac{\sin(nx)}{n}$ on $\mathbb{R}$:
- $|f_n(x)| \leq \frac{1}{n}$ for all $x$
- Converges uniformly to 0 on ALL of $\mathbb{R}$

## Cauchy Criterion for Uniform Convergence

**Theorem**: $(f_n)$ converges uniformly on $D$ if and only if:

$$\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists N \text{ such that } \forall m,n \geq N \text{ and } \forall x \in D, |f_n(x) - f_m(x)| < \epsilon$$

This doesn't require knowing the limit function in advance!

## Preservation Theorems

### Continuity

**Theorem**: If $f_n \to f$ uniformly and each $f_n$ is continuous, then $f$ is continuous.

> [!warning] Pointwise Doesn't Preserve Continuity!
> Example: $x^n$ on $[0,1]$ shows pointwise limit of continuous functions can be discontinuous.

### Integration

**Theorem**: If $f_n \to f$ uniformly on $[a,b]$ and each $f_n$ is integrable, then:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_a^b f_n = \int_a^b f$$

We can interchange limit and integral!

### Differentiation

**Theorem**: If $f_n \to f$ pointwise, $f_n'$ exists and converges uniformly, then $f$ is differentiable and:

$$f' = \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n'$$

Requires stronger hypotheses than continuity/integration!

## Weierstrass M-Test

**Theorem**: For series $\sum f_n(x)$, if there exist constants $M_n$ such that:
1. $|f_n(x)| \leq M_n$ for all $x \in D$
2. $\sum M_n$ converges

Then $\sum f_n$ converges uniformly on $D$.

### Example

$\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\sin(nx)}{n^2}$ converges uniformly on $\mathbb{R}$ because:
- $|\frac{\sin(nx)}{n^2}| \leq \frac{1}{n^2}$
- $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Assuming Pointwise Implies Uniform
> Pointwise convergence is much weaker! Always check the definition carefully.

> [!warning] Interchanging Limits Without Justification
> Can't swap $\lim$ and $\int$ or $\lim$ and $\frac{d}{dx}$ without uniform convergence!

> [!warning] Forgetting Domain Dependence
> A sequence might converge uniformly on $[0,1]$ but not on $[0,\infty)$.

## Practice Problems

Test convergence:

- Does $f_n(x) = \frac{x}{1+nx^2}$ converge uniformly on $[0,1]$?
- Show that $f_n(x) = x^n(1-x)$ converges uniformly on $[0,1]$
- Test $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n^2}$ for uniform convergence on $[-1,1]$

Apply theorems:

- Use the M-test to show $\sum \frac{\cos(nx)}{n^3}$ converges uniformly
- Prove that uniform limit of differentiable functions need not be differentiable
- Show that $\int_0^1 x^n\,dx \to 0$ using uniform convergence

Conceptual:

- Why does uniform convergence preserve continuity but pointwise doesn't?
- Explain why differentiation requires stronger conditions than integration
- What's the relationship between uniform convergence and completeness?

Challenge:

- Prove the Cauchy criterion for uniform convergence
- Show that if $f_n \to f$ uniformly and $g$ is bounded, then $f_n g \to fg$ uniformly
- Research: What is almost uniform convergence and how does it relate to Egorov's theorem?
