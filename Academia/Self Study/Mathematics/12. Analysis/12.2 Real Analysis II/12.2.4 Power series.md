## Definition

A **power series** centered at $a$ is a series of the form:

$$\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n = c_0 + c_1(x-a) + c_2(x-a)^2 + ...$$

> [!info] Tao Reference
> See *Analysis II* (4th ed.), Chapter 11: The Riemann Integral, Sections 11.6-11.8 (pp. 305-315)

---

## Radius of Convergence

### Theorem

For any power series $\sum c_n(x-a)^n$, exactly one of the following holds:

1. Converges only at $x = a$ (radius $R = 0$)
2. Converges for all $x$ (radius $R = \infty$)
3. Converges for $|x-a| < R$ and diverges for $|x-a| > R$

The number $R$ is the **radius of convergence**.

### Finding $R$

**Ratio test**:
$$R = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{c_n}{c_{n+1}}\right|$$

**Root test**:
$$R = \frac{1}{\limsup_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|c_n|}}$$

---

## Interval of Convergence

The **interval of convergence** is $(a-R, a+R)$, possibly including endpoints.

Must check endpoints separately!

### Example

$\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n}$:
- $R = 1$ (ratio test)
- At $x = 1$: harmonic series (diverges)
- At $x = -1$: alternating harmonic (converges)
- Interval: $[-1, 1)$

## Uniform Convergence of Power Series

### Key Theorem

**Theorem**: A power series converges uniformly on any compact subset of its interval of convergence.

Specifically: If $0 < r < R$, then $\sum c_n(x-a)^n$ converges uniformly on $[a-r, a+r]$.

> [!warning] Not Uniform on Entire Interval!
> May not converge uniformly on $(a-R, a+R)$ itself, especially near endpoints.

### Term-by-Term Operations

Within the radius of convergence:

**Differentiation**:
$$\frac{d}{dx}\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n = \sum_{n=1}^\infty nc_n(x-a)^{n-1}$$

**Integration**:
$$\int \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n\,dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{c_n}{n+1}(x-a)^{n+1}$$

Both have the same radius $R$!

## Analytic Functions

### Definition

A function is **analytic** at $a$ if it equals its Taylor series in some neighborhood of $a$.

### Properties

- All analytic functions are $C^\infty$
- Power series define analytic functions
- Sums, products, and compositions of analytic functions are analytic

### Classic Non-Analytic Function

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} e^{-1/x^2} & x \neq 0 \\ 0 & x = 0 \end{cases}$$

All derivatives at 0 are zero, so Taylor series is identically 0, but $f \not\equiv 0$!

## Common Power Series

|Function|Series|Radius|
|---|---|---|
|$e^x$|$\sum \frac{x^n}{n!}$|$\infty$|
|$\sin x$|$\sum \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$|$\infty$|
|$\cos x$|$\sum \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}$|$\infty$|
|$\frac{1}{1-x}$|$\sum x^n$|1|
|$\ln(1+x)$|$\sum \frac{(-1)^{n+1}x^n}{n}$|1|
|$(1+x)^\alpha$|$\sum \binom{\alpha}{n}x^n$|1|

## Applications

### Computing Values

Approximate transcendental functions:
$$e^{0.1} \approx 1 + 0.1 + \frac{0.01}{2} + \frac{0.001}{6} \approx 1.10517$$

### Solving Differential Equations

Power series methods for ODEs without elementary solutions.

### Generating Functions

Encode sequences as coefficients of power series.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Forgetting to Check Endpoints
> Ratio/root tests don't determine convergence at $x = a \pm R$!

> [!warning] Assuming Uniform Convergence Everywhere
> Only uniform on COMPACT subsets inside the radius.

> [!warning] Confusing Radius with Interval
> Radius tells you distance; must check endpoints for full interval.

> [!warning] Differentiating Outside Radius
> Term-by-term operations only valid within radius of convergence!
