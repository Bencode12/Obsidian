## Definition

A **Fourier series** represents a periodic function as an infinite sum of sines and cosines:

$$f(x) \sim \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left[a_n\cos(nx) + b_n\sin(nx)\right]$$

> [!info] Tao Reference
> See *Analysis II* (4th ed.), Chapter 11: Fourier Series, Sections 11.10-11.12 (pp. 320-335)

---

## Fourier Coefficients

For $f$ with period $2\pi$:

$$a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi f(x)\cos(nx)\,dx \quad (n \geq 0)$$

$$b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi f(x)\sin(nx)\,dx \quad (n \geq 1)$$

### Orthogonality Relations

The key to Fourier series:

$$\int_{-\pi}^\pi \cos(mx)\cos(nx)\,dx = \begin{cases} 0 & m \neq n \\ \pi & m = n \neq 0 \\ 2\pi & m = n = 0 \end{cases}$$

Similar relations hold for sine and mixed products.

---

## Examples

### Example 1: Square Wave

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & 0 < x < \pi \\ -1 & -\pi < x < 0 \end{cases}$$

Fourier series:
$$f(x) \sim \frac{4}{\pi}\left[\sin(x) + \frac{\sin(3x)}{3} + \frac{\sin(5x)}{5} + ...\right]$$

```desmos-graph
y = sin(x)
y = sin(x) + sin(3*x)/3
y = sin(x) + sin(3*x)/3 + sin(5*x)/5
x = [-3.14, 3.14]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Adding more terms gives better approximation. Notice Gibbs phenomenon near discontinuities!

### Example 2: Sawtooth Wave

$f(x) = x$ on $(-\pi, \pi)$, extended periodically:

$$f(x) \sim 2\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\sin(nx)$$

## Convergence Theorems

### Pointwise Convergence (Dirichlet)

**Theorem**: If $f$ is piecewise smooth and periodic, then the Fourier series converges to:
- $f(x)$ at points of continuity
- $\frac{f(x^+) + f(x^-)}{2}$ at jump discontinuities

### Uniform Convergence

If $f$ is continuous, periodic, and piecewise smooth, the Fourier series converges uniformly.

### Convergence in Mean ($L^2$)

**Parseval's Identity**:
$$\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi |f(x)|^2\,dx = \frac{|a_0|^2}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty (|a_n|^2 + |b_n|^2)$$

This shows Fourier series converge in the $L^2$ sense!

---

## Complex Form

Using Euler's formula, we can write:

$$f(x) \sim \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty c_n e^{inx}$$

where:
$$c_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi f(x)e^{-inx}\,dx$$

This is often more convenient for calculations!

---

## Properties

### Linearity

Fourier series respect addition and scalar multiplication.

### Differentiation

If $f$ is smooth enough:
$$f'(x) \sim \sum_{n=1}^\infty [-na_n\sin(nx) + nb_n\cos(nx)]$$

Differentiation amplifies high frequencies!

### Integration

Term-by-term integration always works (even when differentiation doesn't).

## Applications

### Signal Processing

Decompose signals into frequency components.

### Heat Equation

Solve PDEs using separation of variables and Fourier series.

### Quantum Mechanics

Wave functions expanded in energy eigenstates (Fourier-like expansions).

### Image Compression

JPEG uses discrete cosine transform (related to Fourier).

---

## Gibbs Phenomenon

Near jump discontinuities, Fourier series overshoot by about 9% regardless of how many terms are used!

The overshoot doesn't disappear but gets narrower as $N \to \infty$.

---

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Pointwise and Uniform Convergence
> Fourier series may converge pointwise but not uniformly, especially with discontinuities.

> [!warning] Forgetting Periodicity
> Fourier series represent PERIODIC extensions. Be careful about boundary behavior!

> [!warning] Assuming Term-by-Term Differentiation Always Works
> Need sufficient smoothness. Discontinuous derivatives cause problems.

> [!warning] Misidentifying the Period
> Standard formulas assume period $2\pi$. Adjust for other periods!
