## Definition

A function $f: D \to \mathbb{R}$ is **continuous** at a point $a \in D$ if:

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$$

Equivalently, using epsilon-delta: $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ such that $|x - a| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(a)| < \epsilon$.

> [!info] Tao Reference
> See *Analysis I* (4th ed.), Chapter 9: Continuous Functions on Intervals, Sections 9.1-9.3 (pp. 227-245)

> [!example] Intuition
> A continuous function has no jumps, holes, or breaks. You can draw it without lifting your pen!

## Key Properties

### Algebraic Operations

If $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $a$, then:

|Operation|Result|
|---|---|
|Sum|$f + g$ is continuous at $a$|
|Product|$f \cdot g$ is continuous at $a$|
|Quotient|$f/g$ is continuous at $a$ (if $g(a) \neq 0$)|
|Composition|$f \circ g$ is continuous at $a$|

### Examples of Continuous Functions

The following are continuous on their domains:
- Polynomials (everywhere)
- Rational functions (where denominator ≠ 0)
- Trigonometric functions ($\sin x$, $\cos x$ everywhere; $\tan x$ where defined)
- Exponential and logarithmic functions
- Absolute value function $|x|$

```desmos-graph
y = sin(x)
y = x^2
y = abs(x)
```

## Types of Discontinuities

### Removable Discontinuity

$\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists but either:
- $f(a)$ is undefined, OR
- $f(a) \neq \lim_{x \to a} f(x)$

**Example**: $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}$ at $x = 1$

Can be "fixed" by redefining $f(a)$.

### Jump Discontinuity

Left and right limits exist but are different:
$$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \neq \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$$

**Example**: Step function, Heaviside function

### Infinite Discontinuity

At least one one-sided limit is $\pm\infty$.

**Example**: $f(x) = 1/x$ at $x = 0$

### Oscillating Discontinuity

The function oscillates infinitely near $a$.

**Example**: $f(x) = \sin(1/x)$ at $x = 0$

## Continuity on Intervals

### Definition

$f$ is **continuous on an interval** $I$ if it's continuous at every point in $I$.

For closed intervals $[a,b]$:
- Continuous on $(a,b)$
- Right-continuous at $a$: $\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = f(a)$
- Left-continuous at $b$: $\lim_{x \to b^-} f(x) = f(b)$

## Fundamental Theorems

### Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)

**Theorem**: If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $y$ is any value between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then there exists $c \in (a,b)$ such that $f(c) = y$.

```desmos-graph
y = x^3 - x
y = 0.5
x = [-2, 2]
```

> [!example] Application
> Prove $x^3 - x - 1 = 0$ has a root in $(1, 2)$:  
> $f(1) = -1 < 0$ and $f(2) = 5 > 0$, so by IVT there's a root between 1 and 2.

### Extreme Value Theorem (EVT)

**Theorem**: If $f$ is continuous on a closed, bounded interval $[a,b]$, then $f$ attains both a maximum and minimum value on $[a,b]$.

That is, $\exists c,d \in [a,b]$ such that:
$$f(c) \leq f(x) \leq f(d) \quad \forall x \in [a,b]$$

> [!warning] Hypotheses Matter!
> Both conditions are necessary:
> - Must be continuous
> - Must be on a CLOSED, BOUNDED interval  
> Counterexample: $f(x) = 1/x$ on $(0,1]$ has no maximum!

### Boundedness Theorem

**Corollary**: If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then $f$ is bounded on $[a,b]$.

This follows from EVT.

## Uniform Continuity

### Definition

$f$ is **uniformly continuous** on $D$ if:

$$\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0 \text{ such that } \forall x,y \in D, |x-y| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| < \epsilon$$

### Key Difference

|Pointwise Continuity|Uniform Continuity|
|---|---|
|$δ$ can depend on $a$ AND $ε$|$δ$ depends ONLY on $ε$|
|Local property|Global property|

### Heine-Cantor Theorem

**Theorem**: If $f$ is continuous on a compact set (closed and bounded in $\mathbb{R}$), then $f$ is uniformly continuous.

> [!example] Counterexample
> $f(x) = 1/x$ is continuous on $(0,1)$ but NOT uniformly continuous (domain not closed).

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Continuity vs. Differentiability
> Continuous does NOT imply differentiable! Example: $|x|$ is continuous everywhere but not differentiable at 0.

> [!warning] Forgetting Domain Restrictions
> Always check where the function is defined. $1/x$ is continuous on its domain $\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$, even though it has a discontinuity at 0.

> [!warning] Misapplying IVT
> IVT requires continuity on a CLOSED interval. Don't apply it to open intervals without justification.

> [!warning] Assuming Inverse is Continuous
> If $f$ is continuous and bijective, $f^{-1}$ need not be continuous unless the domain is compact!

## Practice Problems

Test continuity:

- Is $f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 & x \neq 0 \\ 1 & x = 0 \end{cases}$ continuous at 0?
- Determine all points of discontinuity for $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}$
- Show that $f(x) = x \sin(1/x)$ for $x \neq 0$, $f(0) = 0$ is continuous everywhere

Apply theorems:

- Use IVT to show $x^5 + x - 1 = 0$ has exactly one real root
- Does $f(x) = \tan(x)$ attain a maximum on $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$? Why or why not?
- Prove that a continuous function on $[a,b]$ is bounded

Conceptual:

- Explain why uniform continuity is stronger than pointwise continuity
- Why do we need closed intervals for EVT?
- Can a function be continuous at exactly one point? Construct an example

Challenge:

- Prove that the composition of continuous functions is continuous
- Show that if $f$ is continuous and $f(q) = 0$ for all rational $q$, then $f(x) = 0$ for all $x$
- Research: What is the Cantor function and why is it interesting?
