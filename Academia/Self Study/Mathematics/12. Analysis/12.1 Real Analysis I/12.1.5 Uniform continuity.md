## Definition

A function $f: D \to \mathbb{R}$ is **uniformly continuous** on $D$ if:

$$\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0 \text{ such that } \forall x,y \in D, |x-y| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| < \epsilon$$

> [!info] Tao Reference
> See *Analysis I* (4th ed.), Chapter 9: Continuous Functions on Intervals, Section 9.9 (pp. 258-262)

---

## Pointwise vs. Uniform Continuity

### Key Difference

|Pointwise Continuity|Uniform Continuity|
|---|---|
|$δ$ depends on BOTH $ε$ AND the point $a$|$δ$ depends ONLY on $ε$|
|Local property (at each point)|Global property (across entire domain)|
|Weaker condition|Stronger condition|

### Formal Comparison

**Pointwise**: $\forall a \in D, \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ (where $\delta = \delta(\epsilon, a)$)

**Uniform**: $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ such that $\forall x,y \in D$ (where $\delta = \delta(\epsilon)$ only)

```desmos-graph
y = 1/x
x = [0.1, 5]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> For uniform continuity, the same "width" $δ$ works everywhere. For pointwise continuity, you might need smaller and smaller $δ$ as you move across the domain.

## Examples

### Example 1: Uniformly Continuous

$f(x) = x^2$ on $[0,1]$ is uniformly continuous.

Proof: Given $\epsilon > 0$, choose $\delta = \epsilon/2$. For $x,y \in [0,1]$:
$$|x^2 - y^2| = |x+y||x-y| \leq 2|x-y| < 2\delta = \epsilon$$

### Example 2: NOT Uniformly Continuous

$f(x) = 1/x$ on $(0,1)$ is continuous but NOT uniformly continuous.

As $x \to 0^+$, we need smaller and smaller $\delta$ to maintain the same $\epsilon$.

### Example 3: Linear Functions

$f(x) = mx + b$ is uniformly continuous on all of $\mathbb{R}$.

Choose $\delta = \epsilon/|m|$ (for $m \neq 0$).

## Heine-Cantor Theorem

**Theorem**: If $f$ is continuous on a compact set $K \subset \mathbb{R}$ (i.e., closed and bounded), then $f$ is uniformly continuous on $K$.

This is one of the most important results connecting topology and analysis!

> [!example] Applications
> - Any continuous function on $[a,b]$ is uniformly continuous
> - Explains why pathological behavior only occurs on non-compact domains

## Criteria for Uniform Continuity

### Sufficient Conditions

A function is uniformly continuous if:
1. Domain is compact and $f$ is continuous (Heine-Cantor)
2. $f$ has bounded derivative on its domain
3. $f$ is Lipschitz continuous: $|f(x) - f(y)| \leq L|x-y|$

### Necessary Condition

If $f$ is uniformly continuous on $D$ and $(x_n)$ is Cauchy in $D$, then $(f(x_n))$ is Cauchy.

---

## Lipschitz Continuity

### Definition

$f$ is **Lipschitz continuous** with constant $L$ if:

$$|f(x) - f(y)| \leq L|x-y| \quad \forall x,y \in D$$

### Hierarchy

$$\text{Lipschitz} \implies \text{Uniformly continuous} \implies \text{Continuous}$$

But the converses are false!

### Example

$f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ on $[0,1]$ is uniformly continuous but NOT Lipschitz (derivative unbounded near 0).

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Assuming Continuity Implies Uniform Continuity
> This is only true on COMPACT domains! On open or unbounded intervals, continuous functions may not be uniformly continuous.

> [!warning] Confusing Bounded Derivative with Uniform Continuity
> Bounded derivative IMPLIES uniform continuity, but the converse is false (e.g., $\sqrt{x}$ on $[0,1]$).

> [!warning] Ignoring Domain
> Always specify the domain! $f(x) = x^2$ is uniformly continuous on $[0,1]$ but NOT on $\mathbb{R}$.

## Practice Problems

Test uniform continuity:

- Is $f(x) = \sin(x)$ uniformly continuous on $\mathbb{R}$?
- Show that $f(x) = x^2$ is NOT uniformly continuous on $\mathbb{R}$
- Prove that $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ is uniformly continuous on $[0,\infty)$

Apply theorems:

- Use Heine-Cantor to show $\tan(x)$ is uniformly continuous on $[0, \pi/4]$
- Show that a function with bounded derivative is uniformly continuous
- Prove that the sum of two uniformly continuous functions is uniformly continuous

Conceptual:

- Why does compactness guarantee uniform continuity?
- Explain the difference between local and global properties
- Can a uniformly continuous function be unbounded? Give an example

Challenge:

- Prove that if $f$ is uniformly continuous on $(a,b)$, then $\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$ exists
- Show that composition of uniformly continuous functions is uniformly continuous
- Research: What is Hölder continuity and how does it relate to Lipschitz continuity?
