## Residue Theorem

The **Residue Theorem** is one of the most powerful tools in complex analysis for evaluating integrals.

### Statement

Let $f$ be analytic on and inside a simple closed contour $C$, except for isolated singularities $z_1, z_2, \ldots, z_n$ inside $C$. Then:

$$\oint_C f(z) \, dz = 2\pi i \sum_{k=1}^n \text{Res}(f, z_k)$$

where $\text{Res}(f, z_k)$ is the **residue** of $f$ at $z_k$.

---

## Residues

### Definition

The **residue** of $f$ at an isolated singularity $z_0$ is the coefficient $a_{-1}$ in the Laurent series expansion:

$$f(z) = \cdots + \frac{a_{-2}}{(z-z_0)^2} + \frac{a_{-1}}{z-z_0} + a_0 + a_1(z-z_0) + \cdots$$

Notation: $\text{Res}(f, z_0) = a_{-1}$

### Computing Residues

#### Simple Pole (Order 1)

If $z_0$ is a simple pole:

$$\text{Res}(f, z_0) = \lim_{z \to z_0} (z-z_0)f(z)$$

Or if $f(z) = \frac{g(z)}{h(z)}$ where $h(z_0) = 0$, $h'(z_0) \neq 0$, $g(z_0) \neq 0$:

$$\text{Res}(f, z_0) = \frac{g(z_0)}{h'(z_0)}$$

#### Pole of Order $m$

$$\text{Res}(f, z_0) = \frac{1}{(m-1)!} \lim_{z \to z_0} \frac{d^{m-1}}{dz^{m-1}}[(z-z_0)^m f(z)]$$

#### Essential Singularity

Must find Laurent series and extract $a_{-1}$ coefficient.

## Examples

### Example 1: Simple Poles

Evaluate $\oint_C \frac{1}{z^2-1} \, dz$ where $C$ is $|z|=2$:

1. Singularities: $z = \pm 1$ (both simple poles, both inside $C$)
2. At $z=1$: $\text{Res}(f, 1) = \lim_{z \to 1} \frac{z-1}{(z-1)(z+1)} = \frac{1}{2}$
3. At $z=-1$: $\text{Res}(f, -1) = \lim_{z \to -1} \frac{z+1}{(z-1)(z+1)} = -\frac{1}{2}$
4. Sum: $\frac{1}{2} + (-\frac{1}{2}) = 0$
5. Result: $\oint_C f \, dz = 2\pi i \cdot 0 = 0$

### Example 2: Higher Order Pole

Find $\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{e^z}{z^2} \, dz$:

1. Singularity at $z=0$ is a pole of order 2
2. $\text{Res}(f, 0) = \lim_{z \to 0} \frac{d}{dz}[z^2 \cdot \frac{e^z}{z^2}] = \lim_{z \to 0} e^z = 1$
3. Result: $\oint_C f \, dz = 2\pi i \cdot 1 = 2\pi i$

### Example 3: Using Formula for Simple Pole

Evaluate $\oint_C \frac{z}{z^2+1} \, dz$ where $C$ is $|z|=2$:

1. Singularities: $z = \pm i$ (simple poles)
2. Write as $\frac{g(z)}{h(z)}$ where $g(z) = z$, $h(z) = z^2+1$
3. $h'(z) = 2z$
4. At $z=i$: $\text{Res} = \frac{i}{2i} = \frac{1}{2}$
5. At $z=-i$: $\text{Res} = \frac{-i}{-2i} = \frac{1}{2}$
6. Result: $2\pi i(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}) = 2\pi i$

## Applications to Real Integrals

### Type 1: Trigonometric Integrals

Integrals of form $\int_0^{2\pi} R(\cos\theta, \sin\theta) \, d\theta$:

1. Substitute $z = e^{i\theta}$, so $d\theta = \frac{dz}{iz}$
2. $\cos\theta = \frac{z+z^{-1}}{2}$, $\sin\theta = \frac{z-z^{-1}}{2i}$
3. Convert to contour integral over unit circle
4. Apply residue theorem

### Type 2: Improper Integrals

Integrals $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx$ where $f$ decays sufficiently fast:

1. Consider semicircular contour in upper half-plane
2. Show arc contribution vanishes as radius → ∞
3. Integral equals $2\pi i$ times sum of residues in upper half-plane

> [!example] Classic Result
> 
> $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{dx}{x^2+1} = \pi$ (residue at $z=i$ is $\frac{1}{2i}$)

## Practice Problems

1. Find the residue of $f(z) = \frac{e^z}{z^3}$ at $z=0$.
2. Evaluate $\oint_{|z|=3} \frac{z^2}{(z-1)(z-2)} \, dz$ using residues.
3. Compute $\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{d\theta}{2+\cos\theta}$ using the residue theorem.
4. Find $\oint_C \frac{\sin z}{z^4} \, dz$ where $C$ is $|z|=1$.
5. Evaluate $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{x^2}{x^4+1} \, dx$ using contour integration.
6. Determine the residue of $f(z) = \frac{1}{z^2(z-1)^3}$ at $z=1$.
7. Calculate $\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{ze^z}{(z-1)^2} \, dz$.
8. Use residues to evaluate $\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{d\theta}{5+4\cos\theta}$.
9. Find all residues of $f(z) = \frac{1}{z^4-1}$ and use them to evaluate $\oint_{|z|=2} f(z) \, dz$.
10. Prove that $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\cos x}{x^2+1} \, dx = \frac{\pi}{e}$ using contour integration.
