## Conformal Mappings

A function $f: D \to \mathbb{C}$ is **conformal** at $z_0$ if it preserves angles between curves passing through $z_0$.

### Conditions for Conformality

If $f$ is analytic at $z_0$ and $f'(z_0) \neq 0$, then $f$ is conformal at $z_0$.

- Angles are preserved in both magnitude and orientation
- Local shapes are preserved (up to rotation and scaling)
- The mapping is locally one-to-one

> [!tip] Geometric Interpretation
> 
> Near a point where $f'(z_0) \neq 0$, the mapping looks like a rotation by $\arg f'(z_0)$ followed by scaling by $|f'(z_0)|$.

## Important Conformal Maps

### Linear Transformations

$$f(z) = az + b, \quad a \neq 0$$

- Combination of rotation, scaling, and translation
- Maps lines to lines, circles to circles

### Power Functions

$$f(z) = z^n, \quad n \in \mathbb{N}$$

- Maps sector of angle $\alpha$ to sector of angle $n\alpha$
- Not conformal at $z = 0$ (derivative is zero)

### Exponential Map

$$f(z) = e^z = e^x(\cos y + i\sin y)$$

- Maps horizontal strips to sectors
- Periodic with period $2\pi i$
- Maps strip $0 < \text{Im}(z) < \pi$ to upper half-plane

### Logarithm

$$f(z) = \log z = \ln|z| + i\arg z$$

- Inverse of exponential
- Multi-valued; requires branch cut
- Maps sectors to horizontal strips

### Möbius (Bilinear) Transformations

$$f(z) = \frac{az + b}{cz + d}, \quad ad - bc \neq 0$$

- Maps circles and lines to circles and lines
- Preserves cross-ratio
- Three-point determination: uniquely determined by images of three points

## Examples

### Example 1: Mapping Upper Half-Plane to Unit Disk

The Cayley transform:

$$w = \frac{z - i}{z + i}$$

- Maps upper half-plane $\text{Im}(z) > 0$ to unit disk $|w| < 1$
- Maps real axis to unit circle
- Inverse: $z = i\frac{1+w}{1-w}$

### Example 2: Joukowski Transformation

$$w = z + \frac{1}{z}$$

- Used in aerodynamics to map circles to airfoil shapes
- Maps unit circle to line segment $[-2, 2]$
- Conformal except at $z = \pm 1$

## Riemann Mapping Theorem

**Statement**: Any simply connected domain $D \subsetneq \mathbb{C}$ can be mapped conformally onto the unit disk.

### Implications

- All simply connected domains (except $\mathbb{C}$ itself) are conformally equivalent
- The mapping is unique up to rotation and translation
- Proof is non-constructive (doesn't give explicit formula)

> [!warning] Limitations
> 
> - Requires domain to be simply connected
> - Doesn't apply to entire complex plane
> - Finding explicit mapping can be very difficult

## Applications

### Fluid Dynamics

- Conformal maps preserve Laplace's equation
- Used to transform complex flow regions to simpler geometries
- Streamlines and equipotentials map to streamlines and equipotentials

### Electrostatics

- Maps complicated electrode configurations to simple ones
- Preserves harmonic functions (solutions to Laplace's equation)

### Cartography

- Mercator projection is conformal (preserves angles)
- Useful for navigation but distorts areas

## Practice Problems

1. Show that $f(z) = z^2$ is conformal everywhere except at $z = 0$.
2. Find the image of the unit circle under the Möbius transformation $w = \frac{z-1}{z+1}$.
3. Determine where the mapping $w = e^z$ is conformal.
4. Find a conformal map from the first quadrant to the upper half-plane.
5. Show that Möbius transformations map circles and lines to circles and lines.
6. Verify that the Cayley transform maps the real axis to the unit circle.
7. Find the fixed points of the Möbius transformation $w = \frac{2z+1}{z+2}$.
8. Use a conformal map to solve Laplace's equation in a semi-infinite strip.
9. Prove that if $f$ is conformal at $z_0$, then $f^{-1}$ is conformal at $f(z_0)$.
10. Find a conformal map from the region between two concentric circles to an annulus.
