## Laurent Series

For a function $f(z)$ analytic in an annulus $r < |z - z_0| < R$, the **Laurent series** expansion is:

$$f(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a_n (z - z_0)^n = \cdots + \frac{a_{-2}}{(z-z_0)^2} + \frac{a_{-1}}{z-z_0} + a_0 + a_1(z-z_0) + \cdots$$

### Coefficients

$$a_n = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_C \frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}} \, dz$$

where $C$ is any closed contour in the annulus encircling $z_0$.

### Principal Part and Regular Part

- **Principal part**: Terms with negative powers $\sum_{n=-\infty}^{-1} a_n (z-z_0)^n$
- **Regular (analytic) part**: Terms with non-negative powers $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n (z-z_0)^n$

> [!tip] Key Difference from Taylor Series
> 
> Unlike Taylor series, Laurent series can represent functions with singularities. The principal part captures the behavior near the singularity.

## Classification of Singularities

### Isolated Singularities

A point $z_0$ is an **isolated singularity** if $f$ is analytic in a punctured disk $0 < |z - z_0| < r$.

#### Removable Singularity

- Principal part is zero ($a_n = 0$ for all $n < 0$)
- Function can be "fixed" by defining $f(z_0) = a_0$
- Example: $f(z) = \frac{\sin z}{z}$ at $z = 0$

#### Pole of Order $m$

- Principal part has finitely many terms, highest power is $(z-z_0)^{-m}$
- $a_{-m} \neq 0$ but $a_n = 0$ for $n < -m$
- Example: $f(z) = \frac{1}{(z-1)^3}$ has a pole of order 3 at $z = 1$

#### Essential Singularity

- Principal part has infinitely many terms
- Behavior is wild near the singularity
- Example: $f(z) = e^{1/z}$ at $z = 0$

### Casorati-Weierstrass Theorem

If $z_0$ is an essential singularity of $f$, then in every neighborhood of $z_0$, $f(z)$ comes arbitrarily close to every complex number.

> [!example] Picard's Great Theorem
> 
> Near an essential singularity, $f(z)$ takes on every complex value (with at most one exception) infinitely often.

## Examples

### Example 1: Laurent Expansion

Find the Laurent series of $f(z) = \frac{1}{z(z-1)}$ in the annulus $0 < |z| < 1$:

1. Partial fractions: $\frac{1}{z(z-1)} = -\frac{1}{z} + \frac{1}{z-1}$
2. For $|z| < 1$: $\frac{1}{z-1} = -\frac{1}{1-z} = -(1 + z + z^2 + \cdots)$
3. Result: $f(z) = -\frac{1}{z} - 1 - z - z^2 - \cdots$

### Example 2: Classifying Singularities

Classify the singularities of $f(z) = \frac{z^2 + 1}{z^2(z-1)}$:

- At $z = 0$: Denominator has $z^2$, numerator nonzero → **pole of order 2**
- At $z = 1$: Simple zero in denominator → **simple pole** (order 1)

## Practice Problems

1. Find the Laurent series of $f(z) = \frac{1}{z^2 - 1}$ in the annulus $1 < |z| < \infty$.
2. Classify the singularity of $f(z) = \frac{e^z - 1}{z}$ at $z = 0$.
3. Determine the order of the pole of $f(z) = \frac{\sin z}{z^3}$ at $z = 0$.
4. Find the Laurent series of $f(z) = e^{1/z}$ around $z = 0$ and identify the type of singularity.
5. Expand $f(z) = \frac{1}{(z-2)(z-3)}$ in Laurent series valid for $2 < |z| < 3$.
6. Show that $z = 0$ is an essential singularity of $f(z) = \sin(1/z)$.
7. Find the principal part of the Laurent series of $f(z) = \frac{z}{(z-1)^2}$ at $z = 1$.
8. Prove that if $f$ has a removable singularity at $z_0$, then $\lim_{z \to z_0} f(z)$ exists.
9. Classify all singularities of $f(z) = \tan z = \frac{\sin z}{\cos z}$.
10. Find the Laurent expansion of $f(z) = \frac{1}{z(e^z - 1)}$ around $z = 0$ up to the constant term.
