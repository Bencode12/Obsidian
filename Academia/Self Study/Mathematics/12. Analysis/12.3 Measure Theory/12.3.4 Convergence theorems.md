## Overview

Convergence theorems are the cornerstone of Lebesgue integration theory. They provide conditions under which we can interchange limits and integrals:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n = \int \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n$$

This is not always valid! These theorems tell us when it works.

---

## Fatou's Lemma

### Statement

If $f_n \geq 0$ are measurable functions, then:

$$\int \liminf_{n \to \infty} f_n \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \int f_n$$

### Interpretation

The integral of the limit inferior is **at most** the limit inferior of the integrals. Mass can "escape" in the limit, but cannot appear from nowhere.

> [!example] Example: Mass Escaping
> 
> Let $f_n = \chi_{[n,n+1]}$. Then $f_n \to 0$ pointwise, so $\int \lim f_n = 0$, but $\int f_n = 1$ for all $n$, so $\lim \int f_n = 1$.

---

## Monotone Convergence Theorem (MCT)

### Statement

If $0 \leq f_1 \leq f_2 \leq \cdots$ (monotonically increasing) and $f_n \to f$ pointwise, then:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n = \int f = \int \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n$$

### Why It Works

Monotonicity prevents mass from escaping. The sequence builds up to the limit function without losing information.

### Applications

- Defining integrals of non-negative functions
- Proving properties of infinite series of functions
- Constructing measures from premeasures

---

## Dominated Convergence Theorem (DCT)

### Statement

If $f_n \to f$ pointwise and there exists an integrable function $g$ such that $|f_n| \leq g$ for all $n$, then:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n = \int f = \int \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n$$

Moreover, $\int |f_n - f| \to 0$ (convergence in $L^1$).

### Why It's Powerful

DCT is the most widely used convergence theorem because:
1. Pointwise convergence is often easy to verify
2. Finding a dominating function is usually straightforward
3. It applies to functions that aren't monotone

> [!tip] Strategy for Applying DCT
> 
> 1. Identify the pointwise limit $f(x) = \lim f_n(x)$
> 2. Find an integrable function $g$ with $|f_n(x)| \leq g(x)$ for all $n,x$
> 3. Conclude $\lim \int f_n = \int f$

## Examples

### Example 1: Using MCT

Evaluate $\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^1 \frac{x^n}{1 + x^n} \, dx$.

**Solution**: Note that $\frac{x^n}{1 + x^n} \to 0$ for $x \in [0,1)$ and $= 1/2$ at $x=1$. By MCT (sequence is decreasing after some point, apply to $1 - f_n$):

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^1 \frac{x^n}{1 + x^n} \, dx = 0$$

### Example 2: Using DCT

Show $\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^{\infty} \frac{\sin(x/n)}{x} e^{-x} \, dx = 0$.

**Solution**: 
- Pointwise limit: $\sin(x/n)/x \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$
- Domination: $|\sin(x/n)/x| \leq 1/x$, but need better bound near 0
- Use $|\sin(t)| \leq |t|$: $|\sin(x/n)/x| \leq 1/n$
- Better: $|\sin(x/n)/x \cdot e^{-x}| \leq e^{-x}$ which is integrable
- By DCT: limit = $\int 0 = 0$

## Reverse Fatou's Lemma

If $f_n \leq g$ for some integrable $g$, then:

$$\limsup_{n \to \infty} \int f_n \leq \int \limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n$$

This is the "upper bound" version of Fatou's Lemma.

## Practice Problems

1. Prove Fatou's Lemma using MCT.
2. Give an example where strict inequality holds in Fatou's Lemma.
3. Use MCT to prove that $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \int f_n = \int \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n$ for non-negative $f_n$.
4. Apply DCT to evaluate $\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^1 nx^{n-1} e^{-x} \, dx$.
5. Show that DCT implies MCT.
6. If $f_n \to f$ in $L^1$, does $f_n \to f$ pointwise? Provide counterexample.
7. Prove the Reverse Fatou's Lemma.
8. Let $f_n(x) = n^2 x e^{-nx}$ on $[0,\infty)$. Does $\lim \int f_n = \int \lim f_n$?
9. Use DCT to show continuity of parameter-dependent integrals: if $f(x,t)$ is continuous in $t$ and dominated, then $F(t) = \int f(x,t) dx$ is continuous.
10. Construct a sequence where $\lim \int f_n$ exists but $\int \lim f_n$ does not.
