## Definition

A **sigma-algebra** (or σ-algebra) on a set $X$ is a collection $\mathcal{F}$ of subsets of $X$ satisfying:

1. $X \in \mathcal{F}$ (the whole space is included)
2. If $A \in \mathcal{F}$, then $A^c \in \mathcal{F}$ (closed under complementation)
3. If $A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \in \mathcal{F}$ (closed under countable unions)

The pair $(X, \mathcal{F})$ is called a **measurable space**, and sets in $\mathcal{F}$ are called **measurable sets**.

---

## Key Properties

### Closure Under Countable Operations

From the three axioms, we can derive:

- **Closed under countable intersections**: By De Morgan's laws
  $$\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n = \left(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n^c\right)^c$$

- **Contains the empty set**: Since $\emptyset = X^c$

- **Closed under finite unions and intersections**: Special cases of countable operations

### Examples of Sigma-Algebras

|Sigma-Algebra|Description|
|---|---|
|**Trivial**|$\{\emptyset, X\}$ - smallest possible|
|**Power set**|$\mathcal{P}(X)$ - all subsets, largest possible|
|**Borel sigma-algebra**|Smallest σ-algebra containing all open sets in a topological space|
|**Countable-cocountable**|Sets that are countable or have countable complement|

## The Borel Sigma-Algebra

The **Borel sigma-algebra** $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ on $\mathbb{R}$ is the smallest σ-algebra containing all open intervals. It includes:

- Open intervals $(a, b)$
- Closed intervals $[a, b]$
- Half-open intervals $[a, b)$ and $(a, b]$
- Single points $\{x\}$
- Countable unions and intersections of these

> [!tip] Why Borel Sets Matter
> 
> The Borel σ-algebra is "just right" - large enough to include all sets we care about in analysis, but small enough to avoid pathological sets like Vitali sets (which require the Axiom of Choice to construct).

## Generated Sigma-Algebras

Given any collection $\mathcal{C}$ of subsets of $X$, there exists a **smallest sigma-algebra** containing $\mathcal{C}$, denoted $\sigma(\mathcal{C})$.

### Construction

$$\sigma(\mathcal{C}) = \bigcap \{\mathcal{F} : \mathcal{F} \text{ is a } \sigma\text{-algebra and } \mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\}$$

This is well-defined because:
1. The power set $\mathcal{P}(X)$ is a σ-algebra containing $\mathcal{C}$
2. Intersection of σ-algebras is a σ-algebra

### Example: Borel Sets as Generated σ-Algebra

$$\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) = \sigma(\{(a,b) : a < b, a,b \in \mathbb{R}\})$$

The Borel σ-algebra is generated by open intervals.

## Measurable Functions

A function $f: (X, \mathcal{F}) \to (Y, \mathcal{G})$ between measurable spaces is **measurable** if:

$$f^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F} \quad \text{for all } B \in \mathcal{G}$$

### Key Facts

- Continuous functions are Borel measurable
- Composition of measurable functions is measurable
- Pointwise limits of measurable functions are measurable

> [!example] Connection to Probability
> 
> In probability theory, random variables are measurable functions from a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ to $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$.

## Practice Problems

1. Verify that the trivial σ-algebra $\{\emptyset, X\}$ satisfies all three axioms.
2. Show that if $\mathcal{F}$ is a σ-algebra and $A, B \in \mathcal{F}$, then $A \setminus B \in \mathcal{F}$.
3. Prove that the intersection of any collection of σ-algebras is a σ-algebra.
4. Let $X = \{1, 2, 3\}$. List all possible σ-algebras on $X$.
5. Show that every closed set in $\mathbb{R}$ is a Borel set.
6. If $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous, prove that $f$ is Borel measurable.
7. Construct a σ-algebra on $\mathbb{N}$ that contains all singletons but is not the power set.
8. Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a σ-algebra on $X$ and $E \in \mathcal{F}$. Show that $\mathcal{F}_E = \{A \cap E : A \in \mathcal{F}\}$ is a σ-algebra on $E$.
9. Prove that the set of all countable or cocountable subsets of an uncountable set $X$ forms a σ-algebra.
10. If $f_n: X \to \mathbb{R}$ are measurable functions, show that $\sup_n f_n$ and $\inf_n f_n$ are measurable.
