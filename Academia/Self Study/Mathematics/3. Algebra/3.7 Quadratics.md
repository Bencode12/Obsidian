## What is a Quadratic Equation?

A quadratic equation has the variable raised to the **power of 2** as its highest term.

$$ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \quad (a \neq 0)$$

| Part | Role |
|------|------|
| $a$ | Coefficient of $x^2$ — must not be zero |
| $b$ | Coefficient of $x$ |
| $c$ | Constant term |

$$x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0 \qquad 2x^2 - 3x + 1 = 0$$

---

## Methods of Solving

### 1. Factorisation

Write as a product of two binomials, then set each factor to zero.

$$x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3) = 0$$
$$x + 2 = 0 \implies x = -2 \qquad x + 3 = 0 \implies x = -3$$

> [!tip] Finding factors
> Look for two numbers that **multiply to $c$** and **add to $b$**.
> For $x^2 + 5x + 6$: need $2 \times 3 = 6$ and $2 + 3 = 5$ ✓

---

### 2. Quadratic Formula

Works for **any** quadratic equation.

$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

**Example — Solve $2x^2 - 3x + 1 = 0$**

$$a = 2,\quad b = -3,\quad c = 1$$
$$x = \frac{3 \pm \sqrt{9 - 8}}{4} = \frac{3 \pm 1}{4}$$
$$x = 1 \quad \text{or} \quad x = \frac{1}{2}$$

---

### 3. Completing the Square

Transform $ax^2 + bx + c$ into the form $(x + p)^2 = q$, then solve by taking the square root.

**Example — Solve $x^2 + 6x + 5 = 0$**

$$x^2 + 6x = -5$$
$$x^2 + 6x + 9 = 4 \quad \left(\text{add } \left(\tfrac{6}{2}\right)^2 = 9 \text{ to both sides}\right)$$
$$(x + 3)^2 = 4$$
$$x + 3 = \pm 2 \implies x = -1 \quad \text{or} \quad x = -5$$

---

## The Discriminant — Nature of Roots

The discriminant $D = b^2 - 4ac$ tells you what kind of roots to expect **before solving**.

| $D$ | Roots |
|-----|-------|
| $D > 0$ | Two real, distinct roots |
| $D = 0$ | Two real, equal roots (one repeated root) |
| $D < 0$ | No real roots — two complex roots |

> [!tip] Use the discriminant first
> Check $D$ before choosing a method — if $D < 0$ there are no real solutions, and if $D$ is a perfect square, factorisation will work cleanly.

## Practice Problems

1. Solve by factorisation: $x^2 - 7x + 12 = 0$.
2. Solve using the quadratic formula: $x^2 + x - 6 = 0$.
3. Find the discriminant of $3x^2 - 4x + 2 = 0$ and state the nature of the roots.
4. Solve by completing the square: $x^2 - 4x - 5 = 0$.
5. For what value of $k$ does $x^2 + kx + 9 = 0$ have exactly one real root?
