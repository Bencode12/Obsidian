## **Lesson 11.1.2 — Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)**

**Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)** is one of the most important methods for estimating unknown parameters of a probability distribution. It is used everywhere: statistics, machine learning, econometrics, and scientific modeling.

---

## **11.1.2.1 Core Idea**

Given data (X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n) drawn from a distribution with unknown parameter (\theta), we choose (\hat{\theta}) that **makes the observed data most likely**.

> “Pick the parameter under which the data you saw would be most probable.”

---

## **11.1.2.2 Likelihood Function**

Let (X_i \sim f(x \mid \theta)) independently.  
The **likelihood function** is:

[  
L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} f(X_i \mid \theta)  
]

- Treat data (X_i) as fixed
    
- Treat (\theta) as the variable
    
- We **maximize (L(\theta))**
    

---

## **11.1.2.3 Log-Likelihood**

Products are hard to maximize, so we use logarithms:

[  
\ell(\theta) = \log L(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log f(X_i \mid \theta)  
]

- Log is monotonic → maximizing log-likelihood is equivalent
    
- Turns products into sums
    
- Easier differentiation
    

---

## **11.1.2.4 MLE Definition**

The **Maximum Likelihood Estimator**:

[  
\hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_{\theta} L(\theta)  
]

Procedure:

1. Write likelihood (L(\theta))
    
2. Take log → (\ell(\theta))
    
3. Differentiate w.r.t. (\theta)
    
4. Set derivative = 0
    
5. Solve for (\theta)
    
6. Check maximum (second derivative or logic)
    

---

## **11.1.2.5 Examples**

### **Bernoulli Distribution**

Let (X_i \sim \text{Bern}(p)), (X_i \in {0,1}).

Likelihood:

[  
L(p) = p^{\sum X_i}(1-p)^{n-\sum X_i}  
]

Log-likelihood:

[  
\ell(p) = \Big(\sum X_i\Big)\log p + (n-\sum X_i)\log(1-p)  
]

Derivative → solve:

[  
\hat{p}_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i = \bar{X}  
]

**MLE of probability = sample mean**

---

### **Normal Distribution (Mean Unknown, Variance Known)**

Let (X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)), (\sigma^2) known.

Log-likelihood simplifies to quadratic form.  
Maximization gives:

[  
\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} = \bar{X}  
]

---

### **Normal Distribution (Variance Unknown)**

MLE variance estimator:

[  
\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum (X_i - \bar{X})^2  
]

Note: **Biased**, unlike unbiased estimator using (n-1).

---

## **11.1.2.6 Properties of MLE**

1. **Consistency**  
    [  
    \hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} \xrightarrow{P} \theta  
    ]
    
2. **Asymptotic Normality**  
    For large (n), estimator ≈ normal distribution.
    
3. **Efficiency (Asymptotic)**  
    Approaches lowest possible variance (Cramér–Rao bound).
    
4. **Invariance Property**  
    If (\hat{\theta}) is MLE of (\theta), then (g(\hat{\theta})) is MLE of (g(\theta)).
    

---

## **11.1.2.7 Fisher Information**

Measures how much information data provides about parameter:

[  
I(\theta) = E\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log f(X|\theta)\right)^2\right]  
]

- Higher information → lower variance of estimator
    
- Linked to **Cramér–Rao Lower Bound**
    

---

## **11.1.2.8 Advantages & Limitations**

**Advantages**

- General method
    
- Strong asymptotic properties
    
- Works for complex models
    

**Limitations**

- Can be biased in small samples
    
- Sensitive to model assumptions
    
- May require numerical optimization
