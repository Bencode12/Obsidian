## Method of Moments

### Basic Idea

Match sample moments to population moments to estimate parameters.

### Procedure

For $k$ parameters $\theta_1, ..., \theta_k$:

1. Compute first $k$ population moments: $E[X^r]$ for $r = 1, ..., k$
2. Compute first $k$ sample moments: $m_r = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i^r$
3. Set equal and solve: $E[X^r] = m_r$

### Example: Normal Distribution

Parameters: $\mu, \sigma^2$

- First moment: $E[X] = \mu = m_1 = \bar{X}$
- Second moment: $E[X^2] = \mu^2 + \sigma^2 = m_2$

Solving:
- $\hat{\mu}_{MM} = \bar{X}$
- $\hat{\sigma}^2_{MM} = m_2 - \bar{X}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$

Note: This is the biased variance estimator!

---

## Properties of MOM Estimators

### Advantages

- Simple to compute
- Always exists (if moments exist)
- Consistent under mild conditions

### Disadvantages

- May not be efficient
- Can produce estimates outside parameter space
- Doesn't use full likelihood information

### Asymptotic Distribution

Under regularity conditions:
$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_{MM} - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, V)$$

where $V$ depends on moments.

## Bayesian Estimation

### Bayes' Theorem

$$P(\theta|X) = \frac{P(X|\theta)P(\theta)}{P(X)}$$

- **Prior**: $P(\theta)$ - belief before seeing data
- **Likelihood**: $P(X|\theta)$ - probability of data given parameter
- **Posterior**: $P(\theta|X)$ - updated belief after seeing data
- **Marginal likelihood**: $P(X) = \int P(X|\theta)P(\theta)d\theta$

### Bayesian Point Estimators

**Posterior mean**: $\hat{\theta}_B = E[\theta|X]$

Minimizes posterior expected squared error loss.

**Posterior median**: Minimizes posterior expected absolute error loss.

**MAP (Maximum A Posteriori)**: Mode of posterior distribution.

```desmos-graph
y = normaldist(0, 1)
y = normaldist(1, 0.5)
y = normaldist(0.7, 0.7)
x = [-3, 4]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Prior (blue), likelihood (orange), posterior (green). Data shifts belief toward likelihood while prior provides regularization.

## Conjugate Priors

### Definition

A prior is **conjugate** if posterior is in same family as prior.

Makes computation tractable!

### Common Conjugate Pairs

|Likelihood|Conjugate Prior|Posterior|
|---|---|---|
|Normal ($\sigma^2$ known)|Normal|Normal|
|Normal ($\mu$ known)|Inverse-Gamma|Inverse-Gamma|
|Binomial|Beta|Beta|
|Poisson|Gamma|Gamma|
|Exponential|Gamma|Gamma|

### Example: Normal-Normal

If $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known:

**Prior**: $\mu \sim N(\mu_0, \tau_0^2)$

**Posterior**: $\mu|X \sim N(\mu_n, \tau_n^2)$

where:
$$\mu_n = \frac{\frac{n}{\sigma^2}\bar{X} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2}\mu_0}{\frac{n}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2}}$$

$$\frac{1}{\tau_n^2} = \frac{n}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2}$$

Posterior mean is weighted average of sample mean and prior mean!

## Shrinkage Estimators

### James-Stein Estimator

For estimating multiple normal means simultaneously:

$$\hat{\mu}_i^{JS} = \left(1 - \frac{(p-2)\sigma^2}{\sum X_j^2}\right)X_i$$

where $p \geq 3$ is dimension.

**Surprising result**: James-Stein dominates MLE in terms of MSE when $p \geq 3$!

This shows that "shrinking" estimates toward a common value can improve overall performance.

## Robust Estimation

### Motivation

Standard estimators (mean, variance) are sensitive to outliers.

Robust estimators maintain good performance even with contaminated data.

### Examples

**Trimmed mean**: Remove extreme values, then compute mean.

**Winsorized mean**: Replace extreme values with nearest non-extreme values.

**Median**: Highly robust (breakdown point = 50%).

**MAD (Median Absolute Deviation)**:
$$\text{MAD} = \text{median}(|X_i - \text{median}(X)|)$$

Robust alternative to standard deviation.

### Breakdown Point

Fraction of contamination an estimator can handle before breaking down.

- Mean: 0% (one outlier ruins it)
- Median: 50% (most robust)
- Trimmed mean: depends on trimming proportion

## Minimax Estimation

### Decision Theory Framework

- **Loss function**: $L(\theta, \hat{\theta})$ measures cost of wrong estimate
- **Risk**: $R(\theta, \hat{\theta}) = E[L(\theta, \hat{\theta})]$
- **Minimax estimator**: Minimizes maximum risk

$$\hat{\theta}_{minimax} = \arg\min_{\hat{\theta}} \sup_{\theta} R(\theta, \hat{\theta})$$

Protects against worst-case scenario!

### Example

For normal mean with squared error loss, sample mean is minimax.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Bayesian and Frequentist Approaches
> Bayesian: parameters are random, probability expresses belief. Frequentist: parameters are fixed, probability is long-run frequency.

> [!warning] Using Non-Informative Priors Carelessly
> "Flat" priors aren't always non-informative! They can depend on parameterization.

> [!warning] Ignoring Computational Complexity
> Bayesian methods often require MCMC or other numerical methods for complex models.
