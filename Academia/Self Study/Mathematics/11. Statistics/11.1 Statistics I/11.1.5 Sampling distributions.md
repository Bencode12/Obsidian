## Sampling Distribution of the Mean

### Known Variance

If $X_1, ..., X_n \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known:

$$\bar{X} \sim N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$$

Standardized:
$$Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim N(0, 1)$$

### Unknown Variance

When $\sigma^2$ is unknown, replace with sample variance $S^2$:

$$T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1}$$

This follows Student's t-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom!

```desmos-graph

```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Notice how t-distribution has heavier tails than normal. This accounts for uncertainty in estimating $\sigma$.

## Sampling Distribution of Variance

### Chi-Squared Result

For normal data $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$$\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$$

where $S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$

### Key Properties

- Mean: $E[S^2] = \sigma^2$ (unbiased!)
- Variance: $\text{Var}(S^2) = \frac{2\sigma^4}{n-1}$

As $n \to \infty$, variance → 0 (consistent estimator).

---

## Independence of $\bar{X}$ and $S^2$

### Fundamental Result

For normal samples:
- Sample mean $\bar{X}$ and sample variance $S^2$ are **independent**
- This is unique to the normal distribution!

### Why It Matters

Allows us to construct t-statistics and derive exact distributions for inference.

## Central Limit Theorem Applications

### General Case

Even if data isn't normal, for large $n$:

$$\frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \approx N(0, 1)$$

Rule of thumb: $n \geq 30$ is often sufficient.

### Example: Binomial Proportion

If $X \sim B(n, p)$, then $\hat{p} = X/n$ estimates proportion.

For large $n$:
$$\frac{\hat{p} - p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}} \approx N(0, 1)$$

## Delta Method Applications

### Transforming Estimators

If $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta} - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$:

Then for differentiable $g$:
$$\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{\theta}) - g(\theta)) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, [g'(\theta)]^2\sigma^2)$$

### Example: Log Transformation

Estimate $\log(\mu)$ using $\log(\bar{X})$:

$$\sqrt{n}(\log(\bar{X}) - \log(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} N\left(0, \frac{\sigma^2}{\mu^2}\right)$$

## Order Statistics

### Definition

Arrange sample in ascending order: $X_{(1)} \leq X_{(2)} \leq ... \leq X_{(n)}$

These are **order statistics**.

- $X_{(1)}$: minimum
- $X_{(n)}$: maximum
- $X_{((n+1)/2)}$: median (for odd $n$)

### Distribution of Maximum

For i.i.d. sample with CDF $F$:

$$P(X_{(n)} \leq x) = [F(x)]^n$$

PDF: $f_{X_{(n)}}(x) = n[F(x)]^{n-1}f(x)$

### Example: Uniform Distribution

If $X_i \sim U[0,\theta]$:

- $X_{(n)}$ has PDF: $f(x) = \frac{nx^{n-1}}{\theta^n}$ for $0 \leq x \leq \theta$
- Mean: $E[X_{(n)}] = \frac{n}{n+1}\theta$

Note: $X_{(n)}$ is biased but consistent for $\theta$!

## Joint Distributions

### Bivariate Normal

$(X, Y)$ is bivariate normal if every linear combination is normal.

Parameters: $\mu_X, \mu_Y, \sigma_X^2, \sigma_Y^2, \rho$

**Joint PDF**:
$$f(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_X\sigma_Y\sqrt{1-\rho^2}}\exp\left(-\frac{Q}{2(1-\rho^2)}\right)$$

where $Q = \frac{(x-\mu_X)^2}{\sigma_X^2} - \frac{2\rho(x-\mu_X)(y-\mu_Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y} + \frac{(y-\mu_Y)^2}{\sigma_Y^2}$

### Conditional Distributions

For bivariate normal:
$$Y|X=x \sim N\left(\mu_Y + \rho\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}(x-\mu_X), \sigma_Y^2(1-\rho^2)\right)$$

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Assuming Normality Without Checking
> CLT justifies approximate normality for means, but not for individual observations or small samples!

> [!warning] Confusing Standard Deviation and Standard Error
> SD measures spread of data. SE measures uncertainty of estimator. SE = SD/$\sqrt{n}$.

> [!warning] Using Wrong Degrees of Freedom
> For sample variance, use $n-1$ df, not $n$. For two-sample t-test, df depends on equal/unequal variances.
