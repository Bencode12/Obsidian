## **Lesson 11.1.1 — Estimators & Bias**

This lesson introduces **estimators**, which are the core objects of statistical inference. Everything in statistics—confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, machine learning—rests on how well we estimate unknown parameters.

---

## **11.1.1.1 Population vs Sample**

- **Population parameter**: fixed but unknown quantity
    
    - Mean: (\mu)
        
    - Variance: (\sigma^2)
        
- **Sample statistic**: random variable computed from data
    
    - Sample mean: (\bar{X})
        
    - Sample variance: (S^2)
        

Statistics studies how **sample statistics approximate population parameters**.

---

## **11.1.1.2 Estimators**

An **estimator** (\hat{\theta}) is a **random variable** used to estimate a population parameter (\theta).

- Depends on sample data
    
- Different samples → different estimator values
    

**Examples:**

- (\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i) estimates (\mu)
    
- (S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum (X_i - \bar{X})^2) estimates (\sigma^2)
    

---

## **11.1.1.3 Bias of an Estimator**

The **bias** of an estimator (\hat{\theta}) is:

[  
\text{Bias}(\hat{\theta}) = E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta  
]

- **Unbiased estimator**:  
    [  
    E[\hat{\theta}] = \theta  
    ]
    
- **Biased estimator**:  
    [  
    E[\hat{\theta}] \neq \theta  
    ]
    

Bias measures **systematic error**, not randomness.

---

## **11.1.1.4 Examples**

### **Sample Mean**

Let (X_1, \dots, X_n) be i.i.d. with mean (\mu).

[  
E[\bar{X}] = E\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum X_i\right] = \mu  
]

✅ **Unbiased estimator of (\mu)**

---

### **Naive Sample Variance**

[  
\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum (X_i - \bar{X})^2  
]

[  
E[\hat{\sigma}^2] = \frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2  
]

❌ **Biased (underestimates variance)**

---

### **Corrected Sample Variance**

[  
S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum (X_i - \bar{X})^2  
]

[  
E[S^2] = \sigma^2  
]

✅ **Unbiased estimator of variance**

---

## **11.1.1.5 Mean Squared Error (MSE)**

Bias alone is not enough. We also care about **variance of the estimator**.

[  
\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = E[(\hat{\theta} - \theta)^2]  
]

Decomposition:  
[  
\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) + \text{Bias}(\hat{\theta})^2  
]

- Low bias + low variance = good estimator
    
- Sometimes a **biased estimator with lower variance** is preferable
    

---

## **11.1.1.6 Consistency**

An estimator (\hat{\theta}_n) is **consistent** if:

[  
\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{P} \theta \quad \text{as } n \to \infty  
]

- Bias may exist for finite (n)
    
- Vanishes as sample size increases
    

**Example:**

- Sample mean (\bar{X}) is consistent for (\mu)
    

---

## **11.1.1.7 Summary Table**

|Property|Meaning|
|---|---|
|Unbiased|Correct on average|
|Biased|Systematic error|
|Variance|Random fluctuation|
|MSE|Overall accuracy|
|Consistent|Improves with data|
