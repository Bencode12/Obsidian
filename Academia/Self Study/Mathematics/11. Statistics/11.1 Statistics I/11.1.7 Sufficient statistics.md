## Sufficient Statistics

### Intuition

A statistic $T(X)$ is **sufficient** if it captures all information about $\theta$ contained in the data.

Once you know $T(X)$, the raw data provides no additional information about $\theta$!

### Formal Definition

$T(X)$ is sufficient for $\theta$ if the conditional distribution of $X$ given $T(X) = t$ does not depend on $\theta$.

Notation: $X | T(X) \perp \theta$

---

## Factorization Theorem

### Neyman-Fisher Factorization

$T(X)$ is sufficient for $\theta$ if and only if the likelihood can be factored as:

$$L(\theta; x) = g(T(x), \theta) \cdot h(x)$$

where:
- $g$ depends on data only through $T(x)$ and on $\theta$
- $h$ depends only on data (not on $\theta$)

This is the most practical way to find sufficient statistics!

### Example: Normal Distribution

For $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with both parameters unknown:

$$L(\mu, \sigma^2; x) = (2\pi\sigma^2)^{-n/2}\exp\left(-\frac{\sum(x_i-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

Expand: $\sum(x_i-\mu)^2 = \sum x_i^2 - 2\mu\sum x_i + n\mu^2$

So sufficient statistics are: $T(X) = (\sum X_i, \sum X_i^2)$

Equivalently: $(\bar{X}, S^2)$

## Minimal Sufficiency

### Definition

A sufficient statistic $T$ is **minimal sufficient** if it's a function of every other sufficient statistic.

Minimal sufficient = most data reduction possible while retaining sufficiency!

### Finding Minimal Sufficient Statistics

Use the **likelihood ratio method**:

$T(X)$ is minimal sufficient if:
$$\frac{L(\theta; x)}{L(\theta; y)} \text{ is constant in } \theta \iff T(x) = T(y)$$

### Example: Exponential Distribution

For $X_i \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$:

$$\frac{L(\lambda; x)}{L(\lambda; y)} = \frac{\lambda^n e^{-\lambda\sum x_i}}{\lambda^n e^{-\lambda\sum y_i}} = e^{-\lambda(\sum x_i - \sum y_i)}$$

This is constant in $\lambda$ iff $\sum x_i = \sum y_i$.

Therefore: $T(X) = \sum X_i$ is minimal sufficient!

## Complete Statistics

### Definition

A statistic $T$ is **complete** if:

$$E_\theta[g(T)] = 0 \text{ for all } \theta \implies P_\theta(g(T) = 0) = 1 \text{ for all } \theta$$

No non-trivial unbiased estimator of zero exists!

### Why Completeness Matters

Combined with sufficiency, completeness guarantees uniqueness of optimal estimators.

---

## Lehmann-Scheffé Theorem

### Statement

If $T$ is complete and sufficient, and $\hat{\theta}$ is an unbiased estimator that is a function of $T$, then $\hat{\theta}$ is the **unique UMVUE** (Uniformly Minimum Variance Unbiased Estimator).

### Procedure to Find UMVUE

1. Find a complete sufficient statistic $T$
2. Find any unbiased estimator $\tilde{\theta}$
3. Condition on $T$: $\hat{\theta} = E[\tilde{\theta}|T]$
4. By Rao-Blackwell, $\hat{\theta}$ is UMVUE!

### Example: Normal Mean

For $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known:

- Complete sufficient: $T = \sum X_i$ (or $\bar{X}$)
- Unbiased estimator: $\bar{X}$ itself
- Since $\bar{X}$ is already a function of $T$: $\bar{X}$ is UMVUE!

---

## Rao-Blackwell Theorem

### Statement

If $\tilde{\theta}$ is an unbiased estimator and $T$ is sufficient:

$$\hat{\theta} = E[\tilde{\theta}|T]$$

is also unbiased and has lower (or equal) variance:

$$\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \leq \text{Var}(\tilde{\theta})$$

Rao-Blackwellization improves estimators!

### Example

Estimate $p^2$ for Bernoulli($p$):

- Naive unbiased: $\tilde{\theta} = X_1 X_2$
- Sufficient: $T = \sum X_i$
- Rao-Blackwellized: $\hat{\theta} = E[X_1 X_2 | T] = \frac{T(T-1)}{n(n-1)}$

This has lower variance!

## Exponential Families

### Definition

A distribution is in the **exponential family** if its PDF/PMF can be written as:

$$f(x|\theta) = h(x)\exp\left(\eta(\theta) \cdot T(x) - A(\theta)\right)$$

where:
- $\eta(\theta)$: natural parameter
- $T(x)$: sufficient statistic
- $A(\theta)$: log-partition function
- $h(x)$: base measure

### Examples

|Distribution|Natural Parameter|Sufficient Statistic|
|---|---|---|
|Normal ($\sigma^2$ known)|$\mu/\sigma^2$|$\sum X_i$|
|Poisson|$\log(\lambda)$|$\sum X_i$|
|Binomial|$\log(p/(1-p))$|$\sum X_i$|
|Exponential|$-\lambda$|$\sum X_i$|

### Key Property

For exponential families, $T(X) = \sum T(X_i)$ is complete and sufficient!

---

## Ancillary Statistics

### Definition

A statistic $A(X)$ is **ancillary** if its distribution does not depend on $\theta$.

Contains no information about the parameter!

### Example

For normal sample $X_i \sim N(\mu, 1)$:

- Sample range $R = X_{(n)} - X_{(1)}$ is ancillary for $\mu$
- Distribution of range doesn't depend on location parameter

### Basu's Theorem

If $T$ is complete sufficient and $A$ is ancillary, then $T$ and $A$ are independent!

Powerful result connecting sufficiency, completeness, and independence.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Sufficiency with Completeness
> Sufficiency: captures all information. Completeness: no unbiased estimator of zero. A statistic can be one without the other!

> [!warning] Forgetting to Verify Completeness
> Not all sufficient statistics are complete! Must check separately (often using exponential family properties).

> [!warning] Misapplying Factorization Theorem
> The factorization must hold for ALL $\theta$, not just some values!

- Find sufficient statistic for uniform $U[0,\theta]$

- Prove that $\sum X_i$ is complete for Poisson distribution
- Show that sample mean is complete for normal with known variance

- Find UMVUE for $e^{-\lambda}$ in Poisson distribution
- Derive UMVUE for $\mu^2$ in normal distribution
- Use Rao-Blackwell to improve an estimator
