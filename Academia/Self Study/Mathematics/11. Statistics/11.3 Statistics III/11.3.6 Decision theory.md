## Decision Theory Framework

### Components

1. **Parameter space**: $\Theta$ - possible values of unknown parameter
2. **Action space**: $\mathcal{A}$ - possible decisions/actions
3. **Loss function**: $L(\theta, a)$ - cost of taking action $a$ when truth is $\theta$
4. **Decision rule**: $\delta(X)$ - function mapping data to actions

### Common Loss Functions

**Squared error loss**:
$$L(\theta, a) = (\theta - a)^2$$

Leads to posterior mean as Bayes estimator.

**Absolute error loss**:
$$L(\theta, a) = |\theta - a|$$

Leads to posterior median as Bayes estimator.

**0-1 loss** (classification):
$$L(\theta, a) = \begin{cases} 0 & a = \theta \\ 1 & a \neq \theta \end{cases}$$

Leads to posterior mode (MAP) as Bayes estimator.

---

## Risk Function

### Definition

$$R(\theta, \delta) = E_\theta[L(\theta, \delta(X))]$$

Expected loss when true parameter is $\theta$.

### Bayes Risk

Average risk with respect to prior:

$$r(\pi, \delta) = E_\pi[R(\theta, \delta)] = \int_\Theta R(\theta, \delta)\pi(\theta)d\theta$$

### Bayes Estimator

Minimizes Bayes risk:
$$\delta_\pi = \arg\min_\delta r(\pi, \delta)$$

Equivalently, minimizes posterior expected loss for each observation!

---

## Admissibility

### Definition

A decision rule $\delta$ is **admissible** if no other rule dominates it.

$\delta_1$ **dominates** $\delta_2$ if:
- $R(\theta, \delta_1) \leq R(\theta, \delta_2)$ for all $\theta$
- Strict inequality for some $\theta$

### Key Results

- Bayes rules are admissible (under mild conditions)
- Some admissible rules are not Bayes
- Inadmissible rules should be avoided!

### Stein's Paradox

For estimating normal mean in dimension $p \geq 3$, sample mean is inadmissible under squared error loss!

James-Stein estimator dominates it.

---

## Minimax Estimation

### Minimax Rule

Minimizes maximum risk:

$$\delta_{mm} = \arg\min_\delta \sup_{\theta \in \Theta} R(\theta, \delta)$$

Conservative approach—protects against worst case.

### Least Favorable Prior

Prior $\pi^*$ that maximizes Bayes risk:

$$\pi^* = \arg\max_\pi r(\pi, \delta_\pi)$$

If Bayes rule for least favorable prior has constant risk, it's minimax!

### Example: Normal Mean

For $X \sim N(\theta, 1)$ with squared error loss:

- Sample mean $\bar{X}$ is minimax
- Constant risk: $R(\theta, \bar{X}) = 1/n$ for all $\theta$

## Empirical Bayes

### Idea

Estimate prior from data rather than specifying it subjectively.

Bridges frequentist and Bayesian approaches!

### Parametric Empirical Bayes

Assume prior comes from parametric family $\pi(\theta|\tau)$.

1. Estimate hyperparameter $\tau$ from marginal distribution
2. Use estimated prior for inference

### Example: Normal-Normal

Model:
- $X_i|\theta_i \sim N(\theta_i, \sigma^2)$
- $\theta_i|\mu, \tau^2 \sim N(\mu, \tau^2)$

Estimate $\mu$ and $\tau^2$ from data, then compute posterior.

### James-Stein as Empirical Bayes

James-Stein estimator can be viewed as empirical Bayes with estimated prior variance!

## Hierarchical Models

### Structure

Multiple levels of randomness:

**Level 1**: Data model
$$Y|\theta \sim f(y|\theta)$$

**Level 2**: Prior model
$$\theta|\phi \sim g(\theta|\phi)$$

**Level 3**: Hyperprior
$$\phi \sim h(\phi)$$

### Benefits

- Natural way to incorporate uncertainty at multiple levels
- Borrowing strength across groups
- Flexible modeling of complex structures

### Example: Random Effects Model

$$Y_{ij} = \mu + \alpha_i + \epsilon_{ij}$$
$$\alpha_i \sim N(0, \tau^2)$$
$$\epsilon_{ij} \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$$

Groups share information through common variance components.

## Computational Methods

### Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)

Generate samples from posterior when analytical form unavailable.

**Metropolis-Hastings**:
1. Propose $\theta'$ from proposal distribution $q(\theta'|\theta^{(t)})$
2. Accept with probability:
   $$\alpha = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi(\theta'|x)q(\theta^{(t)}|\theta')}{\pi(\theta^{(t)}|x)q(\theta'|\theta^{(t)})}\right)$$

**Gibbs Sampling**:
Sample each parameter from its full conditional distribution.

Easier when conditionals are standard distributions!

### Convergence Diagnostics

- Trace plots: Check mixing and stationarity
- Autocorrelation: Assess independence of samples
- Gelman-Rubin statistic: Compare multiple chains
- Effective sample size: Account for autocorrelation

## Model Comparison

### Bayes Factors

Compare two models $M_1$ and $M_2$:

$$BF_{12} = \frac{P(x|M_1)}{P(x|M_2)} = \frac{\int P(x|\theta_1, M_1)\pi(\theta_1|M_1)d\theta_1}{\int P(x|\theta_2, M_2)\pi(\theta_2|M_2)d\theta_2}$$

- $BF_{12} > 1$: Evidence for $M_1$
- $BF_{12} > 10$: Strong evidence

### Posterior Model Probabilities

With prior model probabilities $P(M_i)$:

$$P(M_i|x) = \frac{P(x|M_i)P(M_i)}{\sum_j P(x|M_j)P(M_j)}$$

### DIC and WAIC

**DIC (Deviance Information Criterion)**:
$$DIC = \bar{D} + p_D$$

where $\bar{D}$ is posterior mean deviance and $p_D$ is effective number of parameters.

**WAIC (Widely Applicable IC)**: More reliable than DIC for singular models.

Lower values indicate better models!

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Using Improper Priors Carelessly
> Improper priors can lead to improper posteriors! Always verify posterior integrates to 1.

> [!warning] Ignoring MCMC Convergence
> Don't trust results without checking convergence diagnostics. Run multiple chains!

> [!warning] Confusing Credible and Confidence Intervals
> Bayesian credible interval: $P(\theta \in CI|data) = 0.95$. Frequentist CI: Long-run coverage probability.

> [!warning] Overfitting with Complex Hierarchical Models
> More levels ≠ better model. Use model comparison criteria to balance fit and complexity.
