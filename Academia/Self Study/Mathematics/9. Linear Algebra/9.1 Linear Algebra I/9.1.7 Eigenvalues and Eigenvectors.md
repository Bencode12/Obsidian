## Definition

For a linear transformation $T: V \to V$ (or square matrix $A$):

- **Eigenvector** $v \neq 0$ satisfies:
  $$Av = \lambda v$$

- **Eigenvalue** $\lambda$ is the scaling factor.

Geometric interpretation: Eigenvectors are directions that don't rotate, only scale!

---

## Finding Eigenvalues

### Characteristic Equation

Rearrange: $Av = \lambda v \implies (A - \lambda I)v = 0$

Nontrivial solution exists iff:
$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$

This is the **characteristic equation**.

### Characteristic Polynomial

$$p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$$

For $n \times n$ matrix, this is degree $n$ polynomial.

Roots are eigenvalues (counting multiplicity).

---

## Example: 2×2 Matrix

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Characteristic polynomial:
$$\det\begin{pmatrix} 4-\lambda & 1 \\ 2 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10$$

Roots: $\lambda_1 = 5$, $\lambda_2 = 2$

For $\lambda_1 = 5$: Solve $(A - 5I)v = 0$
$$\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = 0 \implies x = y$$

Eigenvector: $v_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$

```desmos-graph
y = x
y = 0
x = 0
x = [-2, 2]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Eigenvector direction (line y=x) is preserved under transformation, only scaled by λ=5.

---

## Finding Eigenvectors

### For Each Eigenvalue $\lambda$

Solve homogeneous system:
$$(A - \lambda I)v = 0$$

Solution space is **eigenspace** $E_\lambda = \ker(A - \lambda I)$.

Dimension of eigenspace = **geometric multiplicity**.

---

## Algebraic vs Geometric Multiplicity

### Definitions

- **Algebraic multiplicity**: Multiplicity as root of characteristic polynomial
- **Geometric multiplicity**: Dimension of eigenspace

### Relationship

$$1 \leq \text{geometric mult.} \leq \text{algebraic mult.}$$

Always true!

### Defective Matrices

If geometric < algebraic for some eigenvalue, matrix is **defective**.

Cannot be diagonalized!

---

## Properties of Eigenvalues

### Trace and Determinant

For $n \times n$ matrix with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$:

$$\text{tr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i$$

$$\det(A) = \prod_{i=1}^n \lambda_i$$

Sum = trace, product = determinant!

### Powers of Matrix

If $Av = \lambda v$, then:
$$A^k v = \lambda^k v$$

Eigenvalues of $A^k$ are $\lambda^k$.

### Inverse

If $A$ invertible with eigenvalue $\lambda$:
$$A^{-1}v = \frac{1}{\lambda}v$$

Eigenvalues of $A^{-1}$ are $1/\lambda$.

---

## Diagonalization

### Definition

Matrix $A$ is **diagonalizable** if there exists invertible $P$ such that:

$$P^{-1}AP = D = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$$

Columns of $P$ are eigenvectors!

### Criterion

$A$ is diagonalizable ⟺ sum of geometric multiplicities = $n$

Equivalently: has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors.

### Sufficient Condition

If $A$ has $n$ **distinct** eigenvalues, then $A$ is diagonalizable.

(But not necessary—can have repeated eigenvalues and still diagonalize.)

---

## Applications

### Computing Powers

If $A = PDP^{-1}$:
$$A^k = PD^kP^{-1} = P\text{diag}(\lambda_1^k, \ldots, \lambda_n^k)P^{-1}$$

Much easier than multiplying matrices!

### Solving Differential Equations

System $\frac{dx}{dt} = Ax$ has solution:
$$x(t) = e^{At}x_0 = Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}x_0$$

where $e^{Dt} = \text{diag}(e^{\lambda_1 t}, \ldots, e^{\lambda_n t})$.

### Stability Analysis

For dynamical system $x_{k+1} = Ax_k$:
- All $|\lambda_i| < 1$: Stable (converges to 0)
- Any $|\lambda_i| > 1$: Unstable (diverges)
- $|\lambda_i| = 1$: Marginal (oscillates or constant)

### Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

Eigenvalues of covariance matrix give variance explained by each component.

Largest eigenvalues = most important directions.

---

## Special Cases

### Symmetric Matrices

Real symmetric matrices ($A = A^T$):
- All eigenvalues are real
- Always diagonalizable
- Eigenvectors can be chosen orthonormal

**Spectral theorem**: Foundation of many applications!

### Triangular Matrices

Eigenvalues are diagonal entries (obvious from characteristic polynomial).

### Orthogonal Matrices

Eigenvalues have absolute value 1: $|\lambda| = 1$.

Can be complex even for real matrices!

---

## Complex Eigenvalues

### When They Occur

Real matrices can have complex eigenvalues (in conjugate pairs).

Example: Rotation matrix
$$\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

has eigenvalues $\lambda = \pm i$.

### Interpretation

Complex eigenvalues indicate rotation/spiral behavior.

No real eigenvectors (no invariant lines).

---

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Assuming All Matrices Are Diagonalizable
> Defective matrices exist! Check geometric multiplicity.

> [!warning] Confusing Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity
> They can differ. Geometric ≤ Algebraic always.

> [!warning] Forgetting That Eigenvectors Must Be Nonzero
> Zero vector technically satisfies Av = λv but is NOT an eigenvector.
