## **Lesson 9.1.7 — Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors**

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors reveal the **intrinsic structure** of linear transformations. They allow us to **diagonalize matrices, understand dynamics, and solve differential equations**.

---

## **9.1.7.1 Definition**

For a linear transformation (T: V \to V) (or square matrix (A)):

- **Eigenvector** (v \neq 0) satisfies:  
    [  
    T(v) = \lambda v  
    ]
    
- (\lambda \in F) is the **eigenvalue** corresponding to (v)
    

Equivalently for a matrix (A):

[  
Av = \lambda v, \quad v \neq 0  
]

> Eigenvectors point in directions that are **stretched or compressed but not rotated** by (T).

---

## **9.1.7.2 Characteristic Equation**

Eigenvalues are solutions of:

[  
\det(A - \lambda I) = 0  
]

- (\det(A - \lambda I)) = **characteristic polynomial**
    
- Degree = size of matrix (n) → at most (n) eigenvalues (counting multiplicity)
    

---

## **9.1.7.3 Finding Eigenvectors**

1. Solve characteristic equation → eigenvalues (\lambda)
    
2. Solve ((A - \lambda I)v = 0) → eigenvectors for each (\lambda)
    
3. Eigenvectors form **eigenspaces** (subspaces)
    

---

## **9.1.7.4 Properties**

- Trace: (\text{tr}(A) = \sum \lambda_i)
    
- Determinant: (\det(A) = \prod \lambda_i)
    
- Similar matrices have same eigenvalues
    
- Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are **linearly independent**
    

---

## **9.1.7.5 Diagonalization**

Matrix (A) is **diagonalizable** if:

[  
A = PDP^{-1}  
]

- (D) = diagonal matrix of eigenvalues
    
- (P) = matrix whose columns are corresponding eigenvectors
    
- **Criterion:** (A) has (n) linearly independent eigenvectors
    

Diagonalization simplifies:

- Powers of (A): (A^k = PD^kP^{-1})
    
- Systems of differential equations
    
- Computation of functions of matrices (e.g., (e^A))
    

---

## **9.1.7.6 Example**

[  
A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}  
]

1. Characteristic polynomial:  
    [  
    \det \begin{bmatrix}2-\lambda & 1 \ 1 & 2-\lambda\end{bmatrix} = (2-\lambda)^2 -1 = \lambda^2 -4\lambda +3  
    ]
    
2. Eigenvalues: (\lambda = 1, 3)
    
3. Eigenvectors:
    

- (\lambda=1): (v = \begin{bmatrix}1\-1\end{bmatrix})
    
- (\lambda=3): (v = \begin{bmatrix}1\1\end{bmatrix})
    

4. Diagonalization:  
    [  
    P = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1 \ -1 & 1\end{bmatrix}, \quad D = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \ 0 & 3\end{bmatrix}, \quad A = PDP^{-1}  
    ]
