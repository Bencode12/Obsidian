## **Lesson 9.1.4 — Matrix Representations of Linear Transformations**

This lesson formalizes **matrices as coordinate representations** of linear maps. The key idea: _matrices depend on bases; linear transformations do not._

---

## **9.1.4.1 From Linear Transformation to Matrix**

Let  
[  
T: V \to W  
]  
be linear, with:

- Basis (B = {v_1,\dots,v_n}) for (V)
    
- Basis (C = {w_1,\dots,w_m}) for (W)
    

The **matrix of (T) relative to these bases** is the matrix ([T]_{C \leftarrow B}) whose columns are:

[  
[T(v_1)]_C,\ [T(v_2)]_C,\ \dots,\ [T(v_n)]_C  
]

Each column is the coordinate vector of (T(v_i)) in basis (C).

---

## **9.1.4.2 Standard Matrix Representation**

If (V = \mathbb{R}^n), (W = \mathbb{R}^m), and both use the **standard basis**, then:

[  
T(x) = Ax  
]

for a unique matrix (A \in M_{m \times n}(\mathbb{R})).

This is the **default matrix representation** used in computations.

---

## **9.1.4.3 Change of Basis**

Let (P) be the matrix whose columns are the new basis vectors expressed in the old basis.

- Coordinate change:  
    [  
    [v]_{\text{old}} = P [v]_{\text{new}}  
    ]
    
- Change of matrix representation:  
    [  
    [T]_{\text{new}} = P^{-1} [T]_{\text{old}} P  
    ]
    

This is called **similarity transformation**.

> Similar matrices represent the _same_ linear transformation in different bases.

---

## **9.1.4.4 Similarity and Invariants**

If matrices (A) and (B) are similar:

[  
B = P^{-1} A P  
]

they share key properties:

- Rank
    
- Determinant
    
- Trace
    
- Eigenvalues
    
- Characteristic polynomial
    

These quantities are **basis-independent invariants**.

---

## **9.1.4.5 Composition of Linear Transformations**

If  
[  
T: V \to W, \quad S: W \to U  
]

then:

[  
[S \circ T] = [S][T]  
]

Matrix multiplication corresponds to **function composition**.

- Order matters.
    
- Explains non-commutativity of matrices.
    

---

## **9.1.4.6 Identity and Inverse**

- Identity transformation ↔ Identity matrix (I)
    
- (T) invertible ↔ matrix representation invertible  
    [  
    [T^{-1}] = [T]^{-1}  
    ]
    

Invertibility is basis-independent.

---

## **9.1.4.7 Canonical Forms (Preview)**

Goal of advanced linear algebra:

- Choose a basis so the matrix becomes **simple**
    

Examples:

- Diagonal form
    
- Jordan canonical form
    
- Spectral decomposition
    

This leads to deep results in **functional analysis, quantum mechanics, and differential equations**.
