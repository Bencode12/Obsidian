## **Lesson 9.2.6 — Linear Operators on Infinite-Dimensional Spaces**

So far, we’ve studied linear algebra in **finite-dimensional spaces** ((\mathbb{R}^n), (\mathbb{C}^n)). Now we extend the concepts to **infinite-dimensional spaces**, like function spaces, which is the foundation of **functional analysis, quantum mechanics, and PDEs**.

---

## **9.2.6.1 Definition**

Let (V) be a vector space (possibly infinite-dimensional) over (\mathbb{R}) or (\mathbb{C}). A **linear operator** (T: V \to V) satisfies:

[  
T(\alpha u + \beta v) = \alpha T(u) + \beta T(v), \quad \forall u,v \in V, \ \alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{F}  
]

**Examples:**

1. **Differentiation**: (T(f) = f'), (V = C^\infty(\mathbb{R}))
    
2. **Integration**: (T(f)(x) = \int_0^x f(t), dt), (V = C([0,1]))
    
3. **Multiplication operators**: (T(f)(x) = x f(x))
    

> Infinite-dimensional operators are **not always represented by matrices**, but they obey the same linearity principles.

---

## **9.2.6.2 Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors**

- Eigenvectors: (v \neq 0) such that (T(v) = \lambda v)
    
- Eigenvalues (\lambda) can form **discrete or continuous spectra**
    

**Example (Differentiation operator):**  
[  
T(f) = f', \quad f(x) = e^{\lambda x} \implies T(f) = \lambda f  
]

- Every exponential function is an eigenvector, eigenvalue = (\lambda)
    

---

## **9.2.6.3 Spectrum of an Operator**

In infinite dimensions, eigenvalues may not capture the full behavior. The **spectrum** (\sigma(T)) of a linear operator (T) includes:

1. **Point spectrum**: eigenvalues ((T(v) = \lambda v))
    
2. **Continuous spectrum**: ((T - \lambda I)) not invertible but dense range
    
3. **Residual spectrum**: ((T - \lambda I)) not invertible, range not dense
    

> This generalizes matrix eigenvalues.

---

## **9.2.6.4 Bounded and Unbounded Operators**

- **Bounded operator** (T): there exists (M) such that  
    [  
    |T(v)| \le M |v|, \quad \forall v \in V  
    ]
    
- **Unbounded operator**: differentiation in (C^\infty([0,1])) is typical
    
- Bounded operators can be treated like matrices in **Hilbert spaces**
    

---

## **9.2.6.5 Hilbert Spaces**

- A **complete inner product space** ((|v_n - v| \to 0 \implies v \in V))
    
- Examples: (L^2([a,b])), space of square-integrable functions
    
- Key: infinite-dimensional analogue of (\mathbb{R}^n) with inner product
    

---

## **9.2.6.6 Operator Diagonalization in Infinite Dimensions**

- Some operators are **diagonalizable** in an orthonormal basis (like Fourier series)  
    [  
    T(f_n) = \lambda_n f_n  
    ]
    
- Used in **Fourier analysis, quantum mechanics**, and solving PDEs
    

**Example:**

- Laplace operator (\Delta) on ([0, \pi]) with Dirichlet boundary conditions
    
- Eigenfunctions: (\sin(nx)), eigenvalues: (-n^2)
