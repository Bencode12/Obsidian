## Orthogonal and Orthonormal Sets

### Definitions

A set $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ in inner product space $V$ is:

**Orthogonal** if:
$$\langle v_i, v_j\rangle = 0 \quad \text{for } i \neq j$$

**Orthonormal** if orthogonal AND $\|v_i\| = 1$ for all $i$.

Equivalently: $\langle v_i, v_j\rangle = \delta_{ij}$ (Kronecker delta).

### Key Property

**Orthogonal sets are linearly independent!**

Proof: If $\sum c_i v_i = 0$, take inner product with $v_j$:
$$c_j\|v_j\|^2 = 0 \implies c_j = 0$$

## Gram-Schmidt Process

### Algorithm

Convert any basis $\{u_1, \ldots, u_n\}$ into orthonormal basis $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$:

1. **Initialize**: $v_1 = u_1$, then $e_1 = v_1/\|v_1\|$

2. **Iterate** for $k = 2, \ldots, n$:
   $$v_k = u_k - \sum_{j=1}^{k-1} \langle u_k, e_j\rangle e_j$$
   $$e_k = v_k/\|v_k\|$$

Subtract projections onto previous vectors!

### Example in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Start with $\{(1,1,0), (1,0,1), (0,1,1)\}$.

Step 1: $v_1 = (1,1,0)$, $e_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1,0)$

Step 2: $v_2 = (1,0,1) - \frac{1}{2}(1,1,0) = (\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{2},1)$

Normalize and continue...

```desmos-graph
y = x
y = 0
x = 0
x = [-1, 2]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Gram-Schmidt makes vectors orthogonal by removing components in directions of previous vectors.

## Orthogonal Projections

### Projection onto Vector

For nonzero $u \in V$, projection of $v$ onto $u$:

$$\text{proj}_u(v) = \frac{\langle v, u\rangle}{\langle u, u\rangle}u = \frac{\langle v, u\rangle}{\|u\|^2}u$$

Scalar coefficient: $\frac{\langle v, u\rangle}{\|u\|}$ (component of $v$ in direction $u$).

### Projection onto Subspace

For subspace $W$ with orthonormal basis $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$:

$$\text{proj}_W(v) = \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i\rangle e_i$$

Coefficients are just inner products—very simple!

### Orthogonal Decomposition

Every $v \in V$ decomposes uniquely:
$$v = w + w^\perp$$

where $w \in W$ and $w^\perp \in W^\perp$.

Specifically: $w = \text{proj}_W(v)$ and $w^\perp = v - \text{proj}_W(v)$.

---

## Best Approximation

### Theorem

For subspace $W$ and vector $v$, the projection $\text{proj}_W(v)$ is the **unique closest point** in $W$ to $v$.

Minimizes $\|v - w\|$ over all $w \in W$.

### Proof

For any $w \in W$:
$$\|v - w\|^2 = \|v - \text{proj}_W(v)\|^2 + \|\text{proj}_W(v) - w\|^2$$

By Pythagorean theorem (difference is orthogonal to $W$).

Minimum when second term is zero, i.e., $w = \text{proj}_W(v)$.

## Least Squares Approximation

### Problem

Solve inconsistent system $Ax = b$ (no exact solution).

Find $x$ minimizing $\|Ax - b\|^2$.

### Normal Equations

Optimal $x$ satisfies:
$$A^T A x = A^T b$$

Geometric interpretation: $Ax = \text{proj}_{\text{col}(A)}(b)$.

### Solution

If $A^T A$ invertible:
$$x = (A^T A)^{-1} A^T b$$

Matrix $(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$ is **pseudo-inverse** $A^+$.

## Fourier Series Connection

### Orthogonal Basis in Function Space

In $L^2[-\pi, \pi]$ with inner product $\langle f,g\rangle = \int_{-\pi}^\pi fg$:

Orthogonal basis: $\{1, \cos x, \sin x, \cos 2x, \sin 2x, \ldots\}$

### Fourier Coefficients

For function $f$:
$$a_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi}\langle f, 1\rangle, \quad a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\langle f, \cos nx\rangle, \quad b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\langle f, \sin nx\rangle$$

Fourier series is orthogonal projection onto span of trig functions!

## QR Factorization

### Statement

Any matrix $A$ with linearly independent columns can be factored as:

$$A = QR$$

where:
- $Q$: orthonormal columns
- $R$: upper triangular with positive diagonal

### Connection to Gram-Schmidt

Columns of $Q$ are result of Gram-Schmidt on columns of $A$.

Entries of $R$ are the coefficients from Gram-Schmidt process.

### Applications

- Solving least squares: $Rx = Q^T b$ (easy back substitution)
- Numerically stable alternative to normal equations
- Eigenvalue algorithms (QR algorithm)

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Forgetting to Normalize
> Orthogonal ≠ orthonormal. Must divide by norm to get unit vectors!

> [!warning] Numerical Instability in Classical Gram-Schmidt
> Use modified Gram-Schmidt or Householder reflections for better stability.

> [!warning] Assuming Projection Formula Works for Non-Orthogonal Bases
> Simple formula only works with orthonormal basis. Otherwise need to solve system.
