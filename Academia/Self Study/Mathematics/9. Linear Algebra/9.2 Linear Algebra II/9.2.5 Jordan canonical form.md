## **Lesson 9.2.5 — Jordan Canonical Form (JCF)**

The **Jordan Canonical Form** generalizes diagonalization to **matrices that are not diagonalizable**. It’s a central concept in advanced linear algebra, control theory, differential equations, and spectral analysis of linear operators.

---

## **9.2.5.1 Motivation**

Not all matrices are diagonalizable:

[  
A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}  
]

- Has a single eigenvalue (\lambda = 1)
    
- Only **one linearly independent eigenvector** → cannot diagonalize
    
- Jordan form gives a **“almost diagonal”** structure capturing the minimal polynomial and eigenstructure.
    

---

## **9.2.5.2 Jordan Block**

A **Jordan block** (J_k(\lambda)) of size (k \times k) associated with eigenvalue (\lambda):

[  
J_k(\lambda) =  
\begin{bmatrix}  
\lambda & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \  
0 & \lambda & 1 & \cdots & 0 \  
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \  
0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda & 1 \  
0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & \lambda  
\end{bmatrix}  
]

- (\lambda) on diagonal
    
- 1’s on superdiagonal
    
- 0’s elsewhere
    

> Each block corresponds to a **chain of generalized eigenvectors**.

---

## **9.2.5.3 Jordan Canonical Form (JCF)**

For any (n \times n) matrix (A) over an algebraically closed field ((\mathbb{C})):

[  
A = P J P^{-1}  
]

where:

- (J) is **block-diagonal**, each block = Jordan block (J_k(\lambda))
    
- (P) invertible, columns are **generalized eigenvectors**
    
- Generalizes diagonalization: if matrix is diagonalizable, all blocks are size 1
    

---

## **9.2.5.4 Generalized Eigenvectors**

- For eigenvalue (\lambda), generalized eigenvector (v) satisfies:  
    [  
    (A - \lambda I)^m v = 0, \quad m \ge 1  
    ]
    
- Chains of generalized eigenvectors form **basis for JCF**
    

**Example:**  
[  
A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad  
(A - I)^2 = 0  
]

- Eigenvector (v_1 = (1,0)^T)
    
- Generalized eigenvector (v_2 = (0,1)^T)
    
- Jordan form: (J = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1\0 & 1\end{bmatrix})
    

---

## **9.2.5.5 Procedure to Find JCF**

1. Find eigenvalues and algebraic multiplicities
    
2. Determine **geometric multiplicities** (dimension of eigenspaces)
    
3. For eigenvalues with geometric multiplicity < algebraic multiplicity:
    
    - Compute **chains of generalized eigenvectors**
        
4. Form **Jordan blocks** and assemble (J)
    
5. Construct (P) from generalized eigenvectors
    

---

## **9.2.5.6 Properties**

- JCF is **unique up to block order**
    
- All powers and exponentials of (A) can be computed using (J)
    
- Simplifies **linear differential equations** and **matrix functions**
