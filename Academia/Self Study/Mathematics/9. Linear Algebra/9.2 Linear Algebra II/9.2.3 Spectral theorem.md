## **Lesson 9.2.3 — Spectral Theorem**

The **Spectral Theorem** is one of the crown jewels of linear algebra. It describes the structure of **symmetric or Hermitian operators**, showing that they are fully **diagonalizable with orthonormal eigenvectors**. This is essential for quantum mechanics, PDEs, and functional analysis.

---

## **9.2.3.1 Statement of the Spectral Theorem**

### **Real Case**

Let (A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}) be **symmetric** ((A^T = A)). Then:

1. All eigenvalues of (A) are **real**.
    
2. There exists an **orthonormal basis** of (\mathbb{R}^n) consisting of eigenvectors of (A).
    
3. (A) is **orthogonally diagonalizable**:  
    [  
    A = Q D Q^T  
    ]
    

- (Q) orthogonal ((Q^T Q = I))
    
- (D) diagonal (eigenvalues on diagonal)
    

### **Complex Case**

Let (A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}) be **Hermitian** ((A^* = A), conjugate transpose). Then:

1. All eigenvalues of (A) are **real**.
    
2. There exists a **unitary basis** of eigenvectors ((U^* U = I)).
    
3. (A = U D U^*)
    

---

## **9.2.3.2 Consequences**

- Symmetric/Hermitian matrices have **complete sets of orthonormal eigenvectors**.
    
- Every linear operator on a finite-dimensional inner product space can be understood through its **eigenstructure**.
    
- Powers, exponentials, and functions of symmetric matrices are easy to compute via diagonalization.
    

[  
f(A) = Q f(D) Q^T  
]

---

## **9.2.3.3 Geometric Interpretation**

- Symmetric matrices **preserve orthogonality** in a rotated coordinate system.
    
- Each eigenvector gives a **principal axis of stretching or compression**.
    
- Example: a 2×2 symmetric matrix rotates and stretches vectors along orthogonal eigenvectors.
    

---

## **9.2.3.4 Examples**

1. **Real symmetric matrix**  
    [  
    A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}  
    ]
    

- Eigenvalues: (\lambda = 1, 3)
    
- Orthonormal eigenvectors: (v_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,-1)), (v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1))
    
- Diagonalization: (A = Q D Q^T), with (Q = [v_1\ v_2])
    

2. **Hermitian matrix**  
    [  
    B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & i \ -i & 2 \end{bmatrix}  
    ]
    

- Eigenvalues: real ((\lambda_1 = 0.3819), (\lambda_2 = 2.618))
    
- Diagonalizable with **unitary matrix** (U)
