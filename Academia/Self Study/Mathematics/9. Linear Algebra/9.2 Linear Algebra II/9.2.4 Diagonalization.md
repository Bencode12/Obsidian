## **Lesson 9.2.4 — Diagonalization in Inner Product Spaces**

Diagonalization is the process of finding a **basis in which a linear operator or matrix is diagonal**. In inner product spaces, diagonalization is tightly linked to **orthogonality, normal operators, and the spectral theorem**.

---

## **9.2.4.1 Definition**

A matrix (A \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}) is **diagonalizable** if there exists an invertible matrix (P) such that:

[  
A = P D P^{-1}  
]

- (D) = diagonal matrix (eigenvalues of (A) on the diagonal)
    
- (P) = matrix whose columns are eigenvectors of (A)
    

> Diagonalization transforms a complicated operator into a **simple, axis-aligned scaling**.

---

## **9.2.4.2 Conditions for Diagonalizability**

1. (A) has **enough linearly independent eigenvectors** ((n) for an (n \times n) matrix).
    
2. **Distinct eigenvalues** automatically guarantee diagonalizability.
    
3. If **eigenvectors are orthogonal**, we can form an **orthonormal basis**, leading to **orthogonal/unitary diagonalization**.
    

---

## **9.2.4.3 Diagonalization of Symmetric/Normal Operators**

- **Symmetric (real) or Hermitian (complex) matrices:** always diagonalizable with an orthonormal basis.
    
- **Normal operators** ((A^_A = AA^_)) are diagonalizable by a unitary matrix.
    
- **Geometric meaning:** the matrix scales along mutually perpendicular axes (eigenvectors).
    

---

## **9.2.4.4 Procedure**

1. Compute eigenvalues (\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n).
    
2. For each eigenvalue, find a basis for its eigenspace.
    
3. Combine all eigenvectors into a matrix (P).
    
4. Form diagonal matrix (D) with eigenvalues on the diagonal.
    
5. Verify (A = P D P^{-1}) (or (A = Q D Q^*) for orthonormal basis).
    

---

## **9.2.4.5 Examples**

**Example 1: Real Symmetric Matrix**  
[  
A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}  
]

1. Eigenvalues: (\lambda = 5, 2)
    
2. Eigenvectors (orthonormal): (v_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1)), (v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,-1))
    
3. Diagonalization:  
    [  
    A = Q D Q^T, \quad Q = [v_1 \ v_2], \quad D = \begin{bmatrix}5 & 0\0 & 2\end{bmatrix}  
    ]
    

**Example 2: Complex Normal Matrix**  
[  
B = \begin{bmatrix}1 & i\-i & 1\end{bmatrix}  
]

- Eigenvalues: (\lambda = 0, 2)
    
- Unitary diagonalization: (B = U D U^*)
    

---

## **9.2.4.6 Benefits of Diagonalization**

- Simplifies computation of powers: (A^k = P D^k P^{-1})
    
- Simplifies functions of matrices: (f(A) = P f(D) P^{-1})
    
- Helps solve systems of differential equations, dynamical systems, and quadratic forms
