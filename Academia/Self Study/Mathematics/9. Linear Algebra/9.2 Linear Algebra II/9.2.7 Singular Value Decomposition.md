## Definition

For any $m \times n$ matrix $A$, there exist orthogonal matrices $U$ ($m \times m$) and $V$ ($n \times n$) such that:

$$A = U\Sigma V^T$$

where $\Sigma$ is $m \times n$ **diagonal matrix** with non-negative entries:

$$\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_2 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \sigma_r \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

where $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ and $r = \text{rank}(A)$.

### Components

- **Left singular vectors**: Columns of $U$ (eigenvectors of $AA^T$)
- **Right singular vectors**: Columns of $V$ (eigenvectors of $A^T A$)
- **Singular values**: $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^T A)} = \sqrt{\lambda_i(AA^T)}$

---

## Connection to Eigenvalues

### Key Relationship

Singular values of $A$ are square roots of eigenvalues of $A^T A$ (or $AA^T$).

If $A$ is square and symmetric with eigenvalues $\lambda_i$:
$$\sigma_i = |\lambda_i|$$

SVD always exists, unlike diagonalization.

### Example

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Eigenvalues: $3, -2$

Singular values: $3, 2$ (absolute values!)

---

## Geometric Interpretation

### Matrix Action

Matrix $A$ transforms unit ball into ellipsoid.

**Singular values** = semi-axes lengths of ellipsoid.

**Left singular vectors** = directions of ellipsoid axes in output space.

**Right singular vectors** = directions in input space that become ellipsoid axes.

### Matrix Rank

$$\text{rank}(A) = \text{number of non-zero singular values}$$

### Matrix Norm

**Spectral norm** (operator norm):
$$\|A\|_2 = \sigma_{\max} = \sigma_1$$

**Frobenius norm**:
$$\|A\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j} a_{ij}^2} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2}$$

---

## Computing SVD

### Algorithm

1. Compute eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $A^T A$
2. Singular values: $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^T A)}$
3. Right singular vectors: eigenvectors of $A^T A$
4. Left singular vectors: $u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i}Av_i$

### Computational Complexity

$O(\min(mn^2, m^2n))$ for $m \times n$ matrix.

More expensive than eigenvalue decomposition, but always stable.

---

## Applications

### Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

Data matrix $X$ with SVD $X = U\Sigma V^T$.

Principal components = right singular vectors (columns of $V$).

Scores = left singular vectors scaled by singular values: $U\Sigma$.

Largest singular values correspond to most variance.

### Low-Rank Approximation

Best rank-$k$ approximation (in Frobenius norm):

$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$

Truncated SVD! Fundamental in data compression and noise reduction.

### Pseudo-Inverse

For $A = U\Sigma V^T$:

$$A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^T$$

where $\Sigma^+$ has $\frac{1}{\sigma_i}$ for non-zero $\sigma_i$, and 0 for zero entries.

Solves least squares: $x = A^+ b$ minimizes $\|Ax - b\|^2$.

### Image Compression

Represent image as matrix, keep only top $k$ singular values.

JPEG algorithm uses related discrete cosine transform.

---

## Numerical Stability

### Advantages

- Always exists (unlike diagonalization)
- Always well-conditioned (unlike Jordan form)
- Orthogonal matrices preserve geometry
- Stable computation possible

### Condition Number

$$\kappa(A) = \frac{\sigma_{\max}}{\sigma_{\min}}$$

Ratio of largest to smallest singular values.

Measures sensitivity of linear system $Ax = b$.

### Numerical Rank

Due to rounding errors, tiny singular values may be zero.

**Numerical rank** = number of "significant" singular values.

---

## Comparison with Other Decompositions

|Decomposition|Applies To|Structure|Stability|
|---|---|---|---|
|LU|Square, nonsingular|A = LU|Moderate|
|QR|Any|A = QR|Good|
|Eigendecomposition|Diagonalizable|A = PDP⁻¹|Poor|
|Schur|Square|A = QTQ*|Good|
|SVD|Any|A = UΣV^T|Excellent|

SVD is most general and stable, but most expensive.

---

## Special Cases

### Symmetric Matrices

For symmetric $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_i$:

- Singular values: $\sigma_i = |\lambda_i|$
- Left singular vectors: columns of $Q$
- Right singular vectors: columns of $Q$

### Square, Invertible Matrices

SVD exists even if not diagonalizable.

### Rank-Deficient Matrices

Zero singular values appear naturally in SVD.

Eigenvalue decomposition may fail for defective matrices.

---

## Algorithms

### Classical Approach

1. Reduce to bidiagonal form using Householder reflections
2. Apply QR algorithm to bidiagonal matrix
3. Accumulate transformations

### Modern Approaches

- Divide-and-conquer
- Jacobi SVD (high accuracy for small matrices)
- Randomized SVD (for large matrices)

---

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Singular Values with Eigenvalues
> They're related but different! SVD always exists, eigenvalues may not.

> [!warning] Assuming SVD Is Cheap to Compute
> More expensive than other decompositions. Consider alternatives for large problems.

> [!warning] Forgetting That A and A^T Have Same Singular Values
> Eigenvalues of A^T A and AA^T are the same, so singular values match.

---
