## Definition

A **pattern** is a regular, repeated arrangement or sequence that we can observe and describe.

Patterns are everywhere in mathematics and nature. Recognizing patterns is one of the most fundamental mathematical skills—it's how we discover rules, make predictions, and understand structure.

> [!example] Why Patterns Matter
> Pattern recognition is the foundation of:  
> - Algebra (finding formulas)  
> - Geometry (symmetry and tessellation)  
> - Number theory (prime patterns)  
> - Calculus (rates of change)  
> - All of science!

## Types of Patterns

### Numerical Patterns

Sequences of numbers following a rule.

**Example 1**: Even numbers: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, ...

Rule: Add 2 each time, or $a_n = 2n$

**Example 2**: Powers of 2: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, ...

Rule: Multiply by 2 each time, or $a_n = 2^{n-1}$

```graph
y = 2^(x-1)
x = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]
```

> [!example] Visual Growth
> The graph shows exponential growth—each step doubles the previous value. This pattern appears in population growth, compound interest, and computer algorithms.

### Geometric Patterns

Shapes that repeat or transform regularly.

**Example**: Tessellations (tiling patterns)

Squares, triangles, and hexagons can tile a plane without gaps.

**Example**: Fractals

Self-similar patterns that repeat at different scales (snowflakes, coastlines, trees).

### Symmetry Patterns

Objects that look the same after certain transformations.

|Type|Description|Example|
|---|---|---|
|Reflection|Mirror image|Butterfly wings|
|Rotation|Looks same after turning|Snowflake (6-fold)|
|Translation|Shift without rotation|Brick wall|
|Glide reflection|Reflect + shift|Footprints|

## Recognizing Patterns

### Step-by-Step Method

**Example**: Find the pattern: 3, 7, 11, 15, 19, ...

**Step 1**: Look at differences between consecutive terms

$$7 - 3 = 4$$
$$11 - 7 = 4$$
$$15 - 11 = 4$$

**Step 2**: Identify the rule

Constant difference of 4 → arithmetic sequence

**Step 3**: Write the formula

$$a_n = 3 + (n-1) \cdot 4 = 4n - 1$$

**Step 4**: Verify

- $n = 1$: $4(1) - 1 = 3$ ✓
- $n = 2$: $4(2) - 1 = 7$ ✓
- $n = 3$: $4(3) - 1 = 11$ ✓

### Common Pattern Types

|Pattern Type|How to Recognize|Example|
|---|---|---|
|Arithmetic|Constant difference|2, 5, 8, 11, ... (+3)|
|Geometric|Constant ratio|3, 6, 12, 24, ... (×2)|
|Quadratic|Second differences constant|1, 4, 9, 16, ... ($n^2$)|
|Fibonacci|Sum of previous two|1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, ...|

## Patterns in Nature

Nature is full of mathematical patterns!

### Spiral Patterns

**Sunflower Seeds**: Arranged in spirals following Fibonacci numbers.

Count the spirals: usually 34 in one direction, 55 in the other (consecutive Fibonacci numbers!).

### Hexagonal Patterns

**Honeycomb**: Bees build hexagonal cells because hexagons tile the plane with minimal perimeter (most efficient use of wax).

```graph
y = sqrt(3)*x
y = -sqrt(3)*x
y = sqrt(3)*(x-2)
y = -sqrt(3)*(x-2)
y = sqrt(3)*(x+2)
y = -sqrt(3)*(x+2)
```

> [!example] Efficiency in Nature
> The hexagonal grid minimizes material while maximizing space. This is why bees "chose" it through evolution!

### Branching Patterns

**Trees and Rivers**: Follow fractal patterns—similar structures at different scales.

### Wave Patterns

**Sand Dunes, Water Ripples**: Periodic patterns described by sine and cosine functions.

## Why Do Patterns Exist?

### Mathematical Reasons

1. **Efficiency**: Nature optimizes (least energy, shortest path, maximum strength)
2. **Symmetry**: Physical laws are symmetric, leading to symmetric patterns
3. **Recursion**: Simple rules repeated create complex patterns
4. **Conservation Laws**: Energy, momentum conservation lead to regular structures

### Philosophical Question

Why is mathematics so effective at describing nature? This is called "The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics" (Eugene Wigner, 1960).

## Using Patterns to Make Predictions

Once you identify a pattern, you can predict future values.

**Example**: A bacteria colony doubles every hour. Starting with 100 bacteria:

- Hour 0: 100
- Hour 1: 200
- Hour 2: 400
- Hour 3: 800

Pattern: $N(t) = 100 \cdot 2^t$

Prediction: After 10 hours, $N(10) = 100 \cdot 2^{10} = 102,400$ bacteria

> [!warning] Limitations
> Patterns don't always continue forever! Real systems have limits (resources, space, etc.). Always question whether a pattern will persist.

## Creating Your Own Patterns

### Exercise 1: Design a Number Pattern

Create a sequence where each term is 3 more than twice the previous term, starting with 1.

Solution:
- $a_1 = 1$
- $a_2 = 2(1) + 3 = 5$
- $a_3 = 2(5) + 3 = 13$
- $a_4 = 2(13) + 3 = 29$

Sequence: 1, 5, 13, 29, ...

### Exercise 2: Geometric Pattern

Draw a pattern where each square has half the side length of the previous one.

This creates a geometric series for the areas!

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Seeing Patterns That Aren't There
> Humans are pattern-seeking creatures. Sometimes we see meaning in randomness (apophenia). Always verify with more data!

> [!warning] Assuming Patterns Continue Forever
> Just because something happens 10 times doesn't mean it'll happen the 11th time. Test your predictions!

> [!warning] Oversimplifying Complex Patterns
> Not all patterns are simple arithmetic or geometric. Some require sophisticated mathematics to describe.
