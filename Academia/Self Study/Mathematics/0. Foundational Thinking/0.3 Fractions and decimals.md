## The Base-10 Number System

> [!info] Why Base-10? Humans have 10 fingers, which is why most cultures developed a base-10 system. Other bases exist (binary = base-2 for computers, hexadecimal = base-16), but decimal is our natural counting system.

|Position|Power of 10|Value|
|---|---|---|
|Ones|$10^0$|$1$|
|Tens|$10^1$|$10$|
|Hundreds|$10^2$|$100$|
|Thousands|$10^3$|$1{,}000$|
|Ten-thousands|$10^4$|$10{,}000$|

## Why Place Matters

The **same digit** can represent very different values depending on its position.

|Number|What the 3 means|
|---|---|
|$3$|$3 \times 1$ = three ones|
|$30$|$3 \times 10$ = three tens|
|$300$|$3 \times 100$ = three hundreds|

> [!warning] Common Misconception Students often think the digit "3" always means three. In reality, it could mean 3, 30, 300, 3000, or even 0.3 depending on its position!

## Zero as a Placeholder

Zero does not always mean nothing — sometimes it **holds a place**.

$$105 = (1 \times 100) + (0 \times 10) + (5 \times 1)$$

Without the zero, $15$ would mean something completely different — the hundreds digit would collapse into the tens position.

> [!tip] Zero is Structural
> 
> Zero tells you a position is empty, not that the number is smaller. It's like a placeholder seat in a row — it reserves the spot.
