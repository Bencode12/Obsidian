## **Lesson 10.3.4 — Martingales**

Martingales are a fundamental concept in **stochastic processes** and advanced probability theory. They formalize the notion of a **“fair game”** over time and are crucial in **financial mathematics, stochastic calculus, and theoretical probability**.

---

### **1. Definition**

Let ((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)) be a probability space with a **filtration** ({\mathcal{F}_n}_{n \ge 0}), i.e., an increasing sequence of σ-algebras representing information over time:

[  
\mathcal{F}_0 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_1 \subseteq \dots \subseteq \mathcal{F}_n \subseteq \dots \subseteq \mathcal{F}  
]

A sequence of integrable random variables ({X_n}) is a **martingale** with respect to ({\mathcal{F}_n}) if:

1. **Adaptedness:** (X_n) is (\mathcal{F}_n)-measurable
    
2. **Integrability:** (E[|X_n|] < \infty)
    
3. **Fair game property:**  
    [  
    E[X_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = X_n \quad \text{for all } n  
    ]
    

- **Supermartingale:** (E[X_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] \le X_n) (expected decrease)
    
- **Submartingale:** (E[X_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] \ge X_n) (expected increase)
    

---

### **2. Intuition**

- Martingale = “**no predictable gain**” over time given current information
    
- Examples: fair gambling game, unbiased random walk
    
- Conditional expectation ensures **future expected value equals current value**
    

---

### **3. Examples**

1. **Simple Random Walk**:
    

- (S_0 = 0), (S_n = X_1 + \dots + X_n), where (X_i = \pm 1) with probability (1/2)
    
- Filtration: (\mathcal{F}_n = \sigma(X_1, \dots, X_n))
    
- (S_n) is a martingale: (E[S_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = S_n)
    

2. **Gambling Game with Fair Coin:**
    

- Let (X_n) = total winnings after (n) bets
    
- If game is fair, ({X_n}) forms a martingale
    

3. **Conditional Expectation Martingale:**
    

- Let (X \in L^1) and (\mathcal{F}_n \subset \mathcal{F})
    
- Define (M_n = E[X \mid \mathcal{F}_n])
    
- Then ({M_n}) is a martingale
    

---

### **4. Key Properties**

1. **Linearity:** If ({X_n}) and ({Y_n}) are martingales, (aX_n + bY_n) is a martingale
    
2. **Stopping Times:** If (\tau) is a stopping time with respect to ({\mathcal{F}_n}), and certain conditions hold:  
    [  
    E[X_\tau] = E[X_0] \quad \text{(Optional Stopping Theorem)}  
    ]
    
3. **Doob’s Martingale Inequalities:** Provide bounds for maximal deviations
    
4. **Convergence Theorems:** Bounded martingales converge almost surely and in (L^1)
    

---

### **5. Applications**

1. **Finance & Option Pricing:** Martingales model “fair” asset price evolution under risk-neutral measures
    
2. **Stochastic Calculus:** Brownian motion and continuous-time martingales form the basis of Itô calculus
    
3. **Probability Theory:** Proving limit theorems, inequalities, and convergence results
    
4. **Queueing & Inventory Systems:** Expected future behavior of stochastic processes
    

---

### **6. Exercises**

1. Let (X_n = S_n) be a simple symmetric random walk. Show ({X_n}) is a martingale.
    
2. Let (X \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]), (\mathcal{F}_n = \sigma(X^n)). Define (M_n = E[X \mid \mathcal{F}_n]). Show ({M_n}) is a martingale.
    
3. Prove that if ({X_n}) is a bounded martingale, then (\lim X_n) exists almost surely.
    
4. Consider a gambling game with biased coin (p \neq 0.5). Determine if ({X_n}) is a martingale, supermartingale, or submartingale.
