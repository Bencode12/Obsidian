## Uniform Integrability

### Definition

A family of random variables $\{X_\alpha\}$ is **uniformly integrable (UI)** if:

$$\lim_{M \to \infty} \sup_\alpha E[|X_\alpha|1_{\{|X_\alpha| > M\}}] = 0$$

Intuitively: Tails are uniformly small across the family.

### Equivalent Conditions

The following are equivalent:
1. $\{X_\alpha\}$ is UI
2. $\sup_\alpha E[|X_\alpha|] < \infty$ and for all $\epsilon > 0$, exists $\delta > 0$ such that $P(A) < \delta \implies \sup_\alpha E[|X_\alpha|1_A] < \epsilon$
3. $\sup_\alpha E[\phi(|X_\alpha|)] < \infty$ for some $\phi$ with $\phi(x)/x \to \infty$

## Importance in Convergence

### Vitali Convergence Theorem

$X_n \xrightarrow{L^1} X$ if and only if:
1. $X_n \xrightarrow{P} X$
2. $\{X_n\}$ is uniformly integrable

Connects convergence in probability and $L^1$!

### Martingale Convergence

Martingale $\{X_n\}$ converges in $L^1$ if and only if it's UI.

Otherwise, only get a.s. convergence (not $L^1$).

### Example: Not UI

Let $X_n = n$ with probability $1/n$, and $X_n = 0$ otherwise.

- $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$
- But $E[|X_n|] = 1$ for all $n$, so not UI
- No $L^1$ convergence!

## Large Deviations Theory

### Motivation

CLT describes typical fluctuations ($O(\sqrt{n})$). Large deviations studies **rare events** (exponentially small probabilities).

### Cramér's Theorem

For i.i.d. $X_i$ with finite MGF, let $\Lambda(t) = \log E[e^{tX_1}]$ (cumulant generating function).

**Rate function** (Legendre-Fenchel transform):
$$I(x) = \sup_t\{tx - \Lambda(t)\}$$

Then for sample mean $\bar{X}_n$:
$$P(\bar{X}_n \geq x) \approx e^{-nI(x)} \quad \text{for } x > \mu$$

Exponential decay rate given by rate function!

### Applications

- Queueing theory (buffer overflow)
- Finance (risk management)
- Statistical mechanics (phase transitions)
- Information theory (error exponents)

## Empirical Processes

### Empirical Distribution Function

For i.i.d. sample $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ from distribution $F$:

$$F_n(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n 1_{\{X_i \leq x\}}$$

Random step function approximating true CDF.

### Glivenko-Cantelli Theorem

$$\sup_x |F_n(x) - F(x)| \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$$

Uniform convergence of empirical CDF to true CDF!

Foundation of nonparametric statistics.

### Donsker's Theorem (Functional CLT)

Define empirical process:
$$G_n(x) = \sqrt{n}(F_n(x) - F(x))$$

Then $G_n \Rightarrow B_F$ (Brownian bridge).

Weak convergence in function space!

## Concentration Inequalities

### Chernoff Bound

For any $t > 0$:
$$P(X \geq a) \leq e^{-ta}M_X(t)$$

Optimize over $t$ for tightest bound.

### Hoeffding's Inequality

For independent bounded variables $X_i \in [a_i, b_i]$:

$$P\left(\left|\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - E[X_i])\right| \geq t\right) \leq 2\exp\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i-a_i)^2}\right)$$

Powerful for sums of bounded variables!

### McDiarmid's Inequality (Bounded Differences)

If changing one coordinate changes $f$ by at most $c_i$:

$$P(|f(X) - E[f(X)]| \geq t) \leq 2\exp\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum c_i^2}\right)$$

Used in machine learning theory!

### Talagrand's Inequality

For convex Lipschitz functions of independent variables, gives optimal concentration.

Deep result with many applications!

## Stein's Method

### Overview

Technique for bounding distance between distributions.

Especially useful for proving CLT with error bounds.

### Stein's Lemma

For standard normal $Z$:
$$E[Zf(Z)] = E[f'(Z)]$$

for smooth test functions $f$.

Characterizes normal distribution!

### Application to CLT

Construct "Stein equation" and bound solution to get Berry-Esseen type results.

More flexible than characteristic function methods!

## Coupling Methods

### Definition

**Coupling** of random variables $X$ and $Y$: Construct $(X', Y')$ on common probability space with $X' \stackrel{d}{=} X$ and $Y' \stackrel{d}{=} Y$.

### Applications

**Total variation distance**:
$$d_{TV}(P, Q) = \inf P(X' \neq Y')$$

where infimum over all couplings.

**Monotone coupling**: For stochastic ordering.

**Maximal coupling**: Achieves total variation distance.

### Example: Coupling Random Walks

Two random walks can be coupled to meet (coalesce) with high probability.

Used in mixing time analysis for Markov chains!

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Assuming UI Without Verification
> Many naturally occurring families are NOT uniformly integrable. Check carefully!

> [!warning] Confusing Different Types of Tail Bounds
> Chernoff, Hoeffding, Bernstein have different assumptions. Use appropriate one!

> [!warning] Misapplying Large Deviations
> LDP gives exponential rate, not exact probability. Prefactor matters for moderate deviations.
