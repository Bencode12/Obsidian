## **Lesson 10.1.1 — Sample Spaces & σ-Algebras**

Probability starts by defining **what can happen** (sample space) and **which events we can measure** (σ-algebra). This sets the stage for formal probability theory.

---

### **1. Sample Space ((\Omega))**

The **sample space** (\Omega) is the set of **all possible outcomes** of a random experiment.

**Examples:**

1. Coin toss: (\Omega = {H, T})
    
2. Roll of a die: (\Omega = {1,2,3,4,5,6})
    
3. Infinite experiment (e.g., measuring temperature): (\Omega = \mathbb{R})
    

> Every element (\omega \in \Omega) is called an **outcome**.

---

### **2. Events**

An **event** (A) is a **subset of (\Omega)**:

[  
A \subseteq \Omega  
]

- Example: rolling an even number on a die: (A = {2,4,6})
    
- The **empty set** (\emptyset) represents an impossible event
    
- The **whole space** (\Omega) represents a certain event
    

---

### **3. σ-Algebra ((\mathcal{F}))**

A **σ-algebra** (\mathcal{F}) is a collection of subsets of (\Omega) satisfying:

1. **Contains the whole space:** (\Omega \in \mathcal{F})
    
2. **Closed under complement:** (A \in \mathcal{F} \implies A^c \in \mathcal{F})
    
3. **Closed under countable unions:** If (A_1, A_2, A_3, \dots \in \mathcal{F}), then  
    [  
    \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i \in \mathcal{F}  
    ]
    

> By closure under complements and unions, σ-algebras are also closed under **countable intersections**.

**Purpose:** Not all subsets may be measurable in infinite or continuous spaces. σ-algebras define **which events can be assigned probabilities**.

---

### **4. Examples of σ-Algebras**

1. **Finite sample space**: (\Omega = {1,2,3})
    

- Power set (\mathcal{F} = 2^\Omega) is the σ-algebra: contains all subsets.
    

2. **Trivial σ-algebra**: (\mathcal{F} = {\emptyset, \Omega})
    

- Only certain or impossible events are measurable.
    

3. **Borel σ-algebra on (\mathbb{R})**:
    

- Generated by all intervals ([a,b])
    
- Fundamental for **continuous probability distributions**
    

---

### **5. Probability Measure**

Once we have ((\Omega, \mathcal{F})), a **probability measure** (P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]) assigns probabilities to events:

1. **Normalization:** (P(\Omega) = 1)
    
2. **Non-negativity:** (P(A) \ge 0, \forall A \in \mathcal{F})
    
3. **Countable additivity:** For disjoint events (A_i):  
    [  
    P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(A_i)  
    ]
    

> Together, ((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)) forms a **probability space**.

---

### **6. Key Points**

- **Sample space** defines “what can happen.”
    
- **Event** is a measurable subset of outcomes.
    
- **σ-algebra** ensures probabilities are well-defined, even for **infinite or uncountable spaces**.
    
- Probability measure satisfies **axioms**.
