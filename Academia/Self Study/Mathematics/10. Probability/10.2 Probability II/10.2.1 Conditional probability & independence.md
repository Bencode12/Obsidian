## **Lesson 10.2.1 — Conditional Probability & Independence**

Conditional probability and independence are core concepts in probability theory, crucial for modeling **dependence between events or random variables**.

---

### **1. Conditional Probability**

The **conditional probability** of event (A) given event (B) ((P(B) > 0)):

[  
P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}  
]

- Intuition: “Probability of (A) happening **if we know** (B) has occurred.”
    
- **Multiplication rule:**  
    [  
    P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B) P(B) = P(B \mid A) P(A)  
    ]
    

---

### **2. Law of Total Probability**

If ({B_1, B_2, \dots, B_n}) is a **partition of (\Omega)** (disjoint events covering all outcomes), then for any event (A):

[  
P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A \mid B_i) P(B_i)  
]

- Useful for **breaking complex events into simpler cases**.
    

---

### **3. Bayes’ Theorem**

Bayes’ theorem allows **updating probabilities** based on new evidence:

[  
P(B_j \mid A) = \frac{P(A \mid B_j) P(B_j)}{\sum_{i=1}^n P(A \mid B_i) P(B_i)}  
]

- Foundation of **Bayesian statistics, inference, and decision making**.
    
- Interpreted as **posterior = likelihood × prior / normalization**.
    

---

### **4. Independence**

1. **Two events (A) and (B)** are independent if:
    

[  
P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B)  
]

- Equivalent to: (P(A \mid B) = P(A)) and (P(B \mid A) = P(B))
    

2. **Multiple events:** (A_1, A_2, \dots, A_n) are independent if **every subset** satisfies:
    

[  
P\Big(\bigcap_{i \in S} A_i\Big) = \prod_{i \in S} P(A_i), \quad \forall S \subseteq {1,\dots,n}  
]

3. **Random variables (X) and (Y)** are independent if:
    

[  
P(X \in A, Y \in B) = P(X \in A) P(Y \in B), \quad \forall A,B  
]

---

### **5. Conditional Independence**

- (X) and (Y) are **conditionally independent given (Z)** if:
    

[  
P(X \in A, Y \in B \mid Z) = P(X \in A \mid Z) P(Y \in B \mid Z)  
]

- Crucial in **Bayesian networks** and **stochastic modeling**.
    

---

### **6. Examples**

1. **Simple conditional probability:**
    

- Deck of 52 cards, (A) = “draw a heart”, (B) = “draw a red card”  
    [  
    P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{13/52}{26/52} = 1/2  
    ]
    

2. **Independence check:**
    

- Toss a coin twice, (A) = “first head”, (B) = “second head”  
    [  
    P(A) = P(B) = 1/2, \quad P(A \cap B) = 1/4 = P(A)P(B) \implies \text{independent}  
    ]
    

3. **Bayes’ theorem example:**
    

- Disease prevalence (P(D) = 0.01), test accuracy: (P(+|D) = 0.99, P(+|D^c) = 0.05)
    
- Posterior probability (P(D|+)) using Bayes’ formula.
