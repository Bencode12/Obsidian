## Stochastic Processes

### Definition

A **stochastic process** is a collection of random variables $\{X_t : t \in T\}$ indexed by time or space.

- **Discrete-time**: $T = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ or integers
- **Continuous-time**: $T = [0, \infty)$ or real numbers

Each outcome $\omega$ gives a **sample path** $t \mapsto X_t(\omega)$.

---

## Markov Chains

### Definition

A **Markov chain** has the **memoryless property**:

$$P(X_{n+1} = j | X_n = i, X_{n-1}, \ldots, X_0) = P(X_{n+1} = j | X_n = i)$$

Future depends only on present, not past!

### Transition Matrix

For discrete state space $\{1, 2, \ldots, m\}$:

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} p_{11} & p_{12} & \cdots & p_{1m} \\ p_{21} & p_{22} & \cdots & p_{2m} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ p_{m1} & p_{m2} & \cdots & p_{mm} \end{pmatrix}$$

where $p_{ij} = P(X_{n+1} = j | X_n = i)$.

**Properties**:
- $p_{ij} \geq 0$ for all $i,j$
- $\sum_j p_{ij} = 1$ for all $i$ (rows sum to 1)

### n-Step Transitions

$$P^{(n)} = P^n$$

Entry $(i,j)$ of $P^n$ gives $P(X_n = j | X_0 = i)$.

## Stationary Distributions

### Definition

A distribution $\pi = (\pi_1, \ldots, \pi_m)$ is **stationary** if:

$$\pi P = \pi$$

Equivalently: $\pi_j = \sum_i \pi_i p_{ij}$ for all $j$.

If $X_0 \sim \pi$, then $X_n \sim \pi$ for all $n$.

### Existence and Uniqueness

For **irreducible** (all states communicate) and **aperiodic** chains:
- Unique stationary distribution exists
- $P^n \to \mathbf{1}\pi^T$ as $n \to \infty$
- Chain converges to stationarity regardless of initial state

### Finding Stationary Distribution

Solve system:
1. $\pi P = \pi$
2. $\sum_i \pi_i = 1$

This gives $m$ equations in $m$ unknowns.

## Classification of States

### Communicating Classes

State $i$ **communicates** with $j$ (write $i \leftrightarrow j$) if:
- Can reach $j$ from $i$ in some steps
- Can reach $i$ from $j$ in some steps

States that communicate form **communicating classes**.

### Recurrence and Transience

**Recurrent state**: Starting from $i$, return to $i$ with probability 1.

**Transient state**: Positive probability of never returning.

In finite chains: at least one recurrent class, no transient states if irreducible.

### Periodicity

**Period** of state $i$:
$$d(i) = \gcd\{n \geq 1 : P(X_n = i | X_0 = i) > 0\}$$

- **Aperiodic**: $d(i) = 1$
- **Periodic**: $d(i) > 1$

Aperiodicity needed for convergence to stationarity!

## Poisson Processes

### Definition

A **Poisson process** with rate $\lambda$ counts events occurring randomly in time.

**Properties**:
1. $N(0) = 0$
2. Independent increments
3. Stationary increments
4. $N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$

### Inter-Arrival Times

Time between consecutive events: $T_i \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$ i.i.d.

Sum of $n$ exponentials: $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n T_i \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$.

### Example Applications

- Customer arrivals at store
- Phone calls at call center
- Radioactive decay events
- Website hits

## Brownian Motion (Wiener Process)

### Definition

**Standard Brownian motion** $\{B_t : t \geq 0\}$ satisfies:

1. $B_0 = 0$
2. Independent increments
3. $B_t - B_s \sim N(0, t-s)$ for $t > s$
4. Continuous sample paths (almost surely)

### Properties

- **Scaling**: $cB_{t/c^2}$ is also Brownian motion
- **Reflection**: $-B_t$ is Brownian motion
- **Martingale**: $E[B_t | B_s] = B_s$ for $t > s$
- **Nowhere differentiable**: Sample paths are continuous but rough!

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Simulated Brownian paths: continuous but highly irregular. Fractal-like structure at all scales.

### Geometric Brownian Motion

$$S_t = S_0 \exp\left((\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2})t + \sigma B_t\right)$$

Used in Black-Scholes model for stock prices!

## Martingales

### Definition

A process $\{X_n\}$ is a **martingale** if:

$$E[X_{n+1} | X_n, X_{n-1}, \ldots, X_0] = X_n$$

Expected future value equals current value. "Fair game."

### Examples

- Random walk: $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ where $X_i = \pm 1$ with equal probability
- Brownian motion: $B_t$
- Gambler's fortune in fair game

### Martingale Convergence Theorem

If $\{X_n\}$ is a martingale bounded in $L^1$, then $X_n$ converges almost surely.

Powerful tool for proving convergence!

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Stationary Distribution with Stationary Process
> Stationary distribution: fixed point of transition matrix. Stationary process: statistical properties don't change over time.

> [!warning] Forgetting Aperiodicity
> Periodic chains oscillate and don't converge to stationary distribution.

> [!warning] Assuming All Processes Are Markov
> Many processes have memory. Check Markov property before applying theory.
