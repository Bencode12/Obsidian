## **Lesson 10.2.3 — Central Limit Theorem (CLT)**

The **Central Limit Theorem** is one of the most important results in probability and statistics. It explains why **normal distributions appear so frequently** in nature and justifies approximating sums of random variables with a Gaussian distribution.

---

### **1. Statement of the CLT**

Let (X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n) be **i.i.d. random variables** with:

[  
E[X_i] = \mu, \quad \text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty  
]

Define the **standardized sum**:

[  
Z_n = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i - n\mu}{\sigma \sqrt{n}} = \frac{\sqrt{n} (\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma}  
]

Then, as (n \to \infty):

[  
Z_n \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)  
]

- Meaning: The distribution of (Z_n) **converges in distribution** to a standard normal (mean 0, variance 1).
    

---

### **2. Intuition**

- Sums or averages of **many independent random variables**, regardless of the original distribution (as long as mean and variance exist), tend to **look Gaussian** when properly normalized.
    
- Explains why **measurement errors, heights, IQ scores**, and many natural phenomena follow approximate normal distributions.
    

---

### **3. Standardization**

To apply the CLT:

1. Compute **sample mean** (\bar{X}_n)
    
2. Subtract population mean (\mu)
    
3. Scale by standard deviation of the sum: (\sigma / \sqrt{n})
    

This gives:

[  
\bar{X}_n \approx N\Big(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\Big)  
]

- For large (n), the **sampling distribution of the mean** is approximately normal.
    

---

### **4. Examples**

1. **Dice rolls**:
    

- Let (X_i) = outcome of i-th die roll (1–6), (\mu = 3.5), (\sigma^2 = 35/12 \approx 2.9167)
    
- Sum of 100 rolls: (S_{100} = \sum X_i)
    
- Standardized sum:  
    [  
    Z = \frac{S_{100} - 100_3.5}{\sqrt{100_2.9167}} \approx N(0,1)  
    ]
    

2. **Coin tosses**:
    

- (X_i = 1) for heads, 0 for tails, (p = 0.5), (n) large
    
- Sample mean (\bar{X}_n) approximates (N(0.5, 0.25/n))
    

---

### **5. Applications**

1. **Confidence intervals**: Estimate population mean from sample mean.
    
2. **Hypothesis testing**: Use normal approximation for sum or average.
    
3. **Monte Carlo simulations**: Approximate distributions of sums.
    
4. **Quality control & finance**: Predict fluctuations, error rates, or returns.
    

---

### **6. Conditions for CLT**

- Variables must be **independent**
    
- Identically distributed (can be relaxed using **Lindeberg–Feller CLT**)
    
- Finite mean (\mu) and variance (\sigma^2)
    

> There are **generalizations** for non-i.i.d. cases and for weak dependence.
