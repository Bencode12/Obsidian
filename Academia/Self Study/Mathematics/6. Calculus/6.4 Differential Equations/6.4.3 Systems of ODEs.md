## Standard Form

**System of first-order ODEs**:
$$\begin{aligned} x_1' &= f_1(t, x_1, \ldots, x_n) \\ x_2' &= f_2(t, x_1, \ldots, x_n) \\ &\vdots \\ x_n' &= f_n(t, x_1, \ldots, x_n) \end{aligned}$$

Vector form: $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{f}(t, \mathbf{x})$

---

## Linear Systems with Constant Coefficients

### Homogeneous System

$$\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}$$

where $A$ is $n \times n$ constant matrix.

### Solution Method

1. Find eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ and eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_i$ of $A$
2. Solutions: $\mathbf{x}_i(t) = e^{\lambda_i t}\mathbf{v}_i$
3. General solution: $\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1\mathbf{x}_1 + \cdots + c_n\mathbf{x}_n$

```desmos-graph
x = e^(0.5*t)*cos(2*t)
y = e^(0.5*t)*sin(2*t)
t = [0, 4*pi]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Spiral trajectory from complex eigenvalues with positive real part.

## Distinct Real Eigenvalues

### Case: $n=2$ with $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$

If $A\mathbf{v}_1 = \lambda_1\mathbf{v}_1$ and $A\mathbf{v}_2 = \lambda_2\mathbf{v}_2$:

$$\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1e^{\lambda_1 t}\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2e^{\lambda_2 t}\mathbf{v}_2$$

### Example

Solve $\mathbf{x}' = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$:

Eigenvalues: $\det(A-\lambda I) = (3-\lambda)(2-\lambda) = 0$

So $\lambda_1 = 3$, $\lambda_2 = 2$.

For $\lambda_1 = 3$: $(A-3I)\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = 0$

Eigenvector: $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$

For $\lambda_2 = 2$: $(A-2I)\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = 0$

Eigenvector: $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$

Solution: $\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1e^{3t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} + c_2e^{2t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$

## Complex Eigenvalues

### Case: $\lambda = \alpha \pm i\beta$

If $A\mathbf{v} = (\alpha + i\beta)\mathbf{v}$ where $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{a} + i\mathbf{b}$:

Real solutions:
$$\mathbf{x}_1(t) = e^{\alpha t}[\mathbf{a}\cos(\beta t) - \mathbf{b}\sin(\beta t)]$$
$$\mathbf{x}_2(t) = e^{\alpha t}[\mathbf{a}\sin(\beta t) + \mathbf{b}\cos(\beta t)]$$

### Example

Solve $\mathbf{x}' = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$:

Characteristic: $\det(A-\lambda I) = (1-\lambda)^2 + 2 = 0$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda = 1 \pm i\sqrt{2}$

For $\lambda = 1 + i\sqrt{2}$: Solve $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v} = 0$

Eigenvector: $\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} i\sqrt{2} \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + i\begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{2} \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$

So $\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$, $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{2} \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$

Solutions:
$$\mathbf{x}_1 = e^t\begin{pmatrix} -\sqrt{2}\sin(\sqrt{2}t) \\ \cos(\sqrt{2}t) \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{x}_2 = e^t\begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{2}\cos(\sqrt{2}t) \\ \sin(\sqrt{2}t) \end{pmatrix}$$

## Repeated Eigenvalues

### Case: $\lambda$ with algebraic multiplicity 2

If only one eigenvector $\mathbf{v}$, find **generalized eigenvector** $\mathbf{w}$:
$$(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{v}$$

Solutions:
$$\mathbf{x}_1(t) = e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}$$
$$\mathbf{x}_2(t) = e^{\lambda t}(t\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w})$$

### Example

Solve $\mathbf{x}' = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$:

Eigenvalue: $\lambda = 2$ (multiplicity 2)

Eigenvector: $(A-2I)\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = 0$

So $\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$

Generalized eigenvector: $(A-2I)\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{v}$

$\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} w_1 \\ w_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$

So $w_2 = 1$, choose $w_1 = 0$: $\mathbf{w} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$

Solutions:
$$\mathbf{x}_1 = e^{2t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{x}_2 = e^{2t}\begin{pmatrix} t \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

## Nonhomogeneous Systems

### General Solution

$$\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{f}(t)$$

Solution: $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_h + \mathbf{x}_p$

### Method 1: Undetermined Coefficients

Guess form of $\mathbf{x}_p$ based on $\mathbf{f}(t)$, similar to scalar case.

### Method 2: Variation of Parameters

If $\Phi(t)$ is fundamental matrix (columns are independent solutions):

$$\mathbf{x}_p(t) = \Phi(t)\int \Phi^{-1}(t)\mathbf{f}(t)\,dt$$

## Phase Plane Analysis

### Equilibrium Points

Points where $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{0}$, i.e., $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.

For invertible $A$: Only equilibrium is $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.

### Classification

|Eigenvalues|Type|Stability|
|---|---|---|
|Real, both negative|Stable node|Asymptotically stable|
|Real, both positive|Unstable node|Unstable|
|Real, opposite signs|Saddle point|Unstable|
|Complex, $\text{Re}(\lambda) < 0$|Stable spiral|Asymptotically stable|
|Complex, $\text{Re}(\lambda) > 0$|Unstable spiral|Unstable|
|Pure imaginary|Center|Stable (not asymptotic)|

## Matrix Exponential

### Definition

$$e^{At} = I + At + \frac{(At)^2}{2!} + \frac{(At)^3}{3!} + \cdots$$

Solution to $\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}$ with $\mathbf{x}(0) = \mathbf{x}_0$:
$$\mathbf{x}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{x}_0$$

### Computing $e^{At}$

If $A$ diagonalizable: $A = PDP^{-1}$

Then: $e^{At} = Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}$ where $e^{Dt} = \text{diag}(e^{\lambda_1 t}, \ldots, e^{\lambda_n t})$

## Applications

### Coupled Spring-Mass Systems

Two masses connected by springs lead to system:
$$\begin{pmatrix} m_1 & 0 \\ 0 & m_2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x_1'' \\ x_2'' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -(k_1+k_2) & k_2 \\ k_2 & -k_2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Convert to first-order system for analysis.

### Predator-Prey Models

Lotka-Volterra equations:
$$\begin{aligned} x' &= ax - bxy \\ y' &= -cy + dxy \end{aligned}$$

Nonlinear system with periodic solutions.

### Electrical Circuits

RLC circuits with multiple loops give systems of ODEs.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Forgetting to Check Diagonalizability
> Not all matrices are diagonalizable. Need generalized eigenvectors for defective matrices.

> [!warning] Confusing Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity
> Number of independent eigenvectors may be less than eigenvalue multiplicity.

> [!warning] Sign Errors in Complex Solutions
> When extracting real/imaginary parts, be careful with signs.
