## Power Series Solutions

### Motivation

Not all ODEs have solutions in elementary functions. Use power series when coefficients are variable.

### Standard Form

$$y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0$$

If $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ are analytic at $x_0$, solution exists as power series.

---

## Ordinary Points

### Definition

Point $x_0$ is **ordinary** if $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ are analytic at $x_0$.

Otherwise: **singular point**.

### Solution Method

Assume solution:
$$y = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n$$

Compute derivatives, substitute into ODE, find recurrence relation for coefficients.

### Example

Solve $y'' - xy = 0$ (Airy's equation) near $x=0$:

Assume: $y = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_nx^n$

Then: $y' = \sum_{n=1}^\infty na_nx^{n-1}$, $y'' = \sum_{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)a_nx^{n-2}$

Substitute: $\sum_{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)a_nx^{n-2} - x\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_nx^n = 0$

Shift index in first sum: $\sum_{n=0}^\infty (n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2}x^n - \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_{n-1}x^n = 0$

Match coefficients:
- $n=0$: $2a_2 = 0$, so $a_2 = 0$
- $n \geq 1$: $(n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2} = a_{n-1}$

Recurrence: $a_{n+2} = \frac{a_{n-1}}{(n+2)(n+1)}$

Two independent solutions from $a_0$ and $a_1$.

---

## Regular Singular Points

### Definition

Point $x_0$ is **regular singular** if:
$$\lim_{x \to x_0}(x-x_0)p(x) \text{ and } \lim_{x \to x_0}(x-x_0)^2q(x)$$

both exist (finite).

### Frobenius Method

Solution has form:
$$y = x^r\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_nx^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_nx^{n+r}$$

where $r$ may not be integer.

### Indicial Equation

Substitute Frobenius series, lowest power gives quadratic in $r$:
$$r(r-1) + p_0r + q_0 = 0$$

where $p_0 = \lim_{x \to 0}xp(x)$ and $q_0 = \lim_{x \to 0}x^2q(x)$.

---

## Three Cases for Roots

Let $r_1, r_2$ be roots of indicial equation with $r_1 \geq r_2$.

|Case|Roots|Solutions|
|---|---|---|
|Distinct, not differing by integer|$r_1 - r_2 \notin \mathbb{Z}$|$y_1 = x^{r_1}\sum a_nx^n$, $y_2 = x^{r_2}\sum b_nx^n$|
|Repeated root|$r_1 = r_2 = r$|$y_1 = x^r\sum a_nx^n$, $y_2 = y_1\ln x + x^r\sum b_nx^n$|
|Differ by integer|$r_1 - r_2 = N \in \mathbb{Z}^+$|$y_1 = x^{r_1}\sum a_nx^n$, $y_2 = Cy_1\ln x + x^{r_2}\sum b_nx^n$|

## Bessel's Equation

### Standard Form

$$x^2y'' + xy' + (x^2 - \nu^2)y = 0$$

Regular singular point at $x=0$.

### Bessel Functions

First kind: $J_\nu(x) = \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^m}{m!\Gamma(m+\nu+1)}\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^{2m+\nu}$

Second kind: $Y_\nu(x)$ (linearly independent from $J_\nu$)

### Properties

- $J_{-
u}(x) = (-1)^
u J_
u(x)$ for integer $
u$
- Zeros of $J_\nu$ are important in applications
- Orthogonality relations for different orders

```desmos-graph

y = besselj(1, x)
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Bessel functions oscillate with decreasing amplitude, like damped sinusoids.

## Legendre's Equation

### Standard Form

$$(1-x^2)y'' - 2xy' + n(n+1)y = 0$$

Regular singular points at $x = \pm 1$.

### Legendre Polynomials

For integer $n$, polynomial solution:
$$P_n(x) = \frac{1}{2^n n!}\frac{d^n}{dx^n}(x^2-1)^n$$

First few:
- $P_0(x) = 1$
- $P_1(x) = x$
- $P_2(x) = \frac{1}{2}(3x^2-1)$
- $P_3(x) = \frac{1}{2}(5x^3-3x)$

### Orthogonality

$$\int_{-1}^1 P_m(x)P_n(x)\,dx = \frac{2}{2n+1}\delta_{mn}$$

---

## Hermite's Equation

### Standard Form

$$y'' - 2xy' + 2ny = 0$$

Entire plane is ordinary point.

### Hermite Polynomials

$$H_n(x) = (-1)^ne^{x^2}\frac{d^n}{dx^n}e^{-x^2}$$

First few:
- $H_0(x) = 1$
- $H_1(x) = 2x$
- $H_2(x) = 4x^2 - 2$
- $H_3(x) = 8x^3 - 12x$

### Applications

Quantum harmonic oscillator wavefunctions involve $e^{-x^2/2}H_n(x)$.

## Convergence of Series Solutions

### Radius of Convergence

Series solution converges at least within distance to nearest singular point.

### Theorem

If $x_0$ is ordinary point, series $\sum a_n(x-x_0)^n$ converges for $|x-x_0| < R$ where $R$ is distance to nearest singularity of $p(x)$ or $q(x)$.

### Example

For $(x^2-1)y'' + xy' + y = 0$ expanded about $x=0$:

Singularities at $x = \pm 1$, so radius of convergence is $R = 1$.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Forgetting to Check Point Type
> Always determine if point is ordinary, regular singular, or irregular singular before choosing method.

> [!warning] Incorrect Index Shifting
> When substituting series, carefully align powers of $x$ before matching coefficients.

> [!warning] Missing Logarithmic Terms
> For repeated roots or roots differing by integer, second solution may involve $\ln x$.
