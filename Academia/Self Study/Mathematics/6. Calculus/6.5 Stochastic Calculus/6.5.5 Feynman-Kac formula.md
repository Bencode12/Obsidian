## Motivation

Deep connection between SDEs and parabolic PDEs. Allows solving PDEs using probability theory.

### Key Insight

Solution to certain PDEs can be represented as expectation of functional of stochastic process.

---

## Feynman-Kac Formula

### Setup

Consider PDE:
$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \mu(x)\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2(x)\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} - ru = 0$$

with terminal condition $u(T, x) = \Phi(x)$.

### Theorem

Solution is:
$$u(t, x) = \mathbb{E}\left[e^{-r(T-t)}\Phi(X_T) \mid X_t = x\right]$$

where $X_s$ satisfies SDE:
$$dX_s = \mu(X_s)\,ds + \sigma(X_s)\,dW_s$$

### Interpretation

PDE solution = discounted expected payoff under stochastic dynamics.

---

## Derivation via Itô's Formula

### Proof Sketch

Apply Itô's formula to $e^{-rs}u(s, X_s)$:

$$d(e^{-rs}u(s, X_s)) = e^{-rs}\left[-ru + \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \mu\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\right]ds + e^{-rs}\sigma\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}dW_s$$

If $u$ satisfies PDE, drift term vanishes:
$$d(e^{-rs}u(s, X_s)) = e^{-rs}\sigma\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}dW_s$$

Take expectation (martingale has zero mean increment):
$$\mathbb{E}[e^{-rT}u(T, X_T)] = e^{-rt}u(t, X_t)$$

Therefore:
$$u(t, x) = \mathbb{E}[e^{-r(T-t)}\Phi(X_T) \mid X_t = x]$$

## Special Cases

### Heat Equation

PDE: $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} = 0$ with $u(T,x) = \Phi(x)$

Here $\mu = 0$, $\sigma = 1$, $r = 0$.

SDE: $dX_s = dW_s$, so $X_s = W_s$.

Solution:
$$u(t, x) = \mathbb{E}[\Phi(x + W_{T-t})] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \Phi(y)\frac{e^{-(y-x)^2/2(T-t)}}{\sqrt{2\pi(T-t)}}\,dy$$

Convolution with Gaussian kernel!

### Black-Scholes PDE

PDE: $\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} - rV = 0$

SDE: $dS_t = rS_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t$ (geometric Brownian motion)

Solution for European call option:
$$V(t, S) = e^{-r(T-t)}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[(S_T - K)^+ \mid S_t = S]$$

Leads to Black-Scholes formula!

## Generalized Feynman-Kac

### With Source Term

PDE:
$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \mathcal{L}u - ru + f(t,x) = 0$$

where $\mathcal{L} = \mu\frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}$.

Solution:
$$u(t,x) = \mathbb{E}\left[e^{-r(T-t)}\Phi(X_T) + \int_t^T e^{-r(s-t)}f(s, X_s)\,ds \bigg| X_t = x\right]$$

Includes running cost/payoff $f$.

### Multidimensional Case

For $\mathbf{X}_t \in \mathbb{R}^n$:
$$d\mathbf{X}_t = \boldsymbol{\mu}(\mathbf{X}_t)\,dt + \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{X}_t)\,d\mathbf{W}_t$$

PDE involves gradient and Hessian:
$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \boldsymbol{\mu} \cdot \nabla u + \frac{1}{2}\text{Tr}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}\boldsymbol{\sigma}^T D^2 u) - ru = 0$$

## Applications in Finance

### Option Pricing

European option with payoff $\Phi(S_T)$:
$$V(t, S) = e^{-r(T-t)}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\Phi(S_T) \mid S_t = S]$$

**Call option**: $\Phi(S) = (S - K)^+$

**Put option**: $\Phi(S) = (K - S)^+$

### Bond Pricing

Zero-coupon bond price:
$$P(t, T) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[e^{-\int_t^T r_s\,ds} \mid \mathcal{F}_t\right]$$

Short rate $r_t$ follows SDE.

### Exotic Options

- **Asian options**: Payoff depends on average price
- **Barrier options**: Payoff depends on whether barrier hit
- **Lookback options**: Payoff depends on maximum/minimum

All priced using Feynman-Kac representation.

## Numerical Methods

### Monte Carlo Simulation

Estimate expectation by simulating many paths:

$$u(t, x) \approx \frac{e^{-r(T-t)}}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \Phi(X_T^{(i)})$$

where $X_T^{(i)}$ are independent simulations.

**Advantages**:
- Works in high dimensions
- Easy to implement
- Handles complex payoffs

**Disadvantages**:
- Slow convergence ($O(1/\sqrt{N})$)
- Statistical error

### Finite Difference Methods

Discretize PDE directly on grid.

**Advantages**:
- Faster convergence for low dimensions
- Deterministic error

**Disadvantages**:
- Curse of dimensionality
- Complex boundary conditions

## Connection to Kolmogorov Equations

### Forward Equation (Fokker-Planck)

For transition density $p(t, x; T, y)$:
$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial T} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial y}[\mu(y)p] + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial y^2}[\sigma^2(y)p]$$

Describes evolution of probability density.

### Backward Equation

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + \mu(x)\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2(x)\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial x^2} = 0$$

Feynman-Kac solves backward equation with terminal condition.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Forgetting Discount Factor
> Must include $e^{-r(T-t)}$ when $r \neq 0$. Not just $\mathbb{E}[\Phi(X_T)]$.

> [!warning] Using Wrong Measure
> For pricing, use risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, not physical measure $\mathbb{P}$.

> [!warning] Confusing Forward and Backward Equations
> Forward: evolves density forward in time. Backward: solves PDE backward from terminal condition.
