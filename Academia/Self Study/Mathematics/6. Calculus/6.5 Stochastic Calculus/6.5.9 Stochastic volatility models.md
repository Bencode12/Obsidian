## Motivation

Standard models assume constant or deterministic parameters. Real markets exhibit:
- Volatility clustering
- Mean reversion in volatility
- Leverage effects

Need **stochastic volatility** models!

---

## Heston Model

### Dynamics

Stock price and variance follow coupled SDEs:

$$\begin{aligned} dS_t &= \mu S_t\,dt + \sqrt{v_t}S_t\,dW_t^1 \\ dv_t &= \kappa(\theta - v_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^2 \end{aligned}$$

where:
- $v_t$: Instantaneous variance (not volatility!)
- $\kappa$: Mean reversion speed
- $\theta$: Long-term variance
- $\xi$: Volatility of volatility (vol-of-vol)
- $\text{Corr}(dW_t^1, dW_t^2) = \rho$

### Features

- Variance follows CIR process (always non-negative if Feller condition holds)
- Correlation $\rho$ captures leverage effect ($\rho < 0$)
- Closed-form characteristic function exists

### Feller Condition

For $v_t > 0$ almost surely:
$$2\kappa\theta \geq \xi^2$$

If violated, variance can hit zero (boundary behavior complex).

## SABR Model

### Dynamics

Stochastic Alpha, Beta, Rho model:

$$\begin{aligned} dF_t &= \sigma_t F_t^\beta\,dW_t^1 \\ d\sigma_t &= \alpha\sigma_t\,dW_t^2 \end{aligned}$$

where:
- $F_t$: Forward price
- $\sigma_t$: Volatility
- $\beta$: Elasticity parameter ($0 \leq \beta \leq 1$)
- $\alpha$: Vol-of-vol
- $\text{Corr}(dW_t^1, dW_t^2) = \rho$

### Special Cases

- **$\beta = 1$**: Lognormal SABR (standard)
- **$\beta = 0$**: Normal SABR
- **$\beta = 1/2$**: Square-root SABR

### Advantages

- Captures volatility smile/skew
- Analytical approximation for option prices
- Widely used in interest rate derivatives

## Local Volatility Models

### Dupire's Formula

Given market call prices $C(K, T)$ for all strikes $K$ and maturities $T$:

Local volatility function:
$$\sigma_{loc}^2(K, T) = \frac{\frac{\partial C}{\partial T} + rK\frac{\partial C}{\partial K}}{\frac{1}{2}K^2\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2}}$$

### Dynamics

$$dS_t = rS_t\,dt + \sigma_{loc}(S_t, t)S_t\,dW_t$$

Volatility depends on current stock level and time.

### Calibration

Fit $\sigma_{loc}(S,t)$ to match all observed option prices exactly.

**Limitation**: Dynamics may be unrealistic (volatility surface moves incorrectly).

## Rough Volatility

### Empirical Observation

Realized volatility exhibits roughness with Hurst exponent $H \approx 0.1$.

Much rougher than Brownian motion ($H = 0.5$)!

### Rough Fractional Stochastic Volatility (RFSV)

$$\log v_t = \log v_0 + \nu B_t^H$$

where $B^H$ is fractional Brownian motion with $H \approx 0.1$.

### Characteristics

- Very irregular volatility paths
- Better fit to implied volatility surfaces
- Explains steep short-end of volatility smile
- Non-semimartingale framework needed

## Multiscale Stochastic Volatility

### Motivation

Volatility has components at different time scales:
- Fast scale: Daily fluctuations
- Slow scale: Regime changes, economic cycles

### Two-Factor Model

$$\begin{aligned} dS_t &= \mu S_t\,dt + f(Y_t, Z_t)S_t\,dW_t^0 \\ dY_t &= \frac{1}{\epsilon}(m - Y_t)\,dt + \frac{\beta}{\sqrt{\epsilon}}\,dB_t^1 \\ dZ_t &= \delta(\theta - Z_t)\,dt + \nu\,dB_t^2 \end{aligned}$$

where:
- $Y_t$: Fast mean-reverting factor ($\epsilon$ small)
- $Z_t$: Slow factor ($\delta$ small)
- $f(Y,Z)$: Combined volatility function

### Asymptotic Analysis

Use singular perturbation theory to derive approximate option prices.

## Jump-Diffusion with Stochastic Volatility

### Bates Model

Combine Heston stochastic volatility with jumps:

$$\begin{aligned} dS_t &= \mu S_t\,dt + \sqrt{v_t}S_t\,dW_t^1 + S_{t-}(J-1)\,dN_t \\ dv_t &= \kappa(\theta - v_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^2 \end{aligned}$$

Captures both continuous volatility changes and sudden jumps.

### SVCJ Model

Stochastic Volatility with Correlated Jumps:
- Jumps in both price and volatility
- Correlation between jump sizes
- More realistic crash modeling

## Calibration Methods

### Objective

Find model parameters that best fit market data (option prices).

### Approaches

#### 1. Least Squares

Minimize sum of squared pricing errors:
$$\min_{\Theta} \sum_{i} [C_{model}(K_i, T_i; \Theta) - C_{market}(K_i, T_i)]^2$$

#### 2. Maximum Likelihood

If historical data available:
$$\max_{\Theta} \log L(\Theta; \text{data})$$

#### 3. Bayesian Methods

Posterior distribution:
$$p(\Theta | \text{data}) \propto L(\text{data} | \Theta)p(\Theta)$$

Use MCMC for sampling.

### Challenges

- **Ill-posed problem**: Many parameter sets fit equally well
- **Overfitting**: Complex models fit noise
- **Computational cost**: Expensive for Monte Carlo methods
- **Parameter stability**: Parameters drift over time

## Comparison of Models

|Model|Pros|Cons|
|---|---|---|
|**Black-Scholes**|Simple, closed-form|Constant vol, no smile|
|**Local Vol**|Exact calibration|Unrealistic dynamics|
|**Heston**|Closed-form char. func.|May not fit all smiles|
|**SABR**|Good for rates|Approximate formulas|
|**Rough Vol**|Empirically accurate|Complex, non-semimartingale|
|**Jump-Diffusion**|Captures crashes|More parameters|

## Applications

### Equity Options

- Heston model widely used for equity derivatives
- Captures volatility skew (OTM puts expensive)
- Important for risk management

### Interest Rate Derivatives

- SABR model industry standard for swaptions
- LIBOR market models with stochastic volatility
- Negative rates require careful treatment

### Foreign Exchange

- Double Heston (two variance factors)
- Both currencies have stochastic volatility
- Complex correlation structure

### Commodity Markets

- Mean-reverting spot prices
- Seasonal volatility patterns
- Storage costs and convenience yields

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Volatility and Variance
> Heston models variance $v_t$, not volatility $\sqrt{v_t}$. Be careful with notation!

> [!warning] Ignoring Feller Condition
> If $2\kappa\theta < \xi^2$, variance can hit zero. Numerical schemes must handle this.

> [!warning] Overfitting Market Data
> Complex models with many parameters may fit today's prices but predict poorly tomorrow.
