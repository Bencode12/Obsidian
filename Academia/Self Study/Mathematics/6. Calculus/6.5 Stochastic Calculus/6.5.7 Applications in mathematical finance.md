## Black-Scholes-Merton Model

### Market Setup

**Risk-free asset** (bond):
$$dB_t = rB_t\,dt, \quad B_0 = 1$$

Solution: $B_t = e^{rt}$

**Risky asset** (stock):
$$dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t^{\mathbb{P}}$$

under physical measure $\mathbb{P}$.

### Risk-Neutral Measure

Under risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$:
$$dS_t = rS_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$$

Drift equals risk-free rate (no arbitrage).

## European Option Pricing

### Call Option

Payoff at maturity $T$: $(S_T - K)^+$

**Black-Scholes formula**:
$$C(S,t) = S N(d_1) - Ke^{-r(T-t)}N(d_2)$$

where:
$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r + \sigma^2/2)(T-t)}{\sigma\sqrt{T-t}}$$
$$d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T-t}$$

and $N(\cdot)$ is standard normal CDF.

### Put Option

Payoff: $(K - S_T)^+$

**Put-call parity**:
$$C - P = S - Ke^{-r(T-t)}$$

Therefore:
$$P(S,t) = Ke^{-r(T-t)}N(-d_2) - SN(-d_1)$$

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Call option payoff is zero below strike, linear above.

## The Greeks

Sensitivity of option price to parameters:

|Greek|Definition|Interpretation|
|---|---|---|
|**Delta** $\Delta$|$\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$|Sensitivity to stock price|
|**Gamma** $\Gamma$|$\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}$|Rate of change of delta|
|**Vega** $\mathcal{V}$|$\frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma}$|Sensitivity to volatility|
|**Theta** $\Theta$|$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}$|Time decay|
|**Rho** $\rho$|$\frac{\partial V}{\partial r}$|Sensitivity to interest rate|

### Delta Hedging

Hold $\Delta$ shares of stock to hedge option position.

Portfolio: $\Pi = V - \Delta S$ is locally risk-free.

## Implied Volatility

### Definition

**Implied volatility** $\sigma_{imp}$: Value of $\sigma$ that makes Black-Scholes price match market price.

Solve:
$$C_{market} = C_{BS}(S, K, T, r, \sigma_{imp})$$

No closed-form solution. Use numerical methods (Newton's method).

### Volatility Smile

Plot $\sigma_{imp}$ vs strike $K$:

- ATM options: Lower implied vol
- OTM/ITM options: Higher implied vol

Contradicts Black-Scholes assumption of constant volatility!

### Interpretation

Market expects:
- Fat tails (extreme moves more likely)
- Volatility clustering
- Jumps or stochastic volatility

## Exotic Options

### Asian Options

Payoff depends on average price:
$$\text{Payoff} = \left(\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T S_t\,dt - K\right)^+$$

**Advantage**: Less susceptible to manipulation.

**Pricing**: No closed-form. Use Monte Carlo or PDE methods.

### Barrier Options

Payoff depends on whether barrier hit:

- **Up-and-out**: Knocked out if $S_t$ hits upper barrier
- **Down-and-in**: Activated if $S_t$ hits lower barrier

**Pricing**: Analytical formulas exist using reflection principle.

### Lookback Options

Payoff depends on maximum/minimum:
$$\text{Call payoff} = S_T - \min_{0 \leq t \leq T} S_t$$

Always ends in-the-money. More expensive than vanilla options.

## Interest Rate Models

### Vasicek Model

Short rate dynamics:
$$dr_t = a(b - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$$

Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. Mean-reverting to level $b$.

**Bond price**: Closed-form solution exists.

**Limitation**: Rates can go negative.

### Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) Model

$$dr_t = a(b - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\sqrt{r_t}\,dW_t$$

Square-root diffusion ensures $r_t \geq 0$.

Feller condition: $2ab \geq \sigma^2$ prevents hitting zero.

### Heath-Jarrow-Morton Framework

Model entire forward curve $f(t, T)$:
$$df(t, T) = \alpha(t, T)\,dt + \sigma(t, T)\,dW_t$$

No-arbitrage condition determines drift $\alpha$ from volatility $\sigma$.

## Credit Risk Models

### Structural Models (Merton)

Company value follows GBM:
$$dV_t = \mu V_t\,dt + \sigma V_t\,dW_t$$

Default occurs if $V_T < D$ (debt level).

Equity = call option on firm value with strike $D$.

### Reduced-Form Models

Default time $\tau$ is random with intensity $\lambda_t$:
$$\mathbb{P}(\tau > t) = \exp\left(-\int_0^t \lambda_s\,ds\right)$$

Intensity can be stochastic.

**Credit default swap (CDS)**: Insurance against default.

## Portfolio Optimization

### Mean-Variance Optimization

Maximize expected return for given variance:
$$\max_{\mathbf{w}} \mathbf{w}^T\boldsymbol{\mu} - \frac{\gamma}{2}\mathbf{w}^T\Sigma\mathbf{w}$$

where $\mathbf{w}$: portfolio weights, $\Sigma$: covariance matrix.

Solution: $\mathbf{w}^* = \frac{1}{\gamma}\Sigma^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}$

### Merton's Portfolio Problem

Maximize expected utility of terminal wealth:
$$\max_{\pi_t} \mathbb{E}[U(X_T)]$$

subject to:
$$dX_t = [rX_t + \pi_t(\mu - r)]\,dt + \pi_t\sigma\,dW_t$$

For CRRA utility $U(x) = \frac{x^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}$:

Optimal fraction in stock: $\pi^* = \frac{\mu - r}{\gamma\sigma^2}$

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Using Historical Volatility for Pricing
> Should use implied volatility from market prices, not historical realized vol.

> [!warning] Ignoring Transaction Costs
> Continuous delta hedging is theoretical ideal. Real hedging has costs.

> [!warning] Assuming Constant Parameters
> Volatility, interest rates, correlations all vary over time.
