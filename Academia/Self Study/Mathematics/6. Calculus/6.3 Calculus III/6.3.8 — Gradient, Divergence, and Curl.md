## Gradient

For a scalar function $f(x,y,z)$:

$$\nabla f = \left\langle \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \right\rangle$$

### Properties

- Points in the direction of **steepest increase**
- Magnitude = rate of steepest ascent
- Gradient is **perpendicular to level surfaces** $f(x,y,z) = c$

```graph
z = x^2 + y^2
```

### Example

For $f(x,y,z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2$:

$$\nabla f = \langle 2x, 2y, 2z \rangle$$

At point $(1,1,1)$: $\nabla f = \langle 2, 2, 2 \rangle$, pointing radially outward from origin.

## Divergence

For a vector field $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R \rangle$:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}$$

### Physical Interpretation

- Measures **outflow from a point** (flux per unit volume)
- Positive divergence → **source** (fluid flowing out)
- Negative divergence → **sink** (fluid flowing in)
- Zero divergence → **incompressible flow**

### Example

For $\mathbf{F} = \langle x^2, y^2, z^2 \rangle$:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 2x + 2y + 2z$$

At $(1,1,1)$: divergence = 6 (strong source).

## Curl

For a vector field $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R \rangle$:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ P & Q & R \end{vmatrix} = \left\langle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}, \frac{\partial P}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial R}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right\rangle$$

### Physical Interpretation

- Measures **rotation/circulation** at a point
- Direction of curl → axis of rotation (right-hand rule)
- Magnitude of curl → strength of rotation
- Zero curl → **irrotational** or **conservative** field

### Example

For $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x, 0 \rangle$ (rotation about $z$-axis):

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \langle 0, 0, 2 \rangle$$

Constant upward curl indicates uniform counterclockwise rotation.

## Key Identities

|Identity|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$\nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}$|Curl of gradient is zero|
|$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0$|Divergence of curl is zero|
|$\nabla \cdot (\nabla f) = \nabla^2 f$|Laplacian operator|

> [!tip] Conservative Fields
> 
> A vector field $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative (path-independent) if and only if $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ (in simply connected regions).

## Practice Problems

1. Find the gradient of $f(x,y,z) = xyz$ at $(1,2,3)$.
2. Calculate the divergence of $\mathbf{F} = \langle x^2y, yz^2, zx^2 \rangle$.
3. Compute the curl of $\mathbf{F} = \langle yz, xz, xy \rangle$.
4. Verify that $\nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}$ for $f(x,y,z) = x^2y + yz^2$.
5. Show that $\mathbf{F} = \langle 2xy, x^2 + 2yz, y^2 \rangle$ is conservative by computing its curl.
6. Find the directional derivative of $f(x,y,z) = e^{xyz}$ at $(1,1,1)$ in direction $\mathbf{v} = \langle 1, 2, 2 \rangle$.
7. If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla \phi$ for some scalar $\phi$, what is $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}$?
8. Calculate $\nabla^2 f$ (the Laplacian) for $f(x,y,z) = x^2 + y^2 - 2z^2$.
9. Determine if $\mathbf{F} = \langle y\cos(xy), x\cos(xy), 0 \rangle$ is conservative.
10. Prove that if $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 0$ everywhere, then $\mathbf{F}$ can be written as $\nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ for some vector field $\mathbf{A}$.
