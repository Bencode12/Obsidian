## Statement of Greens Theorem

Let $C$ be a **positively oriented**, piecewise-smooth, simple closed curve in the plane, and let $D$ be the region bounded by $C$. If $P$ and $Q$ have continuous partial derivatives on an open region containing $D$, then:

$$\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)\,dA$$

|Component|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$C$|Closed curve (boundary)|
|$D$|Region enclosed by $C$|
|Positive orientation|Counterclockwise|

> [!tip] What Greens Theorem Does
> Converts a **line integral** around a closed curve into a **double integral** over the region it encloses. This is often much easier to compute!

```desmos-graph
x^2 + y^2 = 1
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> The line integral around the circle equals the double integral of the "rotation" (curl) over the disk inside.

---

## Alternative Form (Flux Form)

$$\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot \vec{n}\,ds = \iint_D \nabla \cdot \vec{F}\,dA$$

- Left side: **flux** across boundary
- Right side: **divergence** over region

> [!info] Two Forms of Greens Theorem
> - **Circulation form**: $\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D (Q_x - P_y)\,dA$
> - **Flux form**: $\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot \vec{n}\,ds = \iint_D \nabla \cdot \vec{F}\,dA$

## Detailed Example

Evaluate $\oint_C y^2\,dx + x^2\,dy$ where $C$ is the boundary of the unit square $[0,1] \times [0,1]$.

**Using Greens Theorem:**

Step 1: Identify $P$ and $Q$
- $P = y^2$
- $Q = x^2$

Step 2: Compute partial derivatives
- $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = 2x$
- $\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 2y$

Step 3: Apply Greens Theorem

$$\oint_C y^2\,dx + x^2\,dy = \iint_D (2x - 2y)\,dA$$

Step 4: Evaluate the double integral

$$\begin{align*}
\iint_D (2x - 2y)\,dA &= \int_0^1 \int_0^1 (2x - 2y)\,dy\,dx \\
&= \int_0^1 \left[2xy - y^2\right]_{y=0}^{y=1}\,dx \\
&= \int_0^1 (2x - 1)\,dx \\
&= \left[x^2 - x\right]_0^1 \\
&= (1 - 1) - (0 - 0) \\
&= 0
\end{align*}$$

> [!example] Why is the answer zero?
> The vector field $\vec{F} = \langle y^2, x^2 \rangle$ has equal "clockwise" and "counterclockwise" rotation over the square, so they cancel out!

## Another Example: Area Calculation

Greens Theorem can compute areas! Using the formula:

$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}\oint_C x\,dy - y\,dx$$

**Example**: Find the area of an ellipse $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$.

Parameterize: $x = a\cos t$, $y = b\sin t$, $0 \leq t \leq 2\pi$

$$\begin{align*}
\text{Area} &= \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} (a\cos t)(b\cos t) - (b\sin t)(-a\sin t)\,dt \\
&= \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} ab(\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t)\,dt \\
&= \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} ab\,dt \\
&= \frac{1}{2} \cdot ab \cdot 2\pi \\
&= \pi ab
\end{align*}$$

This matches the known formula for ellipse area! ✓

## Conditions and Requirements

For Greens Theorem to apply:

1. **Simple closed curve**: No self-intersections
2. **Positive orientation**: Counterclockwise direction
3. **Continuous partial derivatives**: $P_x$, $P_y$, $Q_x$, $Q_y$ must all be continuous
4. **Piecewise smooth**: Curve can have corners but must be smooth between them

> [!warning] Common Mistakes
> - Forgetting to check orientation (clockwise gives negative result!)
> - Applying to non-simple curves (curves that cross themselves)
> - Using when partial derivatives don't exist (e.g., at singularities)

---

## Connection to Other Theorems

Greens Theorem is a special case of more general theorems:

|Theorem|Dimension|Relates|
|---|---|---|
|Greens Theorem|2D|Line integral ↔ Double integral|
|Stokes Theorem|3D|Line integral ↔ Surface integral|
|Divergence Theorem|3D|Surface integral ↔ Volume integral|

Greens Theorem is essentially **Stokes Theorem in the plane**!
