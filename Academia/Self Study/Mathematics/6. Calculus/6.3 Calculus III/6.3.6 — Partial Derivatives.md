## Definition

For a function $f(x,y)$, the **partial derivative with respect to $x$** is:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h, y) - f(x,y)}{h}$$

- Treat $y$ as **constant**
- Denoted as $f_x$, $\partial_x f$, or $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$

Similarly, **partial with respect to $y$**:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = \lim_{k \to 0} \frac{f(x, y+k) - f(x,y)}{k}$$

- Treat $x$ as constant

## Higher-Order Partial Derivatives

### Second-Order Partials

$$f_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}, \quad f_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}, \quad f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}, \quad f_{yx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x}$$

### Clairaut's Theorem

If $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are continuous, then:

$$f_{xy} = f_{yx}$$

> [!tip] Mixed Partials Commute
> 
> For most well-behaved functions, the order of differentiation doesn't matter. This simplifies calculations significantly.

## Gradient Vector

The **gradient** points in the direction of **maximum increase** of a function:

$$\nabla f(x,y) = \langle f_x, f_y \rangle = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\mathbf{j}$$

### Properties

- Magnitude $|\nabla f|$ gives the **rate of steepest ascent**
- Perpendicular to **level curves** $f(x,y) = c$
- Points toward increasing values of $f$

```graph
z = x^2 + y^2
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> 
> For $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$, the gradient $\nabla f = \langle 2x, 2y \rangle$ points radially outward from the origin, perpendicular to the circular level curves.

## Tangent Plane

For a surface $z = f(x,y)$ at $(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ where $z_0 = f(x_0, y_0)$:

$$z - z_0 = f_x(x_0, y_0)(x - x_0) + f_y(x_0, y_0)(y - y_0)$$

This is the **linear approximation** of the surface near the point.

### Example

Find the tangent plane to $z = x^2 + y^2$ at $(1, 2, 5)$:

1. Compute partials: $f_x = 2x$, $f_y = 2y$
2. Evaluate at $(1,2)$: $f_x(1,2) = 2$, $f_y(1,2) = 4$
3. Tangent plane: $z - 5 = 2(x - 1) + 4(y - 2)$
4. Simplify: $z = 2x + 4y - 5$

## Directional Derivative

Derivative of $f$ in the direction of unit vector $\mathbf{u} = \langle a, b \rangle$:

$$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(x,y) = f_x a + f_y b = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}$$

### Key Facts

- Measures **rate of change along a specific direction**
- Maximum when $\mathbf{u}$ points in direction of $\nabla f$
- Zero when $\mathbf{u}$ is perpendicular to $\nabla f$ (along level curve)

### Formula with Angle

If $\mathbf{u} = \langle \cos\theta, \sin\theta \rangle$:

$$D_{\mathbf{u}} f = |\nabla f| \cos\phi$$

where $\phi$ is the angle between $\nabla f$ and $\mathbf{u}$.

2. Verify Clairaut's theorem for $f(x,y) = x^2y^3 + \sin(xy)$.
3. Calculate the gradient of $f(x,y) = \ln(x^2 + y^2)$ at $(1,1)$.
4. Find the tangent plane to $z = xy$ at $(2, 3, 6)$.
5. Compute the directional derivative of $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ at $(1,2)$ in the direction $\mathbf{v} = \langle 3, 4 \rangle$.
6. In what direction does $f(x,y) = xe^y$ increase fastest at $(2,0)$?
7. Show that $\nabla f$ is perpendicular to the level curve $f(x,y) = c$.
8. Find all second-order partial derivatives of $f(x,y) = x^3y + y^2\cos x$.
9. If $f_x(1,2) = 3$ and $f_y(1,2) = -4$, find the directional derivative in direction $\mathbf{u} = \langle \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \rangle$.
10. Prove that the maximum value of $D_{\mathbf{u}} f$ is $|\nabla f|$.
