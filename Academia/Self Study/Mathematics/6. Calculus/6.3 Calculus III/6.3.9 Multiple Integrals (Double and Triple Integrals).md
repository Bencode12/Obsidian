## Double Integrals

For $f(x,y)$ over a region $R$ in the xy-plane:

$$\iint_R f(x,y) \, dA$$

- Represents **volume under the surface** $z = f(x,y)$ over $R$
- $dA = dx\,dy$ or $dy\,dx$ depending on order of integration

> [!tip] Geometric Interpretation
> A double integral sums up infinitely many infinitesimal columns of height $f(x,y)$ over region $R$. Think of it as adding up volumes of tiny rectangular prisms!

---

### Iterated Integrals

$$\iint_R f(x,y) \, dA = \int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y) \, dy \, dx$$

- Integrate **inner variable first**, then outer
- Change order if necessary to simplify

> [!info] Fubini's Theorem
> For continuous functions, you can integrate in ANY order:
> $$\iint_R f(x,y) \, dA = \int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y) \, dy \, dx = \int_c^d \int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f(x,y) \, dx \, dy$$
>
> **Strategy:** Choose the order that makes integration easier!
>
> **Example:** For $\iint_R xy \, dA$ where $R: 0 \leq x \leq 1, 0 \leq y \leq x$:
> $$\int_0^1 \int_0^x xy \, dy \, dx = \int_0^1 \left[\frac{xy^2}{2}\right]_0^x dx = \int_0^1 \frac{x^3}{2} dx = \left[\frac{x^4}{8}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{8}$$

## Triple Integrals

For $f(x,y,z)$ over a 3D region $V$:

$$\iiint_V f(x,y,z) \, dV$$

- $dV = dx\,dy\,dz$
- Represents **mass, charge, or volume under density**

**Example:**
$\iiint_V 1 \, dV$ gives volume of $V$

---

## Change of Variables (Polar, Cylindrical, Spherical)

> [!warning] Don't Forget the Jacobian!
> When changing coordinates, you MUST multiply by the Jacobian determinant:
> - **Polar:** $dA = r \, dr \, d\theta$ (don't forget the $r$!)
> - **Cylindrical:** $dV = r \, dr \, d\theta \, dz$
> - **Spherical:** $dV = \rho^2 \sin\phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta$
>
> **Common mistake:** Forgetting the extra factor leads to wrong answers!

- **Polar coordinates:** $x = r \cos\theta, y = r \sin\theta, dA = r\,dr\,d\theta$
- **Cylindrical:** $x = r\cos\theta, y = r\sin\theta, z = z, dV = r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz$
- **Spherical:** $x = \rho \sin\phi \cos\theta, y = \rho \sin\phi \sin\theta, z = \rho \cos\phi, dV = \rho^2 \sin\phi \, d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$

---

## Applications

1. **Volume of a solid:**
    $$\iiint_V 1 \, dV$$
2. **Mass with density:**
    $$\iiint_V \rho(x,y,z) \, dV$$
3. **Center of mass:**

$$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{M} \iiint x \rho \, dV, \quad \bar{y} = \frac{1}{M} \iiint y \rho \, dV, \quad \bar{z} = \frac{1}{M} \iiint z \rho \, dV$$

## Practice Problems

1. Evaluate $\iint_R (x + y) \, dA$ where $R = [0,1] \times [0,2]$.
2. Find the volume under $z = x^2 + y^2$ over the unit disk.
3. Set up a triple integral for the volume of a sphere of radius $R$.
4. Convert $\iint_R (x^2 + y^2) \, dA$ to polar coordinates where $R$ is the unit disk.
5. Find the center of mass of a solid hemisphere with constant density.
