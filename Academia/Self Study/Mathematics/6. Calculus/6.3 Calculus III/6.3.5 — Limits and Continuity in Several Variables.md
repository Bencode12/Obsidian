## Limit Definition

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)} f(x,y) = L$$

This means $f(x, y) \to L$ as $(x, y)$ approaches $(a, b)$ **from any direction**. If the value depends on the path taken, the limit does not exist.

> [!warning] Two-variable limits are harder than one-variable limits Checking one or two paths is not enough to prove a limit exists — it must hold for **all** paths.

---

## Path-Dependence Test

To show a limit **does not exist**, find two paths giving different values.

> [!example] $f(x, y) = \dfrac{xy}{x^2 + y^2}$
> 
> Along $y = x$: $f(x, x) = \dfrac{x^2}{2x^2} = \dfrac{1}{2}$
> 
> Along $y = -x$: $f(x, -x) = \dfrac{-x^2}{2x^2} = -\dfrac{1}{2}$
> 
> Different values → **limit does not exist**.

---

## Continuity

$f(x, y)$ is **continuous at $(a, b)$** if:

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)} f(x,y) = f(a,b)$$

Functions that are continuous everywhere they are defined: polynomials, exponentials, $\sin$, $\cos$ — in any number of variables.

## Techniques for Evaluating Limits

|Method|When to use|
|---|---|
|**Direct substitution**|Function is continuous at the point|
|**Factorisation and simplification**|Indeterminate form from cancellation|
|**Polar coordinates**|Limit as $(x, y) \to (0, 0)$|

### Polar Coordinate Method

Set $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$ and take $r \to 0$. If the result is independent of $\theta$, the limit exists.

> [!example] $f(x, y) = \dfrac{x^2 y}{x^2 + y^2}$
> 
> $$f(r, \theta) = \frac{r^3\cos^2\theta\sin\theta}{r^2} = r\cos^2\theta\sin\theta \to 0 \text{ as } r \to 0$$
> 
> Limit exists and equals $0$.
