## Statement of Green's Theorem

Let $C$ be a **positively oriented** (counterclockwise), piecewise-smooth, simple closed curve in the plane, and let $D$ be the region bounded by $C$. If $P$ and $Q$ have continuous partial derivatives on an open region containing $D$, then:

$$\oint_C P \, dx + Q \, dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) \, dA$$

### Alternative Form (Flux Form)

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, ds = \iint_D (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}) \, dA$$

where $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q \rangle$ and $\mathbf{n}$ is the outward unit normal.

---

## Interpretation

- Relates a **line integral around a closed curve** to a **double integral over the region** it encloses
- Circulation form: measures total rotation within region
- Flux form: measures net flow out of region

> [!tip] When to Use
> 
> Green's Theorem is useful when:
> - The line integral is difficult but the double integral is easy
> - The region has simple geometry (rectangle, circle, triangle)
> - You need to convert between circulation and area integrals

---

## Examples

### Example 1: Circulation

Evaluate $\oint_C (x^2 - y) \, dx + (x + y^2) \, dy$ where $C$ is the unit circle:

1. Identify: $P = x^2 - y$, $Q = x + y^2$
2. Compute: $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = 1$, $\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = -1$
3. Apply theorem: $\iint_D (1 - (-1)) \, dA = \iint_D 2 \, dA$
4. Result: $2 \times \text{Area}(D) = 2\pi$

### Example 2: Area Calculation

The area of region $D$ can be computed as:

$$\text{Area} = \oint_C x \, dy = -\oint_C y \, dx = \frac{1}{2}\oint_C x \, dy - y \, dx$$

For ellipse $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$:
- Parameterize: $x = a\cos t$, $y = b\sin t$, $0 \leq t \leq 2\pi$
- Area = $\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} (ab\cos^2 t + ab\sin^2 t) \, dt = \pi ab$

---

## Conditions for Validity

1. Curve $C$ must be **closed and simple** (no self-intersections)
2. Orientation must be **counterclockwise** (positive)
3. Functions $P$ and $Q$ must have **continuous partial derivatives** on region containing $D$
4. Region $D$ should be **simply connected** (no holes) or handle each boundary separately

> [!warning] Common Mistakes
> 
> - Forgetting to check orientation (clockwise gives negative result)
> - Applying to regions with holes without adjusting for inner boundaries
> - Using when partial derivatives are discontinuous (e.g., at origin for certain fields)

## Practice Problems

1. Use Green's Theorem to evaluate $\oint_C y^2 \, dx + x^2 \, dy$ where $C$ is the square with vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(1,1)$, $(0,1)$.
2. Calculate the area enclosed by the astroid $x^{2/3} + y^{2/3} = a^{2/3}$ using Green's Theorem.
3. Verify Green's Theorem for $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x \rangle$ around the unit circle.
4. Evaluate $\oint_C (e^x + y^2) \, dx + (e^y + x^2) \, dy$ where $C$ is the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(0,1)$.
5. Show that $\oint_C \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2} \, dx + \frac{x}{x^2+y^2} \, dy = 2\pi$ for any closed curve enclosing the origin.
6. Use Green's Theorem to find the work done by $\mathbf{F} = \langle xy, x^2 \rangle$ around the boundary of the region between $y = x^2$ and $y = x$.
7. Prove that if $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ everywhere in a simply connected region, then $\oint_C P \, dx + Q \, dy = 0$ for any closed curve.
8. Calculate the flux of $\mathbf{F} = \langle x^2, y^2 \rangle$ across the boundary of the unit square using the flux form of Green's Theorem.
9. Use Green's Theorem to show that the centroid $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ of region $D$ can be computed via line integrals.
10. Evaluate $\oint_C (x^3 - y^3) \, dx + (x^3 + y^3) \, dy$ where $C$ is the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 4$.
