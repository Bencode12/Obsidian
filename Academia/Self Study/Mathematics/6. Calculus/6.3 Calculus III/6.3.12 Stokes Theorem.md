## Statement of Stokes Theorem

Let $S$ be an oriented piecewise-smooth surface bounded by a simple, closed, piecewise-smooth boundary curve $C$ with positive orientation. If $\vec{F}$ is a vector field with continuous partial derivatives on an open region containing $S$, then:

$$\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot d\vec{S}$$

Or equivalently:

$$\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot \vec{n}\,dS$$

|Component|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$C$|Boundary curve of surface $S$|
|$S$|Oriented surface|
|$d\vec{S}$|Vector surface element ($\vec{n}\,dS$)|
|$\nabla \times \vec{F}$|Curl of the vector field|
|$\vec{n}$|Unit normal vector to surface|

> [!tip] What Stokes Theorem Does
> Relates a **line integral** around a closed curve to a **surface integral** of the curl over any surface bounded by that curve. This generalizes Greens Theorem to 3D!

---

## Orientation Convention (Right-Hand Rule)

The orientation of $C$ and $S$ must follow the **right-hand rule**:

1. Curl the fingers of your right hand along $C$ in the direction of integration
2. Your thumb points in the direction of the normal vector $\vec{n}$

```desmos-graph
x^2 + y^2 = 1
y = 0
x = [-1, 1]
```

> [!warning] Orientation Matters!
> Getting the orientation wrong gives the wrong sign. Always use the right-hand rule to check consistency between the curve direction and surface normal.

---

## Connection to Greens Theorem

Stokes Theorem is a **generalization** of Greens Theorem to three dimensions:

- **Greens Theorem**: Special case when $S$ lies in the $xy$-plane
- When $\vec{n} = \mathbf{k}$ (upward normal), Stokes reduces to Greens

**Greens Theorem** (2D):
$$\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)\,dA$$

**Stokes Theorem** (3D):
$$\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot \vec{n}\,dS$$

## Detailed Example 1: Hemisphere

Verify Stokes Theorem for $\vec{F} = \langle -y, x, z \rangle$ where $S$ is the upper hemisphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1$, $z \geq 0$.

### Method 1: Line Integral (LHS)

The boundary $C$ is the unit circle in the $xy$-plane: $x^2 + y^2 = 1$, $z = 0$.

Parameterize: $\vec{r}(t) = \langle \cos t, \sin t, 0 \rangle$, $0 \leq t \leq 2\pi$

$$\begin{align*}
\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} &= \int_0^{2\pi} \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \cdot \vec{r}'(t)\,dt \\
&= \int_0^{2\pi} \langle -\sin t, \cos t, 0 \rangle \cdot \langle -\sin t, \cos t, 0 \rangle\,dt \\
&= \int_0^{2\pi} (\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t)\,dt \\
&= \int_0^{2\pi} 1\,dt \\
&= 2\pi
\end{align*}$$

### Method 2: Surface Integral (RHS)

Compute the curl:
$$\nabla \times \vec{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ -y & x & z \end{vmatrix} = \langle 0, 0, 2 \rangle$$

For the upper hemisphere with upward orientation, we can project onto the $xy$-plane:

$$\iint_S (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot \vec{n}\,dS = \iint_D \langle 0, 0, 2 \rangle \cdot \mathbf{k}\,dA = \iint_D 2\,dA$$

where $D$ is the unit disk. Therefore:

$$\iint_D 2\,dA = 2 \cdot \text{Area}(D) = 2 \cdot \pi(1)^2 = 2\pi$$

Both methods give $2\pi$ ✓

## Detailed Example 2: Planar Surface

Evaluate $\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r}$ where $\vec{F} = \langle z, x, y \rangle$ and $C$ is the boundary of the triangle with vertices $(1,0,0)$, $(0,1,0)$, $(0,0,1)$.

**Using Stokes Theorem:**

Step 1: Compute the curl

$$\nabla \times \vec{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ z & x & y \end{vmatrix} = \langle 1, 1, 1 \rangle$$

Step 2: Find the surface

The triangle lies in the plane $x + y + z = 1$, or $z = 1 - x - y$.

Normal vector: $\vec{n} = \frac{\langle 1, 1, 1 \rangle}{\sqrt{3}}$ (upward orientation)

Step 3: Set up the surface integral

Project onto the $xy$-plane. The region $D$ is the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(0,1)$.

$$\begin{align*}
\iint_S (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot \vec{n}\,dS &= \iint_D \langle 1, 1, 1 \rangle \cdot \langle 1, 1, 1 \rangle\,dA \\
&= \iint_D 3\,dA \\
&= 3 \cdot \text{Area}(D) \\
&= 3 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \\
&= \frac{3}{2}
\end{align*}$$

Therefore: $\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \frac{3}{2}$

## Key Properties

### Independence of Surface

If two surfaces $S_1$ and $S_2$ have the same boundary curve $C$, then:

$$\iint_{S_1} (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot d\vec{S} = \iint_{S_2} (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot d\vec{S}$$

This means you can choose whichever surface is easiest to integrate over!

### Conservative Fields

If $\vec{F}$ is conservative ($\vec{F} = \nabla f$), then $\nabla \times \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, so:

$$\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = 0$$

for any closed curve $C$.

---

## Conditions and Requirements

For Stokes Theorem to apply:

1. **Orientable surface**: Must have a consistent normal direction
2. **Piecewise smooth**: Surface and boundary can have corners but must be smooth between them
3. **Simple boundary**: Curve $C$ should not self-intersect
4. **Continuous partial derivatives**: $\vec{F}$ must be $C^1$ on an open region containing $S$

> [!warning] Common Mistakes
> - Forgetting to check orientation consistency
> - Using when the surface is not orientable (e.g., Mobius strip)
> - Applying to surfaces with multiple boundaries without adjustment
> - Computing curl incorrectly (use determinant formula carefully!)

---

## Applications

Stokes Theorem is used in:

- **Electromagnetism**: Relating electric/magnetic fields (Maxwell's equations)
- **Fluid dynamics**: Circulation and vorticity
- **Physics**: Work done by force fields
- **Engineering**: Simplifying complex line integrals
