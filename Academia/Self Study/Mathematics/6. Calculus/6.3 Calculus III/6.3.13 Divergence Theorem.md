## Divergence Theorem

Let $V$ be a solid region in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with **closed surface** $S$ oriented **outward**, and let $\vec{F} = \langle P, Q, R \rangle$ be a continuously differentiable vector field on an open set containing $V$. Then:

$$\iint_S \vec{F} \cdot \vec{n}\,dS = \iiint_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{F})\,dV$$

|Component|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$S$|Closed surface (boundary of $V$)|
|$V$|Solid region enclosed by $S$|
|$\vec{n}$|Outward unit normal|

> [!tip] What the Divergence Theorem Does
> Converts a **surface integral** (flux) over a closed surface into a **triple integral** of divergence over the volume inside. Much easier in many cases!

---

## Physical Interpretation

- **Left side**: Total flux out of the surface
- **Right side**: Sum of all sources/sinks inside the volume

If $\nabla \cdot \vec{F} > 0$: net source (more flowing out than in)
If $\nabla \cdot \vec{F} < 0$: net sink (more flowing in than out)

> [!info] Conservation Laws
> The Divergence Theorem is fundamental to conservation laws in physics:
> - Fluid dynamics (mass conservation)
> - Electromagnetism (Gauss's Law)
> - Heat transfer (energy conservation)

---

## Example

Find the outward flux of $\vec{F} = \langle x, y, z \rangle$ through the unit sphere.

**Using Divergence Theorem:**
- $\nabla \cdot \vec{F} = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3$
- Volume of unit sphere: $\frac{4}{3}\pi$

$$\iiint_V 3\,dV = 3 \cdot \frac{4}{3}\pi = 4\pi$$

Much easier than computing the surface integral directly!

## Practice Problems

1. Use the Divergence Theorem to find the flux of $\vec{F} = \langle x^2, y^2, z^2 \rangle$ through the surface of the cube $[0,1]^3$.
2. Verify the Divergence Theorem for $\vec{F} = \langle x, y, z \rangle$ over the unit ball.
3. Find the flux of $\vec{F} = \langle xy, yz, zx \rangle$ through the surface of the cylinder $x^2 + y^2 \leq 1$, $0 \leq z \leq 2$.
4. Explain why the Divergence Theorem doesn't apply if the surface is not closed.
