## Definition

A submanifold $L \subset (M^{2n}, \omega)$ is **Lagrangian** if:

1. $\dim L = n$ (half the dimension of $M$)
2. $\omega|_L = 0$ (the symplectic form vanishes on $L$)

Equivalently, $L$ is Lagrangian if $T_p L$ is a Lagrangian subspace of $T_p M$ for all $p \in L$.

> [!example] Why Lagrangian Submanifolds Matter
> Lagrangian submanifolds are fundamental to:  
> - Classical mechanics (configuration space, phase space trajectories)  
> - Quantization (Bohr-Sommerfeld conditions)  
> - Mirror symmetry (special Lagrangians)  
> - Floer homology (intersection theory)  
> - Symplectic topology

## Lagrangian Subspaces

### Linear Algebra

In a symplectic vector space $(V, \omega)$, a subspace $L \subset V$ is **Lagrangian** if:
- $\dim L = \frac{1}{2}\dim V$
- $\omega|_L = 0$

Equivalently: $L = L^\omega$ where $L^\omega = \{v \in V : \omega(v,w) = 0 \text{ for all } w \in L\}$.

### Examples in $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$

With standard symplectic form $\omega = \sum dq^i \wedge dp_i$:

**Coordinate planes**:
- $Q = \{(q,0)\}$ (position space)
- $P = \{(0,p)\}$ (momentum space)

Both are Lagrangian!


> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> In phase space, the position axis and momentum axis are both Lagrangian. They're "maximally isotropic."

## Examples of Lagrangian Submanifolds

### Example 1: Zero Section

For any manifold $Q$, the zero section of $T^*Q$:

$$L_0 = \{(q,0) : q \in Q\} \subset T^*Q$$

is Lagrangian.

More generally, the graph of any closed 1-form $df$ is Lagrangian.

### Example 2: Fibers

For any $q \in Q$, the fiber:

$$T_q^*Q = \{(q,p) : p \in T_q^*Q\} \subset T^*Q$$

is Lagrangian.

### Example 3: Graphs of Symplectomorphisms

If $\phi: (M, \omega) \to (N, \eta)$ is a symplectomorphism, then its graph:

$$\Gamma_\phi = \{(x, \phi(x)) : x \in M\} \subset (M \times N, (-\omega) \oplus \eta)$$

is Lagrangian.

### Example 4: Liouville Tori

For a completely integrable Hamiltonian system, the invariant tori from the Arnold-Liouville theorem are Lagrangian.

### Example 5: Special Lagrangians

In Calabi-Yau manifolds, **special Lagrangian** submanifolds minimize volume in their homology class. These are crucial for mirror symmetry.

## Properties

### Dimension Constraint

If $L \subset M^{2n}$ is Lagrangian, then $\dim L = n$.

**Proof sketch**: Since $\omega|_L = 0$, we have $TL \subset (TL)^\omega$. By nondegeneracy, $\dim(TL)^\omega = 2n - \dim TL$. Therefore $\dim TL \leq n$. Maximality gives $\dim TL = n$.

### Normal Bundle

For a Lagrangian submanifold $L \subset M$:

$$NL \cong T^*L$$

The normal bundle is isomorphic to the cotangent bundle!

This is very special—general submanifolds don't have this property.

### Neighborhood Theorem (Weinstein)

A neighborhood of $L$ in $M$ is symplectomorphic to a neighborhood of the zero section in $T^*L$.

This means Lagrangian submanifolds have **standard neighborhoods**!

## Generating Functions

Lagrangian submanifolds can be described by generating functions.

### Type 1: $S(q,Q)$

The Lagrangian in $T^*\mathbb{R}^n \times T^*\mathbb{R}^n$ is:

$$L = \{(q,p,Q,P) : p = \frac{\partial S}{\partial q}, P = -\frac{\partial S}{\partial Q}\}$$

### Type 2: $S(q,P)$

$$L = \{(q,p,Q,P) : p = \frac{\partial S}{\partial q}, Q = \frac{\partial S}{\partial P}\}$$

These generate canonical transformations!

## Maslov Index

The **Maslov index** is an integer invariant associated to loops of Lagrangian subspaces.

### Definition

For a loop $\gamma: S^1 \to \Lambda(n)$ in the Lagrangian Grassmannian:

$$\mu(\gamma) \in \mathbb{Z}$$

measures how many times the loop crosses a certain cycle.

### Applications

- Quantization conditions (Bohr-Sommerfeld-Maslov)
- Floer homology grading
- Caustics in optics

## Lagrangian Intersection Theory

### Basic Question

Given two Lagrangian submanifolds $L_0, L_1 \subset M$, how many intersection points do they have?

### Arnold Conjecture

For a compact symplectic manifold $M$ and Hamiltonian isotopy $\phi$:

$$|\text{Fix}(\phi)| \geq \sum_{i=0}^{2n} b_i(M)$$

where $b_i$ are Betti numbers.

This was proven using **Floer homology**!

### Transversality

If $L_0$ and $L_1$ intersect transversely, then at each intersection point $p$:

$$T_p M = T_p L_0 \oplus T_p L_1$$

## Exact Lagrangians

A Lagrangian $L \subset (M, \omega)$ is **exact** if there exists a 1-form $\lambda$ such that:
- $d\lambda = \omega$ (Liouville form)
- $\lambda|_L = df$ for some function $f$

### Examples

- Zero section in $T^*Q$ is exact
- Graphs of exact 1-forms $df$ are exact

### Non-Existence Results

**Gromov's theorem**: There are no closed exact Lagrangians in $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ with standard symplectic structure.

## Applications

### Classical Mechanics

- Configuration space $Q$ embeds as zero section in $T^*Q$ (Lagrangian)
- Solutions to Hamilton's equations trace out Lagrangian submanifolds
- Hamilton-Jacobi equation: finding Lagrangian submanifolds

### Geometric Optics

Light rays form Lagrangian submanifolds in phase space. Caustics occur when projections fail to be embeddings.

### Mirror Symmetry

Special Lagrangian submanifolds in Calabi-Yau manifolds correspond to coherent sheaves on the mirror.

### Floer Homology

Counts intersections of Lagrangian submanifolds to define powerful invariants.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Lagrangian with Isotropic
> Isotropic: $\omega|_L = 0$ (any dimension ≤ n)  
> Lagrangian: $\omega|_L = 0$ AND $\dim L = n$ (maximal isotropic)  
> Every Lagrangian is isotropic, but not vice versa!

> [!warning] Assuming All Submanifolds Can Be Lagrangian
> Lagrangian submanifolds must have exactly half the dimension. You can't have a Lagrangian surface in a 6-manifold!

> [!warning] Forgetting Closedness Condition
> The graph of a 1-form $\alpha$ is Lagrangian iff $d\alpha = 0$ (closed), not just any 1-form.

> [!warning] Thinking Lagrangians Are Unique
> There are typically infinitely many Lagrangian submanifolds in a symplectic manifold, often forming infinite-dimensional families.
