## Statement

**Darboux's Theorem**: Every symplectic manifold $(M^{2n}, \omega)$ looks locally like standard symplectic space $(\mathbb{R}^{2n}, \omega_0)$.

More precisely: For any $p \in M$, there exist local coordinates $(q^1, ..., q^n, p_1, ..., p_n)$ centered at $p$ such that:

$$\omega = \sum_{i=1}^n dq^i \wedge dp_i$$

These are called **Darboux coordinates** or **canonical coordinates**.

> [!example] Why This Is Remarkable
> Unlike Riemannian geometry (where curvature is a local invariant), symplectic geometry has NO local invariants!  
> All symplectic manifolds of the same dimension look identical locally.  
> This makes symplectic topology fundamentally different from Riemannian geometry.

## Proof Strategy

The proof uses Moser's trick (homotopy method).

### Step 1: Linear Darboux Theorem

At a point $p$, choose a basis where $\omega_p$ has standard form. This is just linear algebra!

### Step 2: Extend to Neighborhood

Use a homotopy between $\omega$ and $\omega_0$:

$$\omega_t = (1-t)\omega_0 + t\omega$$

Find a time-dependent vector field $X_t$ such that its flow $\phi_t$ satisfies:

$$\phi_t^*\omega_t = \omega_0$$

### Step 3: Solve for Vector Field

Differentiate: $\frac{d}{dt}(\phi_t^*\omega_t) = 0$

This gives: $\phi_t^*(\mathcal{L}_{X_t}\omega_t + \dot{\omega}_t) = 0$

Using Cartan's formula and $d\omega_t = 0$:
$$d(\iota_{X_t}\omega_t) + (\omega - \omega_0) = 0$$

Since $\omega - \omega_0 = d\alpha$ near $p$ (Poincaré lemma), we need:
$$\iota_{X_t}\omega_t = -\alpha$$

Nondegeneracy of $\omega_t$ guarantees a solution!

## Consequences

### No Local Invariants

You cannot distinguish symplectic manifolds by looking at small neighborhoods.

**Contrast**: In Riemannian geometry, curvature distinguishes manifolds locally.

### Flexibility

Symplectic structures are much more flexible than Riemannian metrics.

### Global vs. Local

All interesting symplectic geometry is **global**!

---

## Relative Darboux Theorem

### For Lagrangian Submanifolds

If $L \subset (M, \omega)$ is Lagrangian, then near $L$ there exist coordinates such that:
- $L$ is given by $\{p = 0\}$
- $\omega = \sum dq^i \wedge dp_i$

This is Weinstein's Lagrangian neighborhood theorem!

### For Symplectic Submanifolds

If $N \subset (M, \omega)$ is symplectic, there exist coordinates where:
- $N$ is given by $\{q^{k+1} = ... = q^n = p_{k+1} = ... = p_n = 0\}$
- $\omega = \sum_{i=1}^n dq^i \wedge dp_i$

## Applications

### Normal Forms

Darboux theorem provides canonical local models for:
- Hamiltonian systems near equilibrium
- Lagrangian intersections
- Symplectic fixed points

### Quantization

In geometric quantization, Darboux coordinates allow us to define polarization locally.

### Floer Theory

Local models for pseudoholomorphic curves use Darboux charts.

---

## Limitations

### Only Local

Darboux theorem says nothing about global structure!

**Example**: $S^2$ and $T^2$ both have Darboux coordinates locally, but are globally very different.

### Doesn't Preserve Additional Structure

If $(M, \omega, J)$ is Kähler, Darboux coordinates won't generally preserve the complex structure $J$.

---

## Moser's Trick

The proof technique (Moser's trick) is widely applicable.

### General Setup

Given a family of forms $\omega_t$ with constant cohomology class, find isotopy $\phi_t$ such that:

$$\phi_t^*\omega_t = \omega_0$$

### Other Applications

- Volume forms (Moser's stability theorem)
- Contact structures
- Foliations

## Examples

### Example 1: Standard $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$

Already in Darboux form! Coordinates: $(q^1, ..., q^n, p_1, ..., p_n)$.

### Example 2: Sphere $S^2$

Area form: $\omega = \sin\theta\,d\theta \wedge d\phi$

Near any point (except poles), change coordinates:
- $q = \phi$
- $p = -\cos\theta$

Then: $\omega = dq \wedge dp$ ✓

### Example 3: Cotangent Bundle

$T^*Q$ already has canonical Darboux coordinates $(q^i, p_i)$ from the base coordinates.

## Symplectic vs. Contact

### Contact Darboux Theorem

For contact manifolds $(M^{2n+1}, \alpha)$, there also exist local coordinates where:

$$\alpha = dz - \sum_{i=1}^n y_i dx^i$$

Contact geometry also has no local invariants!

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Thinking Darboux Means Trivial
> Just because symplectic structures look the same locally doesn't mean symplectic geometry is easy! Global phenomena are rich and complex.

> [!warning] Confusing with Riemannian Normal Coordinates
> Riemannian normal coordinates make metric Euclidean AT A POINT. Darboux coordinates make symplectic form standard IN A NEIGHBORHOOD.

> [!warning] Assuming Coordinates Are Unique
> Darboux coordinates are highly non-unique! Any symplectomorphism gives new Darboux coordinates.

> [!warning] Forgetting Dimension
> Darboux theorem only works for even-dimensional manifolds. Odd-dimensional analog is contact geometry.
