## Overview

**Floer homology** is an infinite-dimensional analog of Morse theory, adapted to symplectic geometry. It provides powerful invariants for studying:
- Fixed points of Hamiltonian diffeomorphisms
- Intersections of Lagrangian submanifolds
- 3-manifold topology
- Knot theory

Andreas Floer introduced it in the late 1980s, revolutionizing symplectic topology.

> [!example] Why Floer Homology Matters
> Floer homology solved major conjectures:  
> - Arnold conjecture (fixed points)  
> - Arnold-Givental conjecture (Lagrangian intersections)  
> It connects symplectic geometry to gauge theory, low-dimensional topology, and string theory!

---

## Motivation from Morse Theory

### Finite-Dimensional Morse Theory

For a function $f: M \to \mathbb{R}$ on a compact manifold:
- Critical points of $f$ generate a chain complex
- Gradient flow lines define the boundary operator
- Homology equals singular homology: $H_*(M)$

### Infinite-Dimensional Challenge

In symplectic geometry, we want to study:
- Action functional on loop space (infinite-dimensional!)
- Not compact, not bounded below

Floer's insight: Adapt Morse theory to this setting using elliptic PDEs.

## Hamiltonian Floer Homology

### Setup

Given:
- Symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$
- Hamiltonian $H: S^1 \times M \to \mathbb{R}$ (1-periodic)

### Action Functional

On the free loop space $\mathcal{L}M = C^\infty(S^1, M)$:

$$\mathcal{A}_H(\gamma) = -\int_{D^2} \bar{\gamma}^*\omega + \int_0^1 H(t, \gamma(t))\,dt$$

where $\bar{\gamma}: D^2 \to M$ extends $\gamma$.

**Critical points**: 1-periodic orbits of the Hamiltonian flow!

### Chain Complex

$$CF_*(H) = \bigoplus_{\gamma \in \text{Per}(H)} \mathbb{Z}_2 \cdot \gamma$$

Generated by periodic orbits (with appropriate grading).

### Boundary Operator

Count solutions to the **Floer equation** (perturbed Cauchy-Riemann):

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial s} + J\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} - X_H(u)\right) = 0$$

where $u: \mathbb{R} \times S^1 \to M$ connects two periodic orbits.

$$\partial\gamma_- = \sum_{\gamma_+} \# \mathcal{M}(\gamma_-, \gamma_+) \cdot \gamma_+$$

### Floer Homology

$$HF_*(H, J) = \frac{\ker\partial}{\text{im}\partial}$$

**Key theorem**: Independent of choices $(H, J)$, so it's an invariant of $(M, \omega)$!

## Lagrangian Floer Homology

### Setup

Given:
- Symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$
- Two Lagrangian submanifolds $L_0, L_1 \subset M$

### Chain Complex

$$CF_*(L_0, L_1) = \bigoplus_{p \in L_0 \cap L_1} \mathbb{Z}_2 \cdot p$$

Generated by intersection points (assuming transverse intersection).

### Boundary Operator

Count pseudoholomorphic strips:

$$\begin{align*}
u: \mathbb{R} \times [0,1] &\to M \\
\frac{\partial u}{\partial s} + J\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} &= 0 \\
u(s,0) \in L_0, \quad u(s,1) &\in L_1 \\
\lim_{s\to-\infty} u(s,t) &= p, \quad \lim_{s\to+\infty} u(s,t) = q
\end{align*}$$

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Holomorphic strips connect intersection points. They're like gradient flow lines but in infinite dimensions!

### Floer Homology

$$HF_*(L_0, L_1) = \frac{\ker\partial}{\text{im}\partial}$$

Invariant under Hamiltonian isotopy of $L_0$ or $L_1$.

## Properties

### Invariance

- $HF_*(M, \omega)$ is independent of $(H, J)$
- $HF_*(L_0, L_1)$ is invariant under Hamiltonian isotopy

### Euler Characteristic

For Lagrangian Floer homology:

$$\chi(HF_*(L_0, L_1)) = L_0 \cdot L_1 \quad \text{(algebraic intersection number)}$$

### Relation to Ordinary Homology

For monotone symplectic manifolds:

$$HF_*(M) \cong H_*(M) \otimes \Lambda$$

where $\Lambda$ is the Novikov ring.

## Applications

### Arnold Conjecture

**Statement**: For a nondegenerate Hamiltonian diffeomorphism $\phi$:

$$|\text{Fix}(\phi)| \geq \sum_{i=0}^{2n} b_i(M)$$

**Proof**: $HF_*(H) \cong H_*(M)$ implies at least $\sum b_i$ generators!

### Lagrangian Intersection

If $HF_*(L_0, L_1) \neq 0$, then $L_0 \cap L_1 \neq \emptyset$.

Even stronger: $|L_0 \cap L_1| \geq \text{rank } HF_*(L_0, L_1)$.

### Non-Squeezing Theorem

Gromov's nonsqueezing can be proved using Floer-theoretic methods.

### 3-Manifold Topology

Instanton Floer homology, Heegaard Floer homology, and monopole Floer homology are powerful 3-manifold invariants.

## Technical Challenges

### Compactness

Moduli spaces of holomorphic curves may not be compact due to:
- Bubbling off (sphere bubbles)
- Breaking (multiple levels)

Solution: Gromov compactness + gluing analysis.

### Transversality

Need regularity of moduli spaces. Use:
- Generic almost complex structures
- Perturbations (Kuranishi structures, polyfolds)

### Grading

Define relative grading using Conley-Zehnder index or Maslov index.

Absolute grading requires additional structure (e.g., $c_1(M) = 0$).

## Variants

### Symplectic Field Theory (SFT)

Studies holomorphic curves in symplectizations. Connects to contact homology.

### Fukaya Category

Enhances Lagrangian Floer homology to an $A_\infty$-category. Central to homological mirror symmetry.

### Rabinowitz Floer Homology

Studies leafwise intersection points. Useful for contact type hypersurfaces.

### Equivariant Floer Homology

Incorporates group actions. Related to equivariant cohomology.

---

## Computational Techniques

### Localization

For circle actions, use Atiyah-Bott localization to compute Floer homology.

### Spectral Sequences

Filter the chain complex to get spectral sequences converging to Floer homology.

### Exact Triangles

Long exact sequences relating different Floer groups (e.g., surgery triangles).

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing with Morse Homology
> Morse homology: finite-dimensional, compact manifolds  
> Floer homology: infinite-dimensional, requires elliptic PDEs  
> The analogy is deep but technical details differ significantly!

> [!warning] Ignoring Analytical Issues
> Floer homology isn't just algebra—it requires serious analysis (elliptic regularity, compactness, transversality).

> [!warning] Forgetting Coefficients
> Floer homology often uses Novikov rings or field coefficients. Integer coefficients are subtle!

> [!warning] Assuming It's Always Defined
> Floer homology may not be defined for arbitrary Lagrangians (need monotonicity, exactness, or other conditions).
