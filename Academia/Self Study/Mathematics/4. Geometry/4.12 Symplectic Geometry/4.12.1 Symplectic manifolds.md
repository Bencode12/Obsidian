## Definition

A **symplectic manifold** is a pair $(M, \omega)$ where:
- $M$ is a smooth manifold
- $\omega$ is a **symplectic form**: a closed, nondegenerate 2-form

### Conditions

1. **Closed**: $d\omega = 0$
2. **Nondegenerate**: For each $p \in M$, if $\omega_p(v,w) = 0$ for all $w \in T_p M$, then $v = 0$

Equivalently, $\omega^n = \omega \wedge ... \wedge \omega$ ($n$ times) is a volume form.

> [!example] Why Symplectic Geometry Matters
> Symplectic geometry is the mathematical framework for:  
> - Classical mechanics (phase space)  
> - Hamiltonian dynamics  
> - Quantization  
> - Mirror symmetry in string theory  
> - Low-dimensional topology

---

## Basic Properties

### Dimension

Symplectic manifolds must be **even-dimensional**: $\dim M = 2n$.

**Reason**: Nondegeneracy requires $\omega^n \neq 0$, which is only possible in even dimensions.

### Orientability

Every symplectic manifold is **orientable**.

The orientation is given by the volume form $\omega^n$.

### No Local Invariants

Unlike Riemannian geometry (where curvature is a local invariant), symplectic geometry has **no local invariants**!

This is the content of Darboux's theorem (see 4.6.6).

## Examples

### Example 1: Euclidean Space $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$

Standard coordinates: $(q^1, ..., q^n, p_1, ..., p_n)$

Standard symplectic form:
$$\omega_0 = \sum_{i=1}^n dq^i \wedge dp_i$$

In matrix form:
$$\omega_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is the **canonical symplectic structure** on phase space.

```desmos-graph
y = x
y = -x
x = [-2, 2]
```

> [!example] Physical Interpretation
> In mechanics, $q^i$ are positions and $p_i$ are momenta. The symplectic form encodes the canonical Poisson brackets.

### Example 2: Cotangent Bundles

For any manifold $Q$, the cotangent bundle $T^*Q$ has a canonical symplectic structure.

**Canonical 1-form** (tautological form):
$$\theta_\xi(v) = \xi(d\pi(v))$$

for $\xi \in T^*Q$ and $v \in T_\xi(T^*Q)$, where $\pi: T^*Q \to Q$ is the projection.

**Symplectic form**:
$$\omega = -d\theta$$

In local coordinates $(q^i, p_i)$ on $T^*Q$:
$$\omega = \sum_{i=1}^n dq^i \wedge dp_i$$

### Example 3: Complex Projective Space $\mathbb{CP}^n$

The **Fubini-Study form** makes $\mathbb{CP}^n$ into a symplectic (actually Kähler) manifold.

In homogeneous coordinates $[z_0:...:z_n]$:
$$\omega_{FS} = \frac{i}{2}\partial\bar{\partial}\log(|z_0|^2 + ... + |z_n|^2)$$

This is compatible with the complex structure.

### Example 4: Surfaces

Any orientable surface $\Sigma$ is symplectic!

Take $\omega$ to be any area form (volume form in 2D).

**Example**: Sphere $S^2$ with standard area form.

## Symplectic Linear Algebra

At each point, a symplectic form gives a symplectic vector space.

### Standard Form

On $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, any symplectic form can be written as:
$$\omega = \sum_{i=1}^n dx^i \wedge dy^i$$

in suitable coordinates (linear Darboux theorem).

### Symplectic Basis

A basis $\{e_1, ..., e_n, f_1, ..., f_n\}$ is **symplectic** if:
- $\omega(e_i, e_j) = 0$
- $\omega(f_i, f_j) = 0$
- $\omega(e_i, f_j) = \delta_{ij}$

### Symplectic Group

The **symplectic group** $Sp(2n, \mathbb{R})$ consists of linear transformations preserving $\omega_0$:

$$Sp(2n, \mathbb{R}) = \{A \in GL(2n, \mathbb{R}) : A^T J A = J\}$$

where $J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

---

## Compatible Structures

### Almost Complex Structures

An almost complex structure $J$ is **compatible** with $\omega$ if:
1. $\omega(Jv, Jw) = \omega(v,w)$ ($J$ preserves $\omega$)
2. $g(v,w) = \omega(v, Jw)$ is a Riemannian metric

### Kähler Manifolds

If $(M, \omega, J)$ has:
- $\omega$ symplectic
- $J$ integrable complex structure
- Compatibility condition

Then $M$ is **Kähler**.

**Examples**: $\mathbb{CP}^n$, complex tori, algebraic varieties.

## Symplectomorphisms

A diffeomorphism $\phi: (M, \omega) \to (N, \eta)$ is a **symplectomorphism** if:

$$\phi^*\eta = \omega$$

Symplectomorphisms preserve all symplectic structure.

### Hamiltonian Symplectomorphisms

Generated by Hamiltonian flows (see 4.6.2).

Form a subgroup: $\text{Ham}(M, \omega) \subset \text{Symp}(M, \omega)$.

---

## Volume and Capacity

### Liouville Volume

The **Liouville volume** is:
$$\text{Vol}(M) = \int_M \frac{\omega^n}{n!}$$

This is preserved by symplectomorphisms.

### Symplectic Capacity

Unlike volume, symplectic geometry has **rigidity** phenomena.

**Gromov's Nonsqueezing Theorem**: You cannot symplectically embed a ball $B^{2n}(r)$ into a cylinder $Z^{2n}(R) = B^2(R) \times \mathbb{R}^{2n-2}$ unless $r \leq R$.

This shows symplectic geometry is more rigid than volume-preserving geometry!

## Applications

### Classical Mechanics

Phase space is a symplectic manifold. Time evolution is a symplectomorphism (Liouville's theorem).

### Quantization

Geometric quantization constructs quantum Hilbert spaces from symplectic manifolds.

### Topology

Symplectic techniques solve problems in low-dimensional topology (Floer homology, Seiberg-Witten theory).

### Mirror Symmetry

Pairs of Calabi-Yau manifolds with swapped symplectic and complex structures.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Symplectic with Riemannian
> Symplectic forms are skew-symmetric ($\omega(v,w) = -\omega(w,v)$), while metrics are symmetric. They're fundamentally different structures!

> [!warning] Assuming Odd Dimensions Work
> Symplectic manifolds MUST be even-dimensional. There's no such thing as a symplectic 3-manifold!

> [!warning] Thinking Curvature Matters
> Unlike Riemannian geometry, symplectic geometry has NO local invariants. All symplectic manifolds look the same locally (Darboux theorem).

> [!warning] Forgetting Closedness
> Both conditions matter: $d\omega = 0$ AND nondegeneracy. A nondegenerate 2-form that isn't closed is called an "almost symplectic" structure.
