## Motivation

**Moment maps** encode the symmetries of a symplectic manifold. They generalize angular momentum and are fundamental to symplectic reduction.

---

## Lie Group Actions

### Setup

Let $G$ be a Lie group acting on a symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$ by symplectomorphisms:

$$g^*\omega = \omega \quad \text{for all } g \in G$$

The infinitesimal action gives a Lie algebra homomorphism:

$$\mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{X}(M), \quad \xi \mapsto \xi_M$$

where $\xi_M(p) = \frac{d}{dt}|_{t=0}\exp(t\xi)\cdot p$.

---

## Definition

A **moment map** for the $G$-action is a map:

$$\mu: M \to \mathfrak{g}^*$$

satisfying:

1. **Equivariance**: $\mu(g\cdot x) = \text{Ad}_g^*\mu(x)$
2. **Hamiltonian condition**: For each $\xi \in \mathfrak{g}$:
   $$d\langle\mu, \xi\rangle = \iota_{\xi_M}\omega$$

Equivalently, $\xi_M = X_{\langle\mu,\xi\rangle}$ (the vector field generated by $\xi$ is Hamiltonian).

> [!example] Why Moment Maps Matter
> Moment maps connect:  
> - Symmetries ↔ Conserved quantities (Noether's theorem)  
> - Symplectic geometry ↔ Representation theory  
> - Physics ↔ Geometry  
> They're essential for symplectic reduction and geometric quantization!

---

## Examples

### Example 1: Rotation on $\mathbb{R}^2$

Group: $G = S^1$ acting by rotation  
Lie algebra: $\mathfrak{g} \cong \mathbb{R}$

Action: $e^{i\theta}\cdot(x,y) = (x\cos\theta - y\sin\theta, x\sin\theta + y\cos\theta)$

Vector field: $\xi_M = -y\frac{\partial}{\partial x} + x\frac{\partial}{\partial y}$

Moment map:
$$\mu(x,y) = \frac{1}{2}(x^2 + y^2)$$

This is (half) the angular momentum!

```desmos-graph
x^2 + y^2 = 1
x^2 + y^2 = 2
x^2 + y^2 = 3
```

### Example 2: Translation on $T^*\mathbb{R}^n$

Group: $G = \mathbb{R}^n$ acting by translation on base  
Action: $a\cdot(q,p) = (q+a, p)$

Moment map:
$$\mu(q,p) = p \in (\mathbb{R}^n)^* \cong \mathbb{R}^n$$

This is linear momentum!

### Example 3: $S^1$ Action on $\mathbb{CP}^n$

Standard $S^1$ action on homogeneous coordinates:
$$e^{i\theta}\cdot[z_0:...:z_n] = [e^{i\theta}z_0:z_1:...:z_n]$$

Moment map (with Fubini-Study form):
$$\mu([z]) = \frac{|z_0|^2}{|z_0|^2 + ... + |z_n|^2}$$

Image: $[0,1] \subset \mathbb{R}$

### Example 4: Torus Action

For $T^n = (S^1)^n$ acting on $\mathbb{C}^n$ by:
$$(e^{i\theta_1},...,e^{i\theta_n})\cdot(z_1,...,z_n) = (e^{i\theta_1}z_1,...,e^{i\theta_n}z_n)$$

Moment map:
$$\mu(z_1,...,z_n) = \left(\frac{|z_1|^2}{2},...,\frac{|z_n|^2}{2}\right)$$

Image: First orthant in $\mathbb{R}^n$

## Properties

### Conservation

If the Hamiltonian $H$ is $G$-invariant, then $\mu$ is conserved along the flow:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\mu(\phi_t(x)) = 0$$

This is Noether's theorem in symplectic language!

### Poisson Brackets

For $\xi, \eta \in \mathfrak{g}$:

$$\{\langle\mu, \xi\rangle, \langle\mu, \eta\rangle\} = \langle\mu, [\xi,\eta]\rangle$$

The moment map is a **Poisson map** from $M$ to $\mathfrak{g}^*$ (with Lie-Poisson structure).

### Convexity (Atiyah-Guillemin-Sternberg)

For a torus action on a compact symplectic manifold:

**The image $\mu(M)$ is a convex polytope!**

Moreover, $\mu(M)$ is the convex hull of the images of fixed points.

```desmos-graph
x >= 0
y >= 0
x + y <= 1
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> The moment map image captures the global geometry of the symplectic manifold. For toric varieties, it completely determines the manifold!

## Existence and Uniqueness

### When Do Moment Maps Exist?

A moment map exists if and only if the action is **Hamiltonian**, meaning:
- The action preserves $\omega$
- Each fundamental vector field $\xi_M$ is Hamiltonian

Not all symplectic actions are Hamiltonian!

**Example**: Irrational flow on a torus preserves the symplectic form but has no moment map.

### Uniqueness

If $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$ are two moment maps for the same action, then:

$$\mu_1 - \mu_2 = \text{constant} \in Z(\mathfrak{g}^*)$$

where $Z(\mathfrak{g}^*)$ is the center of $\mathfrak{g}^*$.

For compact connected $G$, moment maps are unique up to addition of a central element.

---

## Coadjoint Orbits

### Kirillov-Kostant-Souriau Form

For $\alpha \in \mathfrak{g}^*$, the coadjoint orbit $\mathcal{O}_\alpha = G\cdot\alpha$ carries a natural symplectic form:

$$\omega_\alpha(\xi_{\mathcal{O}}, \eta_{\mathcal{O}}) = \langle\alpha, [\xi,\eta]\rangle$$

### Universal Property

Any Hamiltonian $G$-space factors through coadjoint orbits:

$$\mu: M \to \mathfrak{g}^* \quad \text{has image in union of coadjoint orbits}$$

---

## Applications

### Symplectic Reduction

Given $\mu: M \to \mathfrak{g}^*$ and a regular value $\alpha$:

$$M/\!/_{\alpha}G = \mu^{-1}(\alpha)/G_\alpha$$

is a symplectic manifold (see 4.6.5).

### Geometric Quantization

Moment maps help construct quantum representations from classical symmetries.

### Toric Geometry

Compact symplectic manifolds with effective $T^n$ action are classified by their moment polytopes.

### Gauge Theory

Moment maps appear in Yang-Mills theory and moduli spaces of connections.

## Computation Techniques

### Method 1: Direct Integration

Solve $d\langle\mu, \xi\rangle = \iota_{\xi_M}\omega$ by integration.

### Method 2: Use Symmetry

For torus actions, moment maps are often quadratic in coordinates.

### Method 3: Normalization

Fix the constant by requiring $\mu$ to vanish at a chosen point.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Momentum with Moment Map
> "Momentum" usually refers to specific conserved quantities. "Moment map" is the general framework encoding all symmetries.

> [!warning] Forgetting Equivariance
> A moment map must be equivariant! Just satisfying $d\langle\mu,\xi\rangle = \iota_{\xi_M}\omega$ isn't enough.

> [!warning] Assuming All Actions Have Moment Maps
> Only Hamiltonian actions have moment maps. Some symplectic actions are not Hamiltonian.

> [!warning] Sign Conventions
> Different authors use different sign conventions for moment maps. Check whether $d\langle\mu,\xi\rangle = \iota_{\xi_M}\omega$ or $-\iota_{\xi_M}\omega$!
