## Overview

**Symplectic reduction** (or Marsden-Weinstein reduction) is a procedure to construct new symplectic manifolds from old ones by quotienting out symmetries.

It's fundamental to mechanics, gauge theory, and algebraic geometry.

---

## The Reduction Procedure

### Setup

Given:
- Symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$
- Lie group $G$ acting Hamiltonially with moment map $\mu: M \to \mathfrak{g}^*$
- Regular value $\alpha \in \mathfrak{g}^*$ of $\mu$

### Construction

The **symplectic reduction** at level $\alpha$ is:

$$M/\!/_{\alpha}G = \mu^{-1}(\alpha)/G_\alpha$$

where $G_\alpha$ is the stabilizer of $\alpha$ under the coadjoint action.

**Special case**: If $\alpha = 0$ and $G$ acts freely:
$$M/\!/G = \mu^{-1}(0)/G$$

### Marsden-Weinstein-Meyer Theorem

If $G$ acts freely and properly on $\mu^{-1}(\alpha)$, then $M/\!/_{\alpha}G$ is a smooth symplectic manifold with:

$$\dim(M/\!/_{\alpha}G) = \dim M - 2\dim G$$

and a unique symplectic form $\omega_{red}$ such that:

$$\pi^*\omega_{red} = i^*\omega$$

where $i: \mu^{-1}(\alpha) \hookrightarrow M$ and $\pi: \mu^{-1}(\alpha) \to M/\!/_{\alpha}G$.

> [!example] Why Symplectic Reduction Matters
> Reduction allows us to:  
> - Eliminate symmetries from mechanical systems  
> - Construct moduli spaces in gauge theory  
> - Build quotients in algebraic geometry (GIT)  
> - Understand singularities and stratifications

## Examples

### Example 1: Harmonic Oscillator with Rotation Symmetry

System: $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $\omega = dx \wedge dy$  
Group: $S^1$ rotating the plane  
Moment map: $\mu(x,y) = \frac{1}{2}(x^2 + y^2)$

Reduction at $\mu = c > 0$:
- $\mu^{-1}(c) = \text{circle of radius }\sqrt{2c}$
- $S^1$ acts transitively on this circle
- $M/\!/_{c}S^1 = \text{point}$

The reduced space is trivial (0-dimensional).

### Example 2: Cotangent Bundle Reduction

System: $T^*G$ for a Lie group $G$  
Action: Left translation on $G$  
Moment map: $\mu(g,p) = p$ (right-invariant momentum)

Reduction at $\mu = e^*$:
$$T^*G/\!/_{e^*}G \cong \{\text{point}\}$$

This reflects that we've quotiented out all symmetry.

### Example 3: Polygon Spaces

Consider $n$ rods of lengths $r_1, ..., r_n$ connected end-to-end in $\mathbb{R}^3$, forming a closed polygon.

Configuration space: Product of spheres modulo rotations
$$M_{\mathbf{r}} = \{(u_1,...,u_n) \in S^2 \times ... \times S^2 : \sum r_i u_i = 0\}/SO(3)$$

This is a symplectic reduction! It appears in robotics and molecular biology.

### Example 4: Moduli Space of Flat Connections

For a surface $\Sigma$ and compact Lie group $G$:

$$\mathcal{M}(\Sigma, G) = \{\text{flat } G\text{-connections on } \Sigma\}/\mathcal{G}$$

This is obtained by symplectic reduction from the space of all connections.

Atiyah-Bott showed this has a natural symplectic structure!

## Properties

### Dimension Formula

$$\dim(M/\!/_{\alpha}G) = \dim M - 2\dim G + \dim G_\alpha$$

For free actions: $\dim(M/\!/G) = \dim M - 2\dim G$.

### Functoriality

Reduction commutes with products:
$$(M_1 \times M_2)/\!/G \cong (M_1/\!/G) \times (M_2/\!/G)$$

### Reduction in Stages

If $H \subset G$ is a subgroup, we can reduce in stages:
$$M/\!/G \cong (M/\!/H)/(G/H)$$

under appropriate conditions.

---

## Singular Reduction

When the action is not free, $\mu^{-1}(\alpha)/G_\alpha$ may be singular.

### Stratification

The quotient decomposes into smooth symplectic strata:

$$M/\!/_{\alpha}G = \bigcup_i S_i$$

Each stratum $S_i$ corresponds to an orbit type.

### Sjamaar-Lerman Theorem

Even with singularities, the reduced space has a well-defined symplectic structure on each stratum.

---

## Relation to Other Constructions

### GIT Quotients

For Kähler manifolds with holomorphic $G^\mathbb{C}$ action:

$$M/\!/G \cong M^{ss}/\!/G^\mathbb{C}$$

This connects symplectic reduction to Geometric Invariant Theory!

### Hamiltonian Reduction

In physics, this is called "reducing by constraints" or "going to the constraint surface."

### Poisson Reduction

More generally, one can reduce Poisson manifolds, not just symplectic ones.

## Applications

### Mechanics

Reduce systems with symmetry (rigid body, N-body problem, fluids).

**Example**: Rigid body → Euler equations via reduction by $SO(3)$.

### Gauge Theory

Moduli spaces of instantons, monopoles, and vortices are constructed by reduction.

### Algebraic Geometry

Quotients of varieties by group actions (toric varieties, quiver varieties).

### Topology

Constructing symplectic manifolds with prescribed properties.

## Computing Reduced Spaces

### Method 1: Direct Computation

1. Find $\mu^{-1}(\alpha)$
2. Identify the $G_\alpha$ action
3. Compute the quotient

### Method 2: Use Invariants

Find $G$-invariant functions on $M$. The reduced space is described by relations among invariants.

### Method 3: Local Models

Near regular points, use the normal form:
$$M \cong G \times_{G_\alpha} (\mathfrak{g}_\alpha^0 \times V)$$

where $V$ is a symplectic vector space.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Forgetting Regular Value Condition
> $\alpha$ must be a regular value of $\mu$ for the reduced space to be smooth. At critical values, you get singularities.

> [!warning] Confusing Quotient Types
> Symplectic reduction: $\mu^{-1}(\alpha)/G_\alpha$ (Marsden-Weinstein)  
> Ordinary quotient: $M/G$ (may not be symplectic!)  
> They're different!

> [!warning] Assuming Freely Acting
> Many interesting reductions have non-free actions, leading to singular spaces. Don't assume freeness!

> [!warning] Dimension Miscalculation
> Remember: $\dim(M/\!/G) = \dim M - 2\dim G$ for free actions. Each symmetry removes 2 dimensions!
