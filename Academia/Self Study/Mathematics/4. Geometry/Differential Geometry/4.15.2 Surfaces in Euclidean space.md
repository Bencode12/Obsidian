## Parametric Surfaces

### Definition

**Parametric surface** in $\mathbb{R}^3$:
$$X: U \to \mathbb{R}^3, \quad (u,v) \mapsto X(u,v)$$

where $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ is open.

Assume $X$ is smooth and **regular**: $X_u \times X_v \neq 0$.

### Examples

**Sphere** (radius $R$):
$$X(\theta, \phi) = (R\sin\phi\cos\theta, R\sin\phi\sin\theta, R\cos\phi)$$

**Torus**:
$$X(u,v) = ((R+r\cos v)\cos u, (R+r\cos v)\sin u, r\sin v)$$

**Helicoid**:
$$X(u,v) = (v\cos u, v\sin u, au)$$

---

## First Fundamental Form

### Metric Coefficients

**First fundamental form** measures lengths and angles on surface:

$$I = E\,du^2 + 2F\,du\,dv + G\,dv^2$$

where:
- $E = \langle X_u, X_u\rangle$
- $F = \langle X_u, X_v\rangle$
- $G = \langle X_v, X_v\rangle$

Matrix form:
$$g = \begin{pmatrix} E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix}$$

### Arc Length

Length of curve $\gamma(t) = X(u(t), v(t))$:
$$L = \int_a^b \sqrt{Eu'^2 + 2Fu'v' + Gv'^2} \, dt$$

### Area Element

Area of surface patch:
$$A = \iint_U \sqrt{EG - F^2} \, du\,dv$$

$\sqrt{EG-F^2} = \|X_u \times X_v\|$ is area scaling factor.

---

## Normal Vector

### Unit Normal

$$N = \frac{X_u \times X_v}{\|X_u \times X_v\|}$$

Orientation depends on order of parameters.

### Tangent Plane

At point $p = X(u_0, v_0)$:
$$T_pS = \text{span}\{X_u, X_v\}$$

Plane spanned by partial derivatives.

---

## Second Fundamental Form

### Shape Operator

**Weingarten map** (shape operator):
$$S_p: T_pS \to T_pS$$

Defined by: $S_p(v) = -D_v N$ (derivative of normal).

Measures how normal changes = how surface bends!

### Second Fundamental Form Coefficients

$$II = L\,du^2 + 2M\,du\,dv + N\,dv^2$$

where:
- $L = \langle X_{uu}, N\rangle$
- $M = \langle X_{uv}, N\rangle$
- $N = \langle X_{vv}, N\rangle$ (confusing notation!)

Matrix:
$$h = \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N \end{pmatrix}$$

---

## Principal Curvatures

### Eigenvalues of Shape Operator

**Principal curvatures** $\kappa_1, \kappa_2$: Eigenvalues of $S_p$.

Directions: Eigenvectors (principal directions).

Orthogonal if $\kappa_1 \neq \kappa_2$!

### Mean and Gaussian Curvature

**Mean curvature**:
$$H = \frac{\kappa_1 + \kappa_2}{2} = \frac{EN - 2FM + GL}{2(EG-F^2)}$$

Average bending.

**Gaussian curvature**:
$$K = \kappa_1 \kappa_2 = \frac{LN - M^2}{EG-F^2}$$

Intrinsic curvature (Theorema Egregium)!

### Classification of Points

|Type|Condition|Example|
|---|---|---|
|Elliptic|$K > 0$|Sphere|
|Hyperbolic|$K < 0$|Saddle|
|Parabolic|$K = 0$, not flat|Cylinder|
|Planar|$K = H = 0$|Plane|

```desmos-graph

x = [-2, 2]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Hyperbolic paraboloid (saddle) has negative Gaussian curvature.

## Theorema Egregium

### Gauss's Remarkable Theorem

**Gaussian curvature is intrinsic!**

Can be computed from first fundamental form alone (no need for embedding).

Formula involves only $E, F, G$ and their derivatives.

### Consequences

- Isometric surfaces have same $K$
- Cannot isometrically map sphere to plane ($K$ differs)
- Foundation of intrinsic geometry

## Geodesics on Surfaces

### Definition

**Geodesic**: Curve on surface with zero geodesic curvature.

Locally shortest path between points!

### Geodesic Equations

For curve $\gamma(t) = X(u(t), v(t))$:

$$u'' + \Gamma_{11}^1 u'^2 + 2\Gamma_{12}^1 u'v' + \Gamma_{22}^1 v'^2 = 0$$
$$v'' + \Gamma_{11}^2 u'^2 + 2\Gamma_{12}^2 u'v' + \Gamma_{22}^2 v'^2 = 0$$

where $\Gamma_{ij}^k$ are Christoffel symbols.

### Examples

- Sphere: Great circles
- Cylinder: Helices, circles, lines
- Plane: Straight lines

---

## Special Surfaces

### Minimal Surfaces

Surface with $H = 0$ everywhere.

Examples: Catenoid, helicoid, Enneper surface.

Minimize area for given boundary!

### Surfaces of Constant Curvature

- $K > 0$: Sphere (model for elliptic geometry)
- $K = 0$: Plane, cylinder (Euclidean geometry)
- $K < 0$: Pseudosphere (model for hyperbolic geometry)

### Developable Surfaces

Surfaces with $K = 0$ that can be flattened without distortion.

Examples: Cylinder, cone, tangent developable.

---

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Mean and Gaussian Curvature
> $H$ is extrinsic (depends on embedding). $K$ is intrinsic!

> [!warning] Forgetting That Normal Depends on Orientation
> Reversing orientation flips sign of second fundamental form.

> [!warning] Assuming All Surfaces Are Orientable
> Möbius strip and Klein bottle are non-orientable!
