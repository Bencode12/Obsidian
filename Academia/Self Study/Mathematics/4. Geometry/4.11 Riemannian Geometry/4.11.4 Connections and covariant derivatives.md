## Motivation

On a curved manifold, tangent spaces at different points are different vector spaces. We can't directly compare vectors at different points!

A **connection** provides a way to:
- Differentiate vector fields
- Compare vectors at different points (parallel transport)
- Define geodesics intrinsically

---

## Affine Connections

### Definition

An **affine connection** (or linear connection) on a manifold $M$ is an operator:

$$\nabla: \mathfrak{X}(M) \times \mathfrak{X}(M) \to \mathfrak{X}(M)$$

written as $\nabla_X Y$ (covariant derivative of $Y$ in direction $X$), satisfying:

1. **Linearity in first argument**: $\nabla_{fX+gY}Z = f\nabla_X Z + g\nabla_Y Z$
2. **Additivity in second argument**: $\nabla_X(Y+Z) = \nabla_X Y + \nabla_X Z$
3. **Leibniz rule**: $\nabla_X(fY) = X(f)Y + f\nabla_X Y$

for all vector fields $X, Y, Z$ and functions $f, g$.

> [!example] Why This Matters
> The connection tells us how vectors change as we move along the manifold. Without it, we can't do calculus on curved spaces!

---

## Levi-Civita Connection

For a Riemannian manifold $(M,g)$, there is a **unique** connection satisfying:

1. **Metric compatibility**: $X\langle Y,Z\rangle = \langle\nabla_X Y, Z\rangle + \langle Y, \nabla_X Z\rangle$
2. **Torsion-free**: $\nabla_X Y - \nabla_Y X = [X,Y]$

This is the **Levi-Civita connection** (Fundamental Theorem of Riemannian Geometry).

### Properties

- Preserves the metric under parallel transport
- Symmetric (no torsion)
- Compatible with the Riemannian structure

---

## Christoffel Symbols

In local coordinates, the connection is determined by **Christoffel symbols**:

$$\nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}}\frac{\partial}{\partial x^j} = \Gamma^k_{ij}\frac{\partial}{\partial x^k}$$

The Christoffel symbols $\Gamma^k_{ij}$ are not tensors—they depend on coordinates!

### Formula for Levi-Civita Connection

$$\Gamma^k_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}g^{kl}\left(\frac{\partial g_{lj}}{\partial x^i} + \frac{\partial g_{li}}{\partial x^j} - \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial x^l}\right)$$

where $g^{kl}$ is the inverse metric.

---

## Covariant Derivative

### For Vector Fields

If $Y = Y^j\frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}$, then:

$$\nabla_i Y^k = \frac{\partial Y^k}{\partial x^i} + \Gamma^k_{ij}Y^j$$

Notation: $\nabla_i Y^k = Y^k_{;i}$ (semicolon denotes covariant derivative)

### For General Tensors

The covariant derivative extends to all tensors via the Leibniz rule.

**Example**: For a $(0,2)$-tensor $T_{ij}$:

$$\nabla_k T_{ij} = \frac{\partial T_{ij}}{\partial x^k} - \Gamma^l_{ki}T_{lj} - \Gamma^l_{kj}T_{il}$$

**Pattern**: 
- Add $\Gamma$ terms for each upper index
- Subtract $\Gamma$ terms for each lower index

---

## Examples

### Example 1: Euclidean Space $\mathbb{R}^n$

Metric: $g_{ij} = \delta_{ij}$ (constant)

Christoffel symbols: $\Gamma^k_{ij} = 0$

Covariant derivative = ordinary partial derivative:
$$\nabla_i Y^k = \frac{\partial Y^k}{\partial x^i}$$

### Example 2: Sphere $S^2$

Metric: $ds^2 = d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2$

Non-zero Christoffel symbols:
- $\Gamma^\theta_{\phi\phi} = -\sin\theta\cos\theta$
- $\Gamma^\phi_{\theta\phi} = \Gamma^\phi_{\phi\theta} = \cot\theta$

For a vector field $Y = Y^\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} + Y^\phi\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}$:

$$\nabla_\theta Y^\theta = \frac{\partial Y^\theta}{\partial\theta}$$
$$\nabla_\theta Y^\phi = \frac{\partial Y^\phi}{\partial\theta} + \cot\theta\,Y^\phi$$
$$\nabla_\phi Y^\theta = \frac{\partial Y^\theta}{\partial\phi} - \sin\theta\cos\theta\,Y^\phi$$
$$\nabla_\phi Y^\phi = \frac{\partial Y^\phi}{\partial\phi} + \cot\theta\,Y^\theta$$

### Example 3: Polar Coordinates in $\mathbb{R}^2$

Metric: $ds^2 = dr^2 + r^2 d\theta^2$

Non-zero Christoffel symbols:
- $\Gamma^r_{\theta\theta} = -r$
- $\Gamma^\theta_{r\theta} = \Gamma^\theta_{\theta r} = \frac{1}{r}$

## Properties of Covariant Derivative

### Linearity

$$\nabla_X(aY + bZ) = a\nabla_X Y + b\nabla_X Z$$

### Leibniz Rule

$$\nabla_X(fY) = X(f)Y + f\nabla_X Y$$

### Commutator Relation

$$
abla_X
abla_Y Z - 
abla_Y
abla_X Z - 
abla_{[X,Y]}Z = R(X,Y)Z$$

where $R$ is the Riemann curvature tensor. This shows curvature measures the failure of covariant derivatives to commute!

### Metric Compatibility

$$\nabla_X g = 0$$

Equivalently: $X\langle Y,Z\rangle = \langle\nabla_X Y, Z\rangle + \langle Y, \nabla_X Z\rangle$

## Computing with Christoffel Symbols

### Step-by-Step Method

1. Write down the metric $g_{ij}$
2. Compute the inverse metric $g^{ij}$
3. Calculate partial derivatives $\frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial x^k}$
4. Use the formula:
   $$\Gamma^k_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}g^{kl}\left(\frac{\partial g_{lj}}{\partial x^i} + \frac{\partial g_{li}}{\partial x^j} - \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial x^l}\right)$$

### Symmetry

Christoffel symbols are symmetric in lower indices:
$$\Gamma^k_{ij} = \Gamma^k_{ji}$$

(This follows from torsion-free condition)

---

## Alternative Approaches

### Koszul Formula

The Levi-Civita connection can be computed directly:

$$\begin{align*}
2\langle\nabla_X Y, Z\rangle &= X\langle Y,Z\rangle + Y\langle X,Z\rangle - Z\langle X,Y\rangle \\
&\quad - \langle X,[Y,Z]\rangle + \langle Y,[X,Z]\rangle + \langle Z,[X,Y]\rangle
\end{align*}$$

This avoids computing Christoffel symbols explicitly!

### Moving Frames

Use orthonormal frames instead of coordinate frames. Often simplifies calculations.

## Applications

### Geodesic Equation

Geodesics satisfy:
$$\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}\dot{\gamma} = 0$$

In coordinates:
$$\frac{d^2x^k}{dt^2} + \Gamma^k_{ij}\frac{dx^i}{dt}\frac{dx^j}{dt} = 0$$

### Curvature Tensor

The Riemann curvature is defined using the connection:
$$R(X,Y)Z = 
abla_X
abla_Y Z - 
abla_Y
abla_X Z - 
abla_{[X,Y]}Z$$

### Parallel Transport

A vector field $V$ along a curve $\gamma$ is parallel if:
$$\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}V = 0$$

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Christoffel Symbols Are Not Tensors
> $\Gamma^k_{ij}$ don't transform like tensors under coordinate changes. Only their combinations in curvature are tensorial!

> [!warning] Confusing Partial and Covariant Derivatives
> $\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i} \neq \nabla_i$ in general! They only agree in flat space with Cartesian coordinates.

> [!warning] Index Placement
> Be careful with upper vs. lower indices. The position matters for transformation properties!

> [!warning] Forgetting Symmetry
> $\Gamma^k_{ij} = \Gamma^k_{ji}$ for the Levi-Civita connection. Use this to simplify calculations.
