## Definition

**Parallel transport** is a way to move vectors along curves while keeping them "parallel" according to the connection.

Given a curve $\gamma: [a,b] \to M$ and a vector $v \in T_{\gamma(a)}M$, parallel transport produces a vector field $V(t)$ along $\gamma$ such that:

1. $V(a) = v$ (starts with the given vector)
2. $\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}V = 0$ (covariant derivative vanishes)

The vector $V(b) \in T_{\gamma(b)}M$ is the **parallel transport** of $v$ along $\gamma$.

> [!example] Why Parallel Transport Matters
> On curved spaces, there's no canonical way to compare vectors at different points. Parallel transport provides a path-dependent identification of tangent spaces, revealing the curvature of the manifold!

---

## Parallel Transport Equation

In local coordinates, if $\gamma(t) = (x^1(t), ..., x^n(t))$ and $V(t) = V^k(t)\frac{\partial}{\partial x^k}$, then:

$$\frac{dV^k}{dt} + \Gamma^k_{ij}\frac{dx^i}{dt}V^j = 0$$

This is a system of linear ODEs!

### Matrix Form

Let $A^k_j(t) = \Gamma^k_{ij}(\gamma(t))\dot{x}^i(t)$. Then:

$$\frac{dV}{dt} + A(t)V = 0$$

Solution exists and is unique by ODE theory.

## Properties

### Linearity

Parallel transport $P_\gamma: T_{\gamma(a)}M \to T_{\gamma(b)}M$ is a **linear isomorphism**.

### Isometry (Levi-Civita Connection)

For the Levi-Civita connection, parallel transport preserves inner products:

$$\langle V(t), W(t)\rangle = \text{constant}$$

Therefore: $\|V(t)\| = \text{constant}$ and angles are preserved.

### Path Dependence

Parallel transport depends on the path! If $\gamma_1$ and $\gamma_2$ are two different paths from $p$ to $q$:

$$P_{\gamma_1} \neq P_{\gamma_2} \quad \text{(in general)}$$

This path dependence measures curvature.

### Composition

If $\gamma$ goes from $p$ to $q$ and $\sigma$ from $q$ to $r$:

$$P_{\sigma \cdot \gamma} = P_\sigma \circ P_\gamma$$

## Examples

### Example 1: Euclidean Space $\mathbb{R}^n$

Christoffel symbols: $\Gamma^k_{ij} = 0$

Parallel transport equation: $\frac{dV^k}{dt} = 0$

Solution: $V^k(t) = \text{constant}$

Parallel transport is just translation—vectors don't change!

```desmos-graph
y = 2
x = [0, 5]
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> In flat space, parallel transport is obvious: keep the vector pointing in the same direction.

### Example 2: Sphere $S^2$

Transport a vector around a latitude circle at colatitude $\theta_0$.

After one complete loop, the vector rotates by angle:

$$\alpha = 2\pi\cos\theta_0$$

**Special cases**:
- Equator ($\theta_0 = \pi/2$): $\alpha = 0$ (no rotation)
- Near pole ($\theta_0 \approx 0$): $\alpha \approx 2\pi$ (full rotation)

This is the **holonomy** of the sphere!

### Example 3: Cone

A cone (minus the apex) is locally flat. Parallel transport around a closed loop gives rotation by the **deficit angle**.

If the cone has opening angle $\beta$, the deficit angle is $2\pi(1-\sin\beta)$.

## Holonomy

The **holonomy group** at $p \in M$ is:

$$\text{Hol}_p(M) = \{P_\gamma : \gamma \text{ is a loop based at } p\} \subset GL(T_p M)$$

It measures how much vectors rotate when transported around closed loops.

### Ambrose-Singer Theorem

The Lie algebra of the holonomy group is generated by curvature operators:

$$\mathfrak{hol}_p = \text{span}\{P_\gamma^{-1}R(X,Y)P_\gamma : X,Y \in T_p M, \gamma \text{ loops}\}$$

Curvature determines holonomy!

### Special Holonomy

Manifolds with restricted holonomy have special geometric structures:

|Holonomy Group|Geometry|
|---|---|
|$SO(n)$|Oriented Riemannian|
|$U(n)$|Kähler|
|$SU(n)$|Calabi-Yau|
|$Sp(n)$|Hyperkähler|
|$G_2$|Exceptional (7D)|
|$Spin(7)$|Exceptional (8D)|

## Parallel Vector Fields

A vector field $V$ on $M$ is **parallel** if:

$$\nabla_X V = 0 \quad \text{for all } X$$

Equivalently, $V$ is parallel along every curve.

### Existence

Parallel vector fields exist globally only if the holonomy is trivial.

**Example**: $\mathbb{R}^n$ has $n$ independent parallel vector fields (constant vector fields).

**Example**: $S^2$ has NO non-zero parallel vector fields (Hairy Ball Theorem).

---

## Relation to Curvature

### Infinitesimal Holonomy

Transport around an infinitesimal parallelogram with sides $\epsilon X$ and $\epsilon Y$:

$$P_\gamma = \text{id} - \epsilon^2 R(X,Y) + O(\epsilon^3)$$

The deviation from identity is measured by curvature!

### Flat Manifolds

A manifold is **flat** (locally isometric to $\mathbb{R}^n$) if and only if:
- $R = 0$ (zero curvature)
- Holonomy is trivial

**Examples**: Torus, Klein bottle (with flat metrics)

## Computing Parallel Transport

### Method 1: Solve ODE

Directly solve the parallel transport equation:
$$\frac{dV^k}{dt} + \Gamma^k_{ij}\dot{x}^i V^j = 0$$

### Method 2: Use Symmetries

If the manifold has Killing vector fields (symmetries), use conserved quantities.

### Method 3: Develop to Tangent Space

For simple curves, "unroll" the manifold onto the tangent space.

**Example**: Parallel transport on a cone → develop to plane → straight lines.

## Applications

### General Relativity

Parallel transport describes how gyroscopes precess in gravitational fields (geodetic effect).

### Gauge Theory

In physics, parallel transport is described by Wilson loops:
$$W(\gamma) = \text{Tr}(P_\gamma)$$

These are fundamental observables in quantum field theory.

### Computer Graphics

Parallel transport is used for:
- Texture mapping on curved surfaces
- Vector field design
- Shape analysis

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Assuming Path Independence
> Parallel transport is generally path-dependent! Only in flat space is it independent of the path.

> [!warning] Confusing with Projection
> Parallel transport is NOT orthogonal projection onto tangent spaces. It's defined by the connection.

> [!warning] Forgetting Initial Condition
> The parallel transport equation needs an initial vector $V(0) = v$. Without it, the solution isn't unique.

> [!warning] Mixing Up Active vs. Passive
> Parallel transport actively moves vectors. Don't confuse it with changing coordinates (passive transformation).
