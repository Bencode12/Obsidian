## Definition

A **Riemannian metric** on a smooth manifold $M$ is a smoothly varying inner product on each tangent space $T_p M$.

Formally, it's a $(0,2)$-tensor field $g$ such that for each $p \in M$:
1. $g_p: T_p M \times T_p M \to \mathbb{R}$ is bilinear
2. $g_p$ is symmetric: $g_p(X,Y) = g_p(Y,X)$
3. $g_p$ is positive definite: $g_p(X,X) > 0$ for $X \neq 0$

Notation: $\langle X, Y \rangle_g = g(X,Y)$ or simply $\langle X, Y \rangle$

> [!example] Why Riemannian Metrics Matter
> The metric allows us to measure:  
> - Lengths of curves  
> - Angles between vectors  
> - Areas and volumes  
> - Distances between points  
> 
> It turns an abstract manifold into a geometric space!

---

## Local Coordinates

In local coordinates $(x^1, ..., x^n)$, the metric is represented by a symmetric positive-definite matrix:

$$g_{ij} = g\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}, \frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}\right)$$

The metric tensor can be written as:

$$g = g_{ij}\,dx^i \otimes dx^j = \sum_{i,j=1}^n g_{ij}\,dx^i dx^j$$

(using Einstein summation convention)

### Matrix Representation

$$[g_{ij}] = \begin{pmatrix} g_{11} & g_{12} & \cdots & g_{1n} \\ g_{21} & g_{22} & \cdots & g_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ g_{n1} & g_{n2} & \cdots & g_{nn} \end{pmatrix}$$

## Examples

### Example 1: Euclidean Space $\mathbb{R}^n$

The standard Euclidean metric in Cartesian coordinates:

$$g_{ij} = \delta_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } i = j \\ 0 & \text{if } i \neq j \end{cases}$$

$$ds^2 = dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2 \quad (\text{in } \mathbb{R}^3)$$

This is the "flat" metric we're familiar with from calculus.

### Example 2: Sphere $S^2$

Using spherical coordinates $(\theta, \phi)$ where $\theta \in [0,\pi]$ (colatitude) and $\phi \in [0,2\pi)$ (longitude):

$$ds^2 = d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2$$

Matrix form:
$$[g_{ij}] = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \sin^2\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

Note: The metric depends on position ($\theta$), showing the sphere is curved!

```desmos-graph
x = $cos(t)$
y = $sin(t)$
t = $[0, 2*pi]$
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> On a sphere, circles of latitude get smaller as you approach the poles. The $\sin^2\theta$ factor captures this!

### Example 3: Hyperbolic Plane (Poincaré Disk)

The Poincaré disk model of hyperbolic geometry:

$$ds^2 = \frac{4(dx^2 + dy^2)}{(1 - x^2 - y^2)^2}$$

This metric makes the unit disk into a model of hyperbolic geometry with constant negative curvature.

### Example 4: Surface of Revolution

For a surface obtained by rotating $y = f(x)$ around the $x$-axis:

$$ds^2 = (1 + f'(x)^2)\,dx^2 + f(x)^2\,d\theta^2$$

## Induced Metric (First Fundamental Form)

When a surface is embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$, the metric is **induced** from the ambient Euclidean metric.

If $\vec{r}(u,v)$ parametrizes the surface, then:

$$g_{ij} = \frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial u^i} \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial u^j}$$

Explicitly:
- $E = g_{11} = \vec{r}_u \cdot \vec{r}_u$
- $F = g_{12} = \vec{r}_u \cdot \vec{r}_v$
- $G = g_{22} = \vec{r}_v \cdot \vec{r}_v$

The first fundamental form:
$$I = E\,du^2 + 2F\,du\,dv + G\,dv^2$$

## Measuring Lengths and Angles

### Length of a Curve

For a curve $\gamma: [a,b] \to M$, its length is:

$$L(\gamma) = \int_a^b \sqrt{g_{\gamma(t)}(\gamma'(t), \gamma'(t))}\,dt = \int_a^b \|\gamma'(t)\|_g\,dt$$

In coordinates, if $\gamma(t) = (x^1(t), ..., x^n(t))$:

$$L(\gamma) = \int_a^b \sqrt{g_{ij}\frac{dx^i}{dt}\frac{dx^j}{dt}}\,dt$$

### Angle Between Vectors

For tangent vectors $X, Y \in T_p M$:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{g_p(X,Y)}{\|X\|_g \|Y\|_g} = \frac{g_p(X,Y)}{\sqrt{g_p(X,X)}\sqrt{g_p(Y,Y)}}$$

### Area Element

On a 2-dimensional Riemannian manifold:

$$dA = \sqrt{\det(g_{ij})}\,dx^1 dx^2 = \sqrt{EG - F^2}\,du\,dv$$

---

## Change of Coordinates

Under a coordinate change $x \to y$, the metric transforms as:

$$g'_{kl} = \frac{\partial x^i}{\partial y^k}\frac{\partial x^j}{\partial y^l}g_{ij}$$

This ensures the metric is well-defined independent of coordinates (it's a tensor!).

---

## Conformal Metrics

Two metrics $g$ and $\tilde{g}$ are **conformally equivalent** if:

$$\tilde{g} = e^{2f}g$$

for some smooth function $f: M \to \mathbb{R}$.

Conformal metrics preserve angles but not lengths.

**Example**: The Poincaré disk metric is conformal to the Euclidean metric.

---

## Pullback Metric

If $f: M \to N$ is a smooth map and $(N,h)$ is Riemannian, the **pullback metric** on $M$ is:

$$(f^*h)_p(X,Y) = h_{f(p)}(df_p(X), df_p(Y))$$

This makes $f$ an isometry onto its image when $f$ is an embedding.

---

## Properties

### Non-degeneracy

Since $g$ is positive definite, $\det(g_{ij}) > 0$ everywhere. This ensures we can define the inverse metric $g^{ij}$.

### Inverse Metric

The inverse matrix $[g^{ij}] = [g_{ij}]^{-1}$ satisfies:

$$g^{ik}g_{kj} = \delta^i_j$$

The inverse metric raises indices: $X^i = g^{ij}X_j$

### Volume Form

The Riemannian volume form is:

$$dV_g = \sqrt{\det(g_{ij})}\,dx^1 \wedge ... \wedge dx^n$$

Total volume: $\text{Vol}(M) = \int_M dV_g$

---

## Isometries

A diffeomorphism $\phi: (M,g) \to (N,h)$ is an **isometry** if:

$$\phi^*h = g$$

Isometries preserve all geometric quantities: lengths, angles, areas, curvature.

**Examples**:
- Rotations and translations in $\mathbb{R}^n$
- Antipodal map on $S^n$

---

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Metric with Distance Function
> The metric tensor $g$ is NOT the distance function $d(p,q)$.  
> The distance is derived from the metric via:  
> $d(p,q) = \inf\{L(\gamma) : \gamma \text{ connects } p \text{ to } q\}$

> [!warning] Forgetting Coordinate Dependence
> The components $g_{ij}$ depend on coordinates, but the metric itself is coordinate-independent. Always distinguish between the tensor and its components!

> [!warning] Assuming Flat Metric
> Not all manifolds admit a flat (Euclidean) metric. Curvature is an intrinsic property!
