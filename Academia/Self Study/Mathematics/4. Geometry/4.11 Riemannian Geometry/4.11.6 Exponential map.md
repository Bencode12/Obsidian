## Definition

The **exponential map** at a point $p \in M$ is:

$$\exp_p: T_p M \to M$$

defined by $\exp_p(v) = \gamma_v(1)$, where $\gamma_v$ is the unique geodesic with:
- $\gamma_v(0) = p$
- $\dot{\gamma}_v(0) = v$

In other words, follow the geodesic starting at $p$ with initial velocity $v$ for unit time.

> [!example] Why the Exponential Map Matters
> The exponential map provides a bridge between the linear world (tangent space) and the curved world (manifold). It's fundamental to:  
> - Normal coordinates  
> - Tubular neighborhoods  
> - Injectivity radius  
> - Comparison geometry

---

## Properties

### Basic Properties

1. **$\exp_p(0) = p$** (zero vector maps to base point)

2. **Smoothness**: $\exp_p$ is smooth on its domain

3. **Differential at zero**: $d(\exp_p)_0 = \text{id}_{T_p M}$

   By the inverse function theorem, $\exp_p$ is a local diffeomorphism near 0.

### Domain

The exponential map may not be defined on all of $T_p M$ if geodesics escape to infinity in finite time.

**Definition**: $M$ is **geodesically complete** if $\exp_p$ is defined on all of $T_p M$ for all $p$.

By the Hopf-Rinow theorem, this is equivalent to completeness as a metric space.

## Normal Coordinates

### Construction

Choose an orthonormal basis $\{e_1, ..., e_n\}$ for $T_p M$. For $q$ near $p$, write:

$$q = \exp_p(x^i e_i)$$

The coordinates $(x^1, ..., x^n)$ are **normal coordinates** centered at $p$.

### Properties

In normal coordinates:

1. **Metric at center**: $g_{ij}(p) = \delta_{ij}$

2. **Christoffel symbols vanish**: $\Gamma^k_{ij}(p) = 0$

3. **Geodesics through $p$ are straight lines**: $\gamma(t) = (tv^1, ..., tv^n)$

4. **Radial geodesics are orthogonal to spheres**: Gauss's lemma

```desmos-graph
x^2 + y^2 = 1
y = x
y = -x
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Normal coordinates "flatten out" the manifold at $p$. Near $p$, it looks Euclidean!

---

## Gauss's Lemma

The exponential map preserves orthogonality to radial directions.

**Statement**: For $v \in T_p M$ and $w \perp v$:

$$\langle d(\exp_p)_v(v), d(\exp_p)_v(w)\rangle = 0$$

### Consequences

- Geodesic spheres are orthogonal to radial geodesics
- Distance from $p$ increases along radial geodesics
- Normal coordinates are "adapted" to the geometry

## Injectivity Radius

### Definition

The **injectivity radius** at $p$ is:

$$\text{inj}(p) = \sup\{r > 0 : \exp_p|_{B_r(0)} \text{ is a diffeomorphism}\}$$

The **global injectivity radius** is:

$$\text{inj}(M) = \inf_{p \in M}\text{inj}(p)$$

### Interpretation

- Within the injectivity radius, $\exp_p$ is one-to-one
- Beyond it, geodesics may intersect (conjugate points or cut locus)

**Examples**:
- $\mathbb{R}^n$: $\text{inj} = \infty$
- $S^n$ (radius $R$): $\text{inj} = \pi R$
- Flat torus: $\text{inj} = \frac{1}{2}$(shortest closed geodesic)

---

## Cut Locus

The **cut locus** of $p$ is the set of points where geodesics from $p$ cease to minimize distance.

### Properties

- Closed set of measure zero
- Outside the cut locus, there's a unique minimizing geodesic
- On the cut locus, either:
  - Multiple minimizing geodesics meet, OR
  - A conjugate point occurs

**Example**: On $S^2$, the cut locus of any point is its antipodal point.

## Metric in Normal Coordinates

Near $p$, the metric has the expansion:

$$g_{ij}(x) = \delta_{ij} - \frac{1}{3}R_{ikjl}x^k x^l + O(|x|^3)$$

where $R_{ikjl}$ is the Riemann curvature tensor at $p$.

### Volume Element

The volume form in normal coordinates:

$$dV = \left(1 - \frac{1}{6}\text{Ric}_{ij}x^i x^j + O(|x|^3)\right)dx^1 ... dx^n$$

Curvature affects volume growth!

## Applications

### Tubular Neighborhoods

For a submanifold $N \subset M$, the exponential map of the normal bundle gives a tubular neighborhood.

### Geodesic Convexity

A set is **geodesically convex** if any two points are connected by a unique minimizing geodesic lying in the set.

Small balls are geodesically convex.

### Comparison Geometry

Compare volumes, distances, and angles with model spaces using the exponential map.

### Numerical Methods

On matrix manifolds (e.g., $SO(n)$, $SPD(n)$), the exponential map is used for optimization.

## Computing the Exponential Map

### Example 1: Euclidean Space

$$\exp_p(v) = p + v$$

Just translation!

### Example 2: Sphere $S^n \subset \mathbb{R}^{n+1}$

For $p \in S^n$ and $v \in T_p S^n$ with $\|v\| = r$:

$$\exp_p(v) = \cos(r)p + \frac{\sin(r)}{r}v$$

Geodesics are great circles.

### Example 3: Hyperbolic Space

In the hyperboloid model, similar formula with hyperbolic functions:

$$\exp_p(v) = \cosh(r)p + \frac{\sinh(r)}{r}v$$

### Example 4: Lie Groups

For a Lie group with bi-invariant metric:

$$\exp_e(X) = \text{matrix exponential } e^X$$

The Riemannian exponential coincides with the Lie group exponential!

## Relation to Other Concepts

### Geodesics

Radial lines in $T_p M$ map to geodesics in $M$.

### Parallel Transport

Parallel transport along radial geodesics is simple in normal coordinates.

### Curvature

The deviation of $\exp_p$ from an isometry measures curvature.

### Jacobi Fields

Jacobi fields describe the differential $d(\exp_p)$.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Not a Global Diffeomorphism
> $\exp_p$ is only a local diffeomorphism. It's generally not injective or surjective globally.

> [!warning] Confusing with Lie Group Exponential
> For general Riemannian manifolds, the exponential map is NOT the same as the Lie group exponential (though they coincide for Lie groups with bi-invariant metrics).

> [!warning] Assuming Linearity
> $\exp_p(v+w) \neq \exp_p(v) + \exp_p(w)$ in general. The exponential map is highly nonlinear.

> [!warning] Forgetting Domain Restrictions
> $\exp_p$ may not be defined for large vectors if geodesics escape to infinity.
