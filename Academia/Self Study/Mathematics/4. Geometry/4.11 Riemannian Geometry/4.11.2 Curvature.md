## Overview

**Curvature** measures how much a Riemannian manifold deviates from being flat (Euclidean). There are several types of curvature, each capturing different aspects of the geometry.

---

## Gaussian Curvature (Surfaces)

For a 2-dimensional surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$, the **Gaussian curvature** at a point is:

$$K = \kappa_1 \kappa_2$$

where $\kappa_1$ and $\kappa_2$ are the principal curvatures (maximum and minimum normal curvatures).

### Interpretation

|Curvature|Shape|Example|
|---|---|---|
|$K > 0$|Elliptic (locally convex)|Sphere, ellipsoid|
|$K = 0$|Parabolic (flat in one direction)|Cylinder, cone, plane|
|$K < 0$|Hyperbolic (saddle-shaped)|Hyperboloid, pseudosphere|

```desmos-graph
y = x^2
y = -x^2
```

> [!example] Visual Understanding
> Positive curvature: surface curves the same way in all directions (like a sphere)  
> Negative curvature: surface curves up in one direction, down in another (like a saddle)

### Theorema Egregium (Gauss, 1827)

**Gaussian curvature is intrinsic**! It can be computed from the metric alone, without reference to the embedding.

Formula in local coordinates:

$$K = \frac{1}{\sqrt{EG-F^2}}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial u}\left(\frac{F_u - E_v}{2\sqrt{EG-F^2}}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial v}\left(\frac{F_v - G_u}{2\sqrt{EG-F^2}}\right)\right]$$

This remarkable theorem shows that curvature is an intrinsic property of the surface!

---

## Sectional Curvature (Higher Dimensions)

For an $n$-dimensional Riemannian manifold, **sectional curvature** generalizes Gaussian curvature.

Given a point $p \in M$ and a 2-plane $\sigma \subset T_p M$, the sectional curvature $K(\sigma)$ is the Gaussian curvature of the surface obtained by exponentiating $\sigma$.

### Properties

- Determines the full curvature tensor
- $K > 0$: manifold is "sphere-like"
- $K = 0$: manifold is flat (locally Euclidean)
- $K < 0$: manifold is "hyperbolic"

**Examples**:
- Sphere $S^n$: $K = 1/R^2$ (constant positive)
- Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^n$: $K = 0$ (flat)
- Hyperbolic space $\mathbb{H}^n$: $K = -1$ (constant negative)

---

## Ricci Curvature

The **Ricci curvature** is a symmetric $(0,2)$-tensor obtained by tracing the Riemann curvature tensor.

In coordinates:

$$\text{Ric}_{ij} = R^k_{ikj}$$

where $R^l_{ijk}$ is the Riemann curvature tensor.

### Interpretation

Ricci curvature measures how volumes deform compared to Euclidean space.

- $\text{Ric} > 0$: volumes grow slower than in Euclidean space
- $\text{Ric} = 0$: volume growth is Euclidean
- $\text{Ric} < 0$: volumes grow faster than in Euclidean space

### Ricci Scalar

The **scalar curvature** is the trace of Ricci curvature:

$$R = g^{ij}\text{Ric}_{ij}$$

This is a single number measuring curvature at each point.

---

## Riemann Curvature Tensor

The full **Riemann curvature tensor** is a $(1,3)$-tensor defined by:

$$R(X,Y)Z = 
abla_X
abla_Y Z - 
abla_Y
abla_X Z - 
abla_{[X,Y]}Z$$

In coordinates:

$$R^l_{ijk} = \frac{\partial \Gamma^l_{jk}}{\partial x^i} - \frac{\partial \Gamma^l_{ik}}{\partial x^j} + \Gamma^m_{jk}\Gamma^l_{im} - \Gamma^m_{ik}\Gamma^l_{jm}$$

where $\Gamma^l_{jk}$ are the Christoffel symbols.

### Symmetries

The Riemann tensor has important symmetries:
1. $R_{ijkl} = -R_{jikl}$ (antisymmetric in first pair)
2. $R_{ijkl} = -R_{ijlk}$ (antisymmetric in second pair)
3. $R_{ijkl} = R_{klij}$ (symmetric under exchange of pairs)
4. First Bianchi identity: $R_{ijkl} + R_{iklj} + R_{iljk} = 0$

These symmetries reduce the number of independent components from $n^4$ to $\frac{n^2(n^2-1)}{12}$.

## Computing Curvature

### For Surfaces in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Using the first and second fundamental forms:

$$K = \frac{LN - M^2}{EG - F^2}$$

where:
- $E, F, G$ are coefficients of the first fundamental form (metric)
- $L, M, N$ are coefficients of the second fundamental form

### Example: Sphere of Radius $R$

Parametrization: $\vec{r}(\theta,\phi) = (R\sin\theta\cos\phi, R\sin\theta\sin\phi, R\cos\theta)$

Metric: $ds^2 = R^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2)$

Gaussian curvature: $K = \frac{1}{R^2}$ (constant!)

### Example: Cylinder

Parametrization: $\vec{r}(z,\theta) = (R\cos\theta, R\sin\theta, z)$

Metric: $ds^2 = dz^2 + R^2 d\theta^2$

Gaussian curvature: $K = 0$ (developable surface!)

> [!example] Key Insight
> A cylinder is locally isometric to a plane! You can unroll it without stretching. This is why $K = 0$.

## Curvature and Topology

### Gauss-Bonnet Theorem

For a compact oriented surface $M$ without boundary:

$$\int_M K\,dA = 2\pi\chi(M)$$

where $\chi(M)$ is the Euler characteristic.

**Examples**:
- Sphere: $\chi(S^2) = 2$, so $\int_{S^2} K\,dA = 4\pi$
- Torus: $\chi(T^2) = 0$, so $\int_{T^2} K\,dA = 0$

This connects local geometry (curvature) to global topology!

### Myers' Theorem

If $\text{Ric} \geq (n-1)k > 0$, then $M$ is compact with finite fundamental group.

Positive Ricci curvature forces compactness!

## Constant Curvature Spaces

Manifolds with constant sectional curvature are completely classified:

|Curvature|Simply Connected Model|Geometry|
|---|---|---|
|$K > 0$|Sphere $S^n$|Spherical/Elliptic|
|$K = 0$|Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^n$|Euclidean/Flat|
|$K < 0$|Hyperbolic space $\mathbb{H}^n$|Hyperbolic|

Any complete simply connected manifold with constant curvature is isometric to one of these!

## Applications

### General Relativity

Einstein's field equations relate curvature to matter-energy:

$$\text{Ric}_{ij} - \frac{1}{2}Rg_{ij} + \Lambda g_{ij} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T_{ij}$$

Spacetime curvature IS gravity!

### Shape Analysis

Curvature helps classify shapes in computer vision and medical imaging.

### Geometric Flows

Ricci flow: $\frac{\partial g}{\partial t} = -2\text{Ric}$

Used to prove the Poincaré Conjecture (Perelman, 2003)!

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Different Curvatures
> Gaussian curvature (surfaces) ≠ Ricci curvature (trace) ≠ Scalar curvature (double trace) ≠ Riemann tensor (full curvature)  
> Each captures different information!

> [!warning] Extrinsic vs. Intrinsic
> Gaussian curvature is intrinsic (Theorema Egregium), but mean curvature is extrinsic (depends on embedding). Don't confuse them!

> [!warning] Sign Conventions
> Different authors use different sign conventions for curvature tensors. Always check the convention being used!
