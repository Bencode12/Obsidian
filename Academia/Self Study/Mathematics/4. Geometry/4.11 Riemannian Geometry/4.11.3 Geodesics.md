## Definition

A **geodesic** is a curve that locally minimizes distance. More precisely, it's a curve $\gamma: I \to M$ whose acceleration vanishes:

$$\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}\dot{\gamma} = 0$$

where $\nabla$ is the Levi-Civita connection.

In other words, geodesics are the "straightest possible" curves on a curved manifold.

> [!example] Why Geodesics Matter
> Geodesics generalize straight lines to curved spaces:  
> - Shortest paths between points  
> - Trajectories of free particles (no forces)  
> - Light rays in general relativity  
> - Fundamental to understanding geometry

---

## Geodesic Equations

In local coordinates $(x^1, ..., x^n)$, if $\gamma(t) = (x^1(t), ..., x^n(t))$, the geodesic equation is:

$$\frac{d^2x^k}{dt^2} + \Gamma^k_{ij}\frac{dx^i}{dt}\frac{dx^j}{dt} = 0$$

for $k = 1, ..., n$, where $\Gamma^k_{ij}$ are the Christoffel symbols.

This is a system of $n$ second-order ODEs!

### Alternative Form

Using the metric directly:

$$\frac{d^2x^k}{dt^2} + \frac{1}{2}g^{kl}\left(\frac{\partial g_{lj}}{\partial x^i} + \frac{\partial g_{li}}{\partial x^j} - \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial x^l}\right)\frac{dx^i}{dt}\frac{dx^j}{dt} = 0$$

## Examples

### Example 1: Euclidean Space $\mathbb{R}^n$

Christoffel symbols: $\Gamma^k_{ij} = 0$ (flat space)

Geodesic equation: $\frac{d^2x^k}{dt^2} = 0$

Solution: $x^k(t) = a^k t + b^k$ (straight lines!)

```desmos-graph
y = 2x + 1
y = -x + 3
```

### Example 2: Sphere $S^2$

Great circles are geodesics on the sphere.

For the unit sphere with metric $ds^2 = d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2$:

The geodesic equations are:
$$\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} - \sin\theta\cos\theta\left(\frac{d\phi}{dt}\right)^2 = 0$$
$$\frac{d^2\phi}{dt^2} + 2\cot\theta\frac{d\theta}{dt}\frac{d\phi}{dt} = 0$$

Solutions are great circles (intersections of the sphere with planes through the origin).

### Example 3: Cylinder

Geodesics on a cylinder are:
- Vertical lines (generators)
- Horizontal circles (parallels)
- Helices (spirals)

When you unroll the cylinder to a plane, geodesics become straight lines!

## Properties of Geodesics

### Local Distance Minimization

Geodesics minimize distance **locally** (but not necessarily globally).

**Example**: On a sphere, there are two geodesics between antipodal points. The shorter one minimizes distance; the longer one doesn't.

### Constant Speed

If $\gamma$ is a geodesic, then $\|\dot{\gamma}(t)\|_g$ is constant.

We usually parametrize geodesics by **arc length** (unit speed): $\|\dot{\gamma}\|_g = 1$.

### Existence and Uniqueness

Given $p \in M$ and $v \in T_p M$, there exists a unique geodesic $\gamma$ with:
- $\gamma(0) = p$
- $\dot{\gamma}(0) = v$

This follows from the theory of ODEs!

### Geodesic Completeness

A Riemannian manifold is **geodesically complete** if every geodesic can be extended for all time ($t \in \mathbb{R}$).

**Hopf-Rinow Theorem**: For connected Riemannian manifolds, the following are equivalent:
1. Geodesically complete
2. Complete as a metric space
3. Closed bounded sets are compact

## Variational Characterization

Geodesics are **critical points** of the energy functional:

$$E(\gamma) = \frac{1}{2}\int_a^b \|\dot{\gamma}(t)\|^2\,dt$$

Alternatively, they minimize the length functional:

$$L(\gamma) = \int_a^b \|\dot{\gamma}(t)\|\,dt$$

> [!tip] Key Insight
> This variational approach connects geodesics to calculus of variations and physics (principle of least action)!

---

## Exponential Map

The **exponential map** at $p \in M$ is:

$$\exp_p: T_p M \to M$$

defined by $\exp_p(v) = \gamma_v(1)$, where $\gamma_v$ is the geodesic with $\gamma_v(0) = p$ and $\dot{\gamma}_v(0) = v$.

### Properties

- $\exp_p(0) = p$
- $d(\exp_p)_0 = \text{id}$ (identity on $T_p M$)
- For small $v$, $\exp_p$ is a diffeomorphism onto its image

The exponential map gives **normal coordinates** near $p$.

---

## Jacobi Fields

**Jacobi fields** describe how geodesics vary in families.

If $\gamma_s(t)$ is a family of geodesics, then $J(t) = \frac{\partial \gamma_s}{\partial s}|_{s=0}$ is a Jacobi field.

### Jacobi Equation

$$\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}J + R(J, \dot{\gamma})\dot{\gamma} = 0$$

where $R$ is the Riemann curvature tensor.

Jacobi fields measure the effect of curvature on geodesics!

---

## Conjugate Points

Two points $p = \gamma(a)$ and $q = \gamma(b)$ on a geodesic are **conjugate** if there exists a non-zero Jacobi field vanishing at both points.

### Significance

- Before the first conjugate point, the geodesic minimizes distance
- After a conjugate point, the geodesic is no longer minimizing
- Conjugate points are related to focusing of geodesics by curvature

**Example**: On $S^2$, antipodal points are conjugate along any great circle.

---

## Applications

### General Relativity

Geodesics in spacetime are the trajectories of free-falling particles and light rays.

The geodesic equation becomes:
$$\frac{d^2x^\mu}{d\tau^2} + \Gamma^\mu_{\alpha\beta}\frac{dx^\alpha}{d\tau}\frac{dx^\beta}{d\tau} = 0$$

### Optimal Control

Finding shortest paths on manifolds appears in robotics, computer vision, and machine learning.

### Cartography

Geodesics on the Earth's surface are important for navigation (great circle routes).

## Computing Geodesics

### Method 1: Direct Integration

Solve the geodesic ODEs numerically or analytically.

### Method 2: Symmetry

Use symmetries (Killing vector fields) to find conserved quantities and reduce the problem.

**Example**: On surfaces of revolution, Clairaut's relation:
$$r\sin\alpha = \text{constant}$$

where $r$ is the distance from the axis and $\alpha$ is the angle with meridians.

### Method 3: Variational Methods

Minimize the energy functional using calculus of variations.

---

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Geodesics Don't Always Minimize Globally
> Geodesics only minimize distance locally! On a sphere, going the "long way" around is still a geodesic but doesn't minimize distance.

> [!warning] Not All Straight-Looking Curves Are Geodesics
> A curve might look straight in one coordinate system but not be a geodesic. Check the geodesic equation!

> [!warning] Parametrization Matters
> Only constant-speed parametrizations of geodesics satisfy $\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}\dot{\gamma} = 0$. Arbitrary reparametrizations don't!
