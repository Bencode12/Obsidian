## Definition

A **Jacobi field** is a vector field $J$ along a geodesic $\gamma$ that satisfies the **Jacobi equation**:

$$\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}J + R(J, \dot{\gamma})\dot{\gamma} = 0$$

where $R$ is the Riemann curvature tensor.

Jacobi fields describe how geodesics vary in one-parameter families.

> [!example] Why Jacobi Fields Matter
> Jacobi fields measure the effect of curvature on geodesics:  
> - Conjugate points (where geodesics focus)  
> - Stability of geodesics  
> - Volume comparison  
> - Index form and Morse theory

---

## Geometric Interpretation

If $\gamma_s(t)$ is a family of geodesics, then:

$$J(t) = \left.\frac{\partial \gamma_s}{\partial s}\right|_{s=0}$$

is a Jacobi field along $\gamma_0$.

```desmos-graph
y = x^2
y = x^2 + 0.1
y = x^2 - 0.1
```

> [!example] Visual Understanding
> Jacobi fields tell us whether nearby geodesics converge or diverge. Positive curvature makes them converge; negative curvature makes them diverge.

## The Jacobi Equation

### In Detail

Let $D_t = \nabla_{\dot{\gamma}}$ be covariant differentiation along $\gamma$. The Jacobi equation is:

$$D_t^2 J + R(J, \dot{\gamma})\dot{\gamma} = 0$$

This is a second-order linear ODE for $J$.

### Initial Conditions

A Jacobi field is uniquely determined by:
- $J(0) \in T_{\gamma(0)}M$ (initial position)
- $D_t J(0) \in T_{\gamma(0)}M$ (initial velocity)

### Dimension

The space of Jacobi fields along $\gamma$ has dimension $2n$ (where $n = \dim M$).

---

## Special Types

### Tangential Jacobi Fields

Jacobi fields of the form:

$$J(t) = (at + b)\dot{\gamma}(t)$$

These correspond to reparametrizing the geodesic or shifting the starting point.

### Normal Jacobi Fields

Jacobi fields orthogonal to $\dot{\gamma}$:

$$\langle J(t), \dot{\gamma}(t)\rangle = 0$$

These capture the "interesting" geometry (curvature effects).

Any Jacobi field splits as:
$$J = J^\top + J^\perp$$

---

## Examples

### Example 1: Euclidean Space $\mathbb{R}^n$

Curvature: $R = 0$

Jacobi equation: $D_t^2 J = 0$

Solution: $J(t) = At + B$ (linear functions)

Geodesics are parallel lines—they neither converge nor diverge.

### Example 2: Sphere $S^n$ (radius $R$)

Sectional curvature: $K = 1/R^2$

For a unit speed geodesic $\gamma$, normal Jacobi fields:

$$J(t) = \sin\left(\frac{t}{R}\right)E(t)$$

where $E(t)$ is parallel along $\gamma$.

**Key observation**: $J(\pi R) = 0$ (vanishes at antipodal point!)

This shows geodesics focus at conjugate points.

### Example 3: Hyperbolic Space $\mathbb{H}^n$

Sectional curvature: $K = -1$

Normal Jacobi fields:

$$J(t) = \sinh(t)E(t)$$

Geodesics diverge exponentially!

## Conjugate Points

### Definition

Points $\gamma(a)$ and $\gamma(b)$ are **conjugate** along $\gamma$ if there exists a non-zero Jacobi field $J$ with:

$$J(a) = J(b) = 0$$

### Interpretation

- Conjugate points are where nearby geodesics intersect
- They mark where geodesics cease to minimize distance
- Related to focusing by curvature

### Examples

- **Sphere**: Antipodal points are conjugate (distance $\pi R$)
- **Euclidean space**: No conjugate points
- **Hyperbolic space**: No conjugate points

---

## Index Form

The **index form** measures the second variation of energy:

$$I(V,W) = \int_a^b \left(\langle D_t V, D_t W\rangle - \langle R(V,\dot{\gamma})\dot{\gamma}, W\rangle\right)dt$$

for vector fields $V, W$ along $\gamma$ vanishing at endpoints.

### Properties

- Symmetric bilinear form
- $I(J,J) = 0$ for Jacobi fields $J$ vanishing at endpoints
- Negative index counts conjugate points (Morse Index Theorem)

---

## Rauch Comparison Theorem

Compares Jacobi fields on manifolds with different curvatures.

**Statement**: If $M$ has sectional curvature $K_M \leq K_N$ (compared to model space $N$), then Jacobi fields on $M$ grow faster than on $N$.

### Consequences

- Positive curvature → geodesics converge
- Negative curvature → geodesics diverge
- Bounds on injectivity radius

## Computing Jacobi Fields

### Method 1: Direct Solution

Solve the Jacobi ODE with given initial conditions.

### Method 2: Variation of Geodesics

Construct a family of geodesics and differentiate.

### Method 3: Parallel Frame

Choose a parallel orthonormal frame $\{E_i(t)\}$ along $\gamma$ and write:

$$J(t) = \sum f_i(t)E_i(t)$$

The Jacobi equation becomes a system of scalar ODEs for $f_i(t)$.

## Applications

### Morse Theory

Jacobi fields determine the index of geodesics as critical points of the energy functional.

### Volume Comparison

The volume of geodesic balls is controlled by Jacobi fields:

$$\text{Vol}(B_r(p)) = \int_{S^{n-1}}\int_0^r \det(d\exp_p)_{tv}\,dt\,dv$$

### Stability Analysis

Jacobi fields determine whether geodesics are stable under perturbations.

### General Relativity

Jacobi fields describe tidal forces (geodesic deviation equation):

$$\frac{D^2\xi^\alpha}{d\tau^2} = -R^\alpha_{\beta\gamma\delta}u^\beta\xi^\gamma u^\delta$$

## Relation to Curvature

### Riccati Equation

If $J$ is a normal Jacobi field, define $S = D_t J \cdot J^{-1}$ (when invertible). Then:

$$D_t S + S^2 + R_\gamma = 0$$

where $R_\gamma(\cdot) = R(\cdot, \dot{\gamma})\dot{\gamma}$.

This Riccati equation relates Jacobi fields directly to curvature.

### Laplacian Comparison

The Laplacian of the distance function $r = d(p,\cdot)$ is:

$$\Delta r = \frac{d}{dr}\log\sqrt{\det(g_{ij})}$$

This is computed using Jacobi fields!

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing with Parallel Fields
> Jacobi fields are NOT parallel ($D_t J \neq 0$ in general). They satisfy a second-order equation, not first-order.

> [!warning] Forgetting Tangential Components
> Not all Jacobi fields are normal to the geodesic. Tangential components correspond to trivial variations.

> [!warning] Assuming Global Existence
> Jacobi fields may blow up at conjugate points. Be careful about the domain.

> [!warning] Sign Conventions
> Different authors use different sign conventions for the curvature tensor. This affects the Jacobi equation sign!
