## Overview

**Comparison theorems** relate geometric quantities on a Riemannian manifold to those on model spaces with constant curvature.

They allow us to deduce global properties from curvature bounds.

> [!example] Why Comparison Theorems Matter
> Comparison geometry lets us:  
> - Bound distances, volumes, and angles  
> - Prove topological results from curvature assumptions  
> - Understand how curvature controls geometry  
> - Establish rigidity theorems

---

## Model Spaces

The three standard model spaces with constant sectional curvature $K$:

|Curvature|Space|Notation|Geometry|
|---|---|---|---|
|$K > 0$|Sphere (radius $1/\sqrt{K}$)|$S^n_K$|Spherical|
|$K = 0$|Euclidean space|$\mathbb{R}^n$|Flat|
|$K < 0$|Hyperbolic space|$\mathbb{H}^n_K$|Hyperbolic|

We compare our manifold $M$ to these models.

---

## Rauch Comparison Theorem

### Statement

Let $\gamma$ be a geodesic in $M$, and $\tilde{\gamma}$ a geodesic in a model space $\tilde{M}$ with constant curvature $K$.

If sectional curvatures satisfy $K_M \leq K$ (or $K_M \geq K$), then Jacobi fields on $M$ grow faster (or slower) than on $\tilde{M}$.

### Precise Form

Let $J$ and $\tilde{J}$ be normal Jacobi fields with same initial conditions. If $K_M \leq K$:

$$\|J(t)\| \geq \|\tilde{J}(t)\|$$

for $t$ before the first conjugate point.

### Interpretation

- **Lower curvature bound** → geodesics diverge more
- **Upper curvature bound** → geodesics converge more

---

## Toponogov Comparison Theorem

### Triangle Comparison

Compare geodesic triangles in $M$ with triangles in model space.

**Statement**: If $K_M \geq K$, then triangles in $M$ are "thinner" than comparison triangles in $S^n_K$.

Specifically, if $\triangle pqr$ in $M$ has side lengths $a,b,c$, and $\triangle \tilde{p}\tilde{q}\tilde{r}$ in $S^n_K$ has same side lengths, then:

$$\angle p \geq \angle \tilde{p}$$

```desmos-graph
x^2 + y^2 = 1
x = 0
y = 0
```

> [!example] Visual Understanding
> Positive curvature makes triangles "fatter" (angles sum > π). Negative curvature makes them "skinnier" (angles sum < π).

### Consequences

- Bounds on angles in terms of side lengths
- Convexity of distance functions
- Structure of cut locus

## Bishop-Gromov Volume Comparison

### Statement

If $\text{Ric}_M \geq (n-1)K$, then the volume ratio:

$$\frac{\text{Vol}(B_r(p))}{\text{Vol}_K(B_r)}$$

is non-increasing in $r$, where $\text{Vol}_K(B_r)$ is the volume of a ball of radius $r$ in the model space with curvature $K$.

### Special Cases

**Ricci curvature bounded below**:
$$\text{Vol}(B_r(p)) \leq \text{Vol}_K(B_r)$$

**Example**: If $\text{Ric} \geq 0$, then:
$$\text{Vol}(B_r(p)) \leq \omega_n r^n$$

where $\omega_n$ is the volume of the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

### Applications

- Bounds on total volume of compact manifolds
- Finiteness theorems
- Convergence theory

## Myers' Theorem

### Statement

If $\text{Ric}_M \geq (n-1)k > 0$, then:

1. $M$ is **compact**
2. $\text{diam}(M) \leq \pi/\sqrt{k}$
3. $\pi_1(M)$ is **finite**

### Proof Idea

Positive Ricci curvature forces geodesics to converge, creating conjugate points within distance $\pi/\sqrt{k}$. By Hopf-Rinow, this implies compactness.

### Examples

- Sphere $S^n$: $\text{Ric} = (n-1)$, diameter = $\pi$ ✓
- Projective space $\mathbb{RP}^n$: Same curvature, diameter = $\pi/2$ ✓

---

## Bonnet-Myers Theorem

A refinement using sectional curvature.

**Statement**: If $K_M \geq k > 0$, then:
$$\text{diam}(M) \leq \frac{\pi}{\sqrt{k}}$$

This is stronger than Myers' theorem (sectional ≥ Ricci).

---

## Synge's Theorem

### Statement

If $M$ is compact, oriented, even-dimensional, and has positive sectional curvature, then $M$ is **simply connected**.

### Corollary

$\mathbb{RP}^{2n}$ cannot have a metric with $K > 0$ (it's not simply connected).

---

## Sphere Theorem (Quarter-Pinched Sphere Theorem)

### Statement

If $M$ is complete, simply connected, and sectional curvatures satisfy:

$$\frac{1}{4} < \frac{K_{\min}}{K_{\max}} \leq 1$$

then $M$ is homeomorphic to $S^n$.

### History

Proven by Berger and Klingenberg (1960s). The constant $1/4$ is sharp!

**Counterexample**: Complex projective space $\mathbb{CP}^n$ has $1/4$-pinched curvature but is not a sphere.

---

## Cheeger-Gromoll Splitting Theorem

### Statement

If $M$ is complete with $\text{Ric} \geq 0$ and contains a line (geodesic minimizing between all pairs of points), then:

$$M \cong N \times \mathbb{R}$$

isometrically.

### Interpretation

Nonnegative Ricci curvature + a line → manifold splits off a Euclidean factor.

---

## Gromov's Compactness Theorem

### Statement

The class of compact $n$-manifolds with:
- $|\text{Sec}| \leq K$
- $\text{Vol} \geq v > 0$
- $\text{diam} \leq D$

is **precompact** in the Gromov-Hausdorff topology.

Only finitely many homotopy types in this class!

---

## Applications

### Rigidity Theorems

If equality holds in a comparison theorem, the manifold is isometric to the model.

**Example**: If $\text{Ric} \geq n-1$ and $\text{Vol}(M) = \text{Vol}(S^n)$, then $M \cong S^n$.

### Convergence Theory

Sequences of manifolds with uniform curvature bounds converge to limit spaces (possibly singular).

### Topology

Curvature bounds restrict possible topologies:
- Positive curvature → finite fundamental group
- Nonnegative curvature → virtually abelian fundamental group

### Physics

Comparison theorems appear in general relativity (singularity theorems, positive mass theorem).

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Sectional and Ricci Curvature
> Sectional curvature bounds are stronger than Ricci bounds. Don't mix them up!

> [!warning] Forgetting Dimension Dependence
> Many theorems depend critically on dimension (e.g., Synge's theorem requires even dimension).

> [!warning] Assuming Sharp Constants
> Not all constants in comparison theorems are sharp. Check the literature!

> [!warning] Global vs. Local
> Some theorems require completeness. Without it, conclusions may fail.
