## Definition

A **cubic curve** is a plane algebraic curve of degree 3, defined by a polynomial equation:

$$f(x,y) = 0, \quad \deg f = 3$$

General form:
$$Ax^3 + Bx^2y + Cxy^2 + Dy^3 + Ex^2 + Fxy + Gy^2 + Hx + Iy + J = 0$$

Cubic curves are the first truly interesting algebraic curves—they can have genus 1 (elliptic curves) and rich geometric structure.

> [!example] Why Cubics Matter
> Cubic curves are fundamental to:  
> - Number theory (elliptic curves, Fermat's Last Theorem)  
> - Cryptography (elliptic curve cryptography)  
> - Group law on curves  
> - Classical geometry (Pascal, Pappus, Chasles theorems)  
> - Physics (pendulum motion, orbital mechanics)

## Classification

### By Genus

|Type|Genus|Singularities|Example|
|---|---|---|---|
|Smooth cubic|1|None|$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$|
|Nodal cubic|0|One node|$y^2 = x^3 + x^2$|
|Cuspidal cubic|0|One cusp|$y^2 = x^3$|

Smooth cubics are **elliptic curves**—they have a group structure!

### Normal Forms

Over $\mathbb{C}$, every smooth cubic can be transformed to **Weierstrass form**:

$$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$$

with discriminant $\Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27b^2) \neq 0$.

## Examples

### Example 1: Elliptic Curve (Smooth Cubic)

Weierstrass form: $y^2 = x^3 - x$

Discriminant: $\Delta = 64 > 0$ (smooth)

This curve has genus 1 and carries a group structure!

```desmos-graph
y^2 = x^3 - x
```

### Example 2: Folium of Descartes

Equation: $x^3 + y^3 = 3axy$

Has a node at the origin. Parametric form:
$$x = \frac{3at}{1+t^3}, \quad y = \frac{3at^2}{1+t^3}$$

### Example 3: Cissoid of Diocles

Equation: $y^2 = \frac{x^3}{2a-x}$

Used historically to solve the Delian problem (doubling the cube).

### Example 4: Semicubical Parabola

Equation: $y^2 = x^3$

Has a cusp at the origin. This is the simplest singular cubic.

```desmos-graph
y^2 = x^3
```

### Example 5: Trident of Newton

Equation: $xy = x^3 + ax^2 + bx + c$

Named after Isaac Newton who classified cubic curves.

## Elliptic Curves

### Definition

An **elliptic curve** is a smooth projective cubic curve with a specified base point.

Over fields of characteristic ≠ 2,3, it has Weierstrass form:
$$E: y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$$

### Group Law

Elliptic curves have a natural **abelian group structure**!

**Geometric construction**:
1. Given points $P, Q$ on $E$, draw the line through them
2. The line intersects $E$ at a third point $R$
3. Reflect $R$ across the x-axis to get $P + Q$

The point at infinity $\mathcal{O}$ serves as the identity element.

```desmos-graph
y = x
y = -x
y^2 = x^3 - x
```

> [!example] Visual Understanding
> The group law comes from the fact that a line intersects a cubic in exactly 3 points (counting multiplicity). This is Bezout's theorem in action!

### Algebraic Formulas

For $P = (x_1, y_1)$ and $Q = (x_2, y_2)$:

If $P \neq Q$:
$$\lambda = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}, \quad x_3 = \lambda^2 - x_1 - x_2, \quad y_3 = \lambda(x_1 - x_3) - y_1$$

If $P = Q$ (doubling):
$$\lambda = \frac{3x_1^2 + a}{2y_1}$$

Then $P + Q = (x_3, -y_3)$.

## Inflection Points

### Definition

A point $P$ on a cubic is an **inflection point** (or flex) if the tangent line at $P$ has contact of order ≥ 3.

Equivalently, the Hessian determinant vanishes at $P$.

### Properties

- A smooth cubic has exactly **9 inflection points** (over $\mathbb{C}$)
- Any line through two inflection points passes through a third
- The inflection points form a configuration related to finite geometry

### Finding Inflection Points

For $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$, the inflection points satisfy:
$$3x^4 + 6ax^2 + 12bx - a^2 = 0$$

---

## Hesse Configuration

The 9 inflection points of a smooth cubic and the 12 lines through them form the **Hesse configuration** $(9_4, 12_3)$:
- 9 points, each on 4 lines
- 12 lines, each through 3 points

This is a beautiful example of combinatorial geometry!

## Newton's Classification

Isaac Newton classified real cubic curves into **72 types** (later refined to 78).

Main categories:
1. **Redundant hyperbolas** (three asymptotes)
2. **Defective hyperbolas** (one or two asymptotes)
3. **Parabolic hyperbolas**
4. **Hyperbolisms**
5. **Divergent parabolas**
6. **Tridents**
7. **Cubic parabolas**

Each type has distinct geometric features.

## Arithmetic of Cubics

### Rational Points

For an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$, the **Mordell-Weil theorem** states:

$$E(\mathbb{Q}) \cong \mathbb{Z}^r \oplus E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}$$

where:
- $r$ is the **rank** (finite but unbounded)
- $E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}$ is the torsion subgroup (classified by Mazur's theorem)

### Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture

Relates the rank $r$ to the behavior of the L-function $L(E,s)$ at $s=1$:

$$\text{ord}_{s=1} L(E,s) = r$$

This is one of the Millennium Prize Problems!

## Applications

### Cryptography

Elliptic curve cryptography (ECC) uses the discrete logarithm problem on elliptic curves:
- Smaller key sizes than RSA
- Equivalent security with less computation
- Used in TLS, Bitcoin, secure messaging

### Physics

- Pendulum period involves elliptic integrals
- Motion in cubic potentials
- String theory and mirror symmetry

### Number Theory

- Fermat's Last Theorem (proved using elliptic curves and modular forms)
- Congruent number problem
- Diophantine equations

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing "Elliptic" with "Ellipse"
> Elliptic curves are NOT ellipses! They're called "elliptic" because they arise from elliptic integrals (arc length of ellipses).

> [!warning] Forgetting the Point at Infinity
> The group law requires the point at infinity as the identity. In projective coordinates, this is essential!

> [!warning] Assuming All Cubics Are Elliptic
> Only SMOOTH cubics are elliptic curves. Singular cubics (nodal, cuspidal) have genus 0 and are rational.

> [!warning] Characteristic Issues
> Weierstrass form $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$ only works in characteristic ≠ 2,3. Other forms are needed in small characteristics.

- Find the singular point of $y^2 = x^3 + x^2$
- Show that $x^3 + y^3 = 1$ is smooth over $\mathbb{C}$

- Compute $P + Q$ for $P = (0,0)$ and $Q = (1,0)$ on $y^2 = x^3 - x$
- Find $2P$ for $P = (2,3)$ on $y^2 = x^3 - 4x + 4$
- Verify that the point at infinity is the identity element

- Explain the geometric meaning of the group law
