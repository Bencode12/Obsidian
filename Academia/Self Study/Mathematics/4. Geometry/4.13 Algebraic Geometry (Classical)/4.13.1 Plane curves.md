## Definition

A **plane algebraic curve** is the set of points $(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ (or $\mathbb{C}^2$) satisfying a polynomial equation:

$$f(x,y) = 0$$

where $f \in \mathbb{R}[x,y]$ (or $\mathbb{C}[x,y]$) is a non-constant polynomial.

The **degree** of the curve is the degree of the polynomial $f$.

> [!example] Why Plane Curves Matter
> Plane curves are the simplest algebraic varieties and provide:  
> - Foundation for algebraic geometry  
> - Connections to number theory (Diophantine equations)  
> - Applications in computer graphics, robotics, cryptography  
> - Beautiful classical geometry (conics, cubics, etc.)

---

## Classification by Degree

|Degree|Name|General Form|Examples|
|---|---|---|---|
|1|Line|$ax + by + c = 0$|Straight lines|
|2|Conic|$ax^2 + bxy + cy^2 + dx + ey + f = 0$|Circle, ellipse, parabola, hyperbola|
|3|Cubic|$f(x,y) = 0$, $\deg f = 3$|Elliptic curves, folium of Descartes|
|4|Quartic|$\deg f = 4$|Lemniscate, Cassini ovals|

## Examples

### Example 1: Lines (Degree 1)

Equation: $ax + by + c = 0$

Parametric form: $(x(t), y(t)) = (x_0 + at, y_0 + bt)$

```desmos-graph
y = 2x + 1
y = -x + 3
```

### Example 2: Circles (Degree 2)

Equation: $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2$

Special case centered at origin: $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$

### Example 3: Parabola (Degree 2)

Standard form: $y = ax^2 + bx + c$

Or: $y^2 = 4px$ (opens right/left)

Focus-directrix property defines the parabola geometrically.

### Example 4: Elliptic Curve (Degree 3)

Weierstrass form: $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$

with discriminant $\Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27b^2) \neq 0$.

These are fundamental in number theory and cryptography!

```desmos-graph
y^2 = x^3 - x
```

### Example 5: Folium of Descartes (Degree 3)

Equation: $x^3 + y^3 = 3axy$

Has a node (self-intersection) at the origin.

## Affine vs. Projective Curves

### Affine Curves

Live in $\mathbb{A}^2$ (affine plane). May have "points at infinity" missing.

### Projective Curves

Live in $\mathbb{P}^2$ (projective plane). Include points at infinity.

**Homogenization**: Given affine $f(x,y) = 0$ of degree $d$, the projective closure is:

$$F(X,Y,Z) = Z^d f\left(\frac{X}{Z}, \frac{Y}{Z}\right) = 0$$

**Example**: Circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ becomes $X^2 + Y^2 = Z^2$ in projective coordinates.

## Parametric Curves

Some curves are easier to describe parametrically:

$$(x(t), y(t)) = (f(t), g(t))$$

where $f, g$ are rational functions.

### Example: Unit Circle

Parametric: $x = \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2}$, $y = \frac{2t}{1+t^2}$

This gives all rational points on the circle!

### Rational Curves

A curve is **rational** if it admits a rational parametrization.

**Criterion**: A curve is rational iff its genus is 0.

## Implicitization

Given parametric equations $x = f(t)$, $y = g(t)$, find the implicit equation $F(x,y) = 0$.

### Method: Elimination Theory

Eliminate the parameter $t$ using resultants or Gröbner bases.

**Example**: For $x = t^2$, $y = t^3$:
- From first equation: $t = \pm\sqrt{x}$
- Substitute: $y = (\pm\sqrt{x})^3 = \pm x^{3/2}$
- Square both sides: $y^2 = x^3$

## Intersection of Curves

### Bezout's Theorem (Preview)

Two plane curves of degrees $m$ and $n$ intersect in exactly $mn$ points (counting multiplicity and points at infinity).

**Example**: Line (degree 1) and conic (degree 2) intersect in 2 points.

See 4.13.5 for the full theorem!

## Singular Points

A point $P$ on the curve $f(x,y) = 0$ is **singular** if:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(P) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(P) = 0$$

### Types of Singularities

|Type|Description|Example|
|---|---|---|
|Node|Two distinct tangent directions|$y^2 = x^3 + x^2$|
|Cusp|Single tangent, curve doubles back|$y^2 = x^3$|
|Tacnode|Two branches tangent|$y^2 = x^4$|

See 4.13.6 for detailed study of singularities!

## Genus of a Curve

The **genus** is a topological invariant measuring complexity.

### Formula for Smooth Plane Curves

For a smooth plane curve of degree $d$:

$$g = \frac{(d-1)(d-2)}{2}$$

**Examples**:
- Line ($d=1$): $g = 0$
- Conic ($d=2$): $g = 0$
- Cubic ($d=3$): $g = 1$ (elliptic curve!)
- Quartic ($d=4$): $g = 3$

### Geometric Interpretation

Genus counts the number of "holes" in the curve (over $\mathbb{C}$).

## Applications

### Number Theory

Finding integer/rational solutions to polynomial equations (Diophantine geometry).

**Example**: Fermat's Last Theorem concerns the curve $x^n + y^n = 1$.

### Cryptography

Elliptic curve cryptography uses the group structure of elliptic curves.

### Computer Graphics

Bezier curves, splines, and NURBS are used for modeling smooth curves.

### Robotics

Path planning uses algebraic curves to describe motion constraints.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Degree with Genus
> Degree is algebraic (polynomial degree). Genus is topological (number of holes). They're related but different!

> [!warning] Forgetting Points at Infinity
> In projective geometry, curves may have additional intersection points "at infinity." Always consider the projective closure!

> [!warning] Assuming All Curves Are Smooth
> Many interesting curves have singularities. Check $\nabla f = 0$ to find them.

> [!warning] Real vs. Complex Curves
> A curve may look disconnected over $\mathbb{R}$ but be connected over $\mathbb{C}$. The topology can be very different!

Classify curves:

- Find the degree and genus of $x^4 + y^4 = 1$
- Determine if $y^2 = x^3 - x$ is smooth
- Show that $x^2 + y^2 = 0$ has only one real point but infinitely many complex points

Find parametrizations:

- Parametrize the hyperbola $x^2 - y^2 = 1$
- Find a rational parametrization of $x^2 + y^2 = 2$
- Show that $y^2 = x^3$ cannot be parametrized by polynomials

Conceptual:

- Why do we need projective geometry to study plane curves?
- Explain the relationship between degree and genus
- What does it mean for a curve to be rational?

Challenge:

- Prove the genus formula for smooth plane curves
- Classify all singular cubic curves up to projective equivalence
- Research: What is the Plücker formula and how does it relate degree, genus, and singularities?
