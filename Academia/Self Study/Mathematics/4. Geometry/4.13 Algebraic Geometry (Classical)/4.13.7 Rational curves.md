## Definition

A **rational curve** is an algebraic curve that is birationally equivalent to the projective line $\mathbb{P}^1$.

Equivalently, a curve $C$ is rational if there exists a parametrization:

$$x = f(t), \quad y = g(t)$$

where $f$ and $g$ are rational functions.

> [!example] Why Rational Curves Matter
> Rational curves are important because:  
> - They're the simplest curves (genus 0)  
> - Admit explicit parametrizations  
> - Fundamental in computer-aided design (Bezier curves, NURBS)  
> - Appear in classification of surfaces  
> - Key to understanding birational geometry

---

## Genus Criterion

### Theorem

A smooth projective curve is rational if and only if its genus is 0.

For plane curves of degree $d$:

$$g = \frac{(d-1)(d-2)}{2}$$

So a smooth plane curve is rational iff $d \leq 2$ (lines and conics).

### Singular Curves

Singular curves can be rational even if their degree suggests higher genus!

**Example**: Nodal cubic has arithmetic genus 1, but geometric genus 0 (rational).

The formula becomes:

$$g_{\text{geometric}} = \frac{(d-1)(d-2)}{2} - \sum \delta_P$$

If this equals 0, the curve is rational.

## Examples

### Example 1: Lines (Degree 1)

Equation: $ax + by + c = 0$

Parametrization: $x = t$, $y = -\frac{a}{b}t - \frac{c}{b}$ (if $b \neq 0$)

Genus: $g = 0$ ✓

### Example 2: Conics (Degree 2)

Circle: $x^2 + y^2 = 1$

Rational parametrization:
$$x = \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2}, \quad y = \frac{2t}{1+t^2}$$

This gives all rational points on the circle!

```desmos-graph
x^2 + y^2 = 1
```

**General method**: If you know one rational point on a conic, you can parametrize it using lines through that point.

### Example 3: Nodal Cubic

Curve: $y^2 = x^3 + x^2 = x^2(x+1)$

Has a node at $(0,0)$.

Parametrization:
$$x = t^2 - 1, \quad y = t(t^2 - 1)$$

Check: $y^2 = t^2(t^2-1)^2 = (t^2-1)^2 \cdot t^2 = x^2(x+1)$ ✓

Genus: $g = \frac{2 \cdot 1}{2} - 1 = 0$ ✓

### Example 4: Cuspidal Cubic

Curve: $y^2 = x^3$

Has a cusp at $(0,0)$.

Parametrization:
$$x = t^2, \quad y = t^3$$

Very simple! This shows the cusp is traced once as $t$ varies.

Genus: $g = \frac{2 \cdot 1}{2} - 1 = 0$ ✓

### Example 5: Folium of Descartes

Curve: $x^3 + y^3 = 3axy$

Parametrization:
$$x = \frac{3at}{1+t^3}, \quad y = \frac{3at^2}{1+t^3}$$

Has a node at the origin.

## Finding Parametrizations

### Method 1: Projection from a Point

If a curve $C$ of degree $d$ has a singular point $P$ of multiplicity $d-1$, then $C$ is rational.

**Procedure**:
1. Choose coordinates so $P = [0:0:1]$
2. Consider lines through $P$: $y = tx$
3. Substitute into the curve equation
4. Solve for $x$ and $y$ in terms of $t$

**Example**: For nodal cubic $y^2 = x^2(x+1)$, use lines $y = tx$ through the node.

### Method 2: Use Known Rational Points

For conics, if you know one rational point, draw lines through it with rational slope.

**Example**: Circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ with point $(-1,0)$.

Line: $y = t(x+1)$

Substitute and solve → rational parametrization!

### Method 3: Birational Maps

Find a birational map to $\mathbb{P}^1$ or another known rational curve.

## Non-Rational Curves

### Elliptic Curves (Genus 1)

Smooth cubics like $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$ are NOT rational.

They cannot be parametrized by rational functions!

This is why elliptic curves have rich arithmetic structure.

### Higher Genus Curves

Curves with $g \geq 2$ are never rational.

**Example**: Smooth quartic ($d=4$): $g = \frac{3 \cdot 2}{2} = 3$ (not rational)

---

## Lüroth's Theorem

### Statement

Every intermediate field between $\mathbb{C}(t)$ and $\mathbb{C}$ is itself a rational function field.

**Consequence**: If a curve is unirational (dominated by $\mathbb{P}^1$), then it's rational.

This holds for curves but FAILS in higher dimensions!

## Applications

### Computer-Aided Design

**Bezier curves**: Parametric curves used extensively in CAD, animation, and font design.

**NURBS** (Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines): General framework for representing rational curves and surfaces.

### Computer Graphics

Rational parametrizations allow efficient rendering and manipulation of curves.

### Number Theory

Finding rational points on curves:
- Conics: Easy (parametrize and substitute rational values)
- Elliptic curves: Hard (Mordell-Weil theorem, BSD conjecture)
- Higher genus: Very hard (Faltings' theorem: finitely many rational points)

### Physics

Rational curves appear in:
- Feynman diagrams
- String worldsheets
- Integrable systems

## Unicursal Curves

Another name for rational curves is **unicursal curves** (meaning "one path").

This emphasizes that they can be traced by a single parameter.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Assuming All Curves Are Rational
> Only genus 0 curves are rational! Smooth cubics (elliptic curves) and higher-degree smooth curves are NOT rational.

> [!warning] Confusing Rational with Parametrizable
> "Rational" means parametrizable by RATIONAL functions. Some curves can be parametrized by transcendental functions but aren't rational.

> [!warning] Forgetting Singularities
> A singular curve may be rational even if its degree suggests otherwise. Always compute the geometric genus!

> [!warning] Real vs. Complex Rationality
> A curve may be rational over $\mathbb{C}$ but not over $\mathbb{R}$. Check the field of definition!
