## Motivation

**Projective geometry** extends Euclidean geometry by adding "points at infinity" where parallel lines meet. This eliminates special cases and reveals deeper symmetries.

> [!example] Why Projective Geometry Matters
> Projective geometry provides:  
> - Unified treatment of conics (all non-degenerate conics are equivalent!)  
> - Natural framework for perspective and computer graphics  
> - Foundation for algebraic geometry  
> - Elegant theorems (Desargues, Pascal, Pappus)  
> - Better behavior of intersections (Bezout's theorem)

---

## The Projective Plane

### Definition

The **real projective plane** $\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{R})$ is the set of lines through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Equivalently, it's $\mathbb{R}^3 \setminus \{0\}$ modulo the equivalence relation:

$$(X,Y,Z) \sim (\lambda X, \lambda Y, \lambda Z) \quad \text{for } \lambda \neq 0$$

### Homogeneous Coordinates

A point in $\mathbb{P}^2$ is written as $[X:Y:Z]$ (homogeneous coordinates).

- $[X:Y:Z] = [\lambda X:\lambda Y:\lambda Z]$ for any $\lambda \neq 0$
- At least one coordinate must be nonzero

### Affine Patch

The subset where $Z \neq 0$ corresponds to the affine plane:

$$[X:Y:Z] \mapsto \left(\frac{X}{Z}, \frac{Y}{Z}\right) \in \mathbb{R}^2$$

Points with $Z = 0$ are "points at infinity."

```desmos-graph
y = x
y = x + 1
y = x - 1
```

> [!example] Visual Interpretation
> Parallel lines in the affine plane meet at a point at infinity in projective space. All lines with the same slope share the same point at infinity!

## Points at Infinity

### Line at Infinity

The **line at infinity** is $L_\infty = \{[X:Y:0] : (X,Y) \neq (0,0)\}$.

This is itself a projective line $\mathbb{P}^1$.

### Direction Correspondence

Each point $[a:b:0]$ at infinity corresponds to lines with slope $b/a$ (or vertical if $a=0$).

**Example**: 
- All horizontal lines meet at $[1:0:0]$
- All vertical lines meet at $[0:1:0]$
- Lines with slope $m$ meet at $[1:m:0]$

## Projective Transformations

### Definition

A **projective transformation** (or collineation, or homography) is a bijection of $\mathbb{P}^2$ that preserves lines.

In homogeneous coordinates, it's given by a $3 \times 3$ invertible matrix:

$$[X:Y:Z] \mapsto [aX+bY+cZ : dX+eY+fZ : gX+hY+iZ]$$

The group of projective transformations is $PGL(3,\mathbb{R}) = GL(3,\mathbb{R})/\mathbb{R}^*$.

### Properties

- Maps lines to lines
- Preserves incidence (if a point is on a line, its image is on the image line)
- Preserves cross-ratio
- Does NOT preserve distances or angles

## Cross-Ratio

### Definition

For four collinear points $A,B,C,D$, the **cross-ratio** is:

$$(A,B;C,D) = \frac{(C-A)(D-B)}{(C-B)(D-A)}$$

In projective geometry, this is well-defined even when points are at infinity!

### Invariance

Cross-ratio is invariant under projective transformations:

$$(A,B;C,D) = (f(A),f(B);f(C),f(D))$$

This is the fundamental invariant of projective geometry!

## Duality Principle

### Statement

In projective geometry, any theorem remains true if we swap:
- "point" ↔ "line"
- "lies on" ↔ "passes through"
- "collinear" ↔ "concurrent"

**Example**: 
- "Two points determine a line" ↔ "Two lines determine a point"
- Pascal's theorem ↔ Brianchon's theorem

This doubles the number of theorems for free!

## Classical Theorems

### Desargues' Theorem

If two triangles are perspective from a point, they are perspective from a line.

**Precisely**: If lines $AA'$, $BB'$, $CC'$ meet at a point, then the intersections $AB \cap A'B'$, $BC \cap B'C'$, $CA \cap C'A'$ are collinear.

This theorem is TRUE in $\mathbb{P}^2$ but requires 3D to prove!

### Pascal's Theorem

For a hexagon inscribed in a conic, the three intersection points of opposite sides are collinear.

The line containing these points is called the **Pascal line**.

### Pappus' Theorem

If $A,B,C$ are collinear and $A',B',C'$ are collinear, then the intersections $AB' \cap A'B$, $BC' \cap B'C$, $CA' \cap C'A$ are collinear.

Pappus' theorem characterizes projective planes over fields!

```desmos-graph
y = 1
x = 0
x = 1
```

## Projective Completion of Curves

### Homogenization

Given an affine curve $f(x,y) = 0$ of degree $d$, its **projective closure** is:

$$F(X,Y,Z) = Z^d f\left(\frac{X}{Z}, \frac{Y}{Z}\right) = 0$$

**Example**: Circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ becomes $X^2 + Y^2 = Z^2$.

### Points at Infinity on Curves

To find points at infinity, set $Z = 0$ in the homogeneous equation.

**Examples**:
- Parabola $y = x^2$: One point at infinity $[0:1:0]$ (tangent)
- Hyperbola $x^2 - y^2 = 1$: Two points at infinity $[1:1:0]$ and $[1:-1:0]$
- Ellipse $x^2 + y^2 = 1$: No real points at infinity (two complex conjugate points)

## Conics in Projective Geometry

### Unified View

All non-degenerate conics are projectively equivalent!

Any smooth conic can be transformed to $X^2 + Y^2 = Z^2$ (or $XY = Z^2$) by a projective transformation.

The distinction between ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola depends only on how the conic intersects the line at infinity.

### Five Points Determine a Conic

Given 5 points in general position (no 3 collinear), there exists a unique conic through them.

This is much cleaner than the affine version!

## Higher Dimensions

### Projective Space $\mathbb{P}^n$

Defined similarly: lines through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$.

Homogeneous coordinates: $[X_0:X_1:...:X_n]$.

### Projective Varieties

Zero sets of homogeneous polynomials in $\mathbb{P}^n$.

These are the main objects of study in algebraic geometry!

---

## Applications

### Computer Graphics

Perspective projection is a projective transformation. Homogeneous coordinates are essential for 3D rendering.

### Camera Calibration

Finding the relationship between 3D world coordinates and 2D image coordinates uses projective geometry.

### Algebraic Geometry

Projective varieties are better behaved than affine varieties (complete, proper).

### Art and Architecture

Renaissance perspective drawing is based on projective geometry.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Projective with Affine
> Projective geometry has points at infinity; affine geometry doesn't. Distances and angles don't make sense in projective geometry!

> [!warning] Forgetting Homogeneity
> Equations in projective space MUST be homogeneous. $X + Y = 1$ is not valid; use $X + Y = Z$ instead.

> [!warning] Assuming Unique Coordinates
> Homogeneous coordinates are NOT unique: $[1:2:3] = [2:4:6]$. Only ratios matter!

> [!warning] Real vs. Complex
> Some theorems require working over $\mathbb{C}$ to see all intersection points. Real projective geometry may miss solutions!
