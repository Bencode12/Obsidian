## Statement

**Bezout's Theorem**: Two projective plane curves of degrees $m$ and $n$, with no common component, intersect in exactly $mn$ points, counting multiplicity.

More precisely, if $C_1$ and $C_2$ are curves defined by homogeneous polynomials $F$ and $G$ of degrees $m$ and $n$:

$$\sum_{P \in C_1 \cap C_2} I(P, C_1 \cap C_2) = mn$$

where $I(P, C_1 \cap C_2)$ is the **intersection multiplicity** at point $P$.

> [!example] Why Bezout's Theorem Matters
> Bezout's theorem is fundamental to algebraic geometry:  
> - Predicts number of intersections  
> - Foundation for intersection theory  
> - Explains why projective geometry is natural  
> - Key tool in enumerative geometry  
> - Connects algebra and geometry

## Examples

### Example 1: Two Lines

Degrees: $m = 1$, $n = 1$

Intersection: $1 \times 1 = 1$ point

Two distinct lines always meet in exactly one point (possibly at infinity).

```desmos-graph
y = x
y = -x + 2
```

### Example 2: Line and Conic

Degrees: $m = 1$, $n = 2$

Intersection: $1 \times 2 = 2$ points

A line meets a conic in 2 points (counting multiplicity).

**Special cases**:
- Tangent line: One point with multiplicity 2
- Secant line: Two distinct points
- No real intersection: Two complex conjugate points

### Example 3: Two Conics

Degrees: $m = 2$, $n = 2$

Intersection: $2 \times 2 = 4$ points

Two conics generally meet in 4 points.

**Example**: Two circles can meet in 0, 1, 2, 3, or 4 points (including complex and points at infinity).

### Example 4: Line and Cubic

Degrees: $m = 1$, $n = 3$

Intersection: $1 \times 3 = 3$ points

This explains the group law on elliptic curves! A line through two points on a cubic meets it at a third point.

## Intersection Multiplicity

### Definition

The **intersection multiplicity** $I(P, C_1 \cap C_2)$ measures how "tangentially" the curves meet at $P$.

### Properties

1. $I(P, C_1 \cap C_2) \geq 1$ if $P \in C_1 \cap C_2$
2. $I(P, C_1 \cap C_2) = 1$ iff $C_1$ and $C_2$ intersect transversely at $P$
3. $I(P, C_1 \cap C_2) > 1$ iff $C_1$ and $C_2$ are tangent at $P$
4. $I(P, C_1 \cap C_2) = I(P, C_2 \cap C_1)$ (symmetry)

### Computing Multiplicity

#### Method 1: Parametrization

If $C_1$ is smooth at $P$, parametrize it as $\gamma(t)$ with $\gamma(0) = P$. Then:

$$I(P, C_1 \cap C_2) = \text{order of vanishing of } G(\gamma(t)) \text{ at } t = 0$$

#### Method 2: Local Coordinates

In local coordinates where $P = (0,0)$, expand both equations and find the lowest degree term.

#### Method 3: Resultants

Use resultant theory to compute multiplicity algebraically.

## Special Cases

### Tangent Intersection

If $C_1$ and $C_2$ are tangent at $P$, then $I(P, C_1 \cap C_2) \geq 2$.

**Example**: Line tangent to a circle: multiplicity 2.

### Higher Order Contact

Curves can have contact of order $k$, giving multiplicity $k+1$.

**Example**: Osculating circle to a curve has contact of order 3.

### Singular Points

If $P$ is singular on either curve, multiplicities can be higher.

**Example**: A line through a node of a cubic intersects with multiplicity ≥ 2.

## Proof Strategy

### Step 1: Work in Projective Space

Affine curves may miss intersection points at infinity. Projective completion ensures we count all intersections.

### Step 2: Use Resultants

For curves $F(X,Y,Z) = 0$ and $G(X,Y,Z) = 0$, eliminate one variable using resultants.

The resultant is a polynomial whose roots correspond to intersection points.

### Step 3: Count with Multiplicity

Show that the degree of the resultant equals $mn$, and each root contributes its multiplicity.

### Alternative: Cohomology

In modern algebraic geometry, use intersection theory in cohomology:

$$[C_1] \cdot [C_2] = mn$$

where $[C_i] \in H^2(\mathbb{P}^2, \mathbb{Z})$ are cohomology classes.

## Generalizations

### Higher Dimensions

For hypersurfaces in $\mathbb{P}^n$:

If $H_1, ..., H_n$ are hypersurfaces of degrees $d_1, ..., d_n$:

$$|H_1 \cap ... \cap H_n| = d_1 \cdots d_n$$

(counting multiplicity, assuming proper intersection)

### Bezout's Inequality

If curves share components, we get an inequality:

$$\sum I(P, C_1 \cap C_2) \leq mn$$

### Affine Version

In affine space, we only get an inequality:

$$|\{(x,y) : f(x,y) = g(x,y) = 0\}| \leq mn$$

Some intersections may be "at infinity."

## Applications

### Enumerative Geometry

Count geometric configurations:
- How many conics are tangent to 5 given conics? (Answer: 3264)
- How many lines meet 4 given lines in $\mathbb{P}^3$? (Answer: 2)

### Computer-Aided Design

Determine intersections of curves and surfaces for rendering and modeling.

### Robotics

Path planning involves finding intersections of constraint varieties.

### Cryptography

Elliptic curve operations rely on Bezout's theorem (line meets cubic in 3 points).

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Forgetting to Count Multiplicity
> Tangent intersections count MORE than once! A tangent line to a conic counts as 2 intersections, not 1.

> [!warning] Working in Affine Space
> In affine space, you may miss points at infinity. Always work projectively for the full count!

> [!warning] Ignoring Complex Solutions
> Over $\mathbb{R}$, you may see fewer than $mn$ points. The missing points are complex conjugates.

> [!warning] Curves with Common Components
> If curves share a component, they intersect in infinitely many points. Bezout's theorem requires no common components!
