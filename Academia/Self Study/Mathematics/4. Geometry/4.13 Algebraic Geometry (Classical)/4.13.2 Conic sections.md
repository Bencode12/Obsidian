## Definition

A **conic section** (or conic) is a plane curve of degree 2. It's the intersection of a plane with a double-napped cone.

General equation:
$$Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$$

where not all of $A, B, C$ are zero.

> [!example] Why Conics Matter
> Conics appear everywhere:  
> - Planetary orbits (ellipses)  
> - Projectile motion (parabolas)  
> - Hyperbolic navigation systems  
> - Reflectors and lenses  
> - Foundation for studying higher-degree curves

---

## Classification

The **discriminant** $\Delta = B^2 - 4AC$ determines the type:

|Discriminant|Type|Example|
|---|---|---|
|$B^2 - 4AC < 0$|Ellipse (or circle)|$x^2 + y^2 = 1$|
|$B^2 - 4AC = 0$|Parabola|$y = x^2$|
|$B^2 - 4AC > 0$|Hyperbola|$x^2 - y^2 = 1$|

### Degenerate Cases

When the determinant of the full matrix is zero, we get degenerate conics:
- Point (degenerate ellipse)
- Line (degenerate parabola)
- Two intersecting lines (degenerate hyperbola)
- Empty set

## The Four Types

### 1. Circle

**Standard form**: $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2$

**Properties**:
- Center: $(h,k)$
- Radius: $r$
- Eccentricity: $e = 0$
- All points equidistant from center

```desmos-graph
x^2 + y^2 = 1
x^2 + y^2 = 4
```

**Parametric**: $x = h + r\cos t$, $y = k + r\sin t$

### 2. Ellipse

**Standard form**: $\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} + \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1$

**Properties**:
- Center: $(h,k)$
- Semi-major axis: $a$ (assuming $a > b$)
- Semi-minor axis: $b$
- Foci: $(h \pm c, k)$ where $c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}$
- Eccentricity: $e = \frac{c}{a} < 1$

**Geometric definition**: Sum of distances to foci is constant ($= 2a$).

**Parametric**: $x = h + a\cos t$, $y = k + b\sin t$

**Area**: $A = \pi ab$

### 3. Parabola

**Standard forms**:
- Vertical: $y = a(x-h)^2 + k$ or $(x-h)^2 = 4p(y-k)$
- Horizontal: $x = a(y-k)^2 + h$ or $(y-k)^2 = 4p(x-h)$

**Properties**:
- Vertex: $(h,k)$
- Focus: $(h, k+p)$ for vertical parabola
- Directrix: $y = k-p$ for vertical parabola
- Eccentricity: $e = 1$

**Geometric definition**: Equidistant from focus and directrix.

```desmos-graph
y = x^2
y = -x^2 + 2
```

**Parametric**: $x = t$, $y = at^2 + bt + c$

### 4. Hyperbola

**Standard form**: $\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1$

**Properties**:
- Center: $(h,k)$
- Vertices: $(h \pm a, k)$
- Foci: $(h \pm c, k)$ where $c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$
- Asymptotes: $y - k = \pm\frac{b}{a}(x-h)$
- Eccentricity: $e = \frac{c}{a} > 1$

**Geometric definition**: Difference of distances to foci is constant ($= 2a$).

**Parametric**: $x = h + a\sec t$, $y = k + b\tan t$

## Eccentricity

The **eccentricity** $e$ measures how much a conic deviates from being circular:

|Conic|Eccentricity|Shape|
|---|---|---|
|Circle|$e = 0$|Perfectly round|
|Ellipse|$0 < e < 1$|Oval|
|Parabola|$e = 1$|Open curve|
|Hyperbola|$e > 1$|Two branches|

Unified definition: A conic is the locus of points where the ratio of distance to focus and distance to directrix equals $e$.

## Matrix Representation

Write the conic in matrix form:

$$\begin{pmatrix} x & y & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} A & B/2 & D/2 \\ B/2 & C & E/2 \\ D/2 & E/2 & F \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = 0$$

Or more compactly: $\mathbf{x}^T Q \mathbf{x} = 0$

### Classification via Eigenvalues

Diagonalize the upper-left $2\times 2$ block. The signs of eigenvalues determine the type:
- Both positive/negative: ellipse
- One zero: parabola
- Opposite signs: hyperbola

## Transformations

### Rotation to Eliminate $xy$ Term

If $B \neq 0$, rotate coordinates by angle $\theta$:

$$\cot(2\theta) = \frac{A-C}{B}$$

This eliminates the cross term, putting the conic in standard form.

### Translation to Center

Complete the square to find the center and translate coordinates.

## Projective Viewpoint

In projective geometry, all non-degenerate conics are equivalent!

The distinction between ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola depends on how the conic intersects the line at infinity:
- **Ellipse**: No real intersection with line at infinity
- **Parabola**: Tangent to line at infinity
- **Hyperbola**: Intersects line at infinity in two points

```desmos-graph
x^2 + y^2 = 1
x^2 - y^2 = 1
```

> [!example] Key Insight
> In projective geometry, there's only ONE type of non-degenerate conic. The affine classification depends on the position relative to the line at infinity!

## Properties

### Reflection Properties

- **Ellipse**: Light from one focus reflects to the other focus
- **Parabola**: Light from focus reflects parallel to axis (and vice versa)
- **Hyperbola**: Light toward one focus reflects as if coming from the other focus

These properties are used in telescopes, satellite dishes, and whispering galleries!

### Optical Applications

- Parabolic mirrors in telescopes
- Elliptical reflectors in lithotripsy (kidney stone treatment)
- Hyperbolic mirrors in Cassegrain telescopes

## Constructing Conics

### Five Points Determine a Conic

Given 5 points in general position (no 3 collinear), there exists a unique conic through them.

### Pascal's Theorem

For a hexagon inscribed in a conic, the three intersection points of opposite sides are collinear.

This is a fundamental theorem in projective geometry!

## Applications

### Celestial Mechanics

Kepler's First Law: Planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.

### Engineering

- Parabolic antennas and reflectors
- Elliptical gears
- Hyperbolic cooling towers

### Navigation

LORAN and other hyperbolic navigation systems use time differences to determine position.

### Architecture

Elliptical domes, parabolic arches, hyperbolic paraboloid roofs.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Standard Forms
> Make sure you identify whether the major axis is horizontal or vertical for ellipses and hyperbolas. The larger denominator goes with the major axis!

> [!warning] Forgetting Degenerate Cases
> Not every quadratic equation gives a "nice" conic. Check for degenerate cases (point, line, empty set).

> [!warning] Mixing Up Focus and Directrix
> For parabolas, the vertex is halfway between the focus and directrix. Don't confuse their positions!

> [!warning] Assuming Circles Are Different
> Circles are just special ellipses with $a = b$ (eccentricity = 0). They're not fundamentally different!
