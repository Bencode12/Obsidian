## Definition

A point $P$ on a plane curve $C: f(x,y) = 0$ is **singular** (or a singularity) if:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(P) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(P) = 0$$

Equivalently, $\nabla f(P) = 0$.

At a singular point, the curve fails to be a smooth manifold—the tangent line is not well-defined.

> [!example] Why Singularities Matter
> Singularities are crucial for:  
> - Classification of curves  
> - Genus calculations  
> - Resolution of singularities (Hironaka's theorem)  
> - Understanding curve topology  
> - Applications in physics (phase transitions, catastrophe theory)

### Simple Singularities

### Classification by Milnor Number

The **Milnor number** $\mu$ measures the complexity of a singularity.

For an isolated singularity at the origin:

$$\mu = \dim_{\mathbb{C}} \frac{\mathbb{C}[[x,y]]}{\langle f_x, f_y \rangle}$$

**Examples**:
- Node: $\mu = 1$
- Cusp: $\mu = 2$
- Tacnode: $\mu = 3$

## Detailed Examples

### Example 1: Node (Ordinary Double Point)

Curve: $y^2 = x^3 + x^2 = x^2(x+1)$

Singularity at $(0,0)$.

**Tangent cone**: $y^2 - x^2 = 0$, giving two tangent lines $y = \pm x$.

The curve crosses itself at the node.

**Delta invariant**: $\delta = 1$ (measures contribution to genus)

### Example 2: Cusp (Cusp of First Kind)

Curve: $y^2 = x^3$

Singularity at $(0,0)$.

**Tangent cone**: $y^2 = 0$, giving one tangent line $y = 0$ (with multiplicity 2).

The curve has a sharp point and reverses direction.

**Parametrization**: $x = t^2$, $y = t^3$

**Delta invariant**: $\delta = 1$

### Example 3: Tacnode

Curve: $y^2 = x^4$

Singularity at $(0,0)$.

Two branches are tangent to each other (both have tangent $y = 0$).

**Delta invariant**: $\delta = 2$

### Example 4: Triple Point

Curve: $x^3 - 3xy^2 = 0$ (three lines through origin)

Factorization: $x(x - \sqrt{3}y)(x + \sqrt{3}y) = 0$

Three distinct tangent lines at $60°$ angles.

**Delta invariant**: $\delta = 3$

## Delta Invariant

### Definition

The **delta invariant** $\delta_P$ measures how much the singularity reduces the genus.

For a plane curve singularity:

$$\delta_P = \frac{\mu_P + r_P - 1}{2}$$

where:
- $\mu_P$ = Milnor number
- $r_P$ = number of branches

### Genus Formula

For a plane curve of degree $d$ with singularities $P_1, ..., P_k$:

$$g = \frac{(d-1)(d-2)}{2} - \sum_{i=1}^k \delta_{P_i}$$

This shows how singularities reduce the genus from the smooth case!

**Example**: Nodal cubic ($d=3$, one node with $\delta=1$):
$$g = \frac{2 \cdot 1}{2} - 1 = 0$$

So a nodal cubic is rational (genus 0), not elliptic!

## Resolution of Singularities

### Blow-up

The **blow-up** is a geometric operation that replaces a singular point with a projective line (exceptional divisor).

**Process**:
1. Replace the point with all possible tangent directions
2. The strict transform of the curve may be smoother
3. Repeat until all singularities are resolved

### Hironaka's Theorem

Every algebraic variety over a field of characteristic 0 admits a resolution of singularities.

This is a deep result in algebraic geometry!

### Example: Resolving a Node

For $y^2 = x^3 + x^2$:

Blow up at the origin → two distinct points on the exceptional divisor → singularity resolved!

## Multiplicity

### Definition

The **multiplicity** $m_P(C)$ of a curve $C$ at point $P$ is the degree of the lowest-degree term in the Taylor expansion of $f$ at $P$.

Equivalently, it's the intersection multiplicity of $C$ with a generic line through $P$.

### Properties

- $m_P(C) = 1$ iff $P$ is smooth
- $m_P(C) \geq 2$ iff $P$ is singular
- For a node: $m = 2$
- For a cusp: $m = 2$
- For a triple point: $m = 3$

---

## Branches

A **branch** (or analytic branch) of a curve at a singular point is an irreducible component of the curve in a small neighborhood.

### Counting Branches

- Smooth point: 1 branch
- Node: 2 branches
- Cusp: 1 branch (but singular!)
- Tacnode: 2 branches (tangent)

The number of branches $r$ appears in the delta invariant formula.

---

## Puiseux Expansion

Near a singular point, branches can be parametrized using **Puiseux series** (fractional power series).

**Example**: For $y^2 = x^3$:

$$x = t^2, \quad y = t^3$$

Or equivalently: $y = \pm x^{3/2}$

This gives a local parametrization of each branch.

## Applications

### Topology of Curves

Singularities determine the topology (genus, number of components) of algebraic curves.

### Physics

- Phase transitions (critical points are singularities)
- Catastrophe theory (classification of singularities)
- String theory (singular Calabi-Yau manifolds)

### Computer Vision

Edge detection and feature extraction use singularity theory.

### Robotics

Singular configurations of robot arms correspond to singularities in configuration space.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Node and Cusp
> Node: two distinct tangents (curve crosses itself)  
> Cusp: one tangent with multiplicity 2 (curve has a sharp point)  
> They look different and have different properties!

> [!warning] Forgetting Complex Singularities
> A curve may appear smooth over $\mathbb{R}$ but have complex singularities. Always check over $\mathbb{C}$ for complete analysis.

> [!warning] Assuming All Double Points Are Nodes
> A double point could be a node, cusp, or tacnode. Check the tangent cone to distinguish them!

> [!warning] Ignoring Contribution to Genus
> Each singularity reduces the genus. Don't use the smooth genus formula for singular curves!
