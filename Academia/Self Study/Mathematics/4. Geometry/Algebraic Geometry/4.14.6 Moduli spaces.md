## Flat Morphisms

### Definition

Morphism $f: X \to Y$ is **flat** if for all $x \in X$:

$$\mathcal{O}_{X,x} \text{ is flat } \mathcal{O}_{Y,f(x)}\text{-module}$$

Algebraically: Tensoring with $\mathcal{O}_{X,x}$ preserves exact sequences.

### Geometric Meaning

Flat morphisms have "continuous" fibers.

No sudden jumps in dimension or structure!

### Examples

- Projection $\mathbb{A}^2 \to \mathbb{A}^1$: Flat
- Family of curves with constant genus: Flat
- Blow-up: NOT flat (fiber dimension jumps)

## Families of Varieties

### Definition

**Family** of varieties over base $S$: Flat morphism $f: X \to S$.

Fiber over $s \in S$: $X_s = f^{-1}(s)$.

### Flatness Criterion

For varieties, flat ⟺ Hilbert polynomial of fibers is constant.

Numerical invariants don't jump!

### Example: Degenerating Conics

Family in $\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{A}^1$:
$$X_t = V(x^2 + y^2 - tz^2) \subseteq \mathbb{P}^2$$

- For $t \neq 0$: Smooth conic (genus 0)
- For $t = 0$: Double line (degenerate)

NOT flat! Arithmetic genus changes.

## Deformation Theory

### Infinitesimal Deformations

Study varieties over dual numbers $k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$.

**First-order deformation** of $X$: Scheme $\mathcal{X}$ flat over $\text{Spec}(k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2))$ with special fiber $X$.

### Tangent Space

Deformations classified by:
$$H^1(X, T_X)$$

where $T_X$ is tangent sheaf.

### Obstructions

Obstructions to extending deformations lie in:
$$H^2(X, T_X)$$

If $H^2(X, T_X) = 0$, all infinitesimal deformations extend.

## Moduli Spaces

### Motivation

Want parameter space for isomorphism classes of varieties.

**Moduli space** $\mathcal{M}$: Points correspond to varieties, geometry reflects degenerations.

### Fine vs Coarse Moduli

**Fine moduli space**: Has universal family $\mathcal{U} \to \mathcal{M}$.

Every family is pullback of universal family.

**Coarse moduli space**: Only represents functor on points.

Often fine moduli doesn't exist (automorphisms)!

### Examples

- $\mathcal{M}_g$: Moduli of curves of genus $g$
- $\mathcal{A}_g$: Moduli of abelian varieties
- $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g$: Deligne-Mumford compactification

## Hilbert Schemes

### Hilbert Functor

For projective space $\mathbb{P}^n$ and Hilbert polynomial $P$:

$$\text{Hilb}^P_{\mathbb{P}^n}(S) = \{\text{closed subschemes } Z \subseteq \mathbb{P}^n_S \text{ flat over } S \text{ with Hilbert poly } P\}$$

### Hilbert Scheme Theorem

Functor $\text{Hilb}^P_{\mathbb{P}^n}$ is representable by projective scheme $\text{Hilb}^P(\mathbb{P}^n)$.

Points = subschemes with given Hilbert polynomial!

### Examples

- $\text{Hilb}^d(\mathbb{P}^2)$ for points: Configuration space
- $\text{Hilb}^{dt+1-g}(\mathbb{P}^r)$: Contains moduli of curves

Foundation for constructing moduli spaces!

## Stable Curves

### Problem with $\mathcal{M}_g$

Not compact! Need to add boundary points.

**Stable curve**: Connected nodal curve with finite automorphism group.

Equivalently: Arithmetic genus $g$, nodes only, each component has enough marked points.

### Deligne-Mumford Compactification

$\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g$: Moduli of stable curves of genus $g$.

Compact projective variety (for $g \geq 2$).

Boundary: $\Delta = \overline{\mathcal{M}}_g \setminus \mathcal{M}_g$ (singular curves).

### Dimension

$$\dim(\mathcal{M}_g) = 3g - 3 \text{ for } g \geq 2$$

- $g=0$: Point ($\mathcal{M}_0$ trivial)
- $g=1$: Modular curve (elliptic curves)
- $g=2$: Dimension 3
- $g=3$: Dimension 6

## GIT and Moduli

### Geometric Invariant Theory

Construct moduli as quotient:
$$\mathcal{M} = X^{ss} // G$$

where $G$ acts on parameter space $X$.

**Semistable points** $X^{ss}$: Orbits don't approach zero.

### Stability Conditions

- **Stable**: Closed orbit, finite stabilizer
- **Semistable**: Closure of orbit doesn't contain zero
- **Unstable**: Excluded from quotient

### Example: Binary Forms

Space of degree $d$ binary forms: $\mathbb{P}^d$.

Action of $SL_2$ by change of coordinates.

Quotient parametrizes forms up to equivalence.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Flat and Smooth
> Flat allows singular fibers. Smooth requires all fibers smooth.

> [!warning] Assuming Moduli Space Is Fine
> Often only coarse moduli exists due to automorphisms.

> [!warning] Forgetting Stability Conditions
> Unstable objects must be excluded for good quotient.

- Show that blow-up is not flat
- Verify flatness of projection from product

- Calculate $H^1(X, T_X)$ for specific variety
- Determine if deformations are obstructed
- Find first-order deformations of curve

- Describe moduli space of elliptic curves
- Calculate dimension of $\mathcal{M}_g$ for small $g$

- Explain difference between fine and coarse moduli
- What makes a curve stable?
