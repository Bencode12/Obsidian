## Divisors

### Prime Divisors

On normal variety $X$, **prime divisor** is irreducible closed subset of codimension 1.

**Weil divisor**: Formal sum
$$D = \sum_{i} n_i D_i$$

where $D_i$ are prime divisors and $n_i \in \mathbb{Z}$ (finite sum).

Group of Weil divisors: $\text{Div}(X)$.

### Principal Divisors

For rational function $f \in K(X)^\times$:
$$(f) = \sum_D v_D(f) \cdot D$$

where $v_D$ is valuation along divisor $D$.

Called **principal divisor**.

### Linear Equivalence

Two divisors $D_1, D_2$ are **linearly equivalent** ($D_1 \sim D_2$) if:
$$D_1 - D_2 = (f) \text{ for some } f \in K(X)^\times$$

**Divisor class group**: $\text{Cl}(X) = \text{Div}(X) / \text{Prin}(X)$

## Cartier Divisors

### Definition

**Cartier divisor**: Global section of sheaf $\mathcal{K}^\times / \mathcal{O}^\times$.

Locally given by rational functions $f_i \in K(X)^\times$ on open cover $\{U_i\}$ such that:
$$f_i/f_j \in \mathcal{O}^\times(U_i \cap U_j)$$

### Relation to Weil Divisors

On smooth variety: Weil divisors = Cartier divisors.

On singular variety: Cartier divisors ⊂ Weil divisors.

Cartier divisors better behaved!

## Line Bundles

### Definition

**Line bundle** (invertible sheaf) $\mathcal{L}$ on $X$: Locally free sheaf of rank 1.

Locally isomorphic to $\mathcal{O}_X$.

### Correspondence

Cartier divisors ↔ Line bundles:
- Divisor $D$ → Line bundle $\mathcal{O}(D)$
- Line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ → Divisor class

Group of line bundles: **Picard group** $\text{Pic}(X)$.

### Sections

Global sections $H^0(X, \mathcal{L})$ form vector space.

Dimension $h^0(X, \mathcal{L})$ important invariant.

---

## Ample Line Bundles

### Definition

Line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ is **ample** if for any coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$:

$$\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n} \text{ is generated by global sections for } n \gg 0$$

Geometrically: Gives embedding into projective space!

### Very Ample

$\mathcal{L}$ is **very ample** if defines closed embedding:
$$X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}(H^0(X, \mathcal{L})^*)$$

Very ample ⟹ Ample.

### Examples

- $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}(1)$: Very ample
- $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}(d)$ for $d > 0$: Very ample
- On curve of genus $g$: Degree $> 2g$ implies very ample

## Intersection Theory

### Curves on Surfaces

For curves $C_1, C_2$ on surface $S$:

**Intersection number** $C_1 \cdot C_2 \in \mathbb{Z}$.

Properties:
- Bilinear
- Symmetric: $C_1 \cdot C_2 = C_2 \cdot C_1$
- Invariant under linear equivalence

### Example: $\mathbb{P}^2$

Lines $L_1, L_2$ in $\mathbb{P}^2$:
$$L_1 \cdot L_2 = 1$$

Curves of degree $d_1, d_2$:
$$C_1 \cdot C_2 = d_1 d_2$$

Bezout's theorem!

### Higher Dimensions

For divisors $D_1, \ldots, D_n$ on $n$-fold:

Intersection number $D_1 \cdot \cdots \cdot D_n \in \mathbb{Z}$.

Defined via Chern classes or cohomology.

## Riemann-Roch Theorem

### For Curves

Let $C$ be smooth projective curve of genus $g$, $D$ a divisor.

$$h^0(C, \mathcal{O}(D)) - h^1(C, \mathcal{O}(D)) = \deg(D) + 1 - g$$

Using Serre duality: $h^1(C, \mathcal{O}(D)) = h^0(C, \mathcal{O}(K-D))$

where $K$ is canonical divisor.

### For Surfaces

Noether's formula:
$$\chi(\mathcal{O}_S) = \frac{1}{12}(K_S^2 + e(S))$$

where $e(S)$ is topological Euler characteristic.

### Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch

General version using Chern characters and Todd class.

Deep connection between topology and algebra!

## Canonical Bundle

### Definition

**Canonical bundle** $K_X = \Omega^n_X$ where $n = \dim(X)$.

Sheaf of top differential forms.

### Canonical Divisor

Any divisor $K$ with $\mathcal{O}(K) \cong K_X$.

Not unique, but unique up to linear equivalence.

### Examples

- $\mathbb{P}^n$: $K_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \mathcal{O}(-n-1)$
- Smooth curve of genus $g$: $\deg(K_C) = 2g - 2$
- Calabi-Yau: $K_X \cong \mathcal{O}_X$ (trivial)

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Weil and Cartier Divisors
> They differ on singular varieties. Use Cartier for general theory.

> [!warning] Forgetting That Divisors Are Defined Up to Linear Equivalence
> Individual divisors matter less than divisor classes.

> [!warning] Assuming All Line Bundles Have Sections
> Many line bundles have $h^0 = 0$. Ampleness guarantees sections for high powers.
