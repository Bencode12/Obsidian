## Motivation for Schemes

### Limitations of Classical Varieties

Classical algebraic geometry has problems:
1. Only works over algebraically closed fields
2. Cannot handle "infinitesimal" information (multiplicities)
3. No good notion of "family" of varieties
4. Difficult to do arithmetic geometry

**Schemes** solve all these issues!

---

## Affine Schemes

### Spectrum of Ring

For commutative ring $R$:
$$\text{Spec}(R) = \{\mathfrak{p} \subseteq R : \mathfrak{p} \text{ is prime ideal}\}$$

Points are prime ideals, not just maximal ideals!

### Zariski Topology

Closed sets: $V(I) = \{\mathfrak{p} : I \subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}$ for ideals $I$.

Basis of open sets: $D(f) = \{\mathfrak{p} : f \notin \mathfrak{p}\}$ for $f \in R$.

### Structure Sheaf

Assign ring $\mathcal{O}_X(D(f)) = R_f$ (localization) to distinguished open $D(f)$.

Glue to get sheaf of rings $\mathcal{O}_X$ on $\text{Spec}(R)$.

**Affine scheme**: $(\text{Spec}(R), \mathcal{O}_{\text{Spec}(R)})$

---

## Examples

### Field

$\text{Spec}(k)$ for field $k$: Single point (zero ideal).

### Integers

$\text{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$: 
- Generic point: $(0)$
- Closed points: $(p)$ for primes $p$

Captures arithmetic!

### Dual Numbers

$\text{Spec}(k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2))$: Single point with "infinitesimal direction".

Encodes tangent space information.

### Affine Line

$\text{Spec}(k[x])$:
- Generic point: $(0)$
- Closed points: $(x-a)$ for $a \in k$ (if $k$ algebraically closed)

More points than classical $\mathbb{A}^1$!

## Schemes

### Definition

**Scheme**: Locally ringed space $(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ that is locally isomorphic to affine schemes.

Can glue affine schemes together!

### Types of Points

|Type|Prime Ideal|Geometric Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|Generic|$(0)$ in domain|Whole irreducible component|
|Closed|Maximal ideal|Classical point|
|Intermediate|Non-maximal prime|Subvariety|

Richer structure than varieties!

---

## Morphisms of Schemes

### Definition

Morphism $(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to (Y, \mathcal{O}_Y)$ consists of:
1. Continuous map $f: X \to Y$
2. Sheaf morphism $f^\#: \mathcal{O}_Y \to f_*\mathcal{O}_X$

Must respect local ring structure.

### Affine Case

For affine schemes $\text{Spec}(A) \to \text{Spec}(B)$:

Equivalent to ring homomorphism $B \to A$ (contravariant!).

### Example: Inclusion

Open immersion $D(f) \hookrightarrow \text{Spec}(R)$ corresponds to localization $R \to R_f$.

---

## Fiber Products

### Definition

Fiber product $X \times_Z Y$ exists in category of schemes.

Universal property: Commutes with maps to $Z$.

### Affine Case

$$\text{Spec}(A) \times_{\text{Spec}(R)} \text{Spec}(B) = \text{Spec}(A \otimes_R B)$$

Tensor product of rings!

### Geometric Meaning

- Intersection of subvarieties
- Base change of families
- Pullback of bundles

Fundamental construction!

---

## Properties of Schemes

### Reduced Scheme

$\mathcal{O}_X$ has no nilpotent elements.

Corresponds to radical ideals in classical case.

### Integral Scheme

Reduced and irreducible.

Function field well-defined.

### Normal Scheme

Local rings are integrally closed domains.

Important for resolution of singularities.

### Regular Scheme

Local rings are regular local rings.

Smooth in differential geometry sense.

---

## Dimension

### Krull Dimension

$$\dim(X) = \sup\{\text{length of chains of irreducible closed subsets}\}$$

Same as Krull dimension of rings.

### Relative Dimension

For morphism $f: X \to Y$, can define dimension of fibers.

Important for flatness and smoothness.

---

## Coherent Sheaves

### Definition

Sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ of $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules is **coherent** if locally finitely presented.

On affine $\text{Spec}(R)$: Corresponds to finitely generated $R$-modules.

### Examples

- Structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$
- Ideal sheaves
- Tangent sheaf
- Line bundles (invertible sheaves)

### Importance

Coherent sheaves form abelian category.

Foundation for cohomology theory.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Confusing Points with Classical Points
> Scheme has many more points (generic points, etc.). Don't think only of closed points!

> [!warning] Forgetting Nilpotents
> Nilpotent elements carry important geometric information (tangent vectors, multiplicities).

> [!warning] Assuming Schemes Are Like Manifolds
> Schemes can be very singular. Not locally Euclidean!

## Practice Problems

Work with spectra:

- Describe $\text{Spec}(k[x,y]/(xy))$ geometrically
- Find all points of $\text{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}[x])$
- Show that $\text{Spec}(k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2))$ has one point

Analyze morphisms:

- Describe morphism corresponding to ring map
- Calculate fiber product of two affine schemes
- Determine image of morphism

Study properties:

- Check if given scheme is reduced/normal/regular
- Calculate dimension of scheme
- Identify coherent sheaves on affine scheme

Conceptual:

- Why do we need prime ideals as points?
- Explain geometric meaning of nilpotents
- What advantages do schemes have over varieties?

Challenge:

- Prove that category of affine schemes is opposite to category of rings
- Show that fiber product exists for general schemes
- Research: What is Grothendieck topology and why is it needed?
