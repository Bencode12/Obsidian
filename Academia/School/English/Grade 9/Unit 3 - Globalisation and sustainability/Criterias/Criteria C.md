## Requirements

- Choose **one aspect** of a sustainable development goal
- Choose **two countries** — one failing to achieve the target, one succeeding
- Speak for **3–4 minutes** — roughly 450–600 words at natural speaking pace

---

## Structure

### 1. Introduction — The Problem and the Target

Answer: _why is this a problem and what is the context?_

- Introduce the SDG target — state it clearly and briefly
- Give the scale of the problem with data
- Explain key terms the audience needs to understand your speech
- Establish your **one clear main idea** — the through-line you will return to in the conclusion

> [!tip] Strong opening moves
> 
> - Open with a striking image, statistic or metaphor — not _"today I will talk about..."_
> - State your main idea early so the audience knows where you are going
> - The introduction should take no more than 45 seconds — don't linger here

---

### 2. Country 1 — Failing to Achieve the Target

Evaluate the situation in a country that is struggling.

- What is the current situation? Give measurable data
- What has the government done? Name specific policies or programmes
- Why have they been unsuccessful? Explain the root causes
- What are their plans for the future?

> [!tip] Evaluate, don't just describe Don't list facts — explain _why_. Use cause and effect language: _"As a result... This led to... Because of this..."_ The audience wants to understand the reasons, not just the outcomes.

---

### 3. Country 2 — Succeeding at the Target

Evaluate the situation in a country that is making progress.

- What is the current situation? Show the measurable improvement
- What have they done that worked? Name specific approaches
- Why have they been successful? Identify the deciding factors
- What are their plans for the future?

> [!tip] Use contrast deliberately The speech gains power from comparison. Use parallel structure: _"While Country A did X and struggled, Country B did Y and succeeded — the difference was not money, it was..."_ This shows analysis, not just description.

---

### 4. Conclusion and Encouragement

Bring it back to the main idea. Leave the audience with something to think or act on.

- Summarise what the two countries show us
- Return to your opening metaphor or image — close the loop
- End with a call to action or a statement about what the future requires

> [!tip] Strong closing moves
> 
> - Echo your opening — if you started with a metaphor, end with it transformed
> - Don't end with _"in conclusion"_ — end with the actual idea
> - Encouragement means showing change is _possible_, not that it's guaranteed

## Delivery Tips

|Tip|Why it matters|
|---|---|
|**One clear main idea**|The audience remembers one thing — decide what it is before you write|
|**Use metaphors**|Abstract data becomes human through comparison and image|
|**Vary your pace**|Slow down on your most important sentence — silence signals weight|
|**Don't read**|Know your structure, not your script — notes are fine, paragraphs are not|
|**Signal transitions**|_"Turning to..."_, _"By contrast..."_, _"And yet..."_ — these guide the listener|
|**Give statistics context**|Never drop a number alone — always follow with _"That means..."_ or _"To put that in perspective..."_|

---

## Metaphors — How to Use Them

A metaphor does what data cannot — it makes the audience _feel_ the problem, not just understand it.

**How to build one:**

1. Take the core problem
2. Find something from everyday life that works the same way
3. State the comparison — then connect it back to your argument

**Structure:** _"[The problem] is like [familiar thing] — [explain the parallel]."_

A good metaphor appears at the **opening**, and then again — transformed — at the **conclusion**. This creates a sense of completeness.

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning]
> 
> - **Too long on the introduction** — the two countries are the substance of the speech, not the background
> - **Describing instead of evaluating** — _what happened_ is less important than _why_ and _what it tells us_
> - **Forgetting future plans** — for both countries, what happens next is part of the evaluation
> - **No clear main idea** — if you can't state your speech's point in one sentence, the audience won't know it either
> - **Statistics without meaning** — a number with no context lands and disappears

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] Opening does not begin with _"Today I will..."_
- [ ] Main idea is stated within the first 30 seconds
- [ ] At least one metaphor used
- [ ] Country 1 includes: situation, what they did, why it failed, future plans
- [ ] Country 2 includes: situation, what they did, why it worked, future plans
- [ ] Contrast between the two countries is made explicit
- [ ] Conclusion returns to the opening image or metaphor
- [ ] Speech ends with encouragement or a call to action
- [ ] Timing is within 3–4 minutes
