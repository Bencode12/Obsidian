### **Core Imperialism Terminology**

- **Imperialism**: A term that, in the nineteenth century, was not viewed negatively by many Europeans; it was often associated with pride and patriotism. It involves the expansion of a nation's power and influence through colonization or other means.
    
- **Empire**: A large group of states or countries ruled over by a single monarch or ruling power. By 1900, European empires controlled **85%** of the globe.
    
- **Colony**: A territory taken over and ruled by a colonial power.
    
    - **Direct Control**: Territories taken over and ruled directly as the imperial power's own territory.
        
    - **Indirect Control**: Territories controlled through agreements, such as those governing trading rights, rather than direct administration.
        

---

### **Motivations and Justifications**

- **Nationalism**: The belief that building a great empire was a sign of a nation's importance, greatness, and superiority.
    
- **Missionary**: A person sent on a religious mission to promote Christianity. In an imperial context, missionaries often believed they were "saving souls," though they frequently undermined local power structures.
    
- **Capitalism**: An economic system that, according to thinkers like Vladimir Lenin and J.A. Hobson, drove imperialism because capitalists sought to maximize profits by expanding markets and reducing labor and material costs.
    
- **Strategic Reasons**: Motives for colonization based on military or logistical needs, such as securing "coaling stations" to fuel steam-powered naval ships or recruiting foreign soldiers.
    
- **White Man's Burden**: A contemporary ideological concept (noted in the works of Rudyard Kipling) suggesting that Europeans had a "duty" to "civilize" parts of the world they deemed primitive.
    

---

### **Technological and Economic Factors**

- **Raw Materials**: Natural resources (like rubber, diamonds, or cocoa) sought by industrialized nations to feed their factories.
    
- **New Markets**: New populations to whom mass-produced goods could be sold once home markets were saturated.
    
- **Tariffs**: Import taxes; imperial powers benefited from trading within their empires because they did not have to pay these taxes.
    
- **Technological Advantages**: Tools that made rapid expansion possible, including **machine guns** (which overwhelmed indigenous weapons), **steam ships** (for faster travel), and **refrigeration** (for exporting perishable goods).
