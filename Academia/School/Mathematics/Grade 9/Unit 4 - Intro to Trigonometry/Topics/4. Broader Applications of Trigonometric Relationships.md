## Beyond the Basic Right Triangle

The methods in [[Trig_Finding_Sides_and_Angles]] apply directly to right triangles. This topic extends those tools to situations that are not immediately right triangles but can be reduced to them, and introduces two new laws for any triangle.

---

## Breaking Non-Right Triangles Into Right Triangles

Any triangle can be split by dropping a **perpendicular (altitude)** from one vertex to the opposite side. This creates two right triangles — each solvable with standard methods.

> [!tip] When to use this If you have an obtuse triangle or an arbitrary triangle with no right angle, drop the altitude and solve two right triangles instead of one. This is the foundation of both the sine rule and the cosine rule.

---

## The Sine Rule

For any triangle with sides $a$, $b$, $c$ opposite to angles $A$, $B$, $C$:

$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$

**Use when:** you know two angles and one side (AAS or ASA), or two sides and a non-included angle (SSA — careful: ambiguous case).

---

## The Cosine Rule

$$a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A$$

Rearranged to find an angle:

$$\cos A = \frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc}$$

**Use when:** you know all three sides (SSS), or two sides and the included angle (SAS).

> [!tip] When $A = 90°$, the cosine rule becomes Pythagoras $\cos 90° = 0$, so the $2bc\cos A$ term vanishes: $a^2 = b^2 + c^2$. The cosine rule is a generalisation of Pythagoras.

---

## Area of Any Triangle

$$A = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$$

where $a$ and $b$ are two sides and $C$ is the **included** angle between them.

For a right triangle this becomes $\frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{leg}_1 \cdot \text{leg}_2$ since $\sin 90° = 1$.

---

## Choosing Between Sine Rule and Cosine Rule

|Given|Use|
|---|---|
|Two angles + any side (AAS, ASA)|Sine rule|
|Two sides + non-included angle (SSA)|Sine rule (check for ambiguous case)|
|Two sides + included angle (SAS)|Cosine rule|
|Three sides (SSS)|Cosine rule|

> [!warning] The ambiguous case (SSA) When you know two sides and a non-included angle, there may be **two possible triangles**, one, or none. Always check whether a second solution exists by testing if $180° - \text{found angle}$ also produces a valid triangle.

## Trigonometry in Circles — Connecting to the Previous Unit

The sine rule has a direct link to circle geometry from [[../Circle_Geometry_MOC|Circle Geometry]]:

$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = 2R$$

where $R$ is the circumradius of the triangle. The ratio of any side to the sine of its opposite angle equals the diameter of the circumscribed circle.

This connects the inscribed angle theorem from [[Central_and_Inscribed_Angles]] directly to the sine rule.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1 — Sine rule to find a side**

Triangle with $A = 40°$, $B = 75°$, $a = 8$. Find $b$.

$C = 180° - 40° - 75° = 65°$

$$\frac{b}{\sin 75°} = \frac{8}{\sin 40°} \implies b = \frac{8 \cdot \sin 75°}{\sin 40°} \approx \frac{8 \cdot 0.9659}{0.6428} \approx 12.02$$

---

**Example 2 — Cosine rule to find a side**

Triangle with $b = 7$, $c = 10$, $A = 50°$. Find $a$.

$$a^2 = 49 + 100 - 2(7)(10)\cos 50° = 149 - 140 \cdot 0.6428 \approx 149 - 89.99 = 59.01$$ $$a \approx 7.68$$

---

**Example 3 — Cosine rule to find an angle**

Triangle with $a = 5$, $b = 7$, $c = 9$. Find angle $C$.

$$\cos C = \frac{25 + 49 - 81}{2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7} = \frac{-7}{70} = -0.1 \implies C = \cos^{-1}(-0.1) \approx 95.7°$$

---

**Example 4 — Area of a triangle**

Two sides 6 and 9, included angle 120°.

$$A = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 6 \cdot 9 \cdot \sin 120° = 27 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{27\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 23.38 \text{ cm}^2$$

## Practice Problems

1. Triangle: $A = 35°$, $B = 80°$, $c = 12$. Find all missing sides and angles.
2. Triangle: $a = 6$, $b = 8$, $C = 60°$. Find $c$ and the area.
3. Triangle: $a = 5$, $b = 7$, $c = 8$. Find all three angles.
4. Triangle: $a = 9$, $A = 40°$, $B = 55°$. Find $b$, $c$ and the area.
5. Two sides 10 and 14, included angle 45°. Find the third side and the area exactly.
6. A triangle has $a = 7$, $b = 10$, $A = 30°$. Find all possible values of $B$ (ambiguous case).
7. A triangle is inscribed in a circle of radius 6. One angle is 40°. Find the side opposite that angle.
