## What is an Identity?

A trigonometric identity is an equation involving trig functions that is **true for all valid values** of the angle — not just a specific one. Identities are used to simplify expressions, prove equalities, and solve equations.

---

## The Fundamental Identity

$$\sin^2\alpha + \cos^2\alpha = 1$$

**Proof:** In a right triangle with hypotenuse $c$, $\sin\alpha = \frac{a}{c}$, $\cos\alpha = \frac{b}{c}$, so $\sin^2\alpha + \cos^2\alpha = \frac{a^2 + b^2}{c^2} = \frac{c^2}{c^2} = 1$ by Pythagoras.

This is the most important identity. Everything else follows from it.

---

## Derived Identities

Dividing $\sin^2\alpha + \cos^2\alpha = 1$ by $\cos^2\alpha$:

$$\tan^2\alpha + 1 = \frac{1}{\cos^2\alpha}$$

Dividing by $\sin^2\alpha$:

$$1 + \frac{1}{\tan^2\alpha} = \frac{1}{\sin^2\alpha}$$

---

## The Tangent Identity

$$\tan\alpha = \frac{\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha}$$

This was established in [[Trig_Sine_Cosine_Tangent]] from the definitions. It is both a definition and an identity.

---

## Complementary Angle Identities

From [[Trig_Sine_Cosine_Tangent]]:

$$\sin\alpha = \cos(90° - \alpha) \qquad \cos\alpha = \sin(90° - \alpha) \qquad \tan\alpha = \frac{1}{\tan(90° - \alpha)}$$

---

## Using Identities to Simplify Expressions

The strategy is to rewrite everything in terms of $\sin$ and $\cos$, then apply $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$.

> [!tip] Three standard moves
> 
> 1. Replace $\tan$ with $\frac{\sin}{\cos}$
> 2. Use $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$ to replace $\sin^2 = 1 - \cos^2$ or $\cos^2 = 1 - \sin^2$
> 3. Factor or cancel

---

## Using Identities to Solve Equations

When a trigonometric equation contains two different functions (e.g. both $\sin$ and $\cos$), use an identity to reduce it to one function, then solve.

**Common substitutions:**

- Replace $\cos^2\alpha$ with $1 - \sin^2\alpha$ to get a quadratic in $\sin\alpha$
- Replace $\sin^2\alpha$ with $1 - \cos^2\alpha$ to get a quadratic in $\cos\alpha$
- Replace $\tan\alpha$ with $\frac{\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha}$ then multiply through by $\cos\alpha$

## Worked Examples

**Example 1 — Simplify**

Simplify $\sin^2\alpha + \cos^2\alpha + \tan^2\alpha$.

$$= 1 + \tan^2\alpha = \frac{1}{\cos^2\alpha}$$

---

**Example 2 — Prove an identity**

Prove: $\dfrac{\sin^2\alpha}{1 - \cos\alpha} = 1 + \cos\alpha$

LHS $= \dfrac{1 - \cos^2\alpha}{1 - \cos\alpha} = \dfrac{(1-\cos\alpha)(1+\cos\alpha)}{1-\cos\alpha} = 1 + \cos\alpha$ = RHS ✓

---

**Example 3 — Solve using identity**

Solve $2\sin^2\alpha - \cos\alpha - 1 = 0$ for $0° \leq \alpha \leq 90°$.

Replace $\sin^2\alpha = 1 - \cos^2\alpha$:

$$2(1 - \cos^2\alpha) - \cos\alpha - 1 = 0$$ $$2 - 2\cos^2\alpha - \cos\alpha - 1 = 0$$ $$-2\cos^2\alpha - \cos\alpha + 1 = 0$$ $$2\cos^2\alpha + \cos\alpha - 1 = 0$$ $$(2\cos\alpha - 1)(\cos\alpha + 1) = 0$$

$\cos\alpha = \frac{1}{2} \implies \alpha = 60°$ (from [[Trig_Exact_Values]])

$\cos\alpha = -1 \implies \alpha = 180°$ — outside $[0°, 90°]$, discard.

**Answer: $\alpha = 60°$**

---

**Example 4 — Find sin given cos**

$\cos\alpha = \frac{3}{5}$, $\alpha$ acute. Find $\sin\alpha$ and $\tan\alpha$.

$$\sin^2\alpha = 1 - \frac{9}{25} = \frac{16}{25} \implies \sin\alpha = \frac{4}{5}$$

$$\tan\alpha = \frac{4/5}{3/5} = \frac{4}{3}$$

---

**Example 5 — Prove**

Prove: $\dfrac{1 - \sin^2\alpha}{\cos\alpha} = \cos\alpha$

$$\frac{\cos^2\alpha}{\cos\alpha} = \cos\alpha \checkmark$$

---

## Identity Proof Technique

When proving an identity $\text{LHS} = \text{RHS}$:

- Work on **one side only** — do not move terms across the equals sign
- Start with the more complicated side
- Aim to reach the simpler side
- Never assume what you are trying to prove

2. Simplify: $(1 - \sin\alpha)(1 + \sin\alpha)$
3. Prove: $\sin^2\alpha(1 + \tan^2\alpha) = \tan^2\alpha$
4. Prove: $\dfrac{\tan\alpha}{\sin\alpha} = \dfrac{1}{\cos\alpha}$
5. Solve for $0° \leq \alpha \leq 90°$: $\sin^2\alpha + \sin\alpha = 0$
6. Solve for $0° \leq \alpha \leq 90°$: $2\cos^2\alpha + 3\sin\alpha - 3 = 0$
7. Given $\tan\alpha = \frac{5}{12}$, find $\sin\alpha$ and $\cos\alpha$ using identities only (no triangle drawing).
8. Prove: $\cos^4\alpha - \sin^4\alpha = \cos^2\alpha - \sin^2\alpha$
