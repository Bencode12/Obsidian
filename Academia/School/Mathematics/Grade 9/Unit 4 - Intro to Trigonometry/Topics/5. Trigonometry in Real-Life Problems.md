## Problem-Solving Strategy

Real-life trig problems give you a situation in words. The method is always:

```
1. Draw a diagram — label all given measurements
2. Identify the right triangle (or reduce to one)
3. Label sides as opposite / adjacent / hypotenuse
4. Choose the correct ratio or law
5. Solve
6. Check the answer makes sense in context
```

> [!tip] The diagram is not optional In almost every real-life trig problem, drawing and labelling a diagram before touching the algebra is what makes the solution clear. Without a diagram, you will often use the wrong sides.

## Key Vocabulary

|Term|Meaning|Diagram note|
|---|---|---|
|**Angle of elevation**|Angle measured **upward** from horizontal to an object above|Measured from the observer's eye level up|
|**Angle of depression**|Angle measured **downward** from horizontal to an object below|Measured from the observer's eye level down|
|**Bearing**|Direction measured clockwise from North (e.g. 045° means NE)|Always three digits|
|**Gradient / slope**|Rise over run — directly $\tan\alpha$ where $\alpha$ is the angle from horizontal||

> [!tip] Elevation and depression are equal alternate angles If you see the angle of elevation from A to B as $\alpha$, then the angle of depression from B to A is also $\alpha$ — they are alternate interior angles on parallel horizontal lines.

## Standard Problem Types

### Heights and distances

A vertical object (tower, tree, cliff) and a horizontal ground. The right angle is always between the vertical and the ground.

$$\tan(\text{elevation angle}) = \frac{\text{height}}{\text{horizontal distance}}$$

### Two observers / two angles

Observer sees an object at one angle from one position, moves, sees it at a different angle. Requires two equations and usually the sine rule or a shared side.

### Navigation and bearings

Draw North lines at each point. The bearing gives the angle from North clockwise. Convert to interior angles of the triangle, then apply the sine or cosine rule.

### Slopes and ramps

The slope angle $\alpha$ satisfies $\tan\alpha = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1 — Angle of elevation**

A person stands 40 m from the base of a tower. The angle of elevation to the top is 55°. Find the height of the tower.

$$h = 40 \cdot \tan 55° \approx 40 \cdot 1.428 = 57.1 \text{ m}$$

---

**Example 2 — Angle of depression**

From the top of a cliff 80 m high, the angle of depression of a boat is 23°. Find the distance from the boat to the base of the cliff.

The angle of depression = 23°, so the angle at the base (from horizontal) is also 23°.

$$d = \frac{80}{\tan 23°} \approx \frac{80}{0.4245} \approx 188.5 \text{ m}$$

---

**Example 3 — Two angles**

From point A, the angle of elevation of a tower top is 30°. From point B, 50 m closer along the same horizontal line, it is 45°. Find the height of the tower.

Let the height be $h$, distance from B to base be $d$.

From B: $h = d\tan 45° = d$, so $d = h$.

From A: $h = (d + 50)\tan 30° = (h + 50) \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$

$$h\sqrt{3} = h + 50 \implies h(\sqrt{3} - 1) = 50 \implies h = \frac{50}{\sqrt{3} - 1} = \frac{50(\sqrt{3}+1)}{2} = 25(\sqrt{3}+1) \approx 68.3 \text{ m}$$

---

**Example 4 — Slope**

A road rises 12 m for every 100 m of horizontal distance. Find the angle of inclination.

$$\tan\alpha = \frac{12}{100} = 0.12 \implies \alpha = \tan^{-1}(0.12) \approx 6.84°$$

---

**Example 5 — Navigation**

A ship sails 20 km on a bearing of 060°, then turns and sails 15 km on a bearing of 150°. Find the straight-line distance back to the start.

The angle between the two legs: $150° - 60° = 90°$ — they are perpendicular.

$$d = \sqrt{20^2 + 15^2} = \sqrt{625} = 25 \text{ km}$$

## Common Mistakes

> [!warning]
> 
> - Confusing angle of elevation with angle of depression — always draw the horizontal reference line
> - Using the wrong side as "opposite" — label the triangle before calculating
> - Forgetting that the angle of depression from the top equals the angle of elevation from the bottom
> - Not checking whether the answer is reasonable (e.g. a tree 500 m tall from a 10° angle at 50 m is clearly wrong)

## Practice Problems

1. A ladder 6 m long leans against a wall, making an angle of 70° with the ground. How high up the wall does it reach?
2. From the top of a building 50 m high, the angle of depression of a car is 32°. How far is the car from the base of the building?
3. A surveyor stands 120 m from a cliff. The angle of elevation to the top is 42°. Find the height.
4. From two points A and B on a horizontal line, 60 m apart, the angles of elevation of a balloon are 40° and 55° respectively (both on the same side). Find the height of the balloon.
5. A ramp rises 1.5 m over a horizontal distance of 8 m. What angle does it make with the ground?
6. A ship leaves port and sails 30 km north, then 45 km east. What bearing must it sail to return directly to port, and how far is it?
7. Two buildings are 80 m apart. From the top of the shorter building (height 25 m), the angle of elevation to the top of the taller building is 18°. Find the height of the taller building.
