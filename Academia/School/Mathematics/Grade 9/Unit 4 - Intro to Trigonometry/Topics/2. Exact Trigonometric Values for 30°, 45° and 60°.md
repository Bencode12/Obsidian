## Why These Three Angles?

These are the only acute angles with exact, clean trigonometric values derivable from simple geometry — a half-equilateral triangle (for 30° and 60°) and a half-square (for 45°). Every other angle requires a calculator. These three appear constantly in exam problems precisely because they give exact answers.

---

## Deriving the Values

### 45° — from a square / isosceles right triangle

Take a right isosceles triangle with legs of length 1. Hypotenuse = $\sqrt{2}$. Both acute angles are 45°.

$$\sin 45° = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \qquad \cos 45° = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \qquad \tan 45° = \frac{1}{1} = 1$$

### 30° and 60° — from an equilateral triangle

Take an equilateral triangle with side length 2. Drop the altitude — it bisects the base, giving a right triangle with hypotenuse 2, base 1, height $\sqrt{3}$.

The angles are 30° (at the top) and 60° (at the base).

For 30°: opposite = 1, adjacent = $\sqrt{3}$, hypotenuse = 2

$$\sin 30° = \frac{1}{2} \qquad \cos 30° = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \qquad \tan 30° = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$$

For 60°: opposite = $\sqrt{3}$, adjacent = 1, hypotenuse = 2

$$\sin 60° = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \qquad \cos 60° = \frac{1}{2} \qquad \tan 60° = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{1} = \sqrt{3}$$

## The Table

| $30°$  | $45°$                 | $60°$                 |                       |
| ------ | --------------------- | --------------------- | --------------------- |
| $\sin$ | $\dfrac{1}{2}$        | $\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ | $\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ |
| $\cos$ | $\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ | $\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ | $\dfrac{1}{2}$        |
| $\tan$ | $\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$ | $1$                   | $\sqrt{3}$            |

> [!tip] Pattern — read sine across, cos is sine backwards $\sin$ goes $\frac{1}{2},\ \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\ \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ — numerators are $\sqrt{1}, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}$. $\cos$ is the same sequence **reversed**. This works because $\cos\alpha = \sin(90° - \alpha)$ — from [[Trig_Sine_Cosine_Tangent]].

---

## How to Memorise the Table — One Triangle

Draw the two triangles once, label all sides, and read off every value. Reproducing the triangles from scratch is faster and more reliable than memorising 9 separate numbers.

**45-45-90:** legs = 1, 1 — hypotenuse = $\sqrt{2}$

**30-60-90:** short leg = 1, long leg = $\sqrt{3}$, hypotenuse = 2

---

## Using Exact Values in Calculations

When a problem gives a 30°, 45° or 60° angle, leave the answer in exact form unless told otherwise.

> [!tip] Rationalising the denominator $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$ — multiply top and bottom by $\sqrt{3}$. $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ — multiply top and bottom by $\sqrt{2}$. In Lithuanian school exams, rationalised form is expected.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Find the exact value of $\sin 30° \cdot \cos 60° + \cos 30° \cdot \sin 60°$.

$$= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{3}{4} = 1$$

_(This is $\sin(30° + 60°) = \sin 90° = 1$ — a preview of [[Trig_Identities]].)_

---

**Example 2.** A right triangle has hypotenuse 8 and one angle 30°. Find the exact lengths of both legs.

Leg opposite 30°: $8 \cdot \sin 30° = 8 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 4$

Leg adjacent to 30°: $8 \cdot \cos 30° = 8 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 4\sqrt{3}$

See [[Trig_Finding_Sides_and_Angles]] for the full method.

---

**Example 3.** Find the exact value of $\tan^2 60° - \sin^2 45°$.

$$= (\sqrt{3})^2 - \left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)^2 = 3 - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{5}{2}$$

## Practice Problems

1. Find the exact value of $2\sin 60° \cdot \cos 30°$.
2. Find the exact value of $\tan 45° + \sin 30° \cdot \cos 60°$.
3. Simplify: $\sin^2 30° + \sin^2 45° + \sin^2 60°$.
4. A right triangle has one leg 6 cm and the opposite angle 45°. Find all sides exactly.
5. A right triangle has hypotenuse $4\sqrt{3}$ and one angle 60°. Find all sides exactly.
6. Find the exact value of $\dfrac{\tan 60°}{\sin 60° + \cos 30°}$.
7. Prove using exact values that $\sin^2\alpha + \cos^2\alpha = 1$ holds for $\alpha = 30°$, $45°$, $60°$.
