A **composite figure** is a shape made by combining or subtracting standard shapes — circles, sectors, segments, triangles, rectangles. The strategy is always the same: **break it into parts you know**.

---

## General Strategy

> [!info] Method
> 
> 1. Identify the component shapes
> 2. Decide: are you **adding** or **subtracting** areas?
> 3. Calculate each part separately
> 4. Combine

For perimeter: trace the **outer boundary only** — internal edges do not count.

---

## Common Composite Shapes

### Circle minus a shape (or shape minus a circle)

$$A = A_{\text{outer}} - A_{\text{inner}}$$

Example: square with a circle cut out → $A = s^2 - \pi r^2$

### Shape plus a semicircle

$$A = A_{\text{rectangle}} + \frac{1}{2}\pi r^2$$

The semicircle perimeter replaces the straight edge — do not double-count that edge.

### Sector plus triangle

This appears when a segment is involved. See [[Arc_and_Segment]] for the breakdown: $$A_{\text{segment}} = A_{\text{sector}} - A_{\triangle}$$

---

## Perimeter of Composite Figures

Perimeter = sum of all **outer edges** only.

> [!warning] When a shape is added to another along a shared edge, that edge disappears from the perimeter. Sketch the figure and trace the outside carefully before writing any formula.

**Typical perimeter components involving circles:**

- Straight edges: use $\ell = $ length of segment
- Curved edges: use arc length $= \dfrac{\alpha}{360°} \cdot 2\pi r$
- If the boundary is a full semicircle: $\pi r$

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** A rectangle 10 × 6 cm has a semicircle attached to one of the short sides (radius = 3 cm). Find the area and perimeter.

$$A = 10 \times 6 + \frac{1}{2}\pi(3)^2 = 60 + 4.5\pi \approx 74.14 \text{ cm}^2$$

Perimeter: two long sides + one short side + semicircle arc (the other short side is replaced by the semicircle): $$P = 10 + 10 + 6 + \pi \cdot 3 = 26 + 3\pi \approx 35.42 \text{ cm}$$

---

**Example 2.** A circle of radius 8 cm has an equilateral triangle inscribed in it. Find the area of the region inside the circle but outside the triangle.

$$A_{\text{circle}} = \pi \cdot 64 = 64\pi$$ For equilateral triangle inscribed in circle of radius $r$: $A_{\triangle} = \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{4} r^2$... wait — the side length is $s = r\sqrt{3}$ for inscribed equilateral triangle: $$s = 8\sqrt{3}, \quad A_{\triangle} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(8\sqrt{3})^2 = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \cdot 192 = 48\sqrt{3}$$ $$A_{\text{region}} = 64\pi - 48\sqrt{3} \approx 201.06 - 83.14 = 117.92 \text{ cm}^2$$

---

**Example 3.** An athletics track has two straight sections of 100 m and two semicircular ends with radius 35 m. Find the total area enclosed.

The enclosed area = rectangle + full circle: $$A = 100 \times 70 + \pi(35)^2 = 7000 + 1225\pi \approx 10{,}848 \text{ m}^2$$

## Key Formulas to Have Ready

|Shape|Area|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|Circle|$\pi r^2$||
|Semicircle|$\frac{1}{2}\pi r^2$||
|Sector|$\frac{\alpha}{360}\pi r^2$|See [[Arc_and_Segment]]|
|Segment|$\frac{\alpha}{360}\pi r^2 - \frac{1}{2}r^2\sin\alpha$|See [[Arc_and_Segment]]|
|Triangle|$\frac{1}{2}bh$ or $\frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$||
|Rectangle|$lw$||
|Square|$s^2$||

## Practice

1. A square of side 12 cm has a circle of radius 4 cm cut from its centre. Find the remaining area.
2. A shape consists of a rectangle 8 × 5 cm with a semicircle on each short end. Find the area and perimeter.
3. A sector with $r = 10$ cm and $\alpha = 120°$ sits on top of an equilateral triangle with the same base as the chord. Find the total area.
4. A running track has straight sections of 80 m and semicircular ends of radius 25 m. Find the total perimeter and enclosed area.
