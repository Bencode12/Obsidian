A geometric proof is a logical argument that uses known facts, theorems, and definitions to show that a statement is always true. In circle geometry, most proofs draw on a small set of repeatedly used tools.

## Proof Structure

Every proof needs three things:

1. **Given** — what you know from the problem
2. **To prove** — what you need to show
3. **Proof** — the logical steps, each justified

> [!tip] How to start when you're stuck
> 
> - Draw the diagram and label everything given
> - Mark any right angles (tangent–radius), equal lengths (radii, tangent segments), or equal angles (same arc)
> - Ask: what do I need? Work backwards from the conclusion to see what intermediate fact would give it to you

## Common Proof Patterns

### Pattern 1 — Show two angles are equal

Usually done by showing both angles subtend the same arc, or both are inscribed angles in the same segment.

**Steps:**

1. Identify the arc each angle subtends
2. If it's the same arc → angles are equal
3. State the reason: _"angles subtending the same arc are equal"_

---

### Pattern 2 — Show a line is tangent

To prove a line is tangent at point A, show that it is **perpendicular to the radius OA**.

**Steps:**

1. Find angle between the line and OA
2. Show it equals 90°
3. Conclude: _"since the line is perpendicular to the radius at A, it is tangent to the circle at A"_

---

### Pattern 3 — Show four points are concyclic (lie on a circle)

Show that the four points satisfy one of:

- Opposite angles sum to 180° (cyclic quadrilateral condition)
- Two points subtend equal angles at the other two points (converse of inscribed angle theorem)

---

### Pattern 4 — Show two triangles are similar or congruent

This often uses the angle relationships from [[Central_and_Inscribed_Angles]]:

- Find two pairs of equal angles (AA similarity)
- Or find equal sides and angles (SAS, ASA congruence)

## Worked Example

**Problem.** AB is a diameter of a circle. C is a point on the circle. The tangent to the circle at C meets the line AB extended at T. Prove that $TC^2 = TA \cdot TB$.

**Given:** AB is a diameter, C is on the circle, TC is tangent at C.

**To prove:** $TC^2 = TA \cdot TB$

**Proof:**

- Since TC is tangent at C and OC is a radius: $\angle OCA = 90°$ — wait, use the Power of a Point directly.
- TC is a tangent from T, AB is a secant from T passing through A and B.
- By the **Power of a Point**: $TC^2 = TA \cdot TB$ ✓

_(reason: for a tangent and secant from the same external point, the square of the tangent equals the product of the two secant distances)_

---

## Worked Example 2

**Problem.** Points A, B, C, D lie on a circle. AB is parallel to CD. Prove that arc AC = arc BD.

**Proof:**

- Since AB ∥ CD, the two arcs between them (AC and BD) are cut off by parallel chords.
- Parallel chords in a circle cut off equal arcs between them.
- Therefore arc AC = arc BD. ✓

_(Alternative: use inscribed angles — $\angle BAC = \angle BDC$ since same arc BC, then argue from the parallel lines)_

## Writing Style

> [!tip] What examiners want to see
> 
> - Every step has a **reason** in brackets or after a dash
> - Reasons use proper theorem names: _"inscribed angle theorem"_, _"tangent–radius perpendicularity"_, _"isosceles triangle (OA = OB = r)"_
> - No skipped steps — if it seems obvious, state it anyway
> - End with a clear concluding statement: _"Therefore... as required"_ or _"∴ proved"_

## Practice Proofs

1. AB is a diameter. C is on the circle. Prove $\angle ACB = 90°$.
2. Two tangents from external point P touch a circle at A and B. Prove PA = PB.
3. ABCD is a cyclic quadrilateral. Prove $\angle DAB + \angle BCD = 180°$.
4. From external point P, a tangent touches at T and a secant passes through A and B. Prove $PT^2 = PA \cdot PB$.
5. Chord AB and chord CD intersect inside a circle at point P. Prove $PA \cdot PB = PC \cdot PD$.
