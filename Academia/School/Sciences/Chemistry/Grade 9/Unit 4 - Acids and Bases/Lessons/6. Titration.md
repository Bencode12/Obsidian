## 📐 Variables — Definitions

> [!info] Independent Variable (IV) The variable that is **deliberately altered** by the experimenter. It is the cause in a cause-and-effect relationship.

> [!info] Dependent Variable (DV) The variable that is **measured or observed** as a result of changes to the IV. It depends on the IV.

> [!info] Controlled Variable (CV) A variable that is **kept constant** throughout the experiment to ensure a fair test — so that any changes in the DV can be attributed only to the IV.

> [!example] Quick examples
> 
> - A person's **age** = independent variable (not changed by other factors you're measuring)
> - A **test score** = dependent variable (depends on study time, sleep, hunger, etc.)

---

## ⚗️ Variables in a Titration Experiment

|Variable type|In a titration|
|---|---|
|**Independent**|Volume of titrant (standard solution) added from the burette|
|**Dependent**|pH of the solution in the conical flask (measured by indicator colour change or pH probe)|
|**Controlled**|Volume of analyte (fixed by pipette); concentration of standard solution; temperature; same indicator and amount added; same glassware throughout|

## 📈 pH Curves — Reading and Interpreting

A **titration curve** (pH curve) is a graph of pH (y-axis) against volume of titrant added (x-axis).

> [!tip] What to look for on a pH curve
> 
> - **Starting pH** — tells you about the initial acid/base strength and concentration
> - **Buffer region** — gradual slope (present in weak acid/strong base titrations)
> - **Equivalence point** — steepest point of the curve (largest pH change per drop added)
> - **End pH** — tells you about the excess reagent

### Typical shapes

|Titration|Start pH|Equivalence point pH|Curve shape|
|---|---|---|---|
|Strong acid + Strong base|Low (~1)|7|Steep S-curve at pH 7|
|Weak acid + Strong base|Medium (~3–4)|> 7|Shallower; buffer region visible|
|Strong acid + Weak base|Low (~1)|< 7|Steep jump below 7|

### Choosing the right indicator from the pH curve

The indicator must change colour **within the steep region** of the titration curve. If the indicator changes colour outside this steep region, the endpoint will not match the equivalence point, introducing systematic error.

- $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ + $\text{NaOH}$ → use **methyl orange** or **phenolphthalein** (both change within the steep region)
- Weak acid + $\text{NaOH}$ → use **phenolphthalein** only (equivalence point is basic, ~pH 8–10)

## 🧮 Titration Calculations

### Core formula

$$n = c \times V \quad \Rightarrow \quad c = \frac{n}{V} \quad \Rightarrow \quad V = \frac{n}{c}$$

where $n$ = moles, $c$ = concentration (mol/L), $V$ = volume (L — remember to convert cm³ ÷ 1000)

### Method — four steps

1. **Write a balanced equation** for the reaction
2. **Calculate moles** of the known solution: $n = c \times V$
3. **Use the mole ratio** to find moles of unknown
4. **Calculate concentration or mass** of unknown

## ✏️ Worked Calculation Problems

### Problem 1 — Titration concentration

**Find the concentration of 25 mL $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$, given that 20.55 mL of 0.5 mol/L $\text{NaOH}$ was needed.**

$$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$n(\text{NaOH}) = 0.5 \times 0.02055 = 0.010275 \text{ mol}$$

Mole ratio $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 : \text{NaOH} = 1:2$, so:

$$n(\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4) = \frac{0.010275}{2} = 0.005138 \text{ mol}$$

$$c(\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4) = \frac{0.005138}{0.025} = \boxed{0.206 \text{ mol/L}}$$

---

### Problem 2 — Mass of hydrogen from sodium + water

**Calculate the mass of hydrogen released if 4.6 g of sodium reacts with water.**

$$2\text{Na}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightarrow 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

$M(\text{Na}) = 23$ g/mol

$$n(\text{Na}) = \frac{4.6}{23} = 0.2 \text{ mol}$$

Mole ratio $\text{Na} : \text{H}_2 = 2:1$:

$$n(\text{H}_2) = \frac{0.2}{2} = 0.1 \text{ mol}$$

$$m(\text{H}_2) = 0.1 \times 2 = \boxed{0.2 \text{ g}}$$

---

### Problem 3 — Mass of salt from NaOH + CO₂

**Calculate the mass of salt formed if 20 g of NaOH reacts with 10 g of CO₂.**

$$2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} + \text{CO}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_{3(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$n(\text{NaOH}) = \frac{20}{40} = 0.5 \text{ mol}, \quad n(\text{CO}_2) = \frac{10}{44} = 0.227 \text{ mol}$$

Check limiting reagent: need 2 mol NaOH per mol $\text{CO}_2$, so 0.227 mol $\text{CO}_2$ needs $0.227 \times 2 = 0.454$ mol NaOH. We have 0.5 mol — so **$\text{CO}_2$ is the limiting reagent**.

$$n(\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3) = n(\text{CO}_2) = 0.227 \text{ mol}$$

$$m(\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3) = 0.227 \times 106 = \boxed{24.1 \text{ g}}$$

---

### Problem 4 — Mass of AlCl₃ from Al(OH)₃ + HCl

**10 g of $\text{Al(OH)}_3$ reacts with 300 mL of 8% HCl (density 1.12 g/cm³). Find mass of $\text{AlCl}_3$.**

$$\text{Al(OH)}_{3(s)} + 3\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{AlCl}_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$n(\text{Al(OH)}_3) = \frac{10}{78} = 0.1282 \text{ mol}$$

Mass of HCl solution: $m = 300 \times 1.12 = 336$ g

Mass of HCl: $m(\text{HCl}) = 336 \times \frac{8}{100} = 26.88$ g

$$n(\text{HCl}) = \frac{26.88}{36.5} = 0.736 \text{ mol}$$

Check limiting reagent: need $3 \times 0.1282 = 0.384$ mol HCl for all $\text{Al(OH)}_3$. We have 0.736 mol — so **$\text{Al(OH)}_3$ is the limiting reagent**.

$$n(\text{AlCl}_3) = n(\text{Al(OH)}_3) = 0.1282 \text{ mol}$$

$$m(\text{AlCl}_3) = 0.1282 \times 133.5 = \boxed{17.1 \text{ g}}$$
