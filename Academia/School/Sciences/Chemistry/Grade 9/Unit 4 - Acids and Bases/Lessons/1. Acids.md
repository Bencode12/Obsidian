## ⚗️ Arrhenius Acids — Definition

The **Arrhenius theory** was proposed by Swedish chemist **Svante Arrhenius in 1884**. He classified compounds as acids or bases based on the ions they produce in water.

> [!info] Arrhenius Definition An **acid** is a substance that produces **hydrogen ions ($\text{H}^+$)** or **hydronium ions ($\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$)** when dissolved in water.

$$\text{HCl} \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \text{H}^+ + \text{Cl}^-$$

In practice, the bare proton $\text{H}^+$ doesn't exist freely in water — it immediately bonds to a water molecule to form the **hydronium ion**:

$$\text{H}^+ + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{H}_3\text{O}^+$$

Both notations ($\text{H}^+$ and $\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$) are accepted and mean the same thing in aqueous chemistry.

---

## 🔬 Acids vs Hydronium Ions

> [!example] Example — HBr In a 2 mol/L aqueous solution of hydrobromic acid (HBr), complete dissociation gives approximately 2 mol/L of $\text{H}^+$ (or equivalently $\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$) ions, since HBr is a strong acid.

$$\text{HBr}_{\text{(aq)}} \rightarrow \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{Br}^-_{\text{(aq)}}$$

## 📋 General Properties of Acids

- pH below 7.0 in aqueous solution
- Sour taste (e.g. citric acid in lemons, acetic acid in vinegar)
- Releases $\text{H}^+$ ions in water
- Changes colour of indicators (e.g. turns blue litmus **red**)
- Reacts with metals, bases, metal oxides, metal carbonates, and salts

## 🧪 Named Acids — Properties & Uses

### Hydrochloric Acid — $\text{HCl}$

Also known as: _marine acid, muriatic acid, spirit of salt_

Colourless, highly corrosive liquid that fumes in air. Industrial muriatic acid is 20–35% HCl; household grade is 10–12%. Found naturally in **gastric juice** (stomach acid). Used for metal cleaning, PVC production, and pH adjustment.

### Nitric Acid — $\text{HNO}_3$

Also known as: _aqua fortis, azotic acid, engraver's acid_

Strong colourless mineral acid. Reacts with proteins to form **yellow nitrated products** (xanthoproteic reaction — useful safety indicator). Used to manufacture **explosives, dyes, fertilisers**, and as an industrial oxidiser.

### Sulphuric Acid — $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$

Also known as: _oil of vitriol, battery acid, dipping acid_

Corrosive strong mineral acid, normally clear to slightly yellow. Causes both **chemical burns** and **thermal burns** (exothermic dehydration reaction with organic matter). Used in **lead-acid batteries, drain cleaners**, and chemical synthesis. The most widely produced industrial chemical globally.

### Phosphoric Acid — $\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4$

Also known as: _orthophosphoric acid, trihydrogen phosphate_

Colourless, odourless. A **weak acid**. Used in **fertilisers, detergents, food and beverages** (gives sharp flavour and acts as preservative in cola drinks), home cleaning products, and as a **rust inhibitor** and dental etchant.

### Carbonic Acid — $\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$

Also known as: _aerial acid, acid of air, dihydrogen carbonate_

A **weak inorganic acid** that only exists in solution — pure $\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$ cannot be isolated. Found in all **carbonated drinks**. The only acid excreted by the **lungs as a gas** ($\text{CO}_2$).

$$\text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$$

### Sulphurous Acid — $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$

Formed when sulphur dioxide dissolves in water. Like carbonic acid, pure anhydrous $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$ has **never been isolated**. Used as a food preservative and bleaching agent.

$$\text{SO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$$

### Hydrogen Sulphide — $\text{H}_2\text{S}$

Also known as: _hydrosulphuric acid, sewer gas, stink damp_

Colourless gas with a distinctive **rotten-egg smell**. Harmless in trace quantities but toxic at higher concentrations. About 95% of $\text{H}_2\text{S}$ emissions are natural (volcanic, bacterial); only ~5% are human-made.

> [!tip] Pattern Acids **without oxygen** (hydroacids) have no corresponding acidic oxide. Acids **with oxygen** (oxoacids) are formed by reacting the acidic oxide with water.

## 🔬 Obtaining Acids

### Hydroacids — direct combination of hydrogen with non-metal

$$\text{H}_2 + \text{Cl}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{HCl}$$

$$\text{H}_2 + \text{S} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{S}$$

### Oxoacids — acidic oxide + water

$$\text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$$

$$\text{SO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$$

$$\text{N}_2\text{O}_5 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\text{HNO}_3$$

$$\text{SO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$$

$$\text{P}_2\text{O}_5 + 3\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4$$

## 🧮 Calculations — Solution Concentration

> [!note] Key formula $$c = \frac{n}{V} \quad \text{where } c = \text{concentration (mol/L)}, ; n = \text{moles}, ; V = \text{volume (L)}$$ For percentage solutions: $m_{\text{solute}} = m_{\text{solution}} \times \frac{%}{100}$ Mass of solution: $m_{\text{solution}} = \rho \times V$
