# 1.3 Heat Conduction

## 🔬 What is Conduction?

**Conduction** is the transfer of heat through a material without the material itself moving.

### How Conduction Works

1. Particles in the hot region vibrate faster
2. They collide with neighboring particles
3. Energy is transferred through collisions
4. Heat flows from hot to cold regions

```
HOT END → → → → → COLD END
(High KE)          (Low KE)
```

---

## 📊 Conductors and Insulators

### Good Conductors
Materials that transfer heat easily:

| Material | Use |
|----------|-----|
| Copper | Cooking pans, heat exchangers |
| Aluminum | Radiators, foil |
| Silver | Best conductor (expensive) |
| Iron | Engine parts |

### Poor Conductors (Insulators)
Materials that resist heat flow:

| Material | Use |
|----------|-----|
| Wood | Handles of cooking utensils |
| Plastic | Insulation |
| Air | Double glazing, loft insulation |
| Fiberglass | Building insulation |
| Wool | Clothing |

---

## 🧮 Factors Affecting Conduction

### Rate of Heat Transfer

```
Q/t = k × A × ΔT / d
```

Where:
- **Q/t** = Rate of heat transfer (J/s or W)
- **k** = Thermal conductivity (W/m·K)
- **A** = Cross-sectional area (m²)
- **ΔT** = Temperature difference (K or °C)
- **d** = Thickness of material (m)

### Key Points:
- **Larger area** → Faster heat transfer
- **Greater temperature difference** → Faster heat transfer
- **Thicker material** → Slower heat transfer
- **Higher conductivity** → Faster heat transfer

## 🧪 Real-World Applications

### 1. Cooking Pans
- Copper/aluminum base for good conduction
- Plastic/wooden handles (insulators)

### 2. Home Insulation
- Fiberglass in walls/loft
- Double glazing (air gap)
- Reduces heat loss

### 3. Thermos Flask
- Double wall with vacuum
- No particles = no conduction
- Keeps drinks hot/cold

### 4. Heat Sinks
- Metal fins on electronics
- Large surface area
- Conducts heat away from components

## ✏️ Practice Questions

### Question 1
**Explain** why metal spoons feel colder than wooden spoons at room temperature.

**Answer:** Metal is a better conductor, so it conducts heat away from your hand faster, making it feel colder.

### Question 2
**Calculate** the rate of heat transfer through a copper rod:
- k = 400 W/m·K
- A = 0.01 m²
- ΔT = 50°C
- d = 0.5 m

**Solution:**
```
Q/t = k × A × ΔT / d
Q/t = 400 × 0.01 × 50 / 0.5
Q/t = 400 W
```

## 📝 Summary

- Conduction transfers heat through particle collisions
- Metals are good conductors
- Non-metals and gases are poor conductors (insulators)
- Rate depends on: material, area, temperature difference, thickness
- Many practical applications in daily life
