## Strand i — Knowledge Questions by Band

### Level 1–2 — State

1. List two factors that affect the freezing point of a substance.
2. State the two main ways to change the internal energy of a body.
3. State the main unit of temperature in the SI system.

> [!example] Model answers — Level 1–2
> 
> 1. Presence of dissolved substances (e.g. salt); pressure
> 2. Doing work on/by the body; heat transfer
> 3. Kelvin (K); Celsius (°C) is also accepted in most contexts

---

### Level 3–4 — Outline

4. Outline one similarity and one difference between boiling and evaporation.
5. Outline how internal energy and temperature of a body are related.
6. Outline what boiling is.
7. Outline why metals are good conductors of heat.

> [!example] Model answers — Level 3–4 **4.** Similarity: both are processes of vaporisation — liquid turning to gas. Difference: evaporation occurs at any temperature only at the surface; boiling occurs at a specific temperature throughout the entire liquid.
> 
> **5.** Internal energy is the total kinetic and potential energy of all the particles in a body. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of those particles — so higher temperature means higher average particle kinetic energy and generally higher internal energy.
> 
> **6.** Boiling is the rapid vaporisation of a liquid that occurs throughout the liquid (not just at the surface) at a fixed temperature called the boiling point, when the vapour pressure equals atmospheric pressure.
> 
> **7.** Metals contain free electrons that can move easily throughout the structure. When one end is heated, these electrons gain kinetic energy and rapidly transfer it to adjacent particles, making metals efficient conductors.

---

### Level 5–6 — Describe

8. Describe three molecular changes that occur during the process of water freezing.
9. Describe what it means for water to have a specific heat of 4200 J/(kg·°C).
10. Describe what specific latent heat of vaporisation is.
11. Describe how convection occurs.

> [!example] Model answers — Level 5–6 **8.** (i) Water molecules lose kinetic energy and slow down. (ii) The distance between molecules decreases as they form a more ordered, rigid crystal lattice structure. (iii) Hydrogen bonds between molecules become permanent rather than temporary, locking molecules into fixed positions in the ice crystal.
> 
> **9.** A specific heat of 4200 J/(kg·°C) means that 4200 joules of energy must be added to 1 kg of water to raise its temperature by 1°C. Equivalently, 1 kg of water releases 4200 J when its temperature drops by 1°C. This is unusually high compared to most substances, which is why water is used in heating systems.
> 
> **10.** The specific latent heat of vaporisation is the amount of energy required to convert 1 kg of a liquid into vapour at its boiling point, without any change in temperature. The energy goes into breaking intermolecular bonds rather than increasing kinetic energy.
> 
> **11.** Convection occurs in fluids (liquids and gases). When a fluid near a heat source is heated, it expands and becomes less dense. The less dense, warmer fluid rises. Cooler, denser fluid sinks to replace it, creating a convection current that continuously circulates and transfers heat throughout the fluid.

---

### Level 7–8 — Explain

12. Explain why temperature does not change during the process of melting (or boiling).
13. Explain how evaporation occurs on a molecular level.
14. Explain what internal energy of a body is.
15. Explain how energy transfer during conduction happens on a molecular level.

> [!example] Model answers — Level 7–8 **12.** During melting, the energy supplied (latent heat of fusion) is used entirely to **break the bonds** between molecules in the solid lattice, converting the ordered solid structure into the disordered liquid state. None of the energy increases the kinetic energy of the molecules — and since temperature is a measure of average molecular kinetic energy, the temperature remains constant until the phase change is complete.
> 
> **13.** In a liquid, molecules move at a range of speeds. At any moment, some surface molecules have enough kinetic energy to overcome the intermolecular attractions holding them in the liquid and escape into the gas phase. Since the fastest molecules escape, the average kinetic energy — and therefore the temperature — of the remaining liquid decreases. This is why evaporation causes cooling. Evaporation happens at any temperature, unlike boiling which requires a specific temperature.
> 
> **14.** Internal energy is the **total** of all kinetic energies (from translational, rotational, and vibrational motion of particles) and all potential energies (from intermolecular forces between particles) of every particle in the body. It is not the same as temperature, which measures only the average kinetic energy per particle.
> 
> **15.** When one end of a solid conductor is heated, the particles there vibrate more vigorously (higher kinetic energy). These particles collide with neighbouring particles, transferring kinetic energy along the material. In metals, free electrons also absorb energy and move rapidly through the lattice, colliding with other atoms and transferring energy — this makes metals much better conductors than non-metals.

## Strand ii — Application Questions by Band

### Level 1–2 — Suggest solutions (familiar)

1. Name two examples of condensation in nature.
2. What property of matter is used in liquid thermometers?
3. Name two benefits of polar bears having white fur.

> [!example] Model answers
> 
> 1. Morning dew on grass; water droplets forming on a cold glass of water; clouds forming in the atmosphere
> 2. Thermal expansion — the liquid expands when heated and contracts when cooled, causing the level to rise and fall
> 3. White fur reflects sunlight (reduces heat absorption by radiation); hollow fur hairs trap air and provide insulation

---

### Level 3–4 — Solve familiar problems

4. Why does a cat roll into a ball to sleep in winter, but stretch out in summer?
5. Reindeer have hollow-haired fur. How does this help them stay warm?
6. Why are icy pavements sprinkled with salt in winter?
7. Why do pavements crack in winter?

> [!example] Model answers **4.** In winter, curling up reduces the **surface area** exposed to cold air, minimising heat loss by conduction, convection, and radiation. In summer, stretching out **maximises** surface area, allowing more heat to be lost to the surroundings and keeping the cat cool.
> 
> **5.** Hollow hairs trap a layer of air inside. Air is a very poor conductor of heat, so it acts as an insulating layer that prevents body heat from escaping to the cold surroundings.
> 
> **6.** Salt dissolved in water **lowers the freezing point** of water below 0°C. The salt-water mixture therefore remains liquid at temperatures where pure water would freeze, preventing or melting ice on the pavement.
> 
> **7.** Water seeps into cracks in pavements. In winter, this water freezes and expands (~9% increase in volume). The expanding ice exerts large forces on the surrounding material, widening and deepening the cracks.

---

### Level 5–6 — Solve familiar + suggest unfamiliar

8. In winter, a person's moustache, beard, and hair can become covered in frost. Explain how this happens.
9. Explain why you can pick up a hot baking tray with a dry towel but not a wet one.
10. Explain how sweating helps cool the body down.
11. Explain how a pressure cooker works.

> [!example] Model answers **8.** When a person breathes out, warm moist air from the lungs is exhaled. As this warm, humid air meets the very cold outdoor air, it cools rapidly. The water vapour in the breath **condenses** directly into ice (deposition/sublimation in reverse) on cold surfaces like hair and facial hair, forming a layer of frost crystals.
> 
> **9.** A dry towel is a poor conductor of heat and contains trapped air pockets — it does not transfer heat quickly to your hands. A wet towel has water filling those air gaps. Water has a much higher thermal conductivity and specific heat, so heat from the tray conducts through the wet towel rapidly, burning your hand. Additionally, as the water evaporates, steam forms, which carries away large amounts of latent heat — further heating your skin.
> 
> **10.** Sweat (water) on the skin's surface **evaporates**. During evaporation, the fastest-moving (most energetic) water molecules escape from the liquid. This removes energy from the remaining liquid — and by extension from the skin. The latent heat of vaporisation of water is very high ($2.3 \times 10^6\ \text{J/kg}$), so even a small amount of evaporation removes a large amount of heat.
> 
> **11.** A pressure cooker is a sealed vessel. As water is heated inside, it produces steam which cannot escape. The trapped steam increases the **pressure** inside the pot. At higher pressure, the boiling point of water rises above 100°C (since boiling occurs when vapour pressure equals external pressure). Food therefore cooks at a higher temperature, reducing cooking time significantly.

---

### Level 7–8 — Solve familiar and unfamiliar

12. Explain why burns from hot steam are worse and more dangerous than burns from boiling water at the same temperature.
    
13. What shape would a candle flame have if the candle were in free fall? Explain.
    

> [!example] Model answers — Level 7–8 **12.** Both steam and boiling water are at 100°C, so initially both transfer the same amount of heat per degree of temperature difference. However, when **steam** contacts skin, it **condenses** into liquid water. During condensation, the steam releases its **latent heat of vaporisation** ($2.3 \times 10^6\ \text{J/kg}$) directly into the skin in addition to the heat from cooling to body temperature. This is an enormous additional amount of energy that boiling water simply does not carry. The total energy transferred to the skin from steam is therefore much greater, causing deeper and more severe burns.
> 
> **13.** On Earth, a candle flame is elongated because hot combustion gases are less dense and rise due to **convection** — cooler, denser air replaces them from below, creating the upward current that pulls the flame into its teardrop shape. In free fall, there is no effective gravity, so there is no buoyancy-driven convection. The hot gases would spread outward equally in all directions. The flame would therefore take on a **spherical (round) shape**, burning more dimly as oxygen must reach the flame by diffusion rather than convection currents.

## Strand ii — Calculation Questions by Band

### Useful constants

|Quantity|Value|
|---|---|
|Specific latent heat of vaporisation of water|$L_v = 2.3 \times 10^6\ \text{J/kg}$|
|Specific latent heat of fusion of water (ice)|$L_f = 3.3 \times 10^5\ \text{J/kg}$|
|Specific heat capacity of water|$c = 4200\ \text{J/(kg·°C)}$|
|Heat of combustion of coal|$q_{coal} = 2.7 \times 10^7\ \text{J/kg}$|
|Heat of combustion of alcohol|$q_{alc} = 2.7 \times 10^7\ \text{J/kg}$|
|Heat of vaporisation of hydrogen|$L_{H_2} = 0.45 \times 10^6\ \text{J/kg}$|

$$Q = mL \qquad Q = mc\Delta T$$

---

### Level 1–2

**Q: How much heat is released by condensing 3 kg of water vapour?**

$$Q = mL_v = 3 \times 2.3 \times 10^6 = \mathbf{6.9 \times 10^6\ \text{J}}$$

---

### Level 3–4

**Q: 3 kg of a material emits $3.3 \times 10^5\ \text{J}$ of heat while hardening. Find the specific latent heat of fusion.**

$$L = \frac{Q}{m} = \frac{3.3 \times 10^5}{3} = \mathbf{1.1 \times 10^5\ \text{J/kg}}$$

**Q: How much heat is needed to melt 3 kg of ice at 0°C?**

$$Q = mL_f = 3 \times 3.3 \times 10^5 = \mathbf{9.9 \times 10^5\ \text{J}}$$

---

### Level 5–6

**Q: How much coal must be burned to evaporate 3 t of water at its boiling point?**

$$Q_{needed} = mL_v = 3000 \times 2.3 \times 10^6 = 6.9 \times 10^9\ \text{J}$$

$$m_{coal} = \frac{Q}{q_{coal}} = \frac{6.9 \times 10^9}{2.7 \times 10^7} \approx \mathbf{255.6\ \text{kg}}$$

**Q: In an experiment, 700 g of liquid is evaporated using 50 g of burning alcohol. Find the latent heat of vaporisation of the liquid.**

$$Q_{released} = m_{alc} \times q_{alc} = 0.05 \times 2.7 \times 10^7 = 1.35 \times 10^6\ \text{J}$$

$$L = \frac{Q}{m_{liquid}} = \frac{1.35 \times 10^6}{0.7} \approx \mathbf{1.93 \times 10^6\ \text{J/kg}}$$

This is close to the latent heat of vaporisation of **ethanol** ($\approx 8.5 \times 10^5\ \text{J/kg}$) or could indicate **water** if measurement error is considered.

---

### Level 7–8

**Q: What mass of water evaporated if water at 0°C was given 4 MJ of heat?**

The water must first be heated from 0°C to 100°C, then evaporated.

$$Q_1 = mc\Delta T = m \times 4200 \times 100 = 420000m$$

$$Q_2 = mL_v = m \times 2.3 \times 10^6 = 2300000m$$

$$Q_{total} = Q_1 + Q_2 = m(420000 + 2300000) = 2720000m$$

$$m = \frac{4 \times 10^6}{2720000} \approx \mathbf{1.47\ \text{kg}}$$

## Strand iii — Graph Analysis Question

_A heating curve graph shows temperature vs heat added for substance X (initially solid). Use the graph to answer:_

### Level 1–2

- What is the melting point of substance X? _(The flat horizontal section during melting)_
- What is the boiling point of substance X? _(The second flat horizontal section)_
- In which state would substance X be found at room temperature (25°C)?

### Level 3–4

- Calculate the amount of heat used to melt the substance. _(Read the heat values at the start and end of the melting plateau from the x-axis, subtract)_

### Level 5–6

- Calculate the specific latent heat of vaporisation if 5 kg of the substance was used. _(Read $\Delta Q$ from the vaporisation plateau, then $L = \Delta Q / m$)_

### Level 7–8

- Using the table of specific latent heats of vaporisation below, determine what substance X could be.

|Material|Specific latent heat of vaporisation (J/kg)|
|---|---|
|Alcohol|$90 \times 10^4$|
|Ammonia|$136 \times 10^4$|
|Petrol|$41.9 \times 10^4$|
|Ether|$40 \times 10^4$|
|Mercury|$29.3 \times 10^4$|

> [!tip] How to answer at 7–8 Calculate $L$ from the graph, then compare it to the table. Identify the match.
