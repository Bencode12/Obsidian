## 📖 Key Definitions

|Concept|Definition|Unit|
|---|---|---|
|**Mechanical motion**|Change in position of a body relative to other bodies|—|
|**Trajectory**|The curve (path) along which an object moves|—|
|**Distance**|Length of a body's trajectory|$[\text{m}]$|
|**Displacement**|Shortest directed line between start and end position|$[\text{m}]$|
|**Velocity**|Ratio of distance travelled to time of movement|$[\text{m/s}]$|
|**Acceleration**|Rate of change of velocity|$[\text{m/s}^2]$|

---

## 📏 Distance vs Displacement

> [!info] Distance **Distance** is a scalar quantity — it measures the total length of the path taken, regardless of direction.

> [!info] Displacement **Displacement** is a vector quantity — it measures the shortest straight-line distance from the starting point to the ending point, including direction.

### Key difference

If you walk 200 m east and then 100 m back west:

- **Distance** = $200 + 100 = 300\ \text{m}$
- **Displacement** = $200 - 100 = 100\ \text{m}$ (east)

If you walk in a complete circle and return to the start:

- **Distance** = circumference of the circle
- **Displacement** = $0\ \text{m}$

> [!example] Worked example — Titas Titas walks 200 m from school towards the library, then turns back and walks 100 m to the shop.
> 
> - Distance = $200 + 100 = \mathbf{300\ \text{m}}$
> - Displacement = $200 - 100 = \mathbf{100\ \text{m}}$ (towards library)

> [!example] Worked example — Lina Lina walks 800 m from home to school in the morning, then walks 800 m back home after school.
> 
> - Distance = $800 + 800 = \mathbf{1600\ \text{m}}$
> - Displacement = $\mathbf{0\ \text{m}}$ (she returned to her starting point)

## ➡️ Scalar and Vector Quantities

|Type|Definition|Examples|
|---|---|---|
|**Scalar**|Described by a number only — no direction needed|Distance, speed, mass, temperature, volume|
|**Vector**|Requires both a number **and** a direction|Displacement, velocity, acceleration, force|

> [!tip] Quick test Ask: "Does direction matter for this quantity?" If yes → vector. If no → scalar.
> 
> - "The temperature is 20°C" — no direction needed → **scalar**
> - "The student walked 100 m north" — direction matters → **vector**

## ⚙️ Velocity and Acceleration — Formulas

### Velocity

$$\boxed{v = \frac{s}{t}}$$

|Symbol|Quantity|Unit|
|---|---|---|
|$v$|Velocity|$[\text{m/s}]$|
|$s$|Displacement|$[\text{m}]$|
|$t$|Time|$[\text{s}]$|

### Acceleration

$$\boxed{a = \frac{v - v_0}{t}}$$

|Symbol|Quantity|Unit|
|---|---|---|
|$a$|Acceleration|$[\text{m/s}^2]$|
|$v_0$|Initial velocity|$[\text{m/s}]$|
|$v$|Final velocity|$[\text{m/s}]$|
|$t$|Time|$[\text{s}]$|

### Average speed

$$\bar{v} = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}$$

> [!warning] Average speed uses total **distance** (not displacement) divided by total time.

## 🔀 Types of Motion

### By trajectory

|Type|Description|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Linear (rectilinear)**|Moves in a straight line|Car on a motorway|
|**Curvilinear**|Moves along a curve|Ball thrown at an angle|
|**Circular**|Moves in a circle|Planets orbiting the sun|

### By speed

|Type|Description|Acceleration|
|---|---|---|
|**Uniform**|Constant speed, constant direction|$a = 0$|
|**Uniformly variable**|Speed changes by equal amounts each second|$a = \text{constant} \neq 0$|
|**Non-uniformly variable**|Speed changes unpredictably|$a$ varies|

---

## 📐 Uniform Linear Motion

> [!info] Definition **Uniform rectilinear motion** — a body moves in a straight line at a **constant speed**. The position changes by equal distances in equal time intervals.

$$a = 0 \qquad s = v \cdot t \qquad x = x_0 + vt$$

|Symbol|Quantity|
|---|---|
|$x_0$|Initial position|
|$v$|Constant velocity (can be positive or negative)|
|$t$|Time|
|$x$|Position at time $t$|

> [!example] Worked example A runner covers 30 m in 4 s at constant speed. Find velocity. $$v = \frac{s}{t} = \frac{30}{4} = \mathbf{7.5\ \text{m/s}}$$

---

## 📈 Uniformly Variable Motion

> [!info] Definition Speed changes by the **same amount** every second — acceleration is constant ($a = \text{const}$).

Key equations:

$$v = v_0 + at$$

$$s = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$

$$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2as$$

---

## 🍎 Free Fall

> [!info] Free fall Free fall is **uniformly variable motion** under gravity alone (no air resistance). The acceleration due to gravity is: $$g = 10\ \text{m/s}^2 \quad \text{(approximation used in school problems)}$$

For an object dropped from rest ($v_0 = 0$):

$$h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \qquad v = gt \qquad t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}}$$

> [!example] Worked example — apple An apple falls freely for 2 s. Find the height it fell from. $$h = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times 2^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times 4 = \mathbf{20\ \text{m}}$$

> [!example] Worked example — ball A ball is dropped from 50 m. Find the time of fall and final velocity. $$t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 50}{10}} = \sqrt{10} \approx \mathbf{3.16\ \text{s}}$$ $$v = gt = 10 \times 3.16 \approx \mathbf{31.6\ \text{m/s}}$$

## 📝 Practice Problems

1. A runner starts from rest and accelerates at $2\ \text{m/s}^2$ for 5 s.
    
    - What is the runner's speed after 5 s?
    - What distance does the runner cover?
2. A car travelling at $20\ \text{m/s}$ brakes uniformly and stops after 4 s.
    
    - What is the car's acceleration?
    - What distance does it cover while braking?
3. A cyclist travels at $5\ \text{m/s}$ for 10 s, then accelerates at $0.5\ \text{m/s}^2$ for 5 s.
    
    - What is the speed after accelerating?
    - What distance is covered during acceleration?
4. A race car completes the 804,672 m Indy 500 race on a circular track (start = finish). What are the distance and displacement?
    
5. A student walks 100 m east in 50 s, then 40 m west in 20 s. Find: total distance, displacement, average speed.
    
6. A student walked 800 m at a casual pace for 10 min, then 200 m running in 40 s (chased by a dog). Calculate the average velocity.
