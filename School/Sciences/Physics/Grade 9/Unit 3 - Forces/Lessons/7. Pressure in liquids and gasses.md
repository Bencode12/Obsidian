## ⚖️ What is Pressure?

> [!info] Definition **Pressure** is the force applied **per unit area** of a surface.

$$\boxed{P = \frac{F}{S}}$$

|Symbol|Quantity|Unit|
|---|---|---|
|$P$|Pressure|Pascal $[\text{Pa}]$|
|$F$|Force|Newton $[\text{N}]$|
|$S$|Surface area|$[\text{m}^2]$|

Rearranging: $F = P \cdot S$

---

## 🔵 Pascal's Law

> [!info] Pascal's Law **Liquids and gases exert pressure equally in all directions.** When there is an increase in pressure at any point in a **confined fluid**, there is an equal increase at every other point in the container.

This means pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted **unchanged** throughout the entire fluid — regardless of the shape of the container.

> [!example] Everyday example — Toothpaste When you squeeze one end of a toothpaste tube, paste comes out the other end. The pressure you apply at one point is transmitted through the fluid (the paste) equally in all directions — including towards the nozzle. This is Pascal's Law in action.

---

## 🔧 Applications of Pascal's Law

### Hydraulic Lift

A hydraulic lift uses Pascal's Law to **multiply force**. A small force applied over a small area creates a pressure that is transmitted through a fluid to a larger area, producing a much greater output force.

$$P_1 = P_2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{F_1}{S_1} = \frac{F_2}{S_2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad F_2 = F_1 \cdot \frac{S_2}{S_1}$$

> [!tip] Key insight The **pressure** is the same on both sides, but because $S_2 > S_1$, the output force $F_2$ is much larger than the input force $F_1$. This is how a car lift, hydraulic press, and brake systems work.

|Device|Application of Pascal's Law|
|---|---|
|Hydraulic car lift|Small force → large lifting force|
|Hydraulic brakes|Foot pedal pressure transmitted to all four brake pads equally|
|Toothpaste tube|Pressure at one end transmitted to nozzle|
|Syringe|Pressure on plunger transmitted to fluid|

## 📏 Pressure at Different Depths

The deeper you go in a liquid, the greater the weight of fluid above you — and therefore the greater the pressure.

$$\boxed{p = \rho \cdot g \cdot h}$$

|Symbol|Quantity|Unit|
|---|---|---|
|$p$|Pressure|$[\text{Pa}]$|
|$\rho$|Density of fluid|$\left[\dfrac{\text{kg}}{\text{m}^3}\right]$|
|$g$|Free-fall acceleration|$\left[\dfrac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}\right]$ ($\approx 9.8$ or $10\ \text{m/s}^2$)|
|$h$|Height (depth) of liquid above the point|$[\text{m}]$|

> [!note] Key observations
> 
> - Pressure **increases with depth** — the deeper you go, the greater $h$, the greater $p$
> - Pressure depends on the **density of the fluid** — denser fluids (e.g. mercury) exert greater pressure at the same depth than less dense fluids (e.g. water)
> - Pressure does **not** depend on the shape of the container or the total volume of liquid — only on depth

---

## 📐 Derivation of the Depth-Pressure Formula

Starting from the basic definition of pressure and working step by step:

$$P = \frac{F}{S}$$

The force here is the **weight** of the fluid column above the point:

$$F = m \cdot g$$

$$\Rightarrow P = \frac{m \cdot g}{S}$$

Replace mass using density: $m = \rho \cdot V$

$$\Rightarrow P = \frac{\rho \cdot V \cdot g}{S}$$

Replace volume using the column of fluid: $V = S \cdot h$

$$\Rightarrow P = \frac{\rho \cdot S \cdot h \cdot g}{S}$$

$S$ cancels:

$$\Rightarrow \boxed{P = \rho \cdot g \cdot h}$$

> [!tip] Why $S$ cancels The area of the surface and the volume of the column are directly linked — a wider column has more volume but also pushes over a larger area. These effects cancel exactly, which is why pressure at a given depth is the same regardless of the container's width.

## 🔗 Communicating Vessels

**Communicating vessels** are containers connected at the bottom so fluid can flow between them freely.

> [!info] Principle In communicating vessels containing a **stationary, homogeneous** (uniform density) liquid, the **liquid level is the same in all vessels**, regardless of their shape or size.

This follows directly from $p = \rho g h$ — for pressure to equalise at the connection point, $h$ must be the same on both sides when $\rho$ is the same.

### Two different liquids

When two **immiscible** (non-mixing) liquids of different densities are used, the levels are **not** equal. Instead, the pressures at the bottom connection point must be equal:

$$\rho_1 g h_1 = \rho_2 g h_2$$

$g$ cancels from both sides:

$$\rho_1 h_1 = \rho_2 h_2$$

$$\boxed{\frac{h_1}{h_2} = \frac{\rho_2}{\rho_1}}$$

> [!tip] Interpretation The **denser** liquid sits at a **lower height** — it doesn't need as tall a column to produce the same pressure. The **less dense** liquid has a **greater height**.
> 
> Example: if liquid 1 is twice as dense as liquid 2, then $h_1 = \frac{h_2}{2}$ — liquid 1's column is half the height of liquid 2's column.

> [!example] Real-life examples of communicating vessels
> 
> - A spirit level — the bubble centres when both sides are at equal height
> - Water towers and plumbing — water finds the same level in all connected pipes
> - Ship locks (canal locks) — water levels are equalised between chambers
> - A U-tube manometer — used to measure pressure differences between fluids

## 📊 Summary of Formulas

|Formula|Description|
|---|---|
|$P = \dfrac{F}{S}$|Basic definition of pressure|
|$F = P \cdot S$|Force from pressure over an area|
|$p = \rho \cdot g \cdot h$|Pressure at depth $h$ in a fluid|
|$\dfrac{F_1}{S_1} = \dfrac{F_2}{S_2}$|Pascal's Law — hydraulic systems|
|$\rho_1 h_1 = \rho_2 h_2$|Communicating vessels — two liquids|
|$\dfrac{h_1}{h_2} = \dfrac{\rho_2}{\rho_1}$|Height ratio in communicating vessels|

## 📝 Practice Problems

1. A hydraulic lift has a small piston of area $0.01\ \text{m}^2$ and a large piston of area $0.5\ \text{m}^2$. A force of 200 N is applied to the small piston. What is the output force on the large piston?
    
2. Calculate the pressure at the bottom of a swimming pool that is 3 m deep. (Use $\rho_{\text{water}} = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3$, $g = 10\ \text{m/s}^2$)
    
3. A diver descends to a depth of 20 m in sea water (density $1025\ \text{kg/m}^3$). Calculate the pressure due to the water at that depth.
    
4. Two immiscible liquids are placed in a U-tube. Liquid A has a height of 12 cm and density $800\ \text{kg/m}^3$. What is the height of liquid B if its density is $1200\ \text{kg/m}^3$?
    
5. Explain why the walls of a dam are built thicker at the bottom than at the top.
    
6. A force of 500 N is applied to a syringe with a plunger area of $2 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2$. What pressure is transmitted through the fluid?
