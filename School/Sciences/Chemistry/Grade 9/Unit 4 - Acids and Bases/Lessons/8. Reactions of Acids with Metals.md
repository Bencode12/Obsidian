## ⚗️ General Rule

> [!info] Acid + Metal reaction $$\text{Metal} + \text{Acid} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{H}_2 \uparrow$$ Acids react with metals that appear **above hydrogen** in the metal activity series. The metal displaces hydrogen from the acid, producing a salt and hydrogen gas.

Many — but **not all** — metals react with acids. The key determining factor is position in the **activity series**.

---

## 📊 Metal Activity Series

$$\text{K} > \text{Na} > \text{Ca} > \text{Mg} > \text{Al} > \text{Cr} > \text{Zn} > \text{Fe} > \text{Ni} > \text{Sn} > \text{Pb} > [\text{H}] > \text{Cu} > \text{Ag} > \text{Au}$$

> [!tip] Rule Metals placed **before (above)** hydrogen react with dilute acids to release $\text{H}_2$ gas. Metals placed **after (below)** hydrogen (Cu, Ag, Au) do **not** react with dilute HCl or dilute $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$.

---

## 📋 Types of Equations

For every acid-metal reaction you need to be able to write three forms:

|Type|Description|
|---|---|
|**Molecular equation**|Full formulas of all compounds|
|**Ionic equation**|All soluble ionic compounds written as separate ions|
|**Net ionic equation**|Spectator ions cancelled — only species that change|

> [!example] How to write a net ionic equation
> 
> 1. Write the balanced molecular equation
> 2. Split all aqueous ionic compounds into their ions (acids, soluble salts)
> 3. Cancel ions that appear identically on both sides (**spectator ions**)
> 4. What remains is the net ionic equation

## ✏️ Worked Examples

### Fe + HCl

**Molecular:** $$\text{Fe}_{(s)} + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{FeCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Ionic:** $$\text{Fe}^0_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + 2\text{Cl}^-_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Fe}^{2+}_{(aq)} + 2\text{Cl}^-_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Net ionic:** $$\text{Fe}^0_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Fe}^{2+}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

---

### Al + H₂SO₄

**Molecular:** $$2\text{Al}_{(s)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Al}_2(\text{SO}_4)_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Ionic:** $$2\text{Al}^0_{(s)} + 6\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + 3\text{SO}_4^{2-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Al}^{3+}_{(aq)} + 3\text{SO}_4^{2-}_{(aq)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Net ionic:** $$2\text{Al}^0_{(s)} + 6\text{H}^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Al}^{3+}_{(aq)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)}$$

---

### Zn + HCl

**Molecular:** $$\text{Zn}_{(s)} + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{ZnCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Ionic:** $$\text{Zn}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + 2\text{Cl}^-_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+}_{(aq)} + 2\text{Cl}^-_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Net ionic:** $$\text{Zn}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

---

### Mg + H₂SO₄

**Molecular:** $$\text{Mg}_{(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{MgSO}_{4(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Ionic:** $$\text{Mg}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + \text{SO}_4^{2-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Mg}^{2+}_{(aq)} + \text{SO}_4^{2-}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Net ionic:** $$\text{Mg}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Mg}^{2+}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

---

### 2Cr + 3H₂SO₄

**Molecular:** $$2\text{Cr}_{(s)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Cr}_2(\text{SO}_4)_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Ionic:** $$2\text{Cr}_{(s)} + 6\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + 3\text{SO}_4^{2-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Cr}^{3+}_{(aq)} + 3\text{SO}_4^{2-}_{(aq)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Net ionic:** $$2\text{Cr}_{(s)} + 6\text{H}^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Cr}^{3+}_{(aq)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)}$$

---

### 2Na + H₂SO₄

**Molecular:** $$2\text{Na}_{(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Ionic:** $$2\text{Na}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + \text{SO}_4^{2-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Na}^+_{(aq)} + \text{SO}_4^{2-}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

**Net ionic:** $$2\text{Na}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Na}^+_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

---

### Non-reactions (below H in activity series)

$$\text{Cu}_{(s)} + \text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{no reaction}$$

$$\text{Ag}_{(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{no reaction}$$

## ⚠️ Exceptions — HNO₃ and Concentrated H₂SO₄

> [!caution] Special acids — no hydrogen gas produced When metals react with **nitric acid ($\text{HNO}_3$)** or **concentrated sulphuric acid**, hydrogen gas is **not** released. Instead, the products are nitrogen oxides ($\text{NO}$, $\text{NO}_2$) or sulphur dioxide ($\text{SO}_2$), and the acid acts as an **oxidising agent**, not just a proton donor. These reactions are covered separately in oxidation-reduction chemistry.

---

## 🌧️ Real-Life Context — Acid Rain and Metals

Acid rain forms when sulphur dioxide ($\text{SO}_2$) and nitrogen oxides ($\text{NO}_x$) emitted by industry and vehicles dissolve in atmospheric water:

$$\text{SO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$$ $$\text{SO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$$ $$\text{N}_2\text{O}_5 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\text{HNO}_3$$

Acidic rainwater then attacks metal structures (bridges, railings, statues) by reacting with the metal surface, gradually corroding it. Iron in particular is vulnerable:

$$\text{Fe} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \rightarrow \text{FeSO}_4 + \text{H}_2$$

**Methods to reduce acid rain damage on metal structures:**

- Protective coatings (paint, galvanisation, anodising)
- Use of corrosion-resistant alloys (stainless steel)
- Reducing $\text{SO}_2$ / $\text{NO}_x$ emissions at source (scrubbers, catalytic converters)
- Regular maintenance and inspection
