## ⚡ Strong Acids — Definition

> [!info] Strong Acid A strong acid is one that is **completely (100%) ionised** in aqueous solution. Every molecule of the acid dissociates into ions — none remains as an intact molecule.

Strong acids conduct electricity well because their solutions are rich in ions.

**Strong acids to memorise:**

$$\text{HCl}, \quad \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4, \quad \text{HNO}_3, \quad \text{HBr}, \quad \text{HI}, \quad \text{HClO}_4$$

$$\text{HCl}_{\text{(aq)}} \rightarrow \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{Cl}^-_{\text{(aq)}}$$

The single arrow ($\rightarrow$) indicates the reaction goes **to completion** — no equilibrium.

---

## 🔬 Weak Acids — Definition

> [!info] Weak Acid A weak acid is one that **partially dissociates** in aqueous solution. An equilibrium is established between the undissociated acid molecules and the ions formed.

$$\text{HA}_{\text{(aq)}} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{A}^-_{\text{(aq)}}$$

The double arrow ($\rightleftharpoons$) indicates a **reversible equilibrium** — both the forward (dissociation) and reverse (recombination) reactions occur simultaneously.

**Common weak acids:**

$$\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3, \quad \text{CH}_3\text{COOH}, \quad \text{H}_2\text{S}, \quad \text{H}_3\text{PO}_4, \quad \text{HF}, \quad \text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$$

> [!warning] HF — Weak but extremely dangerous Hydrofluoric acid is a **weak acid** (partial ionisation), but it is one of the most hazardous laboratory chemicals. It penetrates skin and tissue deeply, binding calcium ions in bones and causing systemic toxicity. Weakness in the chemical sense does not mean safety.

|Property|Strong Acid|Weak Acid|

|Ionisation|Complete (100%)|Partial — equilibrium|
|Equation arrow|$\rightarrow$ (one-way)|$\rightleftharpoons$ (reversible)|

## ⚗️ Dissociation Equations

### Strong acid — complete ionisation

$$\text{HCl}_{\text{(aq)}} \rightarrow \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{Cl}^-_{\text{(aq)}}$$

$$\text{HNO}_{3\text{(aq)}} \rightarrow \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{NO}^-_{3\text{(aq)}}$$

### Weak acid — partial ionisation (equilibrium)

$$\text{H}_2\text{CO}_{3\text{(aq)}} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{HCO}^-_{3\text{(aq)}}$$

$$\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}_{\text{(aq)}} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-_{\text{(aq)}}$$

$$\text{H}_3\text{PO}_{4\text{(aq)}} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{H}_2\text{PO}^-_{4\text{(aq)}}$$

---

## 🔄 Sulphuric Acid — Special Case (Diprotic)

$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ has **two ionisable protons** — it ionises in two stages:

**Stage 1 — Complete (strong):** $$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4\text{(aq)}} \rightarrow \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{HSO}^-_{4\text{(aq)}}$$

**Stage 2 — Partial (weak):** $$\text{HSO}^-_{4\text{(aq)}} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{SO}^{2-}_{4\text{(aq)}}$$

> [!note] Only the first ionisation is complete $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ is classified as a strong acid because the **first** ionisation is 100% complete. The second stage is a reversible equilibrium.

---

## ↩️ Reversible Reactions and Equilibrium

In weak acid solutions, the dissociation is reversible. The reactants can form products, and the products can reform the reactants. At equilibrium, the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction — the concentrations of all species remain constant (though not necessarily equal).

$$\underbrace{\text{HA}}_{\text{acid}} \rightleftharpoons \underbrace{\text{H}^+}_{\text{proton}} + \underbrace{\text{A}^-}_{\text{conjugate base}}$$

This is why weak acids have higher pH values than strong acids at the same concentration — fewer $\text{H}^+$ ions are present in solution.

## 🧴 Application — Choosing the Right Acid

> [!example] Real-life comparison Two acid-based cleaning products are available:
> 
> - **Product A:** Contains $\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4$ (phosphoric acid) — pH 1.5
> - **Product B:** Contains $\text{HCl}$ (hydrochloric acid) — pH 0.5
> 
> **Which is stronger?** Product B is stronger — HCl is a strong acid that completely ionises, giving a much lower pH. Even though $\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4$ has a pH of 1.5 (which is still highly acidic), it is a weak acid that only partially dissociates.
> 
> **How should you work with them?** Both require protective gloves and goggles. The HCl product (pH 0.5) is more aggressive and more likely to cause burns and emit fumes — it requires better ventilation and greater care. Neither should be mixed with bases or bleach.
