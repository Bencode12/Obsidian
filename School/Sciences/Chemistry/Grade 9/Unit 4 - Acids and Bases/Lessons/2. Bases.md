## 🧪 Definition — What is a Base?

> [!info] Arrhenius Definition A **base** is a compound that increases the $\text{OH}^-$ (hydroxide ion) concentration in aqueous solution.

$$\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+_{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-_{(aq)}$$

$$\text{Ca(OH)}_{2(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Ca}^{2+}_{(aq)} + 2\text{OH}^-_{(aq)}$$

---

## 💧 Alkalis vs Insoluble Hydroxides

|Type|Definition|Examples|
|---|---|---|
|**Alkali**|A base that is **soluble** in water|$\text{NaOH}$, $\text{KOH}$, $\text{Ca(OH)}_2$, $\text{Ba(OH)}_2$|
|**Insoluble hydroxide**|A base that does **not** dissolve in water|$\text{Fe(OH)}_3$, $\text{Cu(OH)}_2$, $\text{Zn(OH)}_2$, $\text{Al(OH)}_3$|

> [!tip] All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis.

---

## 📋 General Properties of Alkalis

- Soluble in water; produce $\text{OH}^-$ ions in solution
- pH > 7; feel soapy to the touch
- Change colour of indicators (e.g. turn red litmus **blue**, turn phenolphthalein **pink**)
- Neutralise acids to form salt + water
- React with acidic oxides to form salt + water
- React with certain salt solutions to form insoluble hydroxide precipitates

## ⚗️ Obtaining Alkalis

### Method 1 — Group 1 and 2 metals reacting with water

All Group IA metals (Li, Na, K…) and most Group IIA metals (Ca, Ba…) react vigorously with water to produce an alkali and hydrogen gas:

$$2\text{Na}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightarrow 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

$$\text{Ca}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightarrow \text{Ca(OH)}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

> [!caution] Safety Group 1 metals react **violently** with water — sodium floats and fizzes, potassium ignites spontaneously. These reactions must be observed with protective screens.

### Method 2 — Group 1 and 2 metal oxides reacting with water

Metal oxides (basic oxides) react with water to form the corresponding alkali:

$$\text{CaO}_{(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightarrow \text{Ca(OH)}_{2(aq)}$$

$$\text{Na}_2\text{O}_{(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightarrow 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)}$$

> [!note] Beryllium exception Beryllium (Be) and its oxide do **not** follow this pattern — BeO shows amphoteric behaviour and does not form a simple alkali.

## ⚡ Strong vs Weak Bases

> [!info] Strong base A strong base is **100% ionised** in solution. All hydroxide ions are released completely.

Strong bases: $\text{NaOH}$, $\text{KOH}$, $\text{Ca(OH)}_2$, $\text{Ba(OH)}_2$, $\text{LiOH}$

$$\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+_{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-_{(aq)} \quad \text{(complete)}$$

> [!info] Weak base A weak base is **less than 100% ionised** in solution — an equilibrium is established.

Weak bases: $\text{NH}_3$, $\text{Mg(OH)}_2$, and all insoluble hydroxides

> [!note] Key distinction All strong bases contain $\text{OH}^-$ directly in their formula. A compound like $\text{NH}_3$ has **no $\text{OH}^-$** in its formula, yet it acts as a base by acquiring $\text{H}^+$ from water — so it is necessarily a weak base.

---

## 🧴 Ammonia — A Weak Base

Ammonia ($\text{NH}_3$) is highly soluble in water due to its polarity and ability to form hydrogen bonds. When it dissolves, it reacts with water rather than simply dissolving:

$$\text{NH}_{3(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4^+_{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-_{(aq)}$$

This produces $\text{OH}^-$ ions (making the solution alkaline) but only partially — hence it is a **weak base**. The equilibrium lies to the left; most $\text{NH}_3$ molecules remain undissociated.

## 🔁 Chemical Properties of Hydroxides

### 1 — React with acids (neutralisation)

$$\text{Ba(OH)}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{BaSO}_{4(s)} \downarrow + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{Al(OH)}_{3(s)} + 3\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{AlCl}_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

### 2 — React with acidic oxides

$$\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} + \text{SO}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{3(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{Ca(OH)}_{2(aq)} + \text{N}_2\text{O}_{5(s)} \rightarrow \text{Ca(NO}_3)_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

> [!tip] General pattern $$\text{Base} + \text{Acidic oxide} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Water}$$

### 3 — React with salt solutions to form insoluble hydroxide precipitates

$$2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} + \text{ZnSO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + \text{Zn(OH)}_{2(s)} \downarrow$$

$$2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} + \text{CuSO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + \text{Cu(OH)}_{2(s)} \downarrow$$

These precipitates are characteristically coloured — $\text{Cu(OH)}_2$ is **blue**, $\text{Fe(OH)}_3$ is **rust-red/brown**.

---

## 🌡️ Special Properties of Insoluble Hydroxides — Decomposition

Insoluble (and some soluble) hydroxides **decompose on heating** to form a metal oxide and water:

$$\text{Cu(OH)}_{2(s)} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \text{CuO}_{(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(g)}$$

$$\text{Fe(OH)}_{3(s)} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_{3(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(g)}$$

> [!note] General equation $$\text{Metal hydroxide} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \text{Metal oxide} + \text{Water}$$ Note: $\text{NaOH}$ and $\text{KOH}$ do not decompose at typical lab temperatures.

## 🏭 Uses of Common Bases

### Sodium hydroxide — $\text{NaOH}$ (caustic soda)

Used in bar soaps, detergents, drain cleaners, paper manufacturing (Kraft process), bleach, fabric processing, aluminium ore refining, food curing, pharmaceuticals (anticoagulants, cholesterol medications), and water treatment. ~56% of NaOH produced is used by industry; ~25% goes to the paper industry.

### Potassium hydroxide — $\text{KOH}$ (caustic potash)

Used in soap making, electrolyte in alkaline batteries, electroplating, lithography, food pH adjustment and thickening, paint and varnish removers, liquid drain cleaners (25–36% KOH), and medical diagnostic preparations for fungal visualisation.

### Calcium hydroxide — $\text{Ca(OH)}_2$ (slaked lime)

Used in the Kraft paper process, water and sewage treatment (flocculent), cement, plaster and mortar, soil pH correction in agriculture, food baking, calcium fortification, and pH adjustment. Relatively non-toxic compared to NaOH/KOH.

### Ammonia — $\text{NH}_3$

Used to make ammonium nitrate fertilisers (nitrogen source for crops), household cleaning products, refrigerant gas (absorbs large amounts of heat), plastics, explosives, fabrics, pesticides, dyes, pharmaceuticals, and food and beverage processing. Also used for water purification and pulp/paper production.
