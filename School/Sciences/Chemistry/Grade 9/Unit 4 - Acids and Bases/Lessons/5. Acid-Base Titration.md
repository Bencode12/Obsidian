## 🔬 What is Titration?

> [!info] Volumetric analysis **Volumetric analysis** is a laboratory technique that involves two solutions. One solution — the **standard solution** (titrant) — has a precisely known concentration. The other solution has an **unknown concentration**. The goal is to find the unknown concentration by reacting the two solutions in controlled amounts.

> [!info] Titration **Titration** is the technique used to determine the unknown concentration. A measured volume of one solution is gradually added to a known volume of the other until the reaction is **complete** — as indicated by an indicator colour change.

In acid-base titration, the reaction between acid and base is **neutralisation** — an exothermic reaction:

$$\text{Acid} + \text{Base} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

---

## 📖 Key Terminology

|Term|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Titrant**|The standard solution added from the burette|
|**Analyte**|The solution of unknown concentration in the conical flask|
|**Equivalence point**|The point where stoichiometric amounts of acid and base have completely reacted|
|**End point**|The point where the indicator just changes colour (approximates the equivalence point)|
|**Standard solution**|A solution whose concentration is known to a high degree of accuracy|
|**Burette**|Graduated glass tube with a tap, used to deliver precise volumes of titrant|
|**Pipette**|Delivers a fixed, precise volume of analyte into the conical flask|

> [!tip] End point vs equivalence point The **equivalence point** is theoretical — where moles of acid = moles of base (adjusted for stoichiometry). The **end point** is practical — where the indicator changes colour. A well-chosen indicator makes these nearly identical.

## 🎨 Indicators for Titration

The choice of indicator depends on the **relative strengths** of the acid and base involved.

|Titration type|Best indicator|Why|
|---|---|---|
|Strong acid + Strong base|Litmus or methyl orange|Sharp colour change at pH 7|
|Weak acid + Strong base|**Phenolphthalein**|Equivalence point is > 7 (basic); phenolphthalein changes colour in the pH 8–10 range|
|Strong acid + Weak base|Methyl orange|Equivalence point is < 7 (acidic); methyl orange changes in pH 3–5 range|
|Weak acid + Weak base|Not suitable for a sharp endpoint|—|

> [!warning] Phenolphthalein and methyl orange compared
> 
> - **Phenolphthalein**: colourless in acid → pink/magenta in base (changes at pH ~8–10)
> - **Methyl orange**: red in acid → yellow in base (changes at pH ~3–4) Using the wrong indicator for a titration type can introduce systematic error in the endpoint determination.

## 🧪 Step-by-Step Procedure

1. **Rinse** all glassware — burette with titrant, pipette and conical flask with analyte
2. **Fill the burette** with the standard solution (titrant); record the initial reading
3. **Pipette** a precise volume of the analyte into a conical flask
4. **Add 2–3 drops** of the appropriate indicator to the conical flask
5. **Add the titrant slowly** from the burette into the flask, swirling continuously
6. As you approach the endpoint, **add the titrant drop by drop**
7. **Stop** when the indicator permanently changes colour (lasting > 30 seconds)
8. **Record** the final burette reading; calculate the volume used (titre)
9. **Repeat** until at least two concordant titres are obtained (within 0.1 cm³ of each other)
10. **Average** the concordant results; use this value in calculations

## 🧮 Titration Calculations

### Step-by-step method

> [!info] Four-step calculation method
> 
> 1. Write the **balanced chemical equation**
> 2. Calculate the **moles** of the standard solution: $n = c \times V$
> 3. Use the **mole ratio** from the balanced equation to find moles of the unknown
> 4. Calculate the **concentration** of the unknown: $c = \frac{n}{V}$

### Key formula

$$n = c \times V \quad \text{where } n = \text{mol}, ; c = \text{mol/L}, ; V = \text{L}$$

### Worked example

**Problem:** Find the concentration of a 25 mL $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ solution if 20.55 mL of 0.5 mol/L $\text{NaOH}$ was needed for titration.

**Step 1 — Balanced equation:** $$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

**Step 2 — Moles of NaOH:** $$n(\text{NaOH}) = 0.5 \text{ mol/L} \times 0.02055 \text{ L} = 0.010275 \text{ mol}$$

**Step 3 — Moles of H₂SO₄** (mole ratio 1:2): $$n(\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4) = \frac{0.010275}{2} = 0.005138 \text{ mol}$$

**Step 4 — Concentration of H₂SO₄:** $$c(\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4) = \frac{0.005138}{0.025} = 0.206 \text{ mol/L}$$

## 📈 pH Curves

A **pH curve** (titration curve) plots the pH of the solution in the flask against the volume of titrant added. The shape reveals information about the acid-base strengths.

### Strong acid + Strong base (e.g. HCl + NaOH)

- Starts at low pH (~1–2)
- Gradual rise in pH
- **Very steep jump** at equivalence point — pH changes by ~6 units in one drop
- Equivalence point at **pH 7**
- Ends at high pH (~12–13)

### Weak acid + Strong base (e.g. CH₃COOH + NaOH)

- Starts at higher pH (~3) than a strong acid
- **Buffer region** — gradual pH change in the middle (less steep)
- Equivalence point at **pH > 7** (basic) due to hydrolysis of the conjugate base
- Still has a steep jump at equivalence, but less extreme than strong acid/strong base

> [!tip] Reading pH curves The **steepest point** of the curve = the equivalence point. The indicator must change colour within this steep range to give an accurate endpoint.

## 💻 Technology in Titration — pH Sensors

Modern laboratories use **data probes** (pH sensors/electrodes) to overcome the limitations of human observation:

- A **pH probe** (glass electrode) exploits the presence of hydrogen ions throughout the solution
- It has a **permeable glass membrane** through which $\text{H}^+$ ions can pass, but larger ions cannot
- The probe functions as an **electrochemical cell**, measuring the voltage (potential difference) of the solution
- The relationship between voltage and $[\text{H}^+]$ is determined automatically by the data logger
- This allows continuous, real-time pH recording — plotting a precise titration curve automatically

Advantages over indicator-based endpoint detection: greater precision, no colour bias, can record the entire pH curve (not just the endpoint), usable with coloured solutions where indicators are unreliable.

Other data probes used in lab investigations: temperature sensors, conductivity probes, gas pressure sensors, dissolved oxygen sensors, colorimeters.
