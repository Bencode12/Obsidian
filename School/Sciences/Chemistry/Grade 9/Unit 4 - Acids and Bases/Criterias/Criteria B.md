## 📌 What is Criterion B?

> [!info] Criterion B — Inquiring and Designing Criterion B assesses your ability to **plan and design a scientific investigation** from scratch. You must write a focused research question, a scientifically justified hypothesis, identify and control all variables, and produce a logical, complete, safe method. **Maximum: 8 marks**

|Strand|What you must do at 7–8|
|---|---|
|**i**|**Explain** the problem or question to be tested|
|**ii**|**Formulate and explain** a testable hypothesis using **correct** scientific reasoning|
|**iii**|**Explain** how to manipulate variables and how **sufficient, relevant** data will be collected|
|**iv**|Design a **logical, complete, and safe** method selecting appropriate materials and equipment|

## Strand i — Research Question

Your question must be **focused, testable, and explained** — not just stated.

### Template

> _"How does [independent variable] affect [dependent variable] when [context/controlled conditions]?"_

### What "explain" requires here

You must justify **why** the question is worth investigating — connect it to a concept from the unit (collision theory, ionisation, neutralisation, pH, etc.) and/or a real-world context.

### Chemistry-specific examples

> ❌ **1–2:** "I will test how acid concentration affects the reaction with metal."
> 
> ✅ **7–8:** "This investigation examines how the concentration of hydrochloric acid affects the rate of its reaction with magnesium ribbon, measured as the volume of hydrogen gas produced per 60 seconds. Since HCl is a strong acid that fully dissociates — $\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{H}^+_{(aq)} + \text{Cl}^-_{(aq)}$ — increasing its concentration directly increases $[\text{H}^+]$ in solution, making this an ideal test of collision theory in the context of acid–metal reactions."

> ❌ **1–2:** "I will do a titration to find the concentration of acid."
> 
> ✅ **7–8:** "This investigation determines the unknown concentration of a $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ solution by titrating it against a standard solution of 0.5 mol/L $\text{NaOH}$. Since the neutralisation reaction $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$ has a known mole ratio, the volume of $\text{NaOH}$ needed to reach the equivalence point can be used to calculate the acid concentration precisely."

## Strand ii — Hypothesis

### Template

> _"If [IV] increases/decreases, then [DV] will increase/decrease, because [named scientific concept or theory]."_

### Chemistry-specific scientific reasoning to use

|Investigation type|Theory / concept to reference|
|---|---|
|Acid + metal reaction rate|Collision theory — $[\text{H}^+]$, surface area, temperature|
|Strong vs weak acid reactivity|Degree of ionisation — more $\text{H}^+$ ions available from strong acid|
|Neutralisation / titration|Mole ratio from balanced equation; equivalence point|
|Effect of pH on indicator|Indicator equilibrium — weak acid form vs conjugate base form|
|Acid + carbonate|$\text{CO}_2$ production rate linked to $[\text{H}^+]$ and collision theory|

### Worked hypothesis — acid concentration and Mg

> ❌ **1–2:** "More concentrated acid will react faster with magnesium."
> 
> ✅ **7–8:** "If the concentration of $\text{HCl}$ is increased from 0.5 to 2.5 mol/dm³, then the volume of $\text{H}_2$ gas produced in 60 seconds will increase, because higher concentration means more $\text{H}^+$ ions per unit volume. According to collision theory, a greater number of particles in the same volume increases the frequency of successful collisions between $\text{H}^+$ ions and Mg atoms per unit time, thus increasing the reaction rate. The reaction is: $\text{Mg}_{(s)} + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{MgCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$"

### Worked hypothesis — titration

> ✅ **7–8:** "If the volume of 0.5 mol/L $\text{NaOH}$ added is increased until the equivalence point is reached, then the phenolphthalein indicator will turn from colourless to pink, because the solution transitions from acidic (excess $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$) to alkaline (excess $\text{NaOH}$) at the equivalence point. At this point, $n(\text{NaOH}) = 2 \times n(\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4)$ according to the balanced equation. Phenolphthalein is appropriate because its colour change range (pH 8–10) coincides with the equivalence point of a strong acid–strong base titration."

## Strand iii — Variables

Always identify all three types and **explain** each one.

|Variable|What to state|What to explain|
|---|---|---|
|**IV**|What it is + specific values and range|Why this range was chosen|
|**DV**|What is measured + instrument, unit, precision|How it will be measured and how often|
|**CVs**|List all|Why each one must be kept constant (what changes if it isn't)|

### Controlled variables specific to chemistry investigations

|Variable|Why it must be controlled|
|---|---|
|Temperature|Affects kinetic energy → collision frequency → rate of reaction|
|Surface area / mass of metal|More surface area = more contact with acid = faster rate|
|Volume of acid|Changes the total amount of $\text{H}^+$ available|
|Same type/brand of acid|Different sources may contain impurities|
|Same equipment throughout|Calibration differences between instruments|

> [!tip] Sufficient data — what to state At 7–8 you must **explain** why your number of trials is sufficient. State that you will use a minimum of 5 IV values across a meaningful range, with 3 repeats at each value, to allow calculation of means, identification of anomalies, and identification of a clear trend.

## Strand iv — Method Design

Your method must be **logical** (sequenced correctly), **complete** (replicable by a stranger), and **safe** (risk assessment included).

### Required components

1. **Materials list** — with quantities and precision (e.g. "25 cm³ measuring cylinder, ±0.5 cm³")
2. **Labelled diagram** of apparatus
3. **Risk assessment** table
4. **Numbered step-by-step procedure** in the correct sequence
5. **Pre-drawn data table** with headings and units

### Risk assessment — chemistry-specific hazards

|Hazard|Risk|Control Measure|
|---|---|---|
|$\text{HCl}$ / $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ (corrosive)|Skin/eye burns|Safety goggles + lab coat; rinse with water for 10 min if contact|
|$\text{NaOH}$ (corrosive)|Burns, especially eyes|Goggles; avoid splashing; neutralise spills with dilute acid|
|$\text{H}_2$ gas (flammable)|Fire/explosion|No open flames near experiment; ensure ventilation|
|$\text{H}_2\text{S}$ gas (toxic)|Inhalation hazard|Work in a fume hood; evacuate if large quantities released|
|Glassware (burette, conical flask)|Cuts|Handle carefully; report breakages immediately|
|Hot solutions|Burns|Allow to cool before handling|

## ✏️ Worked Example — Effect of Acid Concentration on Reaction Rate with Mg

### Research Question (Strand i)

This investigation examines how the concentration of hydrochloric acid affects the rate of reaction with magnesium ribbon, measured as the volume of $\text{H}_2$ gas collected in 60 seconds. Since $\text{HCl}$ is a strong acid that fully dissociates ($\text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{H}^+ + \text{Cl}^-$), varying concentration directly changes $[\text{H}^+]$ in solution, making this an ideal test of collision theory applied to acid–metal reactions: $\text{Mg} + 2\text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{MgCl}_2 + \text{H}_2$.

### Hypothesis (Strand ii)

If the concentration of $\text{HCl}$ increases from 0.5 to 2.5 mol/L, then the volume of $\text{H}_2$ collected in 60 seconds will increase proportionally, because a higher $[\text{H}^+]$ increases the frequency of successful collisions between $\text{H}^+$ ions and Mg atoms per second (collision theory), thereby increasing the reaction rate.

### Variables (Strand iii)

- **IV:** Concentration of HCl — 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5 mol/L (5 values × 3 repeats = 15 trials; sufficient to establish a trend and calculate reliable means)
- **DV:** Volume of $\text{H}_2$ gas collected in a gas syringe after 60 seconds (cm³, ±0.5 cm³)
- **CVs:** Mass of Mg ribbon (0.05 g ± 0.001 g, 3 d.p. balance); volume of acid (25 cm³, same pipette); temperature (room temperature, ~20°C — kinetic energy affects collision frequency); same gas syringe throughout (calibration consistency)

## ✏️ Worked Example — Titration Investigation

### Research Question (Strand i)

This investigation determines the unknown concentration of a sulphuric acid solution by titrating it against a standard solution of $\text{NaOH}$ (0.5 mol/L), using phenolphthalein as an indicator. The neutralisation reaction $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$ has a fixed mole ratio of 1:2, which allows the moles of acid to be calculated from the volume of $\text{NaOH}$ used.

### Hypothesis (Strand ii)

If the volume of 0.5 mol/L $\text{NaOH}$ added from the burette reaches the equivalence point, then phenolphthalein will change from colourless to a permanent pale pink, because the solution becomes alkaline (pH > 7) as all $\text{H}^+$ ions from $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ are neutralised by $\text{OH}^-$ ions. Phenolphthalein is the correct indicator because it changes colour in the pH 8–10 range, which coincides with the equivalence point of a strong acid–strong base titration.

### Variables (Strand iii)

- **IV:** Volume of $\text{NaOH}$ added from the burette (cm³)
- **DV:** Indicator colour change / pH at equivalence point
- **CVs:** Volume of $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ in flask (25.00 cm³ by pipette); concentration of $\text{NaOH}$ standard solution (0.5 mol/L); same indicator (3 drops phenolphthalein); temperature; same burette and pipette throughout

## ❌ Common Mistakes

> [!bug] Mistakes that cost marks
> 
> - Hypothesis with no named scientific theory — caps at **3–4**
> - Listing CVs without explaining _why_ each one matters
> - Omitting the risk assessment — method cannot be "safe" → caps at **5–6**
> - Vague measurement: "I will measure the gas" — must specify instrument, unit, and precision
> - Fewer than 5 IV values or fewer than 3 repeats — insufficient data, not explained
> - Method steps out of order — not "logical"
> - Forgetting to include a pre-drawn results table in the method

---

## ✅ Checklist for 7–8

- [ ] Research question is focused, testable, and **explained** with scientific context and relevant equation
- [ ] Hypothesis uses **if… then… because…** structure
- [ ] Hypothesis names a **specific scientific theory or concept** (collision theory, ionisation, mole ratio, etc.)
- [ ] **All three variable types** identified with specific values, units, and instruments
- [ ] Each CV has an explanation of _why_ it must be controlled
- [ ] Number of repeats and data points **justified** as sufficient
- [ ] Method steps are **numbered and in logical sequence**
- [ ] **Risk assessment** table present with specific control measures for all chemistry hazards
- [ ] Materials list with quantities and precision stated
- [ ] Pre-drawn **data collection table** with headings and units included
