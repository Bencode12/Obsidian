## 📌 What is Criterion C?

> [!info] Criterion C — Processing and Evaluating Criterion C assesses your ability to **process experimental data, interpret what it means scientifically, and critically evaluate** how valid and reliable your investigation was. **Maximum: 8 marks**

|Strand|What you must do at 7–8|
|---|---|
|**i**|Correctly collect, organise, **transform**, and present data in numerical and/or visual forms|
|**ii**|Accurately interpret data and explain results using **correct** scientific reasoning|
|**iii**|**Evaluate** the validity of the hypothesis based on the investigation outcome|
|**iv**|**Evaluate** the validity of the method|
|**v**|**Explain** improvements or extensions that would benefit the investigation|

## Strand i — Collecting and Presenting Data

### Raw data table requirements

- Descriptive title
- All column headings with **units in brackets**
- Consistent decimal places matching instrument precision
- **All repeat trials recorded separately** — do not average in the raw data table
- Anomalies left in the raw table (circled or noted, but not deleted)

### Example raw data table — acid + Mg reaction

|Concentration of HCl (mol/L)|Volume of H₂ — Trial 1 (cm³)|Trial 2 (cm³)|Trial 3 (cm³)|
|---|---|---|---|
|0.5|8.2|8.0|8.3|
|1.0|14.6|14.9|14.4|
|1.5|21.1|21.4|20.9|
|2.0|27.3|27.1*|28.9|
|2.5|31.2|31.5|31.4|

_*Possible anomaly — flagged for investigation_

### Transformed data table — required for 7–8

"Transforming" means calculating something **new** from your raw data. Common transformations in this unit:

|Transformation|When to use|
|---|---|
|Mean of repeats (excluding anomalies)|Always — for every set of repeats|
|Rate = volume ÷ time|When comparing reaction rates|
|Uncertainty = (max − min) ÷ 2|To express precision of measurements|
|Moles from $n = c \times V$|Titration calculations|
|Concentration from $c = n/V$|Finding unknown concentration|
|% difference from expected|Comparing experimental to theoretical value|

### Example processed data table

|Concentration of HCl (mol/L)|Mean Volume H₂ (cm³)|Uncertainty (±cm³)|Rate (cm³/min)|
|---|---|---|---|
|0.5|8.17|0.15|8.17|
|1.0|14.63|0.25|14.63|
|1.5|21.13|0.25|21.13|
|2.0|27.30*|0.10|27.30|
|2.5|31.37|0.15|31.37|

_*Trial 2 excluded as anomalous (28.9 cm³ deviates by > 1.5 cm³ from others)_

### Graph requirements

- **Title:** "[DV] vs [IV]"
- **Axes labelled** with units
- **Equal, consistent scale intervals**
- **Line/curve of best fit** (not dot-to-dot)
- **Anomalies circled** and excluded from line of best fit
- Correct graph type: **line graph** for continuous IV (concentration); **bar chart** for discrete/categorical IV

## Strand ii — Interpreting Data

Use this structure for every interpretation:

1. **Describe the trend** — state what happens to the DV as IV increases/decreases. Quote specific data values (not just "it goes up").
2. **Explain the trend** — why does this happen? Reference a **named scientific concept** from the unit.
3. **Address anomalies** — identify any, suggest a likely cause.

### Chemistry-specific scientific reasoning to use

|Observation|Scientific explanation|
|---|---|
|Rate increases with acid concentration|More $\text{H}^+$ ions per unit volume → higher collision frequency (collision theory)|
|Strong acid reacts faster than weak at same concentration|Strong acid fully ionises → more $\text{H}^+$ available; weak acid partially ionises → fewer $\text{H}^+$|
|Fizzing with carbonate|$\text{CO}_2$ gas produced: $2\text{HCl} + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 \rightarrow 2\text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2 \uparrow$|
|Precipitate formation|Insoluble ionic product: $\text{Ba}^{2+} + \text{SO}_4^{2-} \rightarrow \text{BaSO}_4 \downarrow$|
|pH drops sharply near equivalence point in titration|All $\text{OH}^-$ ions neutralised; excess $\text{H}^+$ suddenly dominates|
|Rate levels off at very high concentration|Mg surface area becomes the limiting factor, not $[\text{H}^+]$|

### Worked interpretation — acid + Mg

> ❌ **1–2:** "The results show that as concentration increased, the gas increased."
> 
> ✅ **7–8:** "The data shows a clear positive correlation between HCl concentration and volume of $\text{H}_2$ produced in 60 seconds: mean gas volume increased from 8.2 cm³ at 0.5 mol/L to 31.4 cm³ at 2.5 mol/L — an approximately 3.8-fold increase. This supports collision theory: as $[\text{HCl}]$ increases, the number of $\text{H}^+$ ions per unit volume increases, raising the frequency of successful collisions between $\text{H}^+$ ions and Mg atoms per second ($\text{Mg} + 2\text{H}^+ \rightarrow \text{Mg}^{2+} + \text{H}_2$). However, the rate of increase slows slightly between 2.0 and 2.5 mol/L (from 27.3 to 31.4 cm³), suggesting the Mg surface area may be becoming a limiting factor at higher concentrations. The value recorded for 2.0 mol/L trial 2 (28.9 cm³) appears anomalous — it may have resulted from a slightly larger piece of Mg ribbon than specified."

## Strand iii — Evaluating the Hypothesis

### Structure

1. Briefly restate the hypothesis
2. State whether the data **supports**, **partially supports**, or **refutes** it
3. Quote **specific data** as evidence
4. Explain any discrepancies between prediction and result

### Worked evaluation — acid + Mg

> ❌ **1–2:** "My hypothesis was right."
> 
> ✅ **7–8:** "The hypothesis predicted that increasing $[\text{HCl}]$ would increase the rate of $\text{H}_2$ production due to increased collision frequency (collision theory). This was supported by the data: a positive correlation was observed across all five concentrations, with mean gas volume increasing from 8.2 cm³ to 31.4 cm³ (0.5–2.5 mol/L). However, the relationship is not perfectly linear — the rate of increase slows at higher concentrations, suggesting the hypothesis oversimplified by not accounting for other limiting factors (Mg surface area). The hypothesis is therefore partially supported: the direction of the trend is correct and the mechanism is validated, but the proportional relationship predicted was not fully observed at higher concentrations."

### Worked evaluation — titration

> ✅ **7–8:** "The hypothesis predicted that phenolphthalein would turn pink at the equivalence point, signalling complete neutralisation. This was supported: the colour change consistently occurred after ~20.5 cm³ of $\text{NaOH}$ was added across all three concordant titres (range: 20.50–20.55 cm³). The calculated concentration of $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ (0.206 mol/L) closely matches the expected value. The hypothesis was fully supported, and the choice of indicator was validated as appropriate for a strong acid–strong base system."

## Strand iv — Evaluating the Method

### Key terms

|Term|Meaning in this unit|
|---|---|
|**Validity**|Did the method measure what it was meant to measure? Were all CVs controlled?|
|**Reliability**|Were repeats consistent? Low spread between trials = reliable|
|**Systematic error**|Consistent bias in one direction (e.g. gas leaking from syringe every trial → always underestimates volume)|
|**Random error**|Unpredictable variation (e.g. slight differences in Mg ribbon mass despite cutting by length)|
|**Accuracy**|How close results are to the true/accepted value|
|**Precision**|How close repeat measurements are to each other|

### Worked evaluation — acid + Mg method

> ❌ **3–4:** "The experiment was mostly accurate but there were some errors."
> 
> ✅ **7–8:** "The method showed moderate reliability — most IV values had a range below 0.5 cm³ between repeats, indicating reasonable consistency. However, the range of 1.6 cm³ for the 2.0 mol/L trials (27.3 vs 28.9 cm³) indicates poor precision for that data point. A systematic error likely arose from gas escaping between the time the Mg was added to the acid and the gas syringe being sealed — this would consistently underestimate gas volumes across all trials, reducing validity. A random error arose from measuring Mg by length rather than mass; density variations along the ribbon would cause inconsistent amounts of reactant, contributing to scatter in results."

### Worked evaluation — titration method

> ✅ **7–8:** "The method was highly reliable — three concordant titres were obtained within 0.05 cm³ of each other, indicating excellent precision for a manual burette technique (±0.05 cm³). Validity was supported by the use of a calibrated pipette to deliver a precise 25.00 cm³ of analyte and by rinsing all glassware before use. A potential systematic error is the difficulty of judging the exact moment of colour change — a very pale pink that deepens with one extra drop could cause slight overshooting, meaning the titre is consistently 0.05–0.1 cm³ too large. Using a pH probe and data logger instead of a chemical indicator would eliminate this subjective systematic error."

## Strand v — Improvements and Extensions

> [!tip] Improvement vs Extension **Improvement** = fix a weakness in the same experiment **Extension** = new investigation that logically follows from your findings

### Improvement — must link to a specific weakness

|Weakness identified|Improvement|Why it helps|
|---|---|---|
|Gas leaks when sealing flask|Pre-assemble apparatus; inject acid by syringe through a rubber stopper|Eliminates systematic gas loss; improves validity|
|Mg measured by length|Weigh Mg on 3 d.p. balance (±0.001 g)|Reduces random error in IV control|
|Only 3 repeats at each concentration|Increase to 5 repeats|Improves reliability; easier to identify anomalies; better mean|
|Titration endpoint judged visually|Use a pH probe and data logger to plot a real-time pH curve|Eliminates subjective systematic error at endpoint; provides precise equivalence point|
|Temperature not measured|Add a thermometer to the reaction vessel and record temperature at start and end|Allows detection of temperature-driven rate variation|

### Extension examples — acids and bases unit

> ❌ "I could test temperature as well."
> 
> ✅ **7–8:** "As an extension, the effect of temperature on the rate of the $\text{Mg} + \text{HCl}$ reaction could be investigated at constant concentration (1.0 mol/L HCl), varying temperature from 10°C to 50°C. This would test whether the Arrhenius relationship applies — a graph of $\ln(\text{rate})$ vs $1/T$ should give a straight line if the reaction follows Arrhenius kinetics, and the slope would yield the activation energy $E_a$. This directly extends the current findings by isolating the temperature variable after concentration has been investigated."

> ✅ **7–8:** "An extension could compare the rates of reaction of $\text{HCl}$ and $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$ (ethanoic acid) at the same concentration with Mg. Since $\text{HCl}$ fully ionises and $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$ partially ionises, the $[\text{H}^+]$ in solution differs despite equal molar concentrations. Measuring and comparing reaction rates would directly test whether degree of ionisation — rather than total acid concentration — is the key factor driving reaction rate, extending the unit's conclusions on strong vs weak acids."

## ❌ Common Mistakes

> [!bug] Mistakes that cost marks
> 
> - **No data transformation** (e.g. no mean, no rate calculation) — caps at **5–6**
> - Describing the trend without **quoting specific values** — caps at **3–4**
> - Hypothesis evaluation that only says "supported" without citing data evidence
> - Method evaluation that says "there were some errors" without naming the error type or impact
> - Improvements not linked to a specific identified weakness
> - Extensions that are just another variable with no scientific justification
> - Anomalies ignored rather than identified, excluded, and explained
> - Graph with dot-to-dot joins instead of a best-fit line

## ✅ Checklist for 7–8

- [ ] Raw data table has title, headings, units, and all repeat trials
- [ ] Processed/transformed table present (mean, rate, moles, or other)
- [ ] Anomalies identified, excluded from mean, circled on graph, and explained
- [ ] Graph is correct type, fully labelled, best-fit line drawn
- [ ] Trend described with **specific data values** quoted
- [ ] Trend explained using a **named concept from this unit** (collision theory, ionisation, mole ratio, etc.)
- [ ] Hypothesis evaluated as supported/partially supported/refuted **with data as evidence**
- [ ] Discrepancies between prediction and result discussed
- [ ] **Specific** method weaknesses identified and error type stated (systematic/random)
- [ ] Impact of each weakness on results explained
- [ ] Each improvement directly addresses a named weakness
- [ ] At least one extension suggested with **chemical/scientific justification**
