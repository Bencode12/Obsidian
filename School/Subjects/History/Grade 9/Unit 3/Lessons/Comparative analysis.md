## ⚖️ Political Systems

> [!info] Definition A **political system** is the set of formal institutions, ideologies, and leadership structures through which a state exercises power and makes collective decisions.

### Russian Empire

The Russian Empire was an **absolute autocracy**, ruled by a Tsar with near-unlimited power. The political ideology rested on the conservative triad of _Orthodoxy, Autocracy, Nationality_, actively resisting liberal reform.

- **Government Structure:** Tsar Nicholas II held supreme executive, legislative, and judicial authority
- **Legislature:** The Duma (parliament) was created only after the 1905 Revolution — and remained weak
- **Ideology:** Conservative autocracy; Pan-Slavism and Russian nationalism as state ideologies
- **Control Mechanisms:** Secret police (Okhrana) suppressed dissent; censorship was widespread

### United States

The United States operated as a **federal constitutional republic** with a strong tradition of democratic governance, separation of powers, and individual rights enshrined in the Constitution and Bill of Rights.

- **Government Structure:** President, Congress (Senate + House of Representatives), and Supreme Court
- **Legislature:** Bicameral Congress with elected representatives from all states
- **Ideology:** Liberal democracy; belief in individual liberty, federalism, and checks & balances
- **Civil Liberties:** Free press and constitutional rights — though limited for minorities and women in practice

### Side-by-Side Comparison

|Category|Russian Empire|United States|
|---|---|---|
|System Type|Absolute Autocracy|Federal Constitutional Republic|
|Head of State|Tsar (hereditary monarch)|President (elected, 4-year term)|
|Legislature|Weak Duma (post-1905)|Strong bicameral Congress|
|Ideology|Autocracy, Orthodoxy, Nationalism|Liberal Democracy, Federalism|
|Civil Liberties|Severely restricted|Constitutionally protected (in principle)|
|Political Change|Resisted; reforms only under pressure|Regular elections; gradual reform|

## 💰 Economic Systems

> [!info] Definition An **economic system** is the set of institutions, policies, and relationships by which a society organises the production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services.

### Russian Empire

Russia remained predominantly **agrarian** well into the early 20th century. Industrialisation began in earnest in the 1890s under Finance Minister Sergei Witte, but the rural peasantry remained bound to outdated land systems.

- **Economy Type:** Primarily agricultural — over 80% of the population were peasants after emancipation (1861)
- **Industrialisation:** State-led rapid industrialisation in the 1890s (railways, metallurgy, oil)
- **Trade & Finance:** Relied on foreign loans (especially French capital) to fund industrial growth
- **Land System:** Land ownership concentrated among nobility; peasants paid heavy redemption fees

### United States

The United States experienced explosive growth during the **Gilded Age** (1870s–1900s), becoming the world's leading industrial power by 1900. Laissez-faire capitalism drove rapid but deeply unequal growth.

- **Economy Type:** Mixed capitalist economy with rapid industrialisation (steel, railroads, oil, finance)
- **Key Industries:** Dominated by powerful monopolies — Carnegie (steel), Rockefeller (oil)
- **Trade:** Major exporter of agricultural and manufactured goods; expanding global trade
- **Social Inequality:** Stark divide between industrial tycoons ("robber barons") and workers/immigrants

### Side-by-Side Comparison

|Category|Russian Empire|United States|
|---|---|---|
|Economic Base|Agriculture-dominant|Industrial capitalism|
|Industrialisation|State-led, late (1890s)|Private-led, rapid (1870s–1900s)|
|Key Sectors|Grain, oil, textiles, railways|Steel, oil, railroads, finance|
|Trade Policy|Protectionist; reliant on foreign loans|Expanding exports; protectionist tariffs|
|Labour System|Freed serfs; heavy taxation|Free labour; growing union movement|
|Economic Power|Regional; growing|Global leading economy by 1900|

## 🏘️ Social Systems

> [!info] Definition **Social systems** encompass the structures of class, religion, and social movements that organise relationships within a society and determine access to power and resources.

### Russian Empire

Russian society was highly stratified. The nobility (_dvoryanstvo_) held political and economic privilege, while the vast peasant class lived in poverty. The Russian Orthodox Church played a central role in daily life and legitimised Tsarist rule.

- **Social Hierarchy:** Tsar & royalty → Nobility → Clergy → Merchants → Peasants → Serfs (freed 1861)
- **Religion:** Russian Orthodox Church was the state religion; deeply embedded in culture and politics
- **Social Movements:** Growing revolutionary movements — Socialist Revolutionaries, Bolsheviks, Mensheviks
- **Ethnic Tensions:** Minorities (Poles, Jews, Ukrainians) faced discrimination and violent pogroms

### United States

American society was shaped by rapid immigration, racial segregation, and growing labour conflicts. Despite ideals of equality, deep divides existed along racial, class, and gender lines.

- **Social Hierarchy:** Wealthy industrialists → Middle class → Urban workers → Immigrants → African Americans
- **Religion:** Constitutionally secular; Protestant Christianity dominant culturally
- **Social Movements:** Populist movement, labour unions (AFL), women's suffrage movement, early civil rights
- **Racial Tensions:** Racial segregation enforced by Jim Crow laws; discrimination against immigrants

### Side-by-Side Comparison

|Category|Russian Empire|United States|
|---|---|---|
|Class Structure|Rigid aristocratic hierarchy|Class mobility (in theory); stark inequality|
|Dominant Religion|Russian Orthodox (state religion)|Protestant Christianity (cultural)|
|Treatment of Minorities|Pogroms, forced Russification|Jim Crow laws, nativist restrictions|
|Social Movements|Revolutionary socialist parties|Labour unions, suffragettes, populists|
|Role of Women|Limited rights; traditional roles|Growing suffrage movement; limited rights|
|Urbanisation|Mostly rural; growing industrial cities|Rapid urbanisation; massive immigration|

## 🌍 Foreign Policy

> [!info] Definition **Foreign policy** describes a state's strategy for managing relationships with other nations, including alliances, conflicts, diplomacy, and expansionist ambitions.

### Russian Empire

Russia pursued aggressive expansion driven by Pan-Slavism, the desire for warm-water ports, and rivalry with the Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, and Japanese empires. The humiliating defeat in the Russo-Japanese War (1905) exposed serious weaknesses.

- **Key Alliances:** Allied with France (1894) and later Britain (Triple Entente, 1907)
- **Expansionism:** Expansion into Central Asia ("The Great Game" with Britain) and the Far East
- **Ideology:** Pan-Slavism — Russia as protector of Slavic peoples in the Balkans
- **Key Conflict:** Defeat in Russo-Japanese War (1905) triggered revolutionary unrest at home

### United States

The United States shifted from isolationism toward imperialism during this period. The Spanish-American War (1898) marked a turning point, establishing the US as a Pacific and Caribbean power with overseas territories.

- **Key Doctrines:** Monroe Doctrine — asserted dominance over the Western Hemisphere
- **Expansionism:** Victory in Spanish-American War (1898) — gained Philippines, Cuba, Puerto Rico, Guam
- **Interventionism:** Roosevelt Corollary (1904): US right to intervene in Latin American affairs
- **Asian Relations:** Open Door Policy in China; growing competition with Japan in the Pacific

### Side-by-Side Comparison

|Category|Russian Empire|United States|
|---|---|---|
|Foreign Policy Stance|Aggressive expansionism (land-based)|Expanding imperialism (naval/overseas)|
|Key Alliances|France, Britain (Triple Entente)|No formal alliances (isolationism → imperialism)|
|Main Rivalries|Britain, Japan, Ottoman Empire|Spain (defeated 1898); growing Japan rivalry|
|Expansionist Drive|Warm ports, Pan-Slavism, Central Asia|Monroe Doctrine, Pacific, Caribbean|
|Key Conflict|Russo-Japanese War (1905) — **defeat**|Spanish-American War (1898) — **victory**|
|Nationalism Role|Pan-Slavism fuelled Balkan tensions|Manifest Destiny → overseas empire|

## 🎭 Identity

> [!info] Definition **National identity** encompasses the values, beliefs, cultural symbols, and self-perceptions that define how a nation sees itself — and how others perceive it.

### Russian Empire

Russian national identity was shaped by the trinity of _Orthodoxy, Autocracy, and Nationality_ — reinforcing loyalty to the Tsar, the Church, and the Russian ethnic core. Pan-Slavism promoted Russia as the natural leader of all Slavic peoples.

- **National Values:** "Holy Russia" — a special spiritual destiny to protect Orthodox Christianity
- **Perceptions & Stereotypes:** Seen externally as expansionist, backward, despotic, and mysterious ("the Russian soul")
- **Self-Image:** Noble suffering, resilience, and the vast land as a symbol of greatness
- **Treatment of Diversity:** Non-Russian minorities expected to assimilate (Russification policy)

### United States

American identity was built on Enlightenment ideals — liberty, democracy, and the "American Dream" — combined with a belief in Manifest Destiny: the conviction that the US was divinely chosen to expand and civilise.

- **National Values:** Liberty, individual rights, democracy, opportunity, self-reliance
- **Perceptions & Stereotypes:** Seen externally as dynamic, innovative, brash, wealthy, and increasingly imperialist
- **Self-Image:** "City upon a Hill" — a model nation; growing sense of American exceptionalism
- **Treatment of Diversity:** "Melting pot" ideal vs. reality of racial and ethnic discrimination

### Side-by-Side Comparison

|Category|Russian Empire|United States|
|---|---|---|
|Core National Values|Orthodoxy, Autocracy, Nationality|Liberty, Democracy, Opportunity|
|National Myth|"Holy Russia" — sacred destiny|"City on a Hill" — democratic beacon|
|Expansionist Justification|Pan-Slavism; Slavic brotherhood|Manifest Destiny; civilising mission|
|Self-Image|Resilient, spiritual, vast|Dynamic, free, exceptional|
|External Perception|Despotic, backward, threatening|Brash, wealthy, increasingly imperial|
|Diversity Policy|Russification of minorities|"Melting pot" — assimilation expected|
