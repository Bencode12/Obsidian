**Source:** Brooks, H., Richter, S. & Tanaka, A. (2020). "Evaluating Traditional Thermal Power Production." _Journal of Thermal Energy Systems._ Published March 4, 2020.

### How to write a full bibliography reference (Author–Date format):

> Brooks, H., Richter, S. and Tanaka, A. (2020). 'Evaluating Traditional Thermal Power Production.' _Journal of Thermal Energy Systems._ 4 March 2020.

### Rules for bibliography references

- Authors listed by **surname first**, then initials
- Multiple authors separated by commas; last two joined by "and"
- Year of publication in brackets
- Title of article in single quotation marks
- Name of journal in _italics_
- Full date if available
- If accessed online: include URL and access date

> [!tip] What gets underlined/italicised? The **journal name** (or book title) is italicised. Article titles are in quotation marks. Authors' surnames are not underlined in standard referencing — the worksheet's underlined surnames are just to identify who the authors are.

## 🌍 Part b — Environmental and Economic Implications

### Environmental Advantages

**1. High energy density — less land use than renewables** Fossil fuel power plants generate large amounts of electricity from relatively small physical footprints compared to equivalent wind or solar farms. A single coal plant can power a city without occupying hundreds of square kilometres.

**2. No intermittency — reliable baseload power** Unlike solar or wind, burning fuel generates electricity continuously regardless of weather or time of day. This means fewer backup systems are needed and natural habitats are not disrupted by large battery storage infrastructure.

### Environmental Disadvantages

**1. Greenhouse gas emissions — climate change** Combustion of coal and oil releases $\text{CO}_2$ and other greenhouse gases. Coal combustion: $\text{C} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2$. These gases trap heat in the atmosphere, contributing to global warming and climate change — rising sea levels, extreme weather events, and ecosystem disruption.

**2. Air and water pollution** Burning coal releases $\text{SO}_2$, $\text{NO}_x$, and particulate matter. $\text{SO}_2$ reacts with atmospheric water to form sulphuric acid ($\text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$, $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$), causing acid rain that damages ecosystems, forests, and water quality. Thermal pollution from cooling water discharge also raises river temperatures, harming aquatic life.

### Economic Advantages

**1. Established infrastructure — low transition costs** Existing thermal power plants are already built, staffed, and connected to electricity grids. Running them requires no large upfront capital investment. This is especially important for developing economies where electricity access is a priority.

**2. Relatively low and stable fuel costs (short-term)** Coal and oil have established global markets with predictable pricing. They can be stockpiled, giving energy security and price stability that variable renewables currently cannot fully guarantee.

### Economic Disadvantages

**1. Long-term rising costs — finite resource** Fossil fuels are non-renewable. As reserves become harder to extract, prices will rise. Countries dependent on imported fuel are also vulnerable to geopolitical price shocks (e.g. oil embargoes, sanctions).

**2. External costs — health and environmental damage** Air pollution from fossil fuel combustion causes respiratory diseases, cardiovascular illness, and premature deaths — generating enormous healthcare costs. Environmental clean-up (acid rain damage, oil spills, ash disposal) also represents a major unaccounted economic burden.

## 🗣️ Part c — Argument For or Against Phasing Out Coal and Oil

### Model paragraph — SUPPORTING the phase-out (7–8 level)

> Citizens should support phasing out coal- and oil-burning power plants within 15 years because the long-term environmental and public health costs of continued operation far outweigh the short-term economic convenience. Combustion of fossil fuels releases $\text{CO}_2$ — a greenhouse gas that traps infrared radiation in the atmosphere, driving global warming at a rate that threatens food security, coastal cities, and biodiversity. Additionally, emissions of $\text{SO}_2$ and $\text{NO}_x$ produce acid rain ($\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ and $\text{HNO}_3$ in the atmosphere), which damages ecosystems and human health. While the transition will require investment in renewable energy infrastructure such as wind turbines and solar panels, the technology already exists, costs have fallen dramatically, and the long-term savings in healthcare and environmental remediation make the phase-out economically rational as well as ethically necessary.

### Model paragraph — OPPOSING the phase-out

> Citizens should not support an unconditional 15-year phase-out of all coal- and oil-burning power plants, because the transition timeline is too short to guarantee energy security and economic stability for all citizens, particularly those on lower incomes. Electricity is a fundamental necessity — for heating homes, running hospitals, and powering industries. Premature closure of thermal power plants without fully functional replacement capacity risks blackouts and electricity price spikes that disproportionately affect the most vulnerable people. Furthermore, not all nations have equal access to renewable technology or the capital to finance rapid grid transformation. A more responsible approach would be a phased transition over 25–30 years, paired with investment in renewable infrastructure, energy storage, and retraining of workers currently employed in the fossil fuel industry.

> [!tip] Structure for 7–8
> 
> - State your position clearly
> - Explain the **scientific mechanism** behind your argument (include a reaction equation or physical principle)
> - Acknowledge a counter-argument and address it
> - Reach a clear conclusion

## ⚙️ Part d — How a Steam Turbine Power Plant Works

> [!info] The basic principle A steam turbine power plant converts **chemical energy** (in fuel) → **thermal energy** (heat) → **mechanical energy** (turbine rotation) → **electrical energy** (generator).

### Step-by-step explanation (7–8 level)

**1. Combustion (chemical → thermal energy)** Fuel (coal, oil, or natural gas) is burned in a furnace. The combustion reaction is exothermic — it releases a large amount of thermal energy. For coal: $\text{C} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 + \text{heat}$.

**2. Steam generation (thermal energy → internal energy of water)** The heat from combustion is used to boil water in a boiler. The water absorbs its latent heat of vaporisation ($L_v = 2.3 \times 10^6\ \text{J/kg}$ for water) and becomes high-pressure steam. The temperature and pressure of the steam are very high (typically 500–600°C, 150–300 bar).

**3. Turbine rotation (thermal/pressure energy → mechanical energy)** The high-pressure steam is directed at the blades of a turbine. The steam expands and its pressure drops — the kinetic energy of the steam molecules is transferred to the turbine blades, causing the turbine shaft to rotate at high speed.

**4. Electricity generation (mechanical → electrical energy)** The rotating turbine shaft drives a generator — a coil of wire rotating inside a magnetic field. By electromagnetic induction, this produces alternating current (AC) electricity, which is fed into the national grid.

**5. Condensation and recycling** After passing through the turbine, the low-pressure steam is cooled in a condenser (using river water or cooling towers) and returns to liquid water. This releases the latent heat of condensation. The liquid water is pumped back to the boiler and the cycle repeats.

### Energy flow summary

```
Chemical energy (fuel)
        ↓ combustion
Thermal energy (hot gases)
        ↓ boiler
Internal energy of steam (latent heat)
        ↓ turbine
Mechanical (kinetic) energy (rotating shaft)
        ↓ generator
Electrical energy → national grid
```

> [!warning] Energy losses at every stage At each conversion step, some energy is lost as waste heat to the surroundings. The overall thermal efficiency of a modern steam turbine plant is typically 35–45% — meaning more than half the chemical energy in the fuel is wasted as heat.

## 🔬 Strand iii — Scientific Language to Use

|Term|How to use it|
|---|---|
|**Combustion**|Exothermic chemical reaction of a fuel with oxygen|
|**Latent heat of vaporisation**|Energy needed to convert liquid to gas without temperature change|
|**Conduction / convection / radiation**|Three mechanisms of heat transfer|
|**Greenhouse gas**|Gas that absorbs and re-emits infrared radiation (e.g. $\text{CO}_2$, $\text{CH}_4$)|
|**Acid rain**|Precipitation with low pH due to $\text{SO}_2$ and $\text{NO}_x$ dissolving in atmospheric water|
|**Thermal efficiency**|Ratio of useful energy output to total energy input|
|**Electromagnetic induction**|Production of electric current by changing magnetic flux|
|**Exothermic**|Reaction that releases heat to the surroundings|
|**Non-renewable**|Resource that cannot be replenished on a human timescale|

## 📖 Strand iv — Complete Referencing Guide

### Rule: every factual claim from an external source must be cited

**In-text citation (Author–Date):**

> "Coal combustion releases approximately 820 grams of CO₂ per kilowatt-hour of electricity generated (IPCC, 2014)."

**Reference list — journal article:**

> Brooks, H., Richter, S. and Tanaka, A. (2020). 'Evaluating Traditional Thermal Power Production.' _Journal of Thermal Energy Systems._ 4 March 2020.

**Reference list — website:**

> International Energy Agency. (2023). 'Coal.' _IEA._ https://www.iea.org/fuels-and-technologies/coal. Accessed 18 March 2026.

**Reference list — book:**

> Atkins, P. and de Paula, J. (2014). _Physical Chemistry_ (10th ed.). Oxford University Press.

> [!warning] Completely document = every source At 7–8, there must be a reference list entry for **every** source cited in the text. In-text citations alone are not sufficient — you must also have the full reference list at the end.
