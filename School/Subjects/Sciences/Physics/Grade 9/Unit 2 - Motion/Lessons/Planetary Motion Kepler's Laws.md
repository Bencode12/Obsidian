# Planetary Motion & Kepler's Laws

## 📋 Learning Objectives

- [ ] Describe the motion of planets around the Sun
- [ ] State and apply Kepler's three laws
- [ ] Understand elliptical orbits
- [ ] Calculate orbital periods and speeds

---

## 🌍 Historical Background

### Geocentric Model (Ancient)
- Earth at center of universe
- Planets orbit Earth in perfect circles
- **Ptolemy** (2nd century AD)

### Heliocentric Model (1543)
- **Nicolaus Copernicus**: Sun at center
- Planets orbit Sun in circles
- Revolutionary idea!

### Tycho Brahe's Observations (1570-1600)
- Most accurate planetary observations (no telescope!)
- Data used by Kepler to discover laws

### Johannes Kepler (1609-1619)
- Analyzed Brahe's data
- Discovered three laws of planetary motion
- Orbits are **ellipses**, not circles!

---

## 📐 Kepler's Three Laws

### First Law (Law of Orbits) - 1609

**All planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.**

```
         Planet
           ●
          /   \
         /     \
    Sun ●-------●
   (focus)   (empty focus)
```

**Key Terms:**
- **Ellipse**: Oval-shaped path
- **Focus**: One of two special points
- **Perihelion**: Closest point to Sun
- **Aphelion**: Farthest point from Sun

---

### Second Law (Law of Areas) - 1609

**A line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times.**

```
        ● Planet (slow)
       /|\
      / | \
     /  |  \  Equal Areas
    /   |   \
Sun ●----|----\
          \
           \
            ● Planet (fast)
```

**Implications:**
- Planet moves **faster** when closer to Sun
- Planet moves **slower** when farther from Sun
- **Angular momentum is conserved**

---

### Third Law (Law of Periods) - 1619

**The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of its average distance from the Sun.**

```
T² ∝ r³

or

T²/r³ = constant
```

Where:
- **T** = Orbital period (years)
- **r** = Average distance from Sun (AU)

**For any two planets:**
```
T₁²/T₂² = r₁³/r₂³
```

## 🧮 Calculations

### Example 1: Using Third Law

**Problem:** Mars is 1.52 AU from the Sun. Calculate its orbital period.

**Solution:**
```
T² = r³ (for Earth, T=1, r=1)
T² = (1.52)³ = 3.51
T = √3.51 = 1.87 years
```

### Example 2: Comparing Two Planets

**Problem:** Jupiter is 5.2 AU from Sun. How long is one Jovian year?

**Solution:**
```
T²/r³ = constant
T_Jupiter² / r_Jupiter³ = T_Earth² / r_Earth³
T_J² / 5.2³ = 1² / 1³
T_J² = 5.2³ = 140.6
T_J = √140.6 = 11.86 years
```

### Example 3: Orbital Speed

**Problem:** Earth orbits at 1 AU (1.5×10¹¹ m) in 1 year. Calculate orbital speed.

**Solution:**
```
Circumference = 2πr = 2π(1.5×10¹¹) = 9.42×10¹¹ m
Time = 1 year = 365×24×3600 = 3.15×10⁷ s

v = distance/time = 9.42×10¹¹ / 3.15×10⁷ = 29,900 m/s ≈ 30 km/s
```

## 🌌 Applications

### 1. Satellite Orbits
- Geostationary satellites (T = 24 hours)
- GPS satellites (T = 12 hours)
- Moon orbits (T = 27.3 days)

### 2. Exoplanet Discovery
- Detect planets around other stars
- Measure orbital periods
- Calculate distances from star

### 3. Space Missions
- Planetary flybys
- Orbital insertion
- Hohmann transfer orbits

### 4. Comet Prediction
- Comets have highly elliptical orbits
- Can predict return dates
- Halley's Comet: T = 76 years

## ✏️ Practice Problems

### Problem 1
Saturn orbits at 9.54 AU. Calculate its orbital period.

**Answer:** 29.5 years

### Problem 2
An asteroid orbits the Sun at 2.8 AU. How long is its orbital period?

**Answer:** 4.68 years

### Problem 3
**Explain** why a planet moves faster when closer to the Sun.

**Answer:** Kepler's 2nd Law - equal areas in equal times requires faster motion at perihelion.

### Problem 4
If a planet has an orbital period of 8 years, what is its average distance from the Sun?

**Solution:**
```
T² = r³
8² = r³
64 = r³
r = ∛64 = 4 AU
```

## 📝 Summary

- **1st Law**: Orbits are ellipses, Sun at one focus
- **2nd Law**: Equal areas in equal times (faster near Sun)
- **3rd Law**: T² ∝ r³
- Applies to all orbiting bodies (planets, moons, satellites)
- Newton later explained WHY with gravity
