## 🔬 Phase Changes

### Melting (Fusion)
**Solid → Liquid**

- Particles gain energy
- Vibrate more vigorously
- Break free from fixed positions
- Temperature stays constant during melting

### Solidification (Freezing)
**Liquid → Solid**

- Particles lose energy
- Move closer together
- Form fixed positions
- Temperature stays constant during freezing

---

## 🌡️ Melting Point

### Definition

**Melting Point** is the temperature at which a solid becomes a liquid.

### Key Points

- **Melting point = Freezing point** for the same substance
- **Pure substances** have sharp, definite melting points
- **Impure substances** melt over a range of temperatures
- **Pressure affects** melting point (especially for ice)

### Melting Points of Common Substances

| Substance | Melting Point (°C) |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Ice (Water) | 0 |
| Aluminum | 660 |
| Iron | 1538 |
| Copper | 1085 |
| Lead | 327 |
| Ethanol | -114 |
| Oxygen | -219 |

---

## 🔥 Latent Heat of Fusion

### Definition

**Specific Latent Heat of Fusion (L)** is the energy needed to change 1 kg of solid to liquid at constant temperature.

### Units
- J/kg or kJ/kg

### Formula

```
Q = mL
```

Where:
- **Q** = Heat energy (J)
- **m** = Mass (kg)
- **L** = Specific latent heat of fusion (J/kg)

### Values for Common Substances

| Substance | L (kJ/kg) |
|-----------|-----------|
| Water/Ice | 334 |
| Aluminum | 397 |
| Copper | 205 |
| Lead | 23 |
| Ethanol | 104 |

## 📊 Heating and Cooling Curves

### Heating Curve for Ice → Water → Steam

```
Temperature
    ↑
    │                    ┌──────── Steam heating
100 │────────────┐      │
    │            │      │
    │  Water     │      │
    │  heating   │      │
  0 │────┐       │      │
    │    │       │      │
    │Ice │Melting │Boiling
    │heating       │
────┴────┴───────┴──────┴──→ Time
     A    B       C      D
```

**Sections:**
- **A**: Ice heating (temperature rises)
- **B**: Melting (temperature constant at 0°C)
- **C**: Water heating (temperature rises)
- **D**: Boiling (temperature constant at 100°C)

### Key Observations

1. **Temperature constant during phase change**
2. **Energy used to break bonds** (not increase temperature)
3. **Flat sections = latent heat being absorbed**

## 🧮 Energy Calculations

### Total Energy for Phase Change + Heating

**Example:** Heat 2 kg of ice from -10°C to water at 50°C

**Steps:**
1. Heat ice from -10°C to 0°C
2. Melt ice at 0°C
3. Heat water from 0°C to 50°C

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Q₁ = mcΔT = 2 × 2100 × 10 = 42,000 J
Step 2: Q₂ = mL = 2 × 334,000 = 668,000 J
Step 3: Q₃ = mcΔT = 2 × 4200 × 50 = 420,000 J

Total: Q = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃ = 1,130,000 J = 1130 kJ
```

## 🌍 Applications

### 1. Ice Skating
- Pressure melts ice under blade
- Thin water layer reduces friction
- Water refreezes behind blade

### 2. Road Salt in Winter
- Salt lowers melting point of ice
- Ice melts at temperatures below 0°C
- Prevents ice formation

### 3. Cooling Systems
- Ice packs absorb latent heat
- Keeps food/medicine cold
- Phase change materials in buildings

### 4. Metal Casting
- Melt metal at high temperature
- Pour into mold
- Solidifies into shape

## ✏️ Practice Problems

### Problem 1
Calculate energy to melt 5 kg of ice at 0°C. (L = 334 kJ/kg)

**Solution:**
```
Q = mL = 5 × 334,000 = 1,670,000 J = 1670 kJ
```

### Problem 2
How much energy is released when 2 kg of water freezes at 0°C?

**Solution:**
```
Q = mL = 2 × 334,000 = 668,000 J
(Released, so Q = -668 kJ)
```

### Problem 3
**Explain** why ice is more effective at cooling drinks than cold water at 0°C.

**Answer:** Ice absorbs latent heat (334 kJ/kg) while melting, plus additional energy as it warms up. Cold water only absorbs energy as it warms.

## 📝 Summary

- Melting: solid → liquid at melting point
- Solidification: liquid → solid at freezing point
- Temperature constant during phase change
- Latent heat of fusion: Q = mL
- Ice: L = 334 kJ/kg
- Heating curves show flat sections during phase changes
