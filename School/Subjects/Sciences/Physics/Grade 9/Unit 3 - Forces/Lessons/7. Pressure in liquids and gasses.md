# Pascal's Law

## Core Principles

- Liquids and gases exert pressure in **all directions equally** (isotropic pressure).
- **Pascal's Law** states that when there is an increase in pressure at any point in a confined fluid, there is an **equal increase at every other point** in the container.
- This applies only to **static, incompressible fluids** in a closed system.

---

## Pressure Formula

$$F = P \cdot S$$

Where:
- $F$ — force [N]
- $P$ — pressure [Pa]
- $S$ — surface area [m²]

Rearranged: $P = \dfrac{F}{S}$

---

## Applications of Pascal's Law

### Toothpaste
Squeezing the tube at any point increases pressure uniformly throughout the fluid, pushing paste out of the nozzle — a direct demonstration of Pascal's Law.

### Hydraulic Lift
Uses an incompressible fluid to multiply force. A small force applied to a small piston creates pressure that is transmitted equally to a larger piston, producing a greater output force.

$$\frac{F_1}{S_1} = \frac{F_2}{S_2} \implies F_2 = F_1 \cdot \frac{S_2}{S_1}$$

- $F_1$ — input force [N]
- $S_1$ — area of small piston [m²]
- $F_2$ — output force [N]
- $S_2$ — area of large piston [m²]

**Mechanical advantage** = $\dfrac{S_2}{S_1}$ — the larger the area ratio, the greater the force multiplication.

> Real-world examples: car jacks, hydraulic brakes, excavator arms, dentist chairs.

## Pressure at Different Depths

### Formula

$$p = \rho \cdot g \cdot h$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Unit |
|--------|---------|------|
| $p$ | pressure | Pa |
| $\rho$ | density of fluid | kg/m³ |
| $g$ | freefall acceleration ($\approx 9.81$) | m/s² |
| $h$ | height (depth) of liquid | m |

### Derivation

$$P = \frac{F}{S} \implies F = mg \implies P = \frac{mg}{S}$$

$$m = \rho V \implies P = \frac{\rho V g}{S}$$

$$V = S \cdot h \implies P = \frac{\rho \cdot S \cdot h \cdot g}{S} \implies \boxed{P = \rho g h}$$

The cross-sectional area $S$ cancels out — pressure depends only on depth, not the shape or volume of the container.

### Key Observations
- Pressure **increases with depth**.
- Pressure is **independent of container shape** (e.g., a narrow tube and a wide tank have the same pressure at the same depth if filled with the same fluid).
- Total pressure including atmospheric pressure: $p_{total} = p_0 + \rho g h$, where $p_0 \approx 101{,}325\ \text{Pa}$.

## Communicating Vessels

### Principle
In communicating vessels, a **stationary, homogeneous** liquid always settles to the **same level** in all connected chambers, regardless of their shape or cross-section.

### Different Liquids
When two immiscible liquids of different densities occupy communicating vessels, their column heights are **inversely proportional** to their densities:

$$\rho_1 g h_1 = \rho_2 g h_2$$

$$\boxed{\frac{h_1}{h_2} = \frac{\rho_2}{\rho_1}}$$

- The **denser** liquid has a **shorter** column.
- The **less dense** liquid has a **taller** column.

> **Example:** Water ($\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3$) and oil ($\rho = 800\ \text{kg/m}^3$) in a U-tube. The oil column will be $\frac{1000}{800} = 1.25\times$ taller than the water column.

### Real-World Applications
- Ship locks and canal systems
- Artesian wells (underground water pressure)
- U-tube manometers (measuring pressure difference)
- Blood pressure measurement
