## ⚗️ What is Neutralisation?

> [!info] Neutralisation A **neutralisation reaction** occurs when an acid and a base react to form a **salt** and **water**, through the combination of $\text{H}^+$ ions and $\text{OH}^-$ ions. $$\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

The resulting pH depends on the relative strengths of the acid and base:

|Combination|Resulting pH|
|---|---|
|Strong acid + Strong base|**= 7** (neutral)|
|Strong acid + Weak base|**< 7** (acidic)|
|Weak acid + Strong base|**> 7** (basic)|

---

## 📋 General Equations Summary

|Reaction Type|General Equation|Products|
|---|---|---|
|Acid + Metal hydroxide|$\text{Acid} + \text{Base} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$|Salt + Water|
|Acid + Metal oxide|$\text{Acid} + \text{Metal oxide} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$|Salt + Water|
|Acid + Metal carbonate|$\text{Acid} + \text{Carbonate} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$|Salt + $\text{CO}_2$ + Water|
|Acid + Salt|Condition: precipitate, gas, or weak acid formed|New salt + new acid|
|Acid + Metal|$\text{Acid} + \text{Metal} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{H}_2$|Salt + Hydrogen gas|

## 🔬 Acid + Metal Hydroxide

**Molecular, ionic, and net ionic forms — Al(OH)₃ + HCl:**

$$\text{Al(OH)}_{3(s)} + 3\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{AlCl}_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{Al(OH)}_{3(s)} + 3\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + 3\text{Cl}^-_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Al}^{3+}_{(aq)} + 3\text{Cl}^-_{(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{Al(OH)}_{3(s)} + 3\text{H}^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Al}^{3+}_{(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

**Further examples:**

$$\text{HCl}_{(aq)} + \text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{NaCl}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{KOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{K}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} + \text{Ca(OH)}_{2(aq)} \rightarrow \text{CaCl}_{2(aq)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{Ba(OH)}_{2(aq)} + \text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{BaCl}_{2(aq)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{Cr(OH)}_{3(s)} + 3\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{CrCl}_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

## 🔥 Acid + Metal Oxide

Metal oxides are **basic oxides** — they react with acids in the same way as hydroxides, but produce only salt + water (no other products).

$$\text{MgO}_{(s)} + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{MgCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

Ionic equation: $$\text{MgO}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + 2\text{Cl}^-_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Mg}^{2+}_{(aq)} + 2\text{Cl}^-_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

Net ionic: $$\text{MgO}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Mg}^{2+}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

---

$$\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_{3(s)} + 6\text{HNO}_{3(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Fe(NO}_3)_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

Ionic: $$\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_{3(s)} + 6\text{H}^+_{(aq)} + 6\text{NO}_3^-_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Fe}^{3+}_{(aq)} + 6\text{NO}_3^-_{(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

Net ionic: $$\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_{3(s)} + 6\text{H}^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Fe}^{3+}_{(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

**More examples:**

$$\text{FeO}_{(s)} + \text{HNO}_{3(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Fe(NO}_3)_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{Al}_2\text{O}_{3(s)} + 6\text{HNO}_{3(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{Al(NO}_3)_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{ZnO}_{(s)} + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{ZnCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

## 🫧 Acid + Metal Carbonate

Carbonate + acid produces salt, **carbon dioxide gas**, and water. The fizzing of $\text{CO}_2$ is a visible sign this reaction is occurring.

$$2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_{3(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{NaCl}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} + \text{CO}_{2(g)} \uparrow$$

$$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_{3(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} + \text{CO}_{2(g)} \uparrow$$

$$\text{K}_2\text{CO}_{3(aq)} + 2\text{HNO}_{3(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{KNO}_{3(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} + \text{CO}_{2(g)} \uparrow$$

$$\text{MgCO}_{3(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{MgSO}_{4(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} + \text{CO}_{2(g)} \uparrow$$

> [!tip] Test for $\text{CO}_2$ Pass the gas through limewater ($\text{Ca(OH)}_2$ solution) — it turns **milky white** as $\text{CaCO}_3$ precipitates.

## 🧂 Acid + Salt

Acids react with salts **only if** a precipitate ($\downarrow$), gas ($\uparrow$), or weak acid is formed. This condition must be met for the reaction to occur.

**Forming a precipitate:** $$\text{BaCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{BaSO}_{4(s)} \downarrow + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)}$$

Net ionic: $$\text{Ba}^{2+}_{(aq)} + \text{SO}_4^{2-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{BaSO}_{4(s)} \downarrow$$

**Displacing a weak acid (as gas):** $$2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} + \text{Na}_2\text{S}_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{NaCl}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{S}_{(g)} \uparrow$$

$$\text{HCl}_{(aq)} + \text{AgNO}_{3(aq)} \rightarrow \text{AgCl}_{(s)} \downarrow + \text{HNO}_{3(aq)}$$

$$\text{HCl}_{(aq)} + \text{NaF}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{NaCl}_{(aq)} + \text{HF}_{(aq)} \quad \text{(weak acid formed)}$$

> [!note] Why does a stronger acid displace a weaker one? A **stronger acid** (more fully ionised, more $\text{H}^+$) displaces a **weaker acid** from its salt. The driving force is the formation of the more stable, less ionised product.

## ⚙️ Acid + Metal

$$\text{Metal} + \text{Acid} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{H}_2 \uparrow$$

$$\text{Mg}_{(s)} + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{MgCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} \uparrow$$

$$\text{Zn}_{(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_{4(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} \uparrow$$

$$\text{Al}_{(s)} + 3\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{AlCl}_{3(aq)} + \frac{3}{2}\text{H}_{2(g)} \uparrow$$

## 🌍 Neutralisation in Real Life

### At Home

- **Toothpaste** contains bases ($\text{Na}_2\text{HPO}_4$, sodium bicarbonate) that neutralise the acid produced by mouth bacteria — protecting enamel
- **Baking powder** ($\text{NaHCO}_3$ + acid) releases $\text{CO}_2$ when wet and heated, causing cakes to rise — also used to treat bee and ant stings (acidic)
- **Conditioner** balances hair pH after alkaline shampoo, preventing scale formation on the hair cuticle
- **Vinegar** (acetic acid) is used to treat alkaline wasp stings

### In Agriculture

- **Acidic soil** treated with powdered lime ($\text{CaO}$), limestone ($\text{CaCO}_3$), or wood ash — raises pH to a neutral range suitable for most crops
- **Basic soil** neutralised by decomposing compost, which produces acidic gases

### In Industry

- **Acidic factory waste water** is treated with lime ($\text{CaO}$) before discharge
- **$\text{SO}_2$** from power stations neutralised with lime before gas is released into the atmosphere: $$\text{CaO} + \text{SO}_2 \rightarrow \text{CaSO}_3$$
- **Ammonia ($\text{NH}_3$)** prevents coagulation of latex by neutralising acids produced by bacteria in raw latex

### In Health and Medicine

- **Antacids** contain $\text{Al(OH)}_3$ and $\text{Mg(OH)}_2$ — neutralise excess stomach acid ($\text{HCl}$): $$\text{Al(OH)}_3 + 3\text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{AlCl}_3 + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}$$
- **Bee stings** (acidic) treated with baking soda
- **Wasp stings** (alkaline) treated with vinegar
