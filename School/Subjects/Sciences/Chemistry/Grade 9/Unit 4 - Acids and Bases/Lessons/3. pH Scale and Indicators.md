## рџ§Є Acids and Bases вЂ” Definitions

> [!info] Acid An acid is a molecule or ion capable of **donating a hydrogen ion** ($\text{H}^+$). In aqueous solution, acids increase the concentration of $\text{H}^+$ ions. $$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \rightarrow 2\text{H}^+ + \text{SO}_4^{2-}$$ $$\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4 \rightarrow 3\text{H}^+ + \text{PO}_4^{3-}$$

> [!info] Base A base is a compound that increases the $\text{OH}^-$ ion concentration in aqueous solution. $$\text{Ca(OH)}_2 \rightarrow \text{Ca}^{2+} + 2\text{OH}^-$$ $$\text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+ + \text{OH}^-$$

Strong acids: $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$, $\text{HNO}_3$, $\text{HCl}$

Weak acids: $\text{HF}$, $\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$, $\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4$, $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$, $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$

Alkalis (soluble bases): $\text{NaOH}$, $\text{Ba(OH)}_2$, $\text{KOH}$

Insoluble hydroxides: $\text{Fe(OH)}_3$, $\text{Cu(OH)}_2$, $\text{Ni(OH)}_2$

## рџ’§ Water Autoionisation

Pure water is not completely neutral вЂ” it undergoes a tiny degree of self-ionisation:

$$\text{H}_2\text{O}_{\text{(l)}} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{OH}^-_{\text{(aq)}}$$

At 25В°C, the concentration of both $\text{H}^+$ and $\text{OH}^-$ in pure water is exactly $10^{-7}$ mol/L, giving a pH of 7 вЂ” this is the definition of **neutral**.

## рџ“Џ The pH Scale

The **pH scale** ranks solutions by acidity or alkalinity on a scale from 0 to 14 (and beyond in extreme cases).

|pH Range|Classification|$[\text{H}^+]$ relative to water|
|---|---|---|
|0 вЂ“ 6|**Acidic**|Higher than pure water|
|7|**Neutral**|Equal to pure water|
|8 вЂ“ 14|**Basic (Alkaline)**|Lower than pure water|

> [!tip] Key relationships
> 
> - **Low pH = high $[\text{H}^+]$ = acidic**
> - **High pH = high $[\text{OH}^-]$ = basic**
> - An acidic solution has more $\text{H}^+$ than $\text{OH}^-$
> - A basic solution has more $\text{OH}^-$ than $\text{H}^+$

## рџ”ў pH and Concentration вЂ” The Formula

$$\boxed{pH = -\log c(\text{H}^+)}$$

where $c(\text{H}^+)$ is the concentration of hydrogen ions in mol/L.

|$c(\text{H}^+)$ (mol/L)|pH|
|---|---|
|$10^{-1}$ = 0.1|1|
|$10^{-3}$ = 0.001|3|
|$10^{-5}$|5|
|$10^{-7}$|7 (neutral)|
|$10^{-11}$|11|

> [!warning] Every 1 unit change in pH = 10Г— change in $[\text{H}^+]$ pH 3 is **10 times** more acidic than pH 4, and **100 times** more acidic than pH 5.

> [!example] Worked example If $c(\text{H}^+) = 10^{-5}$ mol/L, then $\text{pH} = -\log(10^{-5}) = 5$

## рџЊї Natural Indicators

Many plants contain pigments that behave like pH indicators:

- **Red cabbage** вЂ” turns red in acid, green/yellow in alkali
- **Turmeric** вЂ” yellow in acid, red/brown in alkali
- **Hydrangea flowers** вЂ” colour changes with soil pH (blue in acidic soil, pink in alkaline)
- **Beetroot**, **blackberries**, **tea** вЂ” all show colour shifts with pH

## рџЊЌ Real-Life Applications

### pH in the Human Mouth

The pH of the human mouth changes throughout the day, especially after eating. After a meal, bacteria produce acids that lower oral pH. Prolonged low pH causes **tooth enamel erosion** (enamel dissolves below pH ~5.5).

Toothpaste is a **weak base** вЂ” regular brushing neutralises the acid produced by bacteria, protecting enamel.

### Carbonated Water

Sparkling water contains dissolved $\text{CO}_2$, which forms **carbonic acid**: $$\text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$$ This gives it a slightly acidic pH (~5вЂ“6). A drop placed on universal indicator paper would shift it towards the acidic (yellow/orange) range.

### Soil pH and Plant Growth

Different plants thrive at different soil pH levels. Acidic soil can be treated with **lime** ($\text{CaO}$) or **limestone** ($\text{CaCO}_3$) to raise pH. Alkaline soil can be acidified with sulphur compounds or organic matter.
