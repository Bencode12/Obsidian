## 📌 What is Criterion D?

> [!info] Criterion D — Reflecting on the Impacts of Science Criterion D assesses your ability to **evaluate how science and technology affect the world** — including society, the environment, economy, and ethics. You must explain how acid/base chemistry is applied to a real problem, discuss its implications across different factors, use precise scientific language throughout, and correctly reference all sources. **Maximum: 8 marks**

|Strand|What you must do at 7–8|
|---|---|
|**i**|**Explain** the ways in which science is applied to address a specific problem or issue|
|**ii**|**Discuss and evaluate** the implications of using science, interacting with a **factor**|
|**iii**|**Consistently** apply scientific language clearly and precisely|
|**iv**|**Completely** document all sources|

## Strand i — How Science is Applied

You must explain the **scientific mechanism** — not just name the application.

### What "explain" requires

- State what the application is and what problem it solves
- Explain **how** the science works (the chemistry behind it)
- Include relevant equations where appropriate
- Connect the science to the specific real-world context

### Strong vs weak example

> ❌ **1–2:** "Acids are used in industry to make fertilisers."
> 
> ✅ **7–8:** "Sulphuric acid ($\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$) is used in the production of phosphate fertilisers through the reaction with calcium phosphate: $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 + \text{Ca}_3(\text{PO}_4)_2 \rightarrow 2\text{CaSO}_4 + 2\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4$. The $\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4$ produced reacts further with phosphate rock to form superphosphate, which is water-soluble and therefore bioavailable to plant roots. This process addresses the global problem of food insecurity by dramatically increasing crop yields through the supply of bioavailable phosphorus, a macronutrient essential for plant cell growth and DNA synthesis."

## Strand ii — Implications and Factors

You must discuss **both positive and negative** implications, linked to at least one named factor, then **evaluate** which implications are most significant.

### The Factors

|Factor|Examples relevant to acids and bases|
|---|---|
|**Environmental**|Acid rain damaging ecosystems; eutrophication from fertiliser runoff; $\text{SO}_2$ / $\text{NO}_x$ emissions|
|**Economic**|Industrial acid production; agricultural yield and food costs; medical costs of acid burns|
|**Social / Health**|Antacid use; dental erosion from acidic drinks; stomach acid regulation; occupational hazards|
|**Ethical**|Use of toxic acids in mining; hazardous waste disposal; equitable access to medicine|
|**Political**|Emissions regulations; international acid rain treaties; industrial discharge laws|

### What "evaluate" requires at 7–8

Evaluation is a **reasoned judgement** — it is not simply listing pros and cons. You must:

- Compare the significance of different implications
- State which implication is most serious or most beneficial, and why
- Consider trade-offs between factors (e.g. economic benefit vs environmental cost)
- Reach an overall conclusion with a recommendation or forward-looking statement

---

## The Factor Framework

Use this structure to plan any Criterion D response:

```
1. State the specific problem or issue
2. Explain the science / application (Strand i — include equations)
3. Factor 1 (e.g. Environmental):
   → Positive implication + scientific explanation
   → Negative implication + scientific explanation
4. Factor 2 (e.g. Economic/Health):
   → Positive implication + scientific explanation
   → Negative implication + scientific explanation
5. Evaluation:
   → Which implication is most significant? Why?
   → Trade-off between factors
   → Overall conclusion / recommendation
6. References (Strand iv)
```

## ✏️ Worked Example — Acid Rain

**Issue:** The formation and environmental impact of acid rain from industrial $\text{SO}_2$ and $\text{NO}_x$ emissions.

### Strand i — How the science is applied / how it works

When sulphur dioxide and nitrogen oxides are released by power stations and vehicle exhausts, they dissolve in atmospheric water vapour to form sulphurous and sulphuric acids, and nitric acid:

$$\text{SO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$$ $$\text{SO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$$ $$\text{N}_2\text{O}_5 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\text{HNO}_3$$

This lowers the pH of rainfall — sometimes to pH 4 or below — which then falls on ecosystems, soil, and built structures. To reduce this, industrial scrubbers neutralise acidic exhaust gases with calcium oxide before release:

$$\text{CaO} + \text{SO}_2 \rightarrow \text{CaSO}_3$$

### Strand ii — Implications (Environmental and Economic factors)

**Environmental — negative:** Acid rain lowers the pH of lakes and rivers, increasing $[\text{H}^+]$ and dissolving aluminium ions ($\text{Al}^{3+}$) from soil into waterways. At pH below 5, most aquatic organisms cannot survive — $\text{Al}^{3+}$ is directly toxic to fish gills, reducing oxygen uptake. Forest dieback occurs as acidified soil leaches essential minerals such as calcium and magnesium that trees require for growth. These effects are largely **irreversible** on human timescales.

**Environmental — positive (of mitigation):** Neutralisation technology (lime scrubbers, catalytic converters) has significantly reduced $\text{SO}_2$ emissions in many countries since the 1980s, allowing partial recovery of some acidified lake ecosystems. Liming of acidified lakes ($\text{CaCO}_3$ added directly) has successfully raised pH in several Scandinavian countries.

**Economic — negative:** Acid rain corrodes limestone and metal structures: $\text{CaCO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \rightarrow \text{CaSO}_4 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2$, causing billions in annual repair costs to heritage buildings, bridges, and infrastructure. Agricultural losses from soil acidification and reduced crop yields add further economic burden.

**Economic — positive:** Industries that produce $\text{SO}_2$ as a by-product (power generation, smelting) benefit economically from the activity generating it. Installation of scrubbers carries high capital cost, which may disproportionately burden developing nations.

### Evaluation

While both factors carry significant implications, the **environmental damage** is arguably more critical overall: it is largely irreversible (ecosystem collapse, soil mineral depletion), crosses national borders, and threatens biodiversity that has no economic substitute. The economic costs of mitigation — though substantial — are finite and recoverable, whereas the ecological losses from acidified lakes and forests may persist for generations. The most defensible course of action is strict international regulation of $\text{SO}_2$ / $\text{NO}_x$ emissions at source, combined with continued investment in neutralisation technology, rather than addressing only the downstream effects.

## ✏️ Worked Example — Neutralisation in Medicine (Antacids)

**Issue:** The use of acid–base chemistry to treat excess stomach acid (hyperacidity).

### Strand i

The stomach produces hydrochloric acid ($\text{HCl}$, approximately 0.1 mol/L, pH ~1–2) to aid digestion by denaturing proteins and activating pepsin. Excess acid production causes heartburn, gastritis, and peptic ulcers. Antacid tablets contain bases — typically aluminium hydroxide ($\text{Al(OH)}_3$), magnesium hydroxide ($\text{Mg(OH)}_2$), or calcium carbonate ($\text{CaCO}_3$) — which neutralise the excess $\text{HCl}$ through acid–base reactions:

$$\text{Al(OH)}_{3(s)} + 3\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{AlCl}_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{CaCO}_{3(s)} + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{CaCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} + \text{CO}_2\uparrow$$

This raises the stomach pH, reducing the corrosive effect of excess acid on the stomach lining.

### Strand ii — Implications (Health and Economic factors)

**Health — positive:** Antacids provide rapid, accessible relief from hyperacidity symptoms. $\text{Mg(OH)}_2$ and $\text{Al(OH)}_3$ are poorly absorbed from the gut, reducing systemic side effects. Neutralising excess $\text{HCl}$ prevents erosion of the stomach lining, reducing the risk of peptic ulcer complications including haemorrhage.

**Health — negative:** Overuse of antacids containing $\text{Al(OH)}_3$ can cause aluminium accumulation, which has been associated with neurological effects. $\text{CaCO}_3$-based antacids can cause "acid rebound" — stimulating additional acid production — meaning symptoms return after the antacid wears off. Long-term antacid use may mask symptoms of more serious conditions (e.g. stomach cancer), delaying diagnosis.

**Economic — positive:** Antacids are low-cost and available over the counter globally, making them economically accessible compared to prescription alternatives (e.g. proton pump inhibitors).

**Economic — negative:** The global antacid market exceeds $10 billion per year — in part because antacids treat symptoms rather than underlying causes (diet, stress, H. pylori infection), creating a cycle of repeat purchases.

### Evaluation

The health benefits of antacids are clear and well-evidenced for short-term use. However, the most significant concern is the potential for long-term use to mask serious underlying pathology — a risk that outweighs the economic accessibility argument. The most responsible approach is to use antacids as short-term relief while addressing root causes through dietary modification, stress reduction, and, where indicated, antibiotic treatment of H. pylori infection.

### Chemistry vocabulary to use in Criterion D responses

|Term|How to use it correctly|
|---|---|
|Dissociation / ionisation|"HCl fully dissociates in water: $\text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{H}^+ + \text{Cl}^-$"|

|Eutrophication|"Excess phosphate causes eutrophication — algal blooms deplete dissolved oxygen"|

|Bioavailable|"Superphosphate fertiliser is water-soluble and therefore bioavailable to plant roots"|

## ❌ Common Mistakes

> [!bug] Mistakes that cost marks
> 
> - Saying "acids have positive and negative effects" without naming a **specific factor** — caps at **1–2**
> - Listing implications without **evaluating** which is most significant — caps at **5–6**
> - Using vague language: "it is bad for nature" instead of specific scientific terminology
> - Including reactions or formulas without **explaining** what they mean in context
> - Forgetting the reference list — costs Strand iv marks regardless of essay quality
> - Discussing only one side (only benefits or only drawbacks)
> - Using informal language or hedging without evidence: "it might be bad"
